## Abstract (verbatim)

> Although radiographic findings at the apical portion of the proximal sesamoid bone (PSB) are often observed in young Thoroughbred foals, conflicting findings, either fractures or apparent secondary ossifications centers, have been reported. Three cases (aged 2, 5, and 7 weeks) were identified in 30 necropsied foals (0–31 weeks old). Histopathologically, the subchondral trabecular woven bone was fractured and exhibited focal necrosis of woven bone, fibrin exudate, and/or fibrosis within the foci. In the 7-week-old case, proliferations of chondrocytes were also observed. These findings suggest that the radiographic findings represented a healing process of the apical PSB fractures associated with the mechanically damaged subchondral trabeculae. Developmental PSB injuries should be taken into consideration during the management of young Thoroughbred foals.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

This paper belongs to feature family **A (injury/soundness)** and specifically to the sub-family of foal-period developmental musculoskeletal injuries that predispose horses to adult breakdown. Apical fracture of the proximal sesamoid bone (PSB) of the forelimb is the **most common forelimb fracture in adult racehorses**, and this paper demonstrates that histopathologically confirmed PSB apical fractures already occur at 2–7 weeks of age in 10% of foals — well before the horse enters training. This establishes a developmental injury trajectory relevant to adult fracture risk.

For the finishing-position pipeline, PSB fractures most directly affect the **scratch/non-starter risk** feature family: horses with unresolved or severe foal-period PSB injury face elevated re-injury risk during high-intensity galloping, which can manifest as sudden performance drop, absence from race records, or career termination. If foal radiographic history is available from JBBA sales repositories, the presence of a PSB apical finding in the forelimb should be flagged as a developmental injury indicator interacting with age (most vulnerable: 2–4-year-olds), surface type (higher VGRF on firm/rough tracks amplifies fracture risk per 27_1514), and cumulative starts.

The paper resolves a longstanding controversy: the radiolucent PSB apical line in foals had been debated as either traumatic fracture or normal secondary ossification centre. The histopathological evidence (focal woven-bone necrosis, fibrin, fibrosis, soft callus) confirms **traumatic origin** in all three examined cases, meaning there is no benign "normal variant" interpretation for these findings.

The link between foal-period PSB injury and adult fracture is indirect but plausible: incomplete healing of apical PSB fractures in foals may produce structural weaknesses (fibrous tissue, reduced trabecular density) that — combined with racing loads — predispose to adult sesamoid fracture. The companion paper 27_1514 notes that PSB fractures are among the most common catastrophic outcomes in JRA racing.

## Background & objective

Equine PSBs are small, paired, almond-shaped bones on the palmar/plantar surface of the fetlock joints. They form a critical component of the suspensory apparatus together with the suspensory and sesamoidean ligaments. Apical PSB fracture of the forelimb is the most common injury in adult racehorses, caused by combined fetlock hyperextension and suspensory tensile forces. PSB ossification begins late in gestation (290–330 days) and is not fully mineralised until 3 months post-partum. In young foals, the poorly developed PSB is vulnerable to tensile forces when foals sprint with mares. Radiographic findings (radiolucent lines) at the apical PSB in foals were observed in 10.7% in a 2006 German study (Kroll et al.), but the histopathological nature — fracture vs. secondary ossification centre — remained unresolved. The objective was to characterise the histopathology of these radiographic findings to determine their true nature.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 30 Thoroughbred foals aged 0–31 weeks, all of which died or were euthanised for causes unrelated to PSB fracture in dissection rooms at Hidaka Training and Research Center, JRA. Three foals (ages 2, 5, and 7 weeks) exhibited radiographic findings and were subject to full histopathological workup.

**Radiography:** Digital radiography system (AeroDR1012, Konica Minolta, Tokyo). All four limbs of each foal examined for PSB apical findings. Confirmed positive cases subjected to additional views to determine medial vs. lateral PSB involvement.

**Histopathology:** Macroscopic examination of PSB tissue, followed by haematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining of serial sections. Microscopic assessment at low and high magnification to characterise necrosis, fibrin, fibrosis, osteoclast activity, cartilage integrity, and presence of soft callus.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

**Prevalence:** Radiographic findings at apical PSB present in 3/30 (10.0%) necropsied foals. Comparable to Kroll et al. 2006 (14/130 = 10.7%). Note: foals in this study included only deceased animals, some of which died without exercise; incidence in healthy, active foals may be higher.

**Case 1 (2-week female):**

- Macroscopy: micro-focal linear lesion within apical portion of medial PSB, left forelimb. Palmar fibrocartilage and dorsal hyaline cartilage intact.
- Histopathology: centrally located necrotised eosinophilic trabecular bones and fibrin. No fibrosis in necrotic area; mild proliferation of fibroblasts in surrounding trabecular lumen.
- Additional finding: radiographically normal lateral PSBs of both hindlimbs showed a linear cleft histologically at the subchondral boundary, filled with fibrin — confirming subtle injuries in radiographically normal PSBs.

**Case 2 (5-week male):**

- Macroscopy: linear lesions in both lateral PSBs (left and right forelimbs), more obvious than Case 1.
- Histopathology (left forelimb PSB): elongated linear-to-zonal focus reaching the cartilage layer. Cartilage necrotised (lost stainability). Thin fibrinous layer with micro-focal necrotised trabeculae surrounded by sparsely proliferated immature fibroblasts and connective tissue. Multiple micro-focal haemorrhages in connective tissue.
- Histopathology (right forelimb PSB): densely proliferated immature fibroblasts and connective tissue. Osteoclasts increased in number around the focus.

**Case 3 (7-week female):**

- Macroscopy: cicatricial lines in medial and lateral PSBs of left forelimb.
- Histopathology (medial PSB): focus filled with loose connective tissue and matured fibroblasts arrayed along vertical axis.
- Histopathology (lateral PSB): higher densities of proliferated connective tissue and fibroblasts than medial PSB. Proliferation of chondrocytes within fibroblast tissue — indicating soft callus formation, confirming that appreciable time had elapsed since injury occurred.

**Severity gradient:** mild linear lesion (Case 1, 2 weeks) → moderate damage with cartilage involvement (Case 2, 5 weeks) → soft callus/healing (Case 3, 7 weeks).

**Key mechanistic finding:** No evidence for secondary ossification centres in any case. Histopathology is consistent with traumatic cancellous bone fracture healing (bleeding → cell proliferation → woven bone → soft callus), not a normal developmental variant. Poor blood supply to PSB explains minimal haemorrhage despite fracture.

## Discussion & interpretation

The authors conclude that the radiographic PSB apical findings in foals are **healed or healing traumatic fractures** rather than secondary ossification centres. The key mechanism is: PSB begins as a cartilaginous nodule undergoing endochondral ossification in late pregnancy; the apical region remains an active site of cartilage cell division until 3 months post-partum; immature woven bone at this site is mechanically weak; tensile forces from suspensory ligaments during foal sprinting cause avulsion fractures at the cartilage-trabecula boundary.

The healing cascade in cancellous bone differs from cortical diaphyseal healing: less haemorrhage, limited external callus, rapid fibrous bridging. The proximal 1/3 of the non-union bone is often mistaken for a bipartite bone (secondary ossification centre), but no evidence of secondary ossification centres was found.

The authors note that incidence in healthy foals is likely underestimated in this study since these animals died without exercise. Severe cases (cartilage damage) may have delayed healing and represent a risk for persistent structural weakness.

## Limitations

- n=30 necropsied foals, n=3 histopathologically examined cases; very small sample.
- Selection bias: all subjects died or were euthanised for non-PSB causes; active, healthy foals not included.
- No follow-up of foals to adult racing career; no direct demonstration that foal-period PSB injury predicts adult fracture.
- Single institution (Hidaka, Hokkaido); generalisability to non-JRA Thoroughbred populations unclear.
- Radiographic detection is insensitive relative to histopathology (Case 1 had histologically visible clefts in radiographically normal PSBs); true incidence exceeds 10%.

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `psb_foal_radiographic_finding` — binary flag: presence of any radiographic abnormality at apical PSB in foal radiographs — source: JBBA/HHBA 2-year-old in-training sales repository or foal health records — expected effect: positive predictor of scratch/non-starter risk, especially at 2yo; may reduce probability of early-career racing debut — interaction with `surface_type` (dirt > turf risk) and `track_condition` (fast/muddy dirt increases VGRF)
- `psb_foal_severity_grade` — ordinal 0–2 (none / linear lesion / cartilage damage) — source: same repository — expected effect: grade 2 (cartilage damage) associated with greater healing delay and structural weakness than grade 1
- `foal_period_lameness_flag` — binary: any recorded lameness in foal period — source: farm/vet records if available — expected effect: positive predictor of musculoskeletal fragility
- Interaction term `psb_finding × age_at_debut`: horses with PSB findings who debut early (2yo) have higher in-race scratch risk than those who debut at 3–4yo after complete healing
- **Do NOT use** PSB foal finding as a predictor of finishing position in mid-career horses with large existing race records (heritability of the structural weakness is not established; most horses heal without residual deficit)
