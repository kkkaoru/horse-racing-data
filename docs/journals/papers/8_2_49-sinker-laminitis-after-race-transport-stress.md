## Abstract (verbatim)

> A case of a racehorse that suddenly developed acute laminitis after participation in a race and succeeding transport was studied. The horse had shown higher γ-GT levels since the preliminary examination two months prior to the crisis until the onset of the disease. However, the horse had been trained on a regular alimentary schedule until the race, because it was free from an abnormal level of biochemical items other than γ-GT and also showed no clinical signs. These pathological characteristics included the palmarly displacement of the distal phalanx (P.III) maintaining parallelism between the hoof wall and the P.III, conspicuous proliferation of a wide range of laminar layers, marked swelling of axillary and cubital lymph nodes, and mild mononuclear cell infiltration to the portal area. Accordingly, this case was considered to be sinker laminitis induced by stress attributable to race participation and transportation, possibly with mild hepatic anomaly laid at the root of the disease.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

This paper belongs to **Feature family A (injury/soundness)**. It is a JRA single-case report documenting a 4-year-old male Thoroughbred at Miho Training Center who developed acute sinker laminitis the day after racing and transport, without any classical trigger (carbohydrate overload, corticosteroids, infection, pituitary pathology). The race and immediate subsequent transport were the only identifiable stressors, making this one of the few documented cases in which race participation alone is sufficient to precipitate catastrophic hoof failure.

For finishing-position prediction the primary relevance is to **injury-risk and scratch/DNF modelling**. The horse was euthanised on day 12, contributing to the "did not start next race" outcome class. The pre-race γ-GT elevation (80 U/L two months before, 62 U/L on day 1 of illness; all other biochemicals normal) is the only prospective biomarker signal identified. If routine JRA/NAR blood biochemistry panels — which are performed regularly at Miho and other training centres — include γ-GT, an elevated pre-race value is a candidate feature for an injury-risk sub-model or for a next-race-start probability model.

The sinker pattern (perpendicular P.III displacement with maintained parallelism to hoof wall) also illustrates why radiological non-detection can delay diagnosis: the classic rotation sign is absent. This diagnostic ambiguity means injury records may under-report sinker events, biasing any injury-label training data.

## Background & objective

**Laminitis definition and types:** Laminitis is ischemic disease of dermal laminar tissue in the hoof, with or without inflammatory changes. Causes include carbohydrate overload, toxaemia, mechanical force, irregular foot dressing, infection, iatrogenic corticosteroids, pituitary neoplasia, pregnancy, and stress (Eustace 1992). Displacement of the distal phalanx (P.III) has two radiological patterns: (1) **rotation** — tip of P.III displaces downward; (2) **sinker** — entire P.III sinks perpendicularly, with hoof wall and P.III maintaining parallelism but separating (Goetz 1989). Sinker type is harder to diagnose radiologically (Eustace et al. 1993) and progresses faster than rotation type.

**Gap in knowledge:** Most reported cases of laminitis in racehorses involve classical triggers. Stress-induced sinker laminitis without classical triggers, developing immediately after racing + transport, had not been well characterised. This necropsy case provided the opportunity to document all clinical, haematological, biochemical, and histopathological features.

## Materials & methods

**Subject:** Single case: 4-year-old male Thoroughbred, Miho Training Center, JRA. Presented with acute support-limb lameness on the right forelimb the day after a provincial race + horse truck transport.

**Clinical timeline:**

- Day −60 (2 months pre-onset): Routine preliminary blood examination
- Day 0: Race + transport; symptoms appeared next day
- Day 1: Initial examination — support-limb lameness right forelimb, standing-under posture, moderately high hoof temperature, strong pulsation of palmar artery, no radiological P.III displacement visible (parallelism maintained)
- Day 4: Blood, serum fluid, and pus oozing from coronary band; severe depression of coronet palpable
- Day 7: Same symptoms in left forelimb; Grade III laminitis (Obel scale)
- Day 9: Grade IV in both forelimbs
- Day 12: Euthanised at owner's request

**Blood tests:** RBC, WBC, PCV: Sysmex K-4500 (TOA-IRYO-DENSHISHA). Serum enzymes, proteins, electrolytes: Hitachi automatic analyser 7250. Samples taken 2 months pre-onset, day 1, and day 7 of illness.

**Treatment:** Oral diclofenac sodium (500 mg/head/SID NSAID) + IV flunixin meglumine (250 mg/head/BID). Farrier treatment not possible as disease progressed too rapidly.

**Necropsy and histopathology:** Full necropsy at Miho; macroscopic and histopathological examination of hooves (cross and sagittal sections), lymph nodes (axillary, cubital), liver. Haematoxylin-eosin (HE) staining; PAS reaction for glycogen.

**Key biochemical finding:** γ-GT was the only abnormal value at any time point. It was elevated at 80 U/L two months before onset (normal upper range ~30–50 U/L in horses), still elevated at 62 U/L on day 1, then returned to normal (41 U/L) by day 7. All other 14 analytes were within normal limits at all three time points.

**Gross pathology:**

- Axillary and cubital lymph nodes: markedly enlarged, approximately 3 × 3 × 5 cm bilaterally
- Laminar layers connecting P.III and hoof wall: thickened 2–3 times normal, with bleeding throughout; bulk of destruction in this zone
- Laminar adjacent to bulb (not connected to P.III): maintained normal thickness
- P.III: sunken with marked destruction of sole dermis; parallelism with hoof wall maintained but separation increased compared with normal
- No abnormalities in other organs

**Histopathology:**

- Axillary/cubital lymph nodes: large amounts of macrophages filling lymph sinus, superficial and deep cortex (Fig. 5). Macrophages consistent with phagocytosis of necrotic laminar debris
- Laminar outer regions: neutrophil and macrophage infiltration, necrosis, gap formation (Fig. 7); acute inflammatory phase
- Laminar middle regions: dyskeratosis (premature/faulty keratinisation) at boundary of inflammatory and regenerative regions (Fig. 8a)
- Laminar deeper regions: elongative proliferation, hyperplasia of prickle cell layer, oedematous vacuolisation around basement membrane (Fig. 8b) — consistent with circulatory disturbance
- Liver: mild, sporadic mononuclear cell infiltration to portal area (Fig. 9); not severe; decreased centrolobular glycogen granules on PAS (possibly responsive to excess steroid release during disease)

## Discussion & interpretation

**Diagnosis:** The combination of perpendicular P.III sinking with maintained hoof wall parallelism, coronary band depression, and extensive laminar thickening/necrosis confirmed sinker laminitis. The maintained parallelism was initially misleading and caused diagnostic delay — no P.III displacement was identified on initial radiographs.

**Aetiology:** Race participation + transport identified as the precipitating stressors. All classical triggers (carbohydrate overload, steroids, infection, pituitary pathology, pregnancy) were excluded. The authors hypothesise that mild pre-existing hepatic dysfunction (evidenced by γ-GT elevation) impaired endotoxin decomposition, allowing stress-induced endotoxaemia to progress to laminitis (Moore et al. 1989 mechanism).

**γ-GT as precursor:** The persistent γ-GT elevation (80→62 U/L over 2 months) without other abnormalities suggests subclinical biliary/hepatic stress. As a biliary enzyme, γ-GT elevation indicates cholestatic or toxic hepatobiliary damage. The authors suggest this "mild hepatic anomaly" was the root condition predisposing the horse to sinker laminitis when racing stress was applied.

**Normal WBC/RBC/PCV:** Contrasts with carbohydrate-induced laminitis; suggests stress-mediated vascular–laminar compromise rather than systemic septic/inflammatory response as the mechanism. The lymph node macrophage accumulation is attributed to the unusually large necrotic area in sinker type (vs. rotation type), which drains to axillary/cubital lymph nodes — the only lymph nodes draining the distal forelimb.

**Radiological diagnostic challenge:** Sinker laminitis with maintained parallelism can be mistaken for no P.III pathology on lateral radiographs. The key is to measure the actual wall-to-P.III distance, not just parallelism. Physical palpation of coronet depression is a more sensitive early sign.

**Speed of progression:** Grade III to IV within 2 days; euthanised 12 days after onset. Sinker laminitis characteristically progresses "simultaneously over extensive laminar with remarkable speed," according to Baxter (1986). The case is consistent with this pattern.

## Limitations

- Single case report; no control group; causation from race + transport cannot be rigorously established
- γ-GT elevation may be unrelated to laminitis pathogenesis (coincidental finding)
- No pre-race pain assessment or gait scoring that could have flagged the horse as at-risk before the race
- Normal analgesic dosing may have masked pain during hoof-tester examination, limiting clinical characterisation
- Hepatic pathology confirmation is limited (only mild/sporadic portal infiltration and glycogen reduction); definitive liver disease was not proven
- JRA data: only one training centre (Miho), 1 horse, 4 years old male

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `gamma_gt_prerace_iu_l` — γ-GT serum concentration (IU/l) from routine blood panel ≤ 2 months before race; **single strongest biochemical signal in this case**; threshold ~50–80 IU/l as "elevated"; expected effect: elevated γ-GT → higher injury/scratch risk → lower finishing probability (or higher DNF probability); **data availability**: present in JRA/NAR pre-race health examination records if biochemistry panels are logged; not routinely published in public databases
- `race_plus_transport_flag` — binary indicator: horse participated in a race AND was transported immediately after; race database records often include whether a horse ran at an away track requiring transport; expected effect: higher short-term injury risk in subsequent starts
- `days_since_last_race` — short rest intervals post-race (e.g., < 7 days) before next start may elevate systemic stress markers; interaction with transport distance
- `transport_distance_km` — distance transported by horse truck on the day of or immediately after racing; not universally available but present in logistics records at major JRA training centres
- `bil_prerace_mg_dl` — bilirubin (BIL); in this case normal pre-onset (2.4 mg/dl) but rose to 5.9 by day 7; not useful as pre-race predictor alone but may flag emerging hepatic stress post-race for injury monitoring models
- `ast_prerace_iu_l` — AST (363 IU/l pre-onset, broadly normal for horses); elevated AST + elevated γ-GT together may be stronger than either alone; interaction term `ast_x_gamma_gt`
- **Caution**: Sinker laminitis is rare; any model trained on JRA/NAR databases will have very few positive labels. This feature family is more useful for injury-risk screening than primary finishing-position regression.
- **Do NOT** use RBC/WBC/PCV as injury-risk indicators for laminitis specifically — this case shows these are uninformative (normal throughout), unlike carbohydrate-induced cases
