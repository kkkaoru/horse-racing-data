## Abstract (verbatim)

> In this study, we examined the relationship between the osmotic fragility and K-Cl cotransport (KCC) activity in the density-separated erythrocytes from thoroughbred horses. Erythrocytes from four healthy thoroughbreds were collected, and separated into five fractions according to their density. The KCC activities of the cells were determined as the K⁺ congener Rb⁺ influx in the cells suspended in isotonic medium in which the main salts were constructed with N-methyl-D-glucamine (NMDG)/NO₃ or NMDG/Cl and the medium pH were varied from 7.0 to 7.8. The decrease in medium pH was associated with an increase in the KCC activities, particularly the KCC activity at pH 7.0 was 4- and 50-fold higher than that at pH 7.4 and 7.8, respectively. The KCC activity in the dense cells was also significantly higher than that in the light cells. The decrease in medium pH and/or the density of erythrocytes was associated with a progressive increase in the osmotic fragility. The osmotic fragility measured in the NMDG/NO₃ medium always showed a tendency to be higher than that incubated in the NMDG/Cl medium in each medium pH and/or cell fraction. These results suggest that 1) the increase in the KCC activity may cause shrinkage of the cell volume and decrease the fragility; and 2) the KCC has a functional interrelation to the change in the erythrocyte fragility with the fall in pH. The KCC of equine erythrocytes therefore plays an important role in the prevention of fragilocytes and protection of haemolysis in blood vessels with exercise.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family: **C (exercise-physiology / fitness) with D (genetics) overtone**. This paper characterises the K-Cl cotransport (KCC) mechanism that protects Thoroughbred erythrocytes from exercise-induced haemolysis. During maximal exercise, blood pH falls to ~7.0 (from resting ~7.4); this 50-fold increase in KCC activity causes cell shrinkage, which paradoxically reduces osmotic fragility and helps prevent haemolysis. The individual variation in this protective mechanism (cited in refs 12, 15 as "osmotic fragility shows individual variation") and its genetic underpinning (SNPs in SLC12A4 gene — Hanzawa et al. 2002, Animal Genetics) suggest it could be a horse-level biomarker of exercise tolerance.

The link to finishing-position prediction is second-order: erythrocyte integrity under acidosis is a component of overall haematological fitness; horses with better KCC-mediated haemoprotection under exercise-acidosis can sustain higher anaerobic output without haemolytic losses. However, this is a basic cell physiology paper with no direct racing outcomes measured. The most actionable feature it motivates is `blood_pH_post_race` as a proxy for anaerobic loading, or — if genotyping data were available — the SLC12A4 SNP status as a `haemolytic_risk_score`.

## Background & objective

High-intensity treadmill exercise promotes osmotic fragility of Thoroughbred erythrocytes, attributable to membrane oxidation from blood pH decrease (Hanzawa et al. 1999; 1996). Prior work showed that: in density-separated equine erythrocytes, higher cell density correlates with lower osmotic fragility (Hanzawa & Watanabe 1995); RVD (regulatory volume decrease) in equine erythrocytes is activated by high-intensity exercise (Hanzawa et al. 1995, 1999); maximal exercise increases erythroid volume and then triggers KCC-mediated RVD. Equine erythrocytes are high-K⁺ cells (unlike most mammalian RBCs) dominated by K-Cl cotransport (KCC) for K⁺ efflux, stimulated by cell swelling, pH decrease, and urea increase. The question was: does KCC protect erythrocytes from pH-induced haemolysis by causing shrinkage? This paper tests the hypothesis by measuring KCC activity and osmotic fragility across five density fractions at three pH levels.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 4 healthy Thoroughbred horses, 5–6 years old, 2 males and 2 females, body weight 457 ± 13 kg.

**Blood collection:** Jugular venepuncture into heparinised tubes. Centrifugation at 1,450 × G, 10 min, 4°C (swing rotor R300S-II, Sakuma). Plasma and buffy coat discarded. Packed cells washed 3× with 10 vol. ice-cold Dulbecco's PBS (pH 7.4, 279 ± 5 mOsm).

**Density separation (Murphy method [Murphy 1973]):** 8 ml packed cells + 2 ml autologous plasma centrifuged at 20,000 × G, 60 min, 30°C (angle-head rotor 50A-7, Sakuma). Cell mass separated into 5 equal fractions by density: LI (light, fraction 1), MD (medium, fraction 3), DE (dense, fraction 5), and RM (remixed, fractions 1–5). Washed 3× with isosmotic NMDG/NO₃ or NMDG/Cl buffer.

**Erythrocyte density (ED, g/ml):** Self-generated continuous Percoll density gradient (75.6% Percoll, pH 7.5), modified Wolowyk method.

**MCV and MCHC:** Automated electronic cell analyser (Celltac MEK-5257, Nihon Kohden).

**KCC activity (Cl⁻-dependent Rb⁺ influx):** Rb⁺ (K⁺ congener) influx measured in isotonic media. Flux medium composition: 10 mM RbNO₃ (or RbCl), 135 mM NMDG/NO₃ (or NMDG/Cl), 10 mM Tris/MOPS, 5 mM glucose, 0.1 mM ouabain, 1 mM EGTA (295 ± 5 mOsm, pH 7.4, 37°C). pH adjusted to 7.0 with L-lactate (~5 mM final) or to 7.8 with NaOH (~2.5 mM final); iso-osmolality maintained. Haematocrit 5% (vol/vol). Aliquots taken at 20, 40, 60, 80 min; washed with isosmotic 110 mM MgCl₂ + 10 mM Tris-HCl. KCC activity = Rb⁺ influx in NMDG/Cl medium minus Rb⁺ influx in NMDG/NO₃ medium (Cl⁻-independent flux). Rb⁺ measured by flame photometry at 780 nm; Hb by spectrophotometry at 527 nm. Units: mmol/l cells/hr.

**Osmotic fragility (HL₀.₅₆):** Haemolysis rate in 0.56% NaCl after 10 min incubation at 37°C in NMDG/NO₃ or NMDG/Cl medium. Expressed as % haemolysis.

**Statistical analysis:** One-way and two-way ANOVA. Multiple comparisons by LSD method. Significance at P < 0.05. Values: mean ± SE, n=4 animals.

## Discussion & interpretation

The 50-fold activation of KCC at pH 7.0 vs. pH 7.8 is the key mechanistic finding. During intense exercise, blood pH falls to approximately 7.0–7.1, activating KCC to its maximum. KCC co-transports K⁺ + Cl⁻ + H₂O out of the cell, causing shrinkage. This shrinkage reduces membrane tension and osmotic fragility, protecting against haemolysis. The density-dependent KCC activation (DE > MD > LI at low pH) is attributed to cell volume regulation: dense cells have smaller volume and higher MCHC; they may represent cells that have already undergone volume reduction. The individual variation in osmotic fragility (cited) and the SNP in SLC12A4 (the KCC gene) that correlates with fragility (Hanzawa et al. 2002) open a genomics-of-performance angle.

## Limitations

- n = 4 horses; only 2 male, 2 female, 5–6 yr; no replication across ages, training states, or exercise conditions.
- In vitro experiment; exogenous pH manipulation with L-lactate/NaOH; may not perfectly replicate the complex in vivo milieu during exercise.
- KCC activation characterised in isolated fractions; in vivo the mixed circulating pool (RM fraction) is more relevant, and pH changes are dynamic over the course of a race.
- No racing performance data; no correlation of individual KCC activity or osmotic fragility with race times or finishing positions.
- The SLC12A4 SNP data (Hanzawa et al. 2002) are described as "preliminary associations" — not yet validated at population scale.
