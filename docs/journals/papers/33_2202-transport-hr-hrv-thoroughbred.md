## Abstract (verbatim)

> This study aimed to investigate the effect of transportation and restraint in a van on heart rate (HR) and HR variability in Thoroughbreds. Eight healthy Thoroughbreds were exposed to four conditions, each for a duration of 30 min: stall rest (REST), restraint inside a van (VAN), restraint inside a van with the engine running (V + E), and road transportation (TRANS). Electrocardiograms were recorded to determine HR, low-frequency (LF) power, high-frequency (HF) power, and LF/HF ratio. During TRANS, HR was significantly greater than during REST and V + E. There was a significant increase during VAN compared with REST. These results demonstrated that restraint inside the transport vehicle was one of the major stressors that may cause physiological changes during transportation.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

**Feature family C (exercise-physiology/fitness) and E (environment/pre-race stress).** Thoroughbreds are transported to racetracks in vans before every race; this study measures the autonomic stress response to 30 minutes of van restraint and road transport in 3-year-old JRA horses. Key predictions from the results:

1. **Restraint alone (VAN, engine off) raises HR by ~8 bpm above stall rest.** The horses in this study had been pre-acclimated to van loading — yet VAN HR was significantly elevated vs. REST (43.4 vs. 35.3 bpm, P = 0.03). In racing contexts, unadapted horses or those travelling unusual distances may show larger HR responses. If pre-race paddock HR data were available, horses showing unusually elevated HR relative to their own baseline could be flagged for stress-impaired performance.

2. **Road transport (TRANS) adds further sympathetic activation.** TRANS HR = 53.9 bpm vs. 35.3 at REST (+53% elevation), and HF power (parasympathetic index) was significantly suppressed (303 vs. 528 msec², P = 0.047). Transport stress is not just loading-related (van restraint) but includes combined vibration, noise, and restraint during movement.

3. **Engine on/off (VAN vs. V+E) makes no significant difference to HR or HRV** (HR: 43.4 vs. 41.5 bpm; HF: 398 vs. 343 msec², both non-significant). Vibration from engine alone is not a major stressor compared to restraint. This has a practical implication: horses parked in vans at a racecourse (engine running) are not experiencing meaningfully different stress than in a stationary van.

4. **LF/HF ratio did not significantly change** across any condition (REST: 4.6, VAN: 5.2, V+E: 5.4, TRANS: 5.5). The LF/HF ratio is often used as a sympathovagal balance index, but here it was insensitive to transport stress, while HF power (absolute) was. This confirms that absolute HF power is a more appropriate parasympathetic indicator for this type of stress in horses.

5. **Time-domain metrics (SDRR, CVRR) were insensitive.** SDRR (169, 165, 165, 157 msec) and CVRR (9.97, 12.5, 11.55, 14.41%) showed no significant changes despite HR and HF power differences — frequency-domain spectral analysis is more sensitive than time-domain HRV for detecting transport stress.

Net: these normative values for 3-year-old JRA Thoroughbreds establish the expected autonomic response to transport, providing a reference for individual-horse deviations as potential pre-race stress features. Indirect relevance to performance prediction; more directly relevant to transport-associated respiratory disease (RDT) risk modelling (see 32_2029).

## Background & objective

Transport is an inherent stressor in horse racing — every race requires van transport, ranging from intra-track paddock moves to multi-hour interstate transfers. Transport stress is cumulative and linked to respiratory disease (RDT/shipping fever), immune suppression (Bannai et al. 2021: decreased herpesvirus-neutralizing antibodies after 12-hr transport), and poor race-day readiness. However, the relative contributions of different transport stress components — restraint, engine vibration, road motion — had not been isolated. Authors hypothesized that restraint alone (even without movement) would produce measurable autonomic changes, distinct from road transport, and that spectral HRV analysis would detect these differences.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 8 healthy Thoroughbreds (4 males, 4 females; all 3 years old; mean BW 471 ± 38 kg SD). Housed in individual stalls (3.6 m × 3.6 m), straw bedding, 7 hr/day pasture turnout. Fed concentrate 3×/day (06:00, 16:00, 19:00); ad libitum hay and water. Pre-acclimated to van by multiple short transports (< 10 min) before the experiment. Ethics: Animal Care Committee, Equine Research Institute, JRA.

**Experimental design:** Randomized crossover, 4 conditions × 30 min each, on different days (within-subject):

- **REST:** stall rest (no van)
- **VAN:** loaded into 9-horse diesel van (2 horses per trial), heads loosely cross-tied, rubber mat floor, engine off, 30 min stationary
- **V+E:** same as VAN but engine running, van stationary (not moved)
- **TRANS:** same as VAN, van driven on a general road for 30 min

All experiments 13:00–15:00 to control for diurnal variation.

**Instrumentation:** Holter-type ECG (SM-60, Fukuda Denshi); base-apex electrode placement; ECG recorded 30 min per condition. No sex-related differences detected; not used as a comparison factor.

**HRV analysis:** Softron ECG Processor System (Softron Co., Ltd., Tokyo). R-waves detected; RR-interval tachogram computed. Noise excluded manually (values outside 75–125% of mean). 512-point data sets resampled at 200 msec. Hamming window + fast Fourier transform (FFT). LF power: 0.01–0.07 Hz (sympathetic + vagal). HF power: 0.07–0.6 Hz (parasympathetic/vagal). LF/HF ratio. Time domain: SDRR (standard deviation of RR intervals), CVRR (coefficient of variation of RR intervals).

**Statistics:** One-factor repeated measures ANOVA + Holm–Sidak post-hoc pairwise comparison. P ≤ 0.05 significant. Data presented as mean ± SE.

## Discussion & interpretation

The key mechanistic conclusion is that **physical restraint inside the van** is the primary stress element, not engine vibration or sound (VAN ≈ V+E). Road motion (vibration, noise, postural compensation for vehicle movement) adds the further activation seen in TRANS. This aligns with earlier work (Smith et al. 1994, 1996; Ohmura et al. 2006) and extends it by isolating the restraint component in a controlled crossover.

The decrease in HF power during TRANS indicates reduced parasympathetic tone — increased sympathetic dominance without a proportional increase in LF power (absolute) or LF/HF ratio. The authors note that adrenergic agents may not always increase LF power (citing Ahmed et al. 1994; Tulppo et al. 2005; Wodey et al. 2003), and that HR may be a more sensitive detector of sympathetic activation than LF power for this type of stress. Thus, monitoring heart rate (even without HRV decomposition) is the most practical measure of transport stress.

Long-duration transport (prior work by Ohmura et al. 2006, 2012 on 12+ hr road and air transport) would produce larger and more sustained autonomic changes, including the immune suppression documented by Bannai et al. 2021 for 12-hr transport. The 30-min duration in this study represents the minimum observed in Japanese racing (intra-city track transfers); intercity transport (e.g., Hokkaido → Tokyo) is much longer.

## Limitations

- n = 8 horses, all 3-year-old JRA Thoroughbreds; results may not generalise to older horses, different fitness levels, or maiden/unraced horses unfamiliar with van loading
- Pre-acclimated horses (multiple short van exposures before experiment); stress responses in non-acclimated or anxious horses would likely be larger
- Only 30 min per condition; cumulative effects of longer transport not captured
- TRANS route was a "general road"; highway vs. secondary road vibration, speed, turns, and braking may differ and affect results
- No cortisol or ACTH measurements; the study cannot confirm whether the sympathetic activation translates to a hormonal stress response
- LF band (0.01–0.07 Hz) is lower than in human HRV studies (0.04–0.15 Hz); this is specific to equine HRV methodology (horses have lower resting HR)
- No measurement after transport (recovery profile unknown)

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `transport_distance_km` — estimated travel distance from home stable to race venue — from race entry venue data and stable registration address — proxy for cumulative transport stress; longer transport correlates with greater sympathetic activation, HRV suppression, and RDT/respiratory disease risk; expected weak negative effect on race-day performance; interaction with `days_since_last_race` (recovery time)
- `transport_duration_hr` — travel time in hours — from stable-venue distance and typical road speed — more direct than distance for duration-dependent immune effects (Bannai et al. 2021: immune suppression after 12 hr transport)
- `venue_home_venue_flag` — binary: is the race at the horse's home track? — 1 if no transport required — expected positive effect (no transport stress); relevant for JRA centralized training stables (Ritto, Miho) vs. distant tracks
- `pre_race_hr_baseline` — resting HR in stable the morning of race day, if wearable HR data available — deviation from individual horse's own baseline (z-score) — elevated pre-race HR (> 45 bpm) as a stress flag — data not currently in JRA race entry systems but potentially derivable from stable monitoring
- `hf_power_rest_baseline` — HF power at REST for individual horse (~528 msec² for 3-year-old JRA horses in this study) — reference normative value for calibrating individual deviations; use 528 ± 82 msec² as the population prior
- **Do NOT use** SDRR or CVRR as transport stress indicators — these time-domain metrics were insensitive in this study; frequency-domain HF power is more sensitive
- **Do NOT use** LF/HF ratio as a single transport stress indicator — non-significant across all conditions including TRANS; HF power (absolute) is more appropriate
- **Cross-feature note:** transport-associated immune suppression (from longer trips) is the mechanistic link to RDT (32_2029) — combine transport duration feature with respiratory disease history as a composite pre-race health flag
