## Abstract (verbatim)

> We investigated the effects of low intensity exercise during the breaking period on cardiorespiratory function in twelve Thoroughbred yearlings. The horses were broken to handling and riding according to typical breaking program, and breaking was defined as a period of about 8 weeks covering bitting, saddling, longeing exercise and track exercise at trot and slow canter. Each horse performed an incremental exercise test on a treadmill before (pre-test) and after (post-test) the breaking period. Vo2max was increased significantly after the breaking period (pre-test: 134.8 ± 8.9 ml/kg/min, post-test: 151.3 ± 7.9 ml/kg/min). There were no significant difference between the peak HR and La values before and after breaking. Breaking also caused no change in the relationship between speed and HR or La. The peak PCV value was significantly higher after breaking period. These results suggest that Thoroughbreds already have considerably high cardiorespiratory function at the early stage of their lives before breaking, and also suggest that cardiorespiratory function is enhanced through the breaking period, which is necessarily experienced by young racehorses.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **C (exercise-physiology / fitness)**. This paper provides age-stage reference values for VO2max, PCV, HR, and blood lactate in Thoroughbred yearlings at the start of formal training (the breaking period) — before any structured race training. The pre-breaking VO2max of 134.8 ± 8.9 ml/kg/min already exceeds the level typical of detrained adult horses (~similar, per Evans & Rose 1988) and rises to 151.3 ± 7.9 ml/kg/min after breaking, confirming that (1) Thoroughbreds possess high aerobic capacity even before breaking, and (2) even low-intensity exercise during breaking achieves meaningful VO2max gains (~12% relative increase).

For a finishing-position model, these data establish developmental reference points for fitness. A horse whose VO2max after breaking substantially exceeds the study mean of 151 ml/kg/min has a greater aerobic ceiling entering formal training, which is likely to translate to better race performance, particularly at longer distances. The simultaneous PCV increase (52.8 → 55.4%) with no change in peak HR or peak LA confirms that the VO2max improvement is mediated by enhanced O2 delivery (splenic reserve expansion) rather than peripheral extraction or anaerobic threshold shifts — a mechanistic insight for feature design.

The lack of any change in speed–HR or speed–LA relationships despite VO2max improvement means that submaximal LA and HR at a given speed (commonly measured in routine fitness tests) are **not sensitive to breaking-induced fitness gains** — only VO2max or VO2-200 (VO2 at HR 200) detects the improvement. This cautions against relying exclusively on submaximal indices when evaluating yearlings early in training.

## Background & objective

Standard Thoroughbred training begins with the breaking period (~8 weeks, typically starting at 14–18 months of age) covering bitting, saddling, longeing, and introductory trot/canter. No longitudinal (pre/post) cardiorespiratory data existed for JRA Thoroughbred yearlings at this stage — only cross-sectional comparisons. Persson et al. (1983) conducted a longitudinal training study in Standardbred yearlings but not Thoroughbreds. The study aimed to measure whether low-intensity breaking exercise is sufficient to alter cardiorespiratory function and to provide normative values at this developmental stage.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 12 Thoroughbred yearlings; all on pasture ~7 hr/day before breaking period.

**Age and weight (Table 1, means ± SD):**

|                  | Pre-test     | Post-test    |
| ---------------- | ------------ | ------------ |
| Age (months)     | 16.8 ± 0.9   | 21.6 ± 0.9   |
| Body weight (kg) | 425.4 ± 37.6 | 455.3 ± 41.5 |

Mean body weight gain: ~30 kg over 8-week period (growth + exercise).

**Breaking program (Table 2):**

| Week      | Activities                                                                       |
| --------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Week 1    | Accustom to bit, girth, longeing exercise (trot)                                 |
| Week 2    | Accustom to saddling, driving, longeing (trot and canter)                        |
| Week 3    | Accustom to riding, riding at longeing (trot and canter)                         |
| Week 4    | Riding at longeing (trot and canter); riding in indoor hall (30 m × 50 m square) |
| Week 5    | Riding at indoor hall (trot and canter)                                          |
| Weeks 6–8 | Riding at indoor course (1,000 m straight) (trot and canter)                     |

Canter distance in Week 8: up to 1,000 m at 400 m/min. Training 5 days/week; 7 hr/day pasture on remaining 2 days.

**Treadmill test protocol:** Mustang-2200 (Kagra, Switzerland); horses acclimatised with respiratory gas mask prior to testing. Both pre-test and post-test performed:

- Warm-up: 5 min level trotting at 4.0 m/s + 5 min rest
- Incremental steps (1 min each): 1.8 → 2.7 → 3.4 → 4.5 → 5.4 → 6.8 → 9.0 → 9.8 → 10.8 → 11.6 m/s (10% incline); pre-test only had an extra 7.8 m/s step between 6.8 and 9.0 m/s
- Termination criterion: horse cannot maintain position without assistance
- Cool-down: 15 min walk at 1.0 m/s (0% incline)

**Measurements:**

- VO2 and VCO2: bias-flow ventilatory system (Erickson et al. 1994); continuous during test; determined during final 30 sec of each step
- HR: Polar Bandage-XL heart rate monitor; continuous; calculated during last 30 sec of each step
- Blood LA (mmol/L): YSI-1500 Sport; jugular catheter; samples during last 15 sec of each speed step
- PCV (%): microhaematocrit method; same blood samples as LA
- VO2-200: VO2 at HR 200 beats/min, calculated from regression line of HR vs. VO2

**Statistics:** Analysis of variance for repeated measurements; P < 0.05; results as mean ± SD.

**VO2-200 (means ± SD):**

- Pre-test: 106.4 ± 13.1 ml/kg/min
- Post-test: 121.2 ± 9.3 ml/kg/min
- Tendency to increase (not reported as statistically significant but directionally consistent with VO2max increase)

**Absolute vs. relative VO2max:**

- Absolute VO2max increase: 57.1 → 69.1 L/min = **+21.0%** (body weight gain partly contributes)
- Relative VO2max increase: 134.8 → 151.3 ml/kg/min = **+12.2%** (growth-corrected)

**Speed–HR and speed–La relationships:** No significant change pre vs. post (Fig. 2): submaximal HR and La at any given speed were unchanged by breaking.

**VO2 and VCO2 at submaximal speeds (Fig. 1):** VO2 and VCO2 tended to be higher at post-test at submaximal speeds (trend, not significant at individual steps).

**Pre-test context comparisons:**

- Pre-test peak PCV (52.8%) was higher than corresponding values in previous cross-sectional studies of similar-age horses (Harris et al. 1990; Rose et al. 1990; Seeherman & Morris 1991) — possibly attributable to pasture activity before breaking.
- Pre-test VO2max (134.8 ml/kg/min) was higher than previously published values for similarly aged horses and similar to detrained adult values (Evans & Rose 1988) — reflecting the naturally high aerobic base in Thoroughbreds even before formal training.
- Post-test VO2max (151.3 ml/kg/min) was similar to that reported for age-matched horses (Hiraga et al. 1995 LSD training study).

**Breaking exercise intensity estimate:** Authors estimated HR during breaking exercise at 40–50% HRmax based on Knight et al. (1991) findings on 40% VO2max training effects in detrained adults — sufficient to elicit aerobic adaptation.

## Discussion & interpretation

The significant ~12% relative increase in VO2max despite low-intensity exercise confirms that even the introductory breaking period (bitting, longeing, trot/canter at 400 m/min) is physiologically sufficient for aerobic adaptation in Thoroughbreds. However, the authors acknowledge that the simultaneous ~30 kg growth during this period confounds the interpretation: the absolute VO2max increase (+21%) exceeds the relative increase (+12%), reflecting cardiovascular expansion accompanying physical growth. The lack of HR and LA response changes means that submaximal field tests cannot detect this breaking-induced improvement.

The parallel PCV increase (52.8 → 55.4%) with VO2max increase replicates prior correlations between PCV and aerobic capacity (Seeherman & Morris 1991), consistent with splenic and red-cell volume expansion as the primary mechanism. The fact that peak HR and peak LA did not change indicates peripheral utilisation and heart rate ceiling were not training-limiting during this period — only O2 delivery capacity (PCV/VO2) improved.

## Limitations

- n = 12 yearlings from a single JRA training facility (Hidaka Yearling Training Farm); possible selection bias.
- No untreated control group (all horses underwent breaking); pre–post differences could be partly growth-driven.
- 8-week observation window corresponds to a natural growth period (ages 16.8 → 21.6 months); separating exercise effect from growth effect is not possible without an unbroke control group.
- Individual body weight ranges are wide (pre: 425 ± 38 kg; post: 455 ± 42 kg), reflecting genetic diversity; relative VO2max reduces but does not eliminate this variance.
- No race outcome data linked to these horses; the predictive value of these breaking-period VO2max values for eventual race performance remains unconfirmed in this paper.

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `vo2max_pre_breaking` — VO2max before breaking (ml/kg/min) — JRA treadmill records, yearling stage — baseline aerobic capacity; reference 117–153 ml/kg/min range; individual horses above 153 likely have above-average aerobic potential entering formal training
- `vo2max_post_breaking` — VO2max after breaking (ml/kg/min) — JRA treadmill records — post-breaking aerobic fitness entering formal training; reference 138–164 ml/kg/min; positively correlated with early career performance at distance
- `vo2max_breaking_gain` — post − pre VO2max (ml/kg/min) — derived from treadmill records — magnitude of aerobic response to breaking; larger gain may indicate higher trainability
- `pcv_post_breaking` — peak PCV after breaking (%) — JRA blood records — proxy for splenic red-cell reserve post-breaking; reference 51–59%; higher values correlated with higher VO2max
- `vo2_200_post_breaking` — VO2 at HR 200 beats/min (ml/kg/min) — JRA treadmill records — submaximal aerobic indicator; reference ~121 ml/kg/min post-breaking; tends to increase with training
- `bw_growth_breaking` — body weight gain during breaking period (kg) — JRA registration records — confounds relative vs. absolute VO2max; use relative (ml/kg/min) VO2max for model features; flag horses with unusually low or high growth rates
- **DO NOT** use peak HR or peak LA from breaking-period tests to detect aerobic fitness gains — these are insensitive to this type of improvement; only VO2max or VO2-200 capture the adaptation
- **Interaction:** `vo2max_post_breaking` × `distance` — aerobic ceiling benefit from higher VO2max is amplified at longer distances (>1,600 m); sprint performance is less dependent on VO2max and more on anaerobic capacity
