## Abstract (verbatim)

> We investigated the effects of incline on cardiopulmonary function in five trained 2-year-old Thoroughbred horses during exercise on a high-speed treadmill. The horses began exercise at 1.5 m/s for 2 min followed by 1 min step increments at 4.0, 7.0, 8.0, 9.2, and 10.4 m/s. Each horse performed the exercise test on separate days with at least 2 days interval at four different inclines (0, 3, 6 and 10%). The sequence of exercise test was randomized. The major findings were: 1) Oxygen uptake (VO₂), carbon dioxide production (VCO₂), minute ventilation (VE), tidal volume (VT), heart rate (HR), cardiac output (Qt), and % O₂ extraction increased linearly with speed and incline, however the regression estimates of the change in VO₂/change in speed (ΔVO₂/ΔSp) increased with increasing incline. 2) VE increased linearly with speed and was a function of increased VT and respiratory frequency (f), however the linear increase in VE with incline was solely due to increased VT as f was unchanged. 3) The VE/VO₂ slope for 0% incline was greater than the slope 3%, 6%, 10% incline. 4) The increase in arterio-mixed venous oxygen content difference (Ca-vDO₂) with speed and incline was mainly due to the decrease in CvO₂, whereas CaO₂ increased only slightly with incline and speed. 5) The increased Qt with speed and incline was due to increases in HR, as stroke volume was unchanged with speed and incline.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **C (exercise-physiology/fitness)**. This study precisely quantifies how treadmill incline adds cardiopulmonary cost in 2-year-old Thoroughbreds. The five major findings have direct implications for how training-log and race-condition data should be interpreted.

The most important practical finding is that **stroke volume (SV) remains constant at ~1.4 litres across all inclines and speeds**, meaning cardiac output increases exclusively through heart rate. This validates HR-based fitness monitoring as a linear proxy for cardiac output regardless of gradient — heart rate monitors on training tracks (even those with gradients) can be trusted to reflect true cardiac demand.

The ΔVO₂/ΔSpeed slopes increasing with incline (9.00 at 0% → 11.54 at 3% → 12.65 at 6% → 13.04 at 10%) quantify the extra metabolic cost per m/s of speed increase on each gradient. An equivalent 25 ml/kg/min VO₂ increment requires a ΔSp of 2.78 m/s at 0%, 2.17 m/s at 3%, 1.98 m/s at 6%, 1.92 m/s at 10%. This means that training on inclined surfaces is more efficient for inducing aerobic adaptation: less speed is needed to reach the same VO₂ level. For venues where the training track has a significant gradient, the metabolic cost at any given recorded speed is underestimated by flat-track norms.

The VO₂ and Qt at 10% incline vs 0% at 10.4 m/s: 1.57-fold and 1.25-fold increases respectively. Blood lactate (La) increased from 1.6±0.5 mmol/l (0%) to 7.0±0.3 mmol/l (10%) at 10.4 m/s — a 4.4-fold increase. PaO₂ fell from 94.7±3.3 mmHg (0%) to 74.9±1.8 mmHg (10%) at 10.4 m/s, showing significant arterial hypoxaemia at steep inclines.

## Background & objective

Running speed and treadmill incline are the two main methods for increasing exercise workload in equine exercise testing. While HR, VO₂, and VE are known to increase linearly with speed, the detailed pattern of cardiopulmonary responses to graded incline — particularly the relative contributions of VT vs. f to ventilation changes, the behaviour of cardiac output components (HR vs. SV), and O₂ extraction dynamics — was not fully characterised. This study aimed to determine the quantitative effect of four incline levels (0, 3, 6, 10%) on comprehensive cardiopulmonary function.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 5 trained 2-year-old Thoroughbred horses; body weight 454.0±16.5 kg (mean±SE). All had undergone surgical elevation of the left common carotid artery to subcutaneous position ≥4 months prior (for arterial catheterisation). Trained on a track for ≥12 weeks (6 days/week) after surgery, similar to race preparation, and acclimatised to treadmill exercise with a respiratory gas collection mask.

**Exercise protocol:** Mustang-2200 high-speed treadmill (Kagra, Switzerland). Warm-up: 5 min level trotting at 4 m/s, then 5 min rest. Exercise: 1.5 m/s × 2 min, followed by 1 min steps at 4.0, 7.0, 8.0, 9.2, and 10.4 m/s. Four inclines: 0%, 3%, 6%, 10%; tests on separate days ≥2 days apart; order randomised per horse.

**Arterial blood:** Catheter in elevated carotid artery (local anaesthesia). **Mixed venous blood:** 7F Swan-Ganz catheter via left jugular vein to pulmonary artery, pressure-monitored. Both catheters flushed with heparinised saline.

**Gas measurements:**

- VO₂, VCO₂, VE: bias flow ventilatory system (Erickson et al. 1994)
- f: from airflow signal; VT = VE averaged per 15 breaths
- Mixed venous temperature: cardiac output computer on Swan-Ganz catheter

**Cardiorespiratory variables:**

- HR: Polar PE-3000, last 30 s at each step
- Blood gases, O₂ saturation, Hb: ABL-510 Radiometer (Copenhagen) at 37°C, corrected to mixed venous temperature
- CaO₂ = 1.39 × [Hb] × O₂saturation + 0.003 × PaO₂
- Qt (cardiac output) by Fick equation: Qt = VO₂ / (CaO₂ - CvO₂)
- SV = Qt / HR
- La (mixed venous): YSI-1500 Sport

**Statistics:** ANOVA for repeated measures; P<0.05.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

### VO₂ and VCO₂ responses

At each incline, VO₂ and VCO₂ increased linearly with speed (with a trend to level off at highest speed at 10% incline).

**ΔVO₂/ΔSpeed regression slopes (ml/kg/min per m/s):**
| Incline | ΔVO₂/ΔSp slope |
|---------|----------------|
| 0% | 9.00 |
| 3% | 11.54 |
| 6% | 12.65 |
| 10% | 13.04 |

At 10.4 m/s: VO₂ increased 1.57-fold from 0% to 10% incline; VCO₂ increased 1.70-fold (both P<0.05).

**Average ΔSp required for 25 ml/kg/min VO₂ increment:**

- 0%: 2.78 m/s
- 3%: 2.17 m/s
- 6%: 1.98 m/s
- 10%: 1.92 m/s

### Ventilation responses

VE and VT increased linearly with speed and incline. At 10.4 m/s, VE and VT each increased 1.26-fold from 0% to 10% incline (P<0.05).
**Respiratory frequency (f):** increased linearly with speed but was **not significantly affected by incline** at higher speeds (7.0 m/s and above) — VE increase with steepening incline was **solely due to increased VT**.
**VE/VO₂ slope by incline:** 11.0 at 0%, significantly greater than at 3%, 6%, 10% (P<0.05). VE increases less than VO₂ on steeper inclines, suggesting greater metabolic efficiency relative to ventilatory demand.

### O₂ cost

O₂ cost (ml/kg/m) decreased with speed (running economy improvement); decrease was larger at 10% than 0%. O₂ cost increased linearly with steepening incline — energy to raise body mass against gravity adds to forward locomotion cost.

### Cardiac output and stroke volume

**Qt (cardiac output):** increased linearly with speed and incline; at 10.4 m/s, 1.25-fold increase from 0% to 10% (P<0.05).
**SV (stroke volume):** remained essentially constant at ~1.4 litres across all speeds and inclines (no significant change).
**HR:** increased linearly with speed and incline; at 10.4 m/s, 1.20-fold increase from 0% to 10% (P<0.05). Tended to plateau at 10% incline at highest speeds.
**Conclusion:** Qt increase was driven entirely by HR; SV invariable.

At 10% incline at speeds ≥8.0 m/s, O₂ delivery plateaued (constant Qt × slightly decreasing CaO₂), while % O₂ extraction continued to rise linearly — so further VO₂ increases at near-maximal 10% incline work came through greater peripheral extraction rather than higher O₂ delivery.

## Discussion & interpretation

The linearity of VO₂ increase with incline (not previously confirmed across four inclines) means that incline can serve as a calibrated substitute for increasing speed in exercise testing. The higher metabolic cost per unit speed increase on steeper inclines explains why short-speed-duration hill training can match or exceed flat-track interval training in aerobic conditioning effect per session. The VE increase being driven by VT (not f) on steeper inclines is mechanically explained by posterior gut-mass displacement reducing diaphragm compression and allowing greater tidal volume.

The key finding that SV is invariant (~1.4 l) across all conditions — while HR alone determines Qt changes — is consistent with Butler et al. (1993) and Evans and Rose (1987), though Hopper et al. (1991) had reported SV increases in a different protocol. The constant SV means that training-induced cardiac adaptations in the horse are primarily chronotropic (resting bradycardia from conditioning) rather than stroke-volume-mediated, making resting HR the primary ECG fitness marker (consistent with 6_3_79 findings).

The significant arterial hypoxaemia at 10% incline (PaO₂ 74.9 mmHg vs 94.7 mmHg at 0%) at top speed indicates diffusion limitation — the horse cannot fully oxygenate blood at maximal effort on inclines, suggesting that inclined high-speed work is close to the physiological ceiling.

## Limitations

- Very small n (5 horses); no statistical power for detecting modest effects
- 2-year-old horses only; responses may differ in mature racehorses
- All tests at 2-day minimum intervals; fatigue carryover cannot be completely excluded
- Swan-Ganz catheterisation is highly invasive; not applicable to routine monitoring
- Blood gas corrected to mixed venous (not arterial) temperature — minor systematic bias
- Only JRA Thoroughbreds; responses may differ in NAR or steeplechase breeds

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `track_gradient_pct` — gradient of the track or training surface in % — expected effect: higher gradient → higher metabolic cost per recorded speed, so speed-based features should be normalised by gradient if gradient data are available; VO₂ increases 1.57-fold from 0% to 10% at 10.4 m/s — data availability: JRA training track grades are known but typically not in race records
- `speed_at_incline` — if training work speed is recorded alongside track gradient, the incline-corrected speed (equivalent flat speed) is more meaningful than raw speed — derivation: from ΔVO₂/ΔSp slopes: equivalent flat speed at incline i ≈ raw_speed × (slope_0 / slope_i)
- `resting_heart_rate_proxy` — since SV is constant, resting HR is the primary fitness metric; low resting HR indicates high cardiac fitness — data availability: not in standard race records without wearable ECG data
- `blood_lactate_at_race_speed` — at 10.4 m/s on 10% incline, La reaches 7.0±0.3 mmol/l; this benchmark calibrates what blood lactate levels are expected in racing conditions — data availability: not routinely measured in race-day settings
- **Interaction note:** `training_speed × track_gradient` interaction is warranted; at flat 10.4 m/s La=1.6 mmol/l, at 10% incline La=7.0 mmol/l — the metabolic intensity is fundamentally different
