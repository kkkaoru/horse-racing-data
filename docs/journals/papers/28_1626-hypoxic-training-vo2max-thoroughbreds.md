## Abstract (verbatim)

> Hypoxic training is effective for improving athletic performance in humans. It increases maximal oxygen consumption (V̇O₂max) more than normoxic training in untrained horses. However, the effects of hypoxic training on well-trained horses are unclear. We measured the effects of hypoxic training on V̇O₂max of 5 well-trained horses in which V̇O₂max had not increased over 3 consecutive weeks of supramaximal treadmill training in normoxia which was performed twice a week. The horses trained with hypoxia (15% inspired O₂) twice a week. Cardiorespiratory valuables were analyzed with analysis of variance between before and after 3 weeks of hypoxic training. Mass-specific V̇O₂max increased after 3 weeks of hypoxic training (178 ± 10 vs. 194 ± 12.3 ml O₂ (STPD)/(kg × min), P<0.05) even though all-out training in normoxia had not increased V̇O₂max. Absolute V̇O₂max also increased after hypoxic training (86.6 ± 6.2 vs. 93.6 ± 6.6 l O₂ (STPD)/min, P<0.05). Total running distance after hypoxic training increased 12% compared to that before hypoxic training; however, the difference was not significant. There were no significant differences between pre- and post-hypoxic training for end-run plasma lactate concentrations or packed cell volumes. Hypoxic training may increase V̇O₂max even though it is not increased by normoxic training in well-trained horses, at least for the durations of time evaluated in this study. Training while breathing hypoxic gas may have the potential to enhance normoxic performance of Thoroughbred horses.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **C (exercise physiology/fitness)**. VO₂max is the single strongest physiological predictor of aerobic racing capacity in Thoroughbreds — horses with higher mass-specific VO₂max can sustain higher speeds for longer. This paper demonstrates that VO₂max is not at a fixed ceiling even in well-trained racehorses: a specific hypoxic stimulus (15% O₂) increased mass-specific VO₂max by 16 ml O₂/(kg·min) (+9.0%) and absolute VO₂max by 7 L/min (+8.1%) after just 3 weeks, when 3 weeks of maximal normoxic training had failed to produce any increase.

For the prediction model, this result has two implications. First, a horse's current VO₂max state (if estimable) is highly predictive of finishing position — the higher the aerobic capacity, the better the expected performance, especially at distances ≥1400 m where aerobic metabolism dominates. Second, when a horse's VO₂max has plateaued despite continued training (a detectable pattern from V200 data), further normoxic training does not improve it. This means training-load proxies (gallop count, training distances) have diminishing returns once a ceiling is reached — the prediction model should not expect linear training-load → performance improvements for horses at peak fitness.

The null result for end-run plasma lactate (23.1 ± 3.3 vs. 23.2 ± 6.8 mmol/L, P>0.05) and PCV (65.0 ± 3.3 vs. 66.1 ± 3.2%, P>0.05) despite VO₂max improvement suggests that blood lactate concentration and hematocrit at peak exercise are not reliable single-marker proxies for aerobic capacity in already-fit horses. This cautions against treating any single blood marker as a comprehensive fitness feature.

## Background & objective

Thoroughbred horses have mass-specific VO₂max approximately twice that of elite human endurance athletes, but they also experience severe exercise-induced hypoxemia (arterial O₂ tension <75 mmHg at VO₂max). The authors hypothesised that because horses become hypoxemic during maximal exercise anyway, additional hypoxic stimulation during training might further increase VO₂max through mechanisms distinct from simply increasing training volume or intensity. This is motivated by human literature showing hypoxic training increases hemoglobin mass and aerobic performance even in elite athletes. The specific gap addressed: effects of hypoxic training on horses already at a normoxic training plateau.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 5 healthy Thoroughbreds (1 male, 3 geldings, 1 female); mean age 7.0 ± 2.0 (SD) yr; mean BW 483 ± 24 kg. All horses were chronically trained on a 6%-incline treadmill in normoxia, running twice/week. No lameness or unsoundness detected during experiment. Approved by JRA Animal Use and Care Committee and Animal Welfare and Ethics Committee.

**Normoxic training period (pre-selection):** Horses ran supramaximal treadmill intervals twice per week for 3 consecutive weeks. VO₂max values during these 3 weeks were 178 ± 9.1, 180 ± 10.0, 178 ± 10.1 ml O₂/(kg·min) — plateau confirmed; no significant improvement. Horses then selected for hypoxic intervention.

**Normoxic warm-up protocol:** 1.7 m/sec × 2 min → 3.5 m/sec × 3 min → then 2-min intervals at 1.7, 4.0, 7.0, 10.0, 12.0, 13.0, and 14.0 m/sec to exhaustion (6%-incline).

**Hypoxic training:** Semi-open flow mask delivering 15.1 ± 0.2% inspired O₂ (vs. normoxic 20.95%). Protocol: warm-up 1.7 m/sec × 2 min, 4.0 × 2 min, 7.0 × 2 min, then increased speed targeting exhaustion at 2–3 min. Exhaust speed achieved: 12.1 ± 0.45 m/sec. Twice per week for 3 weeks (5 hypoxic sessions total). On 3 other days/week: walking machine at 7 km/hr × 1 hr. Remaining 2 days: stall rest.

**VO₂ measurement:** Open-flow mask (25-cm diameter) with rheostat-controlled 3.8-kW blower, bias-flow 6,000–8,000 L(ATP)/min. O₂ and CO₂ analyzed with METS-900 (VISE Medical). Nafion drying tube; electronic mass flowmeters (Model DPM3, Kofloc). A/D hardware: DI-720-USB (DATAQ), Windaq Pro+ software.

**Blood sampling:** 14-gauge Teflon catheter in left jugular vein placed before exercise. PCV by centrifugation at 12,000×g for 5 min. Plasma lactate by Biosen C-Line analyzer (EKF-diagnostic).

**HR measurement:** Polar S810 heart rate monitor.

**Pre- and post-training evaluation protocol:** Incremental normoxic test at 6%-incline to exhaustion, same protocol as normoxic training. Running distance calculated as sum of (speed × 2 min) per step.

**Statistical analysis:** One-way ANOVA comparing pre- and post-hypoxic training values. P ≤ 0.05 considered significant.

**Pre-hypoxic training normoxic VO₂max values (3 consecutive weeks):** 178 ± 9.1, 180 ± 10.0, 178 ± 10.1 ml O₂/(kg·min) — confirming plateau.

**Hypoxic exercise session parameters:**

- Inspired O₂ fraction: 15.1 ± 0.2%
- Running speed eliciting exhaustion in 2–3 min: 12.1 ± 0.45 m/sec
- Total running distance during hypoxic training session: 2,987 ± 567 m (vs. 5,174 ± 759 m normoxic)

**Key quantitative findings:**

- Mass-specific VO₂max increase: +16 ml O₂/(kg·min) (+9.0%), P<0.05
- Absolute VO₂max increase: +7.0 L/min (+8.1%), P<0.05
- Total running distance increase: +12% (not significant, P=0.29)
- RER decreased from 1.14 to 1.10 (P<0.05), suggesting slightly less anaerobic contribution relative to aerobic work after hypoxic training
- End-run lactate: unchanged (23.1 vs. 23.2 mmol/L, P>0.05)
- PCV: unchanged (65.0 vs. 66.1%, P>0.05)
- Body mass: maintained (483 → 482 kg, no significant loss)

## Discussion & interpretation

The authors interpret the VO₂max improvement as resulting from the hypoxemic stimulus during hypoxic exercise — horses breathing 15% O₂ experience greater arterial desaturation than normoxic training, providing a stronger signal for adaptations at the O₂ transport/utilisation level. The unchanged PCV suggests the mechanism is non-hematological (unlike altitude training in humans where increased hemoglobin mass accounts for >50% of VO₂max gains). Possible mechanisms include: improved mitochondrial density, enhanced O₂ diffusion capacity in muscle, changes in cardiac output, or improved O₂ extraction efficiency.

The unchanged end-run plasma lactate despite higher VO₂max is notable: net anaerobic capacity (reflected by lactate accumulation rate) was not significantly altered. This suggests aerobic and anaerobic adaptations can dissociate — improvement in aerobic ceiling does not automatically improve anaerobic buffer capacity.

Body weight maintenance during hypoxic training (only acute hypoxic exposure, not chronic altitude residency) contrasts with altitude training in humans, where weight loss is common. The horses lived at normoxia and only breathed hypoxic gas during treadmill sessions.

In the prior Nagahisa et al. (2016) study, hypoxic training increased VO₂max in _untrained_ horses even when running speeds were equated. In the present study, all-out normoxic training had reached a plateau. This suggests that for well-trained racehorses at their normoxic ceiling, hypoxic challenge provides an independent stimulus unavailable through more normoxic volume or intensity.

## Limitations

- Very small sample: n = 5 horses; low statistical power for detecting secondary outcomes
- No control group (all horses received hypoxic training; no sham condition)
- Subjects were older ex-racehorses (mean 7.0 yr), not currently in competition; generalizability to active young Thoroughbreds (2–4 yr) uncertain
- Only 5 hypoxic training sessions over 3 weeks; longer interventions might produce additional gains (or diminishing returns)
- Arterial O₂ tension was not measured (only venous PCV and lactate); the hypoxemia mechanism remains presumptive
- Running protocol was not identical between normoxic test and hypoxic training (different speeds/durations), limiting direct intensity comparison
- No follow-up measurements; unknown persistence of VO₂max improvement after return to normoxic training

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `vo2max_ml_per_kg_min` — mass-specific VO₂max measured on treadmill — source: JRA Equine Research Institute training data (not in public JRA race records) — expected effect: strong positive correlation with race speed, especially ≥1400 m — if available from ERI, the single most informative continuous fitness feature
- `v200` — running velocity (m/sec or km/hr) at heart rate = 200 beat/min — source: JRA training monitoring data — expected effect: high V200 → high aerobic fitness → better finishing position in distance races — recommended proxy when VO₂max not measured directly
- `training_plateau_flag` — binary: horse's recent VO₂max or V200 has not improved despite ≥3 weeks of training — source: derived from sequential V200 measurements — expected effect: 0 (improving) → better future performance expected; 1 (plateau) → performance ceiling reached, no expected improvement — caution: this data is unlikely in public JRA records
- `end_run_lactate` — plasma lactate at exhaustion (mmol/L) — source: veterinary/training records — expected effect: weakly correlated with aerobic capacity (null result in this paper at 23 mmol/L) — NOT a reliable proxy in already fit horses; do not over-weight
- `pcv_packed_cell_volume` — % — source: veterinary blood panel — expected effect: theoretically positive (more RBCs → more O₂ carrying capacity); BUT PCV was unchanged despite VO₂max improvement in this study — not a reliable single fitness marker in trained horses; treat as weak/unstable feature
- `rer_respiratory_exchange_ratio` — V̇CO₂/V̇O₂ ratio at maximal exercise — source: treadmill testing only — expected effect: lower RER at same intensity → greater reliance on fat oxidation → better aerobic fitness — significant decrease (1.14→1.10) observed post-hypoxic training — only available from lab testing
- `aerobic_capacity_proxy` — composite: V200 + training load index — source: training records — note: for JRA model, best available proxy for VO₂max is V200 from training GPS; actual VO₂max not routinely measured
- **CAUTION:** lactate and PCV alone should NOT be used as fitness proxies in well-trained Thoroughbreds (null results in this study); require context of VO₂max or V200 data
