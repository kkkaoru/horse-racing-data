## Abstract (verbatim)

> Plasma K accumulation during exercise results from the balance between exchange through biological membranes (mainly muscle fibres and erythrocytes), distribution to other tissues and the haemoconcentration. In the present study, the effect of exercise and training on plasma K concentrations and its relationships with other physiological variables have been analysed in two equine breeds. Twenty male Andalusian (AN) and ten Angloarabian (AA) horses, 7 females and 3 males, were subjected to two standardised exercise tests, composed of four workloads, before and after training. Heart rate (HR) was monitored and venous blood was withdrawn at rest, before each exercise level and during recovery. The following parameters were analysed: packed cell volume (PCV), plasma K, lactate (LA) and total protein (TPP). Furthermore, the horses were filmed and three kinematic parameters were studied: stride duration (SD), frequency (SF) and length (SL). Exercise induced an increase in K from 6 and 8 m/sec in AA and AN horses respectively, a steady-state until the end of the exercise and a decrease after 2 min of recuperation. Some interbreed differences existed, with higher K levels in the AN horses, due to the higher relative exercise intensity, stride frequency and haemoconcentration. K was correlated with HR, PCV, TPP, SL, SD and SF. Training caused a decrease in K in AN, but not in AA horses. Plasma K seems to be a good indicator of the physical effort intensity, fitness and training degrees, but it was not related to the magnitude of the glycolytic response to exercise.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family: **C (exercise-physiology/fitness)**. This paper characterises plasma potassium (K) as a multi-dimensional fitness biomarker that integrates cardiovascular demand (HR), haemoconcentration (PCV), and locomotor kinetics (stride frequency, duration, length) during standardised incremental exercise. Its value for finishing-position prediction lies in the following points.

First, plasma K is correlated with heart rate (r=0.420 in AN, r=0.340 in AA) but not with lactate, meaning K and lactate capture different dimensions of exercise stress. This supports including both K-based and lactate-based fitness indices as complementary, non-redundant features in a model if blood panel data are available, rather than treating them as redundant surrogates.

Second, the training-induced reduction of exercise K in Andalusian horses — but not Angloarabians — demonstrates that breed-specific fitness adaptation determines whether aerobic conditioning is detectable in plasma electrolytes. For JRA/NAR Thoroughbred pipelines, this implies that blood-panel features derived from exercise tests require breed-specific baselines, and that K-reduction signals genuine aerobic improvement specifically in horses with a high pre-training workload intensity relative to their aerobic ceiling.

Third, the correlation between K and stride frequency (r=0.370 AN, r=0.340 AA) and stride duration (r=0.270/0.210) provides a kinematic bridge: gait-analysis measurements obtainable from exercise video could serve as non-invasive proxies for blood electrolyte fitness status.

The paper is based on non-JRA breeds (Spanish Andalusian and Angloarabian) and exercise tests not directly representative of race conditions, so the specific K thresholds reported do not translate directly to JRA/NAR pipelines. However, the mechanistic understanding of K as a relative-intensity proxy and training-adaptation indicator informs how to interpret and normalise any blood panel data, and justifies HR-based features (V200, HR at a standard speed) as practical stand-ins for invasive electrolyte panels.

## Background & objective

Plasma K accumulation during exercise derives from: (1) K efflux rate from active muscle (~0.3–0.9 nmol K/impulse/g muscle); (2) Na⁺-K⁺ pump reuptake (higher in fast-twitch than slow-twitch fibres); (3) haemoconcentration from sweat losses and fluid shifts; (4) K release from erythrocytes. At maximal intensity, plasma K can exceed 8 mmol/L in horses. At prolonged submaximal intensities, K follows a different pattern: rises to steady-state, then falls to below-resting levels during recovery.

Prior work by the same group showed that Andalusian horses had higher HR, PCV, and plasma lactate than Angloarabians during the same absolute exercise test, attributed to different muscle fibre composition (AN: more type I, fewer type IIA than AA), enzyme profiles (AN: higher LDH and glycogen phosphorylase, lower citrate synthase and HAD), and locomotor pattern (AN: greater vertical stride component). The present study asks whether plasma K tracks these breed differences and how training modifies the K response.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 30 horses total: 20 Andalusian (AN) stallions + 10 Angloarabian (AA; 9 females, 1 stallion [paper says "7 females and 3 males"]); aged 3–4 years; withers height ~159.9 ± 2.25 cm (AN) and ~160.5 ± 2.13 cm (AA). Animals with poor performance or physical abnormalities excluded.

**Training programmes:**

- AN horses: 5-week pre-training (walk/trot, 20 min/day, 5×/week); then 12-week programme with daily walk (20–30 min), trot (25–30 min), canter/gallop (12–15 min), 5 days/week. Total 22 weeks.
- AA horses: Phase A (8 weeks): 35–40 min/day, 5×/week (5 min walk, 20 min trot, 5–10 min gallop). Phase B (16 weeks): jumping training with 15–20 jumps at 0.80–1.30 m height, 3 days/week. Total 24 weeks.

**Standardised exercise test (SET):**

- 4 workloads at 5, 6, 7, and 8 m/s, 1,000 m per workload with 2-min rest between workloads
- Preceded by 1,000 m warm-up at 4 m/s trot
- Real velocities: 4.94 ± 0.37, 6.08 ± 0.47, 7.08 ± 0.49, 8.13 ± 0.33 m/s (not significantly different from targets)
- SET1: AN at week 10 (post-break-in), AA at week 8 (end of phase A)
- SET2: AN at week 22, AA at week 24

**Heart rate monitoring:** Polar Sport Heart Rate Tester, data stored every 5 sec; mean HR per workload after stabilisation.

**Blood sampling:** Jugular venous blood at rest, immediately after warm-up, within first 30 sec after each workload, then at 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 15, and 30 min recovery (active walking at ~2.5 m/s).

- PCV: Sysmex-F820 semiautomatic analyser
- Plasma lactate (LA): enzymatic method (Analox Champion PLM5, L-lactate oxidoreductase)
- Plasma K and total plasma protein (TPP): selective electrode analyser (Ciba-Corning 644) and refractometer (Atago) respectively
- Blood divided into EDTA-3K (haematology) and heparin-lithium (plasma) fractions; centrifuged rapidly on ice; analysed within 12 hr

**Functional indices calculated:**

- VLA2 and VLA4: velocity at plasma lactate of 2 and 4 mmol/L (extrapolated from exponential LA–velocity curve)
- V150 and V200: velocity at HR of 150 and 200 bpm (extrapolated from linear HR–velocity relationship)

**Kinematics:** Horses filmed from left side with Sony Video Camcorder Hi8 placed perpendicular to movement plane. 5 trot strides and 3–4 gallop strides averaged. Stride duration (SD, s), frequency (SF, strides/min), length (SL, m) derived from temporal-velocity analysis.

**Statistics:** Paired t-test for within-breed exercise × training comparisons; Pearson correlation for K vs physiological variables; significance threshold p<0.05.

- AN horses: K significantly decreased after training (p<0.05) at rest, during workloads, and recovery; reduced haemoconcentration (PCV 57.89% → 51.90%) likely contributor

## Discussion & interpretation

The K response pattern (increase → steady-state → rapid recovery) is typical of prolonged submaximal exercise, where K release from contracting muscle is matched by Na⁺-K⁺ pump reuptake and intercompartmental fluid shifts. Higher K in AN horses reflects their higher relative exercise intensity (~80% vs ~62% VO₂max), not intrinsically different K regulation: when fitness levels are matched (SET2 AN vs SET1 AA), interbreed K differences are attenuated.

The surprising absence of a K–lactate correlation in AN SET1 is explained by two possible mechanisms: (1) blood acidosis from high LA may reduce muscle-plasma K diffusion rate at very high LA levels (>14 mmol/L); (2) hypovolemia from heavy haemoconcentration may limit muscle-plasma lactate clearance. This dissociation is meaningful: it means that in horses working near their aerobic ceiling, K reflects cardiovascular demand while LA reflects glycolytic load, and the two signals are genuinely complementary.

Training effect: In AN horses, post-training reduction in K mirrors the reduction in PCV (haemoconcentration reduced) and in LA, consistent with a shift toward more oxidative metabolism (confirmed histologically by increased type IIA fibres and citrate synthase activity in prior studies by the same group). In AA horses, training for jumping (strength/anaerobic) did not produce aerobic adaptation in K or HR; any early aerobic adaptation may have occurred before SET1 was performed.

## Limitations

- Non-Thoroughbred breeds (Andalusian, Angloarabian); results not directly generalisable to JRA/NAR Thoroughbred context. Thoroughbreds have far higher V200 (~9–10 m/s) than the AN horses tested (~7.7 m/s).
- Training programmes differed between breeds (endurance vs jumping), precluding direct breed comparison of training effects.
- Small sample sizes (20 AN, 10 AA); individual variation substantial, especially in AA (larger SDs).
- K measurement from jugular venous blood; arterial and muscle interstitial K would be physiologically more direct but impractical in field.
- Plasma K, not whole-blood K; erythrocyte K contribution to hyperkalemia not directly quantified.
- Absolute exercise velocities (up to 8 m/s) are below racing speed (~16–18 m/s for JRA Thoroughbreds); extrapolation of K thresholds to race conditions is uncertain.

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `V200` (velocity at HR 200 bpm) — derived from linear HR–speed relationship during standardised exercise — source: exercise telemetry if available from training logs — strong fitness indicator; higher V200 → lower HR at any given submaximal speed → better aerobic capacity; V200 increased with aerobic training in AN horses, breed-specific baseline needed; available from some JRA research datasets (see Ohmura et al. 2002, Kobayashi et al. 1999)
- `V150` (velocity at HR 150 bpm) — same derivation as V200 but at lower absolute intensity — less sensitive than V200 for trained horses but useful for identifying grossly unfit horses
- `VLA4` (velocity at blood lactate 4 mmol/L) — aerobic threshold proxy — requires blood sampling equipment; not available from standard JRA/NAR race records but used in JRA research facilities
- `post_exercise_HR_recovery_rate` — rate of HR decline at 2, 4, 6 min post-exercise — proxy for cardiac efficiency and training state; lower resting HR and faster recovery → better fitness → expected negative correlation with finishing position (better finishers recover faster)
- `stride_frequency_at_race_speed` — strides/min during gallop at near-race speed — correlates with K (r=0.370 AN, r=0.340 AA), HR, and PCV; proxy for biomechanical efficiency; potentially derivable from video-analysis or accelerometer data
- `stride_length_at_race_speed` — m/stride — AA had significantly longer SL than AN (4.017 vs 2.035 m/stride at 8 m/s); positively correlated with HR and fitness; longer stride → lower stride frequency at same speed → lower cardiac demand
- **Caution:** Plasma K itself is not available from standard JRA/NAR race records. Its main value is as mechanistic validation for using HR-based and gait-based features as non-invasive fitness surrogates. The breed-specificity finding (AN vs AA) implies that absolute K thresholds cannot be transferred across breeds without re-calibration.
