## Abstract (verbatim)

> A 2-year-old Thoroughbred racehorse had LF lameness that began post high-speed exercise and persisted for two days before the horse once again became sound. Diagnostic analgesia localized the lameness to the LF distal metacarpus, and a standing MRI identified a unicortical condylar fracture. A single 5.5 mm cortical screw was placed in lag fashion. The horse began hand walking at 14 days, racetrack jogging at 30 days, and racing at 5 months after the day of surgery. Placement of a single lag screw ahead of the tip of the crack in unicortical condylar fracture may be useful for reducing the recovery period for horses returning to training and racing.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

This paper belongs to feature family **A (injury/soundness)**. MC3/MT3 condylar fractures are the most common fracture type and the second most common cause of euthanasia in North American Thoroughbreds (and the most common in Europe). They arise from bone stress injury (BSI), a pathologic continuum progressing from stress reaction → stress fracture → gross fracture, driven by cumulative high-speed exercise fatigue.

For finishing-position prediction, the key implications are:

1. **BSI risk accumulator**: cumulative high-speed exercise distance (furlongs of works per week/month) is the principal biomechanical driver of MC3/MT3 BSI. A horse with a very dense recent high-speed work schedule, especially if it has shown a brief transient lameness that resolved, is at elevated fracture risk — which in turn predicts either DNF (catastrophic fracture) or scratch/performance decline.

2. **Return-to-racing timeline as a signal**: unicortical condylar fracture treated with lag screw → first race ~5 months post-surgery (this case: 157 days). Bicortical condylar fracture → first race ~11 months post-surgery (literature average). A gap between a horse's last race and current entry of 5–6 months may indicate recovery from a unicortical fracture (with good prognosis), while 10–12 months may indicate bicortical fracture recovery (more guarded prognosis).

3. **Prodromal lameness pattern**: brief lameness 2 days post-race → resolution → continuation of training is a classic prodrome of BSI. In race records, this might manifest as a 2–4 week gap in training intensity (if training data available) before a return to competition. A horse that has "bounced back quickly" from a brief lameness episode could be at higher risk of fracture in subsequent races.

4. **Bone mineral density paradox**: a 5–8% increase in BMD from training can increase bone toughness by >60% but also increases brittleness. This suggests an inverted U-shaped relationship between training load and fracture resistance — both undertrained and overtrained horses are at risk (undertrained: insufficient density; overtrained: brittle, accumulated fatigue damage).

## Background & objective

MC3/MT3 condylar fractures are the most common fracture in Thoroughbred racehorses; they are also a leading cause of euthanasia (most common in Europe; second most common in North America after biaxial proximal sesamoid bone fractures). In California, racehorse fatalities injure the jockey in 54% of associated spills. Reducing condylar fracture risk is therefore critical for welfare, safety, and economic reasons.

The Fredrickson BSI classification scale (originally developed for human tibial stress reactions) classifies BSI in 4 grades: (1) stress reaction (bone marrow edema, periosteal reaction), (2) stress fracture without visible fracture line, (3) stress fracture with fracture line, (4) gross/complete fracture. Grade 4 BSI of the distal palmar MC3 = unicortical condylar fracture. Only MRI (not radiography, scintigraphy, or CT) can reliably detect Grades 1–3; Grade 4 is the earliest reliably detectable stage on advanced 3D imaging.

The study aimed to document whether early diagnosis of a unicortical (grade 4) fracture via standing MRI and immediate lag-screw fixation enables faster return to racing than the bicortical fracture standard (11 months), and to propose a biomechanical hypothesis for how the screw affects crack propagation.

## Materials & methods

**Subject:** Single 2-year-old intact male Thoroughbred racehorse ("Wait"), south Florida training centre. 6 months in race training prior to fracture.

**Injury history and clinical presentation:**

- First race: June 10, 2016, 5 furlongs, south Florida racetrack
- Fracture event: June 23, 2016, 4-furlong high-speed work → immediate grade 2/5 LF lameness
- Lameness persisted 2 days then resolved spontaneously

**Physical examination during lameness:**

- Grade 2/5 left front lameness
- LFF cold to the touch; no joint distension; negative fetlock flexion response
- Positive nerve block: diagnostic analgesia of lateral metacarpal nerve eliminated LF lameness
- Differential diagnosis: lateral condylar fracture or distal MC3 periosteal reaction

**Imaging:**

- Standard radiographic views (dorsopalmar, lateromedial, DLPMO, DMPLO): unable to visualise fracture
- Flexed dorsodisto-palmaroproximal oblique: "subtle but suspicious line in the lateral parasagittal groove of the distal lateral MC3 bone"
- Standing MRI (July 5, 2016): Hallmarq Veterinary Imaging, 0.27 Tesla; standard orthogonal planes + T1W gradient echo, T2\*W gradient echo, STIR sequences
  - T1W: area of moderate signal decrease (periphery) + hyperintense line from palmar lateral parasagittal groove
  - T2\*W: moderate signal increase area surrounded by black phase-cancellation line + hyperintense fracture line
  - STIR: marked signal increase (bone marrow edema) in axial lateral condyle and parasagittal groove
  - Diagnosis: unicortical palmar lateral condylar fracture = Fredrickson BSI Grade 4; no sesamoid bone injury
  - Fracture location: only the 2 most palmar/distal MRI slices showed the crack

**Surgery (July 6, 2016):**

- General anaesthesia; right lateral recumbency; left forelimb extended
- Single 5.5 mm cortical lag screw placed lateral-to-medial in lateral supracondylar fossa of left front MC3
- Screw positioned dorsal and proximal to the fracture crack front (ahead of crack tip)
- Richardson (2006) technique for condylar fracture screw placement

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

**Rehabilitation outcomes:**

- Hand walking at 14 days post-surgery
- Racetrack jogging at 30 days post-surgery
- First high-speed work (2 F) at 82 days post-surgery
- **First race: 157 days (5 months 4 days) post-surgery** — finished 2nd in 6 F race
- **First win: 183 days (5 months 30 days) post-surgery** — finished 1st by 5½ lengths in 6 F race
- Total racing career post-surgery: 5 races (2nd, 1st, 3rd, 8th, 1st) before sesamoid fracture 322 days post-surgery
- Retired due to sesamoid fracture — **independent injury; no evidence of LF medial sesamoid injury on original MRI**

**Literature benchmarks reproduced from paper:**

- Unicortical condylar fracture, >90% return-to-racing rate when treated early (multiple sources)
- Bicortical condylar fracture, average time to first race post-surgery: ~11 months (Richardson 2006)
- This case unicortical: 5 months 4 days = approximately half the bicortical timeline
- Ramzan et al. 2015 (45 unicortical fractures, 2006–2013): most common rehabilitation = 8 weeks total stall/horse-walker (2–4 weeks total confinement + 30–90 min/day horse-walker); only 5/45 treated with lag screw; outcomes not specified for lag-screw subset vs. conservative
- Fracture counts from Jacklin & Wright 2012: 174 condylar fractures in 167 Thoroughbreds (1999–2009); majority of condylar fractures are parasagittal and begin as unicortical fractures in the palmar (plantar) lateral parasagittal groove

**Bone material science data from paper:**

- Bone composition: ~25% weight collagenous fibres (tension strength/stiffness) + ~50% weight rigid calcium crystals (compression strength/stiffness)
- Bone mineral density (BMD): training increases BMD by 5–8% → improves toughness by >60% but increases brittleness (paradox of brittle ceramics)
- Equine compact bone: stiff and strong in compression = brittle post-yield behaviour (Les et al. 2002 J. Orthop. Res. 20: 607–614)
- Three crack propagation modes: Mode 1 (opening/tensile, perpendicular to crack plane) = most common; Mode 2 (sliding); Mode 3 (tearing)
- Proposed mechanism: single lag screw placed dorsal and proximal to crack front → opposes the lateromedial tensile (Mode 1) force → reduces crack propagation during training and racing

## Discussion & interpretation

The "unicortical" terminology (introduced by Ramzan et al. 2015 as cited in this paper) precisely describes the fracture stage identified only by MRI: the crack has penetrated the palmar cortex but has not yet extended through to the dorsal cortex (bicortical). This stage is extra-articular to the fetlock joint, explaining the absence of synovial effusion and joint distension — the classic diagnostic pitfalls that lead to missed or delayed diagnosis.

The fracture progresses from palmar lateral parasagittal groove → proximal dorsal → dorsal cortex under the transverse tensile (lateromedial) forces of the galloping gait. The screwed case in this report returned to racing in half the time of bicortical repairs, consistent with the hypothesis that the lag screw disrupts crack propagation by opposing Mode 1 tensile forces ahead of the crack tip.

The materials science analysis (analogous to ceramic fracture mechanics) highlights that horse bone at the MC3 condyle is particularly brittle because of high mineral content from adaptation to training loads. This brittleness means that once a crack initiates, propagation can be rapid and non-linear — consistent with the clinical observation that prodromal lameness can resolve and then catastrophic fracture occurs without further warning.

The sesamoid fracture at 322 days post-surgery is explicitly noted as a new independent injury unrelated to the condylar fracture treatment, confirming that the early return to training did not predispose to the new injury.

The authors explicitly note that the standing MRI is critical to this approach: it is the only imaging modality that identifies bone marrow edema (BSI Grade 1–3), and identifying fractures at the unicortical stage (Grade 4) before they progress to bicortical stage allows both a shorter recovery time and a better prognosis.

## Limitations

- **Single case report**: no control group; the 5-month return time is the fastest documented for this fracture type post-screw fixation, but a single case cannot establish whether the screw vs. conservative management accounts for the speed
- **Standing MRI access**: 0.27 Tesla Hallmarq units are not universally available; most diagnosis of early BSI in field settings requires a high index of suspicion and the ability to arrange referral quickly
- **Selection bias**: the horse presented for evaluation during the 2-day lameness window; many cases resolve faster and are never diagnosed; the true rate of unicortical condylar fractures in training populations is unknown
- **Screw hypothesis untested**: the biomechanical argument for how the single screw affects crack propagation is theoretical; has not been validated in a materials testing machine as the authors acknowledge
- **2-year-old, male, Florida racetrack**: may not generalise to older horses, other surfaces, or other training regimes

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `high_speed_work_density` — cumulative furlongs at high speed in preceding 30/60/90 days; principal BSI risk accumulator; source: JRA training records (if available) or race gap patterns; expected positive association with condylar fracture risk → negative on finishing position via scratch/DNF/underperformance
- `lameness_gap_2_14_days` — transient lameness lasting 2–14 days followed by return to work (visible in race gap + training records as brief interruption); classic BSI prodrome; expected: flag for elevated fracture risk in subsequent 60 days
- `months_since_last_race` — gap of 4–6 months signals probable unicortical fracture recovery (good prognosis, ~>90% return rate); gap of 9–13 months signals probable bicortical fracture recovery (more variable prognosis); source: race date differencing; interaction with age (younger horses have higher return rates)
- `age_at_fracture_injury` — 2-year-olds have highest fracture incidence (skeletal immaturity + rapid onset of high-speed training); source: age at first race date
- `career_race_distance_cumulative` — total career furlongs raced; proxy for accumulated skeletal fatigue; expected positive association with BSI risk in high-intensity flat racers
- `race_distance_m_surface` — short dirt sprint (1,000–1,400 m) → highest loading rate on MC3 from starts; surface hardness modulates peak loads (sand/turf vs. dirt)
- `condylar_fracture_history_flag` — if horse had a condylar fracture in prior career, note time since surgery: unicortical (5–6 months) = good prognosis; bicortical (11–12 months) = guarded; source: injury records
- `bmd_training_load_paradox` — horses in intense training phases (high BMD) are paradoxically at higher fracture risk from increased brittleness; this non-linear risk is not directly observable from race data but may be proxied by win rate → sudden regression after peak performance periods
- **Caution:** MC3/MT3 unicortical fractures are clinically silent (no effusion, negative flexion, negative intra-articular block) — lameness records undercount fracture prevalence; race-gap features will miss many cases
