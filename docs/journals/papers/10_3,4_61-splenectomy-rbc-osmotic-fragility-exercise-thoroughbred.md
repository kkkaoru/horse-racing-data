## Abstract (verbatim)

> Effects of treadmill exercise on osmotic fragility of circulating erythrocytes in jugular venous blood were investigated before and after splenectomy in 3 control and 3 splenectomized thoroughbreds. Both before and after the operation, the fragility of circulating erythrocytes significantly decreased with warming-up, but significantly increased with incremental exercise, and again significantly decreased with cooling down. The fragility of circulating erythrocytes was lower than that of the autologous splenic red cells, and it gradually decreased after the splenectomy. These result suggest that: 1) frequent accumulation in the spleen accelerates the fragility of erythrocytes; and 2) heavy exercise increases fragility, but light exercise decreases it, regardless of the release of red cells from the spleen into the circulation.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **C (exercise-physiology/fitness)**. This is one of the primary empirical papers underlying the Hanzawa & Watanabe review (docid 11_3_51). It provides the key experimental evidence that the equine spleen is a direct determinant of aerobic performance capacity through its role in mobilising a large erythrocyte reserve during exercise.

The central finding for finishing-position prediction is quantitative: **splenectomised Thoroughbreds show a 14% reduction in peak running speed** (8.75 vs. 10.20 m/s, p < 0.05) despite identical running time and blood lactate profile — meaning reduced O₂-carrying capacity from loss of splenic RBC reserve directly limits peak speed. This is the cleanest single-study demonstration that oxygen transport, proxied by PCV increase during exercise, is a primary physiological gate on racehorse performance.

For the prediction pipeline, this means: any blood marker or veterinary finding that correlates with reduced splenic function, chronically elevated RBC fragility, or impaired PCV augmentation during exercise is a candidate negative performance feature. Conversely, high pre-race PCV or haematocrit (reflecting a healthy, fully-functioning spleen and adequate erythropoiesis) is a positive performance feature.

The paper also confirms that **blood lactate does not differ between groups despite different peak speeds** — lactate is equated to exercise intensity, not performance ceiling. This is an important caution: lactate alone is not a direct performance predictor; it must be interpreted relative to the speed at which it was measured.

## Background & objective

Prior conflicting reports existed: Boucher et al. (1985) and Sakurai et al. (1967) found exercise increased erythrocyte osmotic fragility (EOF) in Thoroughbreds; Smith et al. (1989) found it decreased. Hanzawa et al. (1995, 1996, 1998/1999 series) proposed the reconciling hypothesis that (1) splenic storage degrades RBC quality, (2) aerobic exercise decreases EOF, and (3) anaerobic exercise increases EOF — all independent of whether splenic release occurred. This paper provides the controlled splenectomy experiment to directly test whether splenic RBC release was the causal factor in exercise-induced EOF changes. A preliminary report appeared in Equine Vet. J. Suppl. 18 (1995).

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 6 Thoroughbreds — 5 two-year-olds and 1 five-year-old; 3 males, 1 gelding, 2 females; bodyweight 459 ± 14 kg. Divided into:

- Group C (n = 3): sham operation — 18th rib removed, wound sutured immediately; spleen left intact
- Group S (n = 3): full splenectomy — spleen surgically extirpated after removal of the 18th rib on the left side

**Timing:** Surgical procedures performed at least 6 months before exercise testing. Pre-operative and post-operative (2 months post-op) exercise tests performed.

**Conditioning:** Horses conditioned on treadmill (Mustang-2200, Kagra, Switzerland) 5 days/week for 1 month before each exercise test; 15 min treadmill exercise including 2 min submaximal exercise.

**Exercise protocol:**

1. Warm-up: 4 min at 4 m/s
2. Rest: 5 min (0% incline)
3. Walk: 4 min at 1.5 m/s
4. Trot: 2 min at 4.4 m/s
5. Canter to gallop: 3–4 incremental speed steps until fatigue (7% incline)
6. Cool down: 20 min

**Blood sampling:** Jugular venepuncture at:

- 30 min before treadmill start (resting condition)
- Immediately after (within 30 sec of) warm-up
- Immediately after incremental exercise test
- Immediately after cooling down
- Splenic blood collected from surgically removed spleen immediately after splenectomy

**Measurements:**

- Osmotic fragility (EOF): haemolysis rate (HL, %) in 0.56% NaCl hypotonic solution, after washing with ice-cold Dulbecco's phosphate buffered saline
- PCV: microhaematocrit centrifuge
- Blood lactate [La⁻]: YSI-1500 Sport automated lactate analyser
- Heart rate: Polar PE-3000 computer

**Statistics:** One-way ANOVA; multiple comparisons by LSD (P < 0.05).

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

**Peak running velocity and running time (Table 1):**

| Condition                           | Group C (m/s) | Group S (m/s)   |
| ----------------------------------- | ------------- | --------------- |
| Running velocity before operation   | 10.4 ± 0.18   | 10.4 ± 0.15     |
| Running velocity after operation    | 10.20 ± 0.19  | **8.75 ± 0.27** |
| Running time before operation (min) | 4.01 ± 0.33   | 3.98 ± 0.26     |
| Running time after operation (min)  | 4.00 ± 0.22   | 4.17 ± 0.21     |

Running velocity after operation: Group S significantly lower than Group C (p < 0.05, LSD). Running time was **not significantly different** between groups before or after operation.

**Heart rate (HR) during exercise:**

- Both groups: warm-up significantly increased HR; incremental exercise increased it further; cooling down decreased it
- Before operation: no significant difference between Groups C and S in HR
- After operation: HR at warm-up and at last incremental exercise step in Group S was **significantly higher** than Group C and both groups before operation (p < 0.05, LSD)
- After cooling down: no difference between C and S

**PCV during exercise:**

- Group C before and after operation: warm-up → PCV significantly increased; incremental exercise → PCV increased further; cooling down → PCV returned to initial resting level
- Group S before operation: same pattern as Group C
- **Group S after splenectomy: no change in PCV during the entire exercise test**
- PCV at warm-up and at last incremental step in Group S after splenectomy was **significantly lower** than Group C and both groups before operation (p < 0.05, LSD)

**Blood lactate [La⁻] during exercise:**

- Both groups, both time points: [La⁻] did not change with warm-up; significantly increased with incremental exercise; decreased with cooling down
- **No significant differences between Groups C and S in [La⁻]** at any condition, either before or after operation

**Splenic RBC osmotic fragility:**

- Splenic red cells (collected at splenectomy): HL = **81 ± 8.9%**
- This was **significantly higher** than circulating erythrocytes in Group S at the same time

**Circulating RBC osmotic fragility (HL) over time (Fig. 1):**

- Before operation: HL was similar in Groups C and S
- Group C: no change in HL from 1 month before to 8 months after operation
- Group S: **significant decrease in HL at month 2 post-splenectomy**; lower values remained constant through month 8
- At month 2 post-operation: HL in Group C was significantly higher than Group S (p < 0.05, LSD)

**EOF response to exercise (Fig. 5):**

- Group S HL was **significantly lower** than Group C regardless of exercise condition (before and after operation)
- Group S: warm-up → HL significantly decreased; incremental exercise → HL significantly increased; cooling down → HL significantly decreased — **same directional pattern as Group C**
- Group C pattern: same as Group S but with higher absolute HL values

**Key mechanistic interpretation:** The increase in PCV during exercise in Group C (and in Group S before splenectomy) was due to splenic RBC release. The physical stress of increased blood flow friction during galloping, plus chemical stress of metabolic acidosis (elevated [La⁻]) during incremental exercise, independently raised EOF in both groups. The release of lysolecithin from the spleen (via epinephrine-triggered release) contributed to additional fragility increase in Group C during heavy exercise.

Splenic storage progressively degrades RBC membranes: the 81 ± 8.9% HL of splenic RBCs vs. circulating RBCs confirms that stored cells are substantially more fragile. Once these cells are released during exercise, they increase average circulating fragility in intact horses. After splenectomy, the circulating pool loses these chronically fragile cells and overall fragility decreases.

## Discussion & interpretation

The main conclusion is clearly established: splenic RBC release does not cause acute EOF changes during exercise. Rather, frequent accumulation in the spleen accelerates fragility, and the exercise-induced EOF changes (decrease with light exercise, increase with heavy exercise) are driven by blood pH and temperature changes independent of splenic release.

The 14% reduction in peak running speed after splenectomy confirms that the splenic erythrocyte reservoir is essential for achieving racing-level velocities, because it provides the oxygen-carrying capacity increase needed for maximal aerobic performance. Despite lower peak speed, splenectomised horses maintained similar running time and identical lactate levels — indicating they exercised at subjectively maximal effort but with a lower physiological ceiling.

The reason cooling down decreases EOF despite potentially ongoing splenic RBC release in Group C is interpreted as: (a) increased blood pH and temperature from aerobic ventilation provide protection, and (b) osmotically sensitive cells destroyed during the incremental phase are no longer present. The temporary rise in HL after cooling down toward resting level in the warm-up and cooling phases is explained by gradual re-equilibration.

## Limitations

- n = 6 (3 per group); statistical power is very limited; individual variation in splenic size and function could substantially affect results
- Both 2-year-olds and one 5-year-old included; age effects on splenic function not separated
- Single breed, limited sex variation (3 male, 1 gelding, 2 female)
- 6+ months between surgery and first exercise test; long-term adaptation of haematological system to splenectomy is substantial — the experimental condition represents full adaptation, not acute splenic loss
- No measurement of splenic contraction force or dynamics; the mechanism of splenic release rate during exercise is inferred

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `pre_race_PCV` — packed cell volume (%) measured pre-race — source: veterinary panel — expected effect: higher PCV → greater O₂-carrying capacity available; highest discriminative value at maximal effort distances (sprint races) — data availability: not standard in public race records; JRA/NAR vet records
- `PCV_exercise_delta` — PCV immediately post-exercise minus resting PCV — derivation: from paired blood draws around a trial run — expected effect: larger delta → healthier splenic response → higher aerobic capacity; this is a direct measure of splenic function — data availability: research context only
- `haematocrit_trend` — trend in resting PCV across last 3–4 measurements — source: periodic blood panel data if available — expected effect: rising trend → improving condition and fitness; falling trend → overtraining, illness, or splenic dysfunction
- `HR_at_standard_speed` — heart rate at a fixed submaximal speed (e.g., 7 m/s) from a field test or workout monitoring — derivation: from HR monitoring during training runs — expected effect: lower HR at standard speed → better cardiovascular fitness and presumably better splenic O₂-delivery; relationship to peak speed directly supported by the splenectomy model — data availability: JRA uses HR monitors in some training contexts
- `blood_lactate_at_standard_speed` — blood lactate at a fixed submaximal speed — derivation: paired HR/lactate test — expected effect: lower lactate at given speed → horse is more aerobically fit → positive for performance — data availability: same as HR; occasional research programme data
- CAUTION: `blood_lactate` alone without associated speed cannot be used as a performance predictor — this paper confirms that groups with very different peak speeds can have **identical lactate** profiles. Lactate is a measure of exercise intensity relative to aerobic threshold, not of absolute performance ceiling.
