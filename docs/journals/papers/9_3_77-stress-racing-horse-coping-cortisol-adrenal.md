## Abstract (verbatim)

> The hormones of the adrenal axis help athletes adapt to the stress of training and racing. When performance is poor, dysfunction of this axis is often suspected, but confirming this has been difficult. We have found three hallmarks of chronic stress in horses that are easily monitored: 1) loss of the circadian cortisol rhythm, 2) decrease in corticosteroid-binding globulin and an increase in free cortisol, and 3) reduced ACTH (& hence cortisol) response to a small dose (2 µg) of equine/human corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH). The presence of some or all of these abnormalities is a good indication that the horse is not coping with the stresses imposed upon him.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **C (exercise-physiology / fitness)** and **E (environment / stress management)**. This review identifies three measurable biomarker hallmarks of chronic overtraining ("not coping") in racehorses: (1) abolition of the circadian cortisol rhythm, (2) reduced corticosteroid-binding globulin (CBG) capacity with elevated free (biologically active) cortisol, and (3) blunted pituitary ACTH response to a standardised 2 µg CRH challenge. Horses displaying these abnormalities are performing below their physiological capability — the "not coping" state directly predicts underperformance.

For a finishing-position model, total plasma cortisol (the standard clinical measurement) is shown to be an unreliable and misleading feature because chronic stress leaves total cortisol unchanged or variable across individuals, even though free cortisol is consistently elevated. This finding is important: if cortisol data are available in training records, only free cortisol (or CBG capacity) should be used, not total cortisol. The distinction between acute race-day cortisol surge (benign, recovers within 24 h) and chronic overtraining cortisol dysregulation (performance-degrading) provides the conceptual basis for context-sensitive cortisol feature construction.

The "morning-after" cortisol suppression finding is directly applicable: a horse sampled in the 24 h after a hard race will show artificially low cortisol — any cortisol feature should be flagged as "post-race day" to avoid misclassifying recovered horses as chronically stressed.

The anticipatory ACTH/cortisol rise at the racetrack (before racing begins) is a documented source of noise in pre-race physiological features. If pre-race cortisol measurements are ever used, the transport/novelty environment effect must be controlled.

## Background & objective

The paper opens with the overtraining syndrome: poor performance, chronic fatigue, apathy, weight loss, increased illness susceptibility, and disturbed adrenal axis function. Prior conflicting studies found overtrained horses with either low basal cortisol and reduced ACTH response (Persson et al. 1980), normal basal cortisol but decreased exercise-cortisol response (Golland et al. 1996), or normal basal cortisol but increased ACTH response (Bruin et al. 1994). The authors argue these discrepancies arise because total cortisol is an inaccurate measure of adrenal axis function. The review synthesises ~10 years of the authors' own research programme at Lincoln University to propose three reliable, diagnosable hallmarks of the not-coping state.

## Materials & methods

**Primary empirical study (Alexander & Irvine 1998, J. Endocrinol.):** Social-instability chronic stress model. 17 "newcomer" horses confined 3–4 hr/day in a small yard with 4 dominant resident mares for up to 2 weeks. Newcomers returned to separate paddock after each session (denied the chance to integrate). Morning jugular blood collected before each day's stress exposure. Residents bled on days 1 and 13.

**Racing cohort:** 16–17 Thoroughbred/Standardbred racehorses sampled the morning after a hard race; 4 control racehorses not recently raced.

**Racetrack anticipation study:** Standardbred racehorses sampled several hours before racing at the racetrack; compared with trained stable horses bled at the same time of day.

**Measurements:** Total plasma cortisol (RIA); free cortisol (equilibrium dialysis or calculated from CBG binding); CBG binding capacity (equilibrium saturation binding assay, nmol/L); plasma ACTH (RIA); CRH challenge: 2 µg human/equine CRH intravenous bolus, ACTH measured in pituitary venous blood (non-surgical collection method unique to these authors) or jugular blood.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

**Circadian cortisol rhythm:**

- Normal horses: clear circadian rhythm with morning peak (0600–0900 hr) and evening trough (1800–2100 hr).
- Mild stress (novel indoor barn housing, horses normally on pasture): morning peak levels not significantly different between housed and control horses; evening trough levels **significantly higher** in housed horses — the trough is filled in by continued cortisol secretion. Circadian rhythm obliterated within days.
- Implication for sampling: loss of rhythm cannot be diagnosed from a single sample; requires samples at both peak and trough times in the horse's normal, relaxed home environment.

**CBG and free cortisol (social instability model):**

- After 3–4 days of confinement stress: CBG binding capacity in newcomers **significantly lower** than before stress; free cortisol concentrations **significantly higher**.
- Total cortisol: unchanged on average; direction of change varied among individuals — some increased, some decreased, some unchanged.
- Free cortisol: elevated in **ALL newcomers** regardless of what total cortisol did (key finding).
- Changes in CBG and free cortisol maintained into the second week.
- By week 2: all newcomers had **lost weight** and developed **upper respiratory tract infections** (symptoms of chronic hypercortisolemia / overtraining syndrome).
- Residents: no change in CBG or free cortisol between days 1 and 13.

**CBG after acute race stress (quantitative data):**
| Condition | CBG binding capacity (nmol/L) | n |
|-----------|------------------------------|---|
| Immediately before racing | 186 ± 10 | 17 |
| Morning after racing | 187 ± 12 | 16 |

No significant difference — a single race does not lower CBG. The stress must be prolonged beyond one day.

**Morning-after cortisol (quantitative from Fig. 2):**

- Racehorses morning after a hard race: mean total cortisol **suppressed below** the "normal day" baseline in racehorses not recently raced; n = 16 racehorses after race vs. n = 4 controls.

**CRH challenge (2 µg IV):**

- Normal healthy horses: unmistakable rise in ACTH secretion.
- Chronically stressed newcomers (social instability model): ACTH response to CRH **blunted or absent**.
- CRH pre-administration appears elevated in chronically stressed newcomers (preliminary measurements only).

**Anticipatory adrenal axis activation:**

- Standardbred racehorses at racetrack several hours before racing: plasma cortisol **significantly higher** than trained horses bled at the same time of day in stable.
- Authors' own racehorses showed ACTH and AVP secretion and cortisol rise **immediately before** going onto the track, even before exercise began.

**Markers summary table:**

| Marker                       | Normal horses                             | Chronically stressed horses             |
| ---------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| Total cortisol               | Normal range; clear AM/PM rhythm          | Variable, rhythm obliterated            |
| Free cortisol                | Low (~10% of total; ~90% bound to CBG)    | Elevated (consistently)                 |
| CBG binding capacity         | Normal                                    | Reduced (after 3–4 days chronic stress) |
| ACTH response to 2 µg CRH IV | Clear rise                                | Blunted or absent                       |
| Circadian cortisol rhythm    | Present: peak 0600–0900, trough 1800–2100 | Obliterated even by mild novelty stress |
| Body weight                  | Stable                                    | Decreasing                              |
| Upper respiratory infections | Rare                                      | Increased                               |

## Discussion & interpretation

The authors propose that the discrepancy in prior overtraining studies reflects universal reliance on total cortisol, which is physiologically masked: when CBG is down-regulated by chronic stress, free cortisol rises even if total cortisol stays constant or falls, because the free fraction redistributes across a lower CBG pool. The three hallmarks (rhythm loss, CBG/free cortisol shift, blunted CRH response) provide a coherent diagnostic framework. The blunted CRH-ACTH response likely results from elevated free cortisol negative feedback at the pituitary plus potential CRH receptor down-regulation from prolonged CRH hypersecretion. The behavioral consequences of elevated CRH — increased arousal, anxiety, appetite suppression — align with the irritability, weight loss, and apathy reported in overtrained horses. The "morning-after" cortisol suppression is attributed to compensatory negative feedback: the HPA axis strives to maintain a mean daily cortisol level, so a raceday surge is followed by an offsetting underactivity period lasting ~1–2 days.

## Limitations

- Social instability model (new horse meeting dominant mares) may not perfectly replicate overtraining stress; mechanisms could differ.
- n sizes in the racing cohort studies are small (n = 16–17 racehorses); no formal power analysis.
- The definitive free cortisol and CBG data are in Alexander & Irvine (1998) J. Endocrinol. (a companion paper), not reproduced quantitatively here beyond the CBG pre/post-race values; the full chronic stress CBG and free cortisol numerical data require the companion paper.
- Non-surgical pituitary venous blood collection method is unique to these authors; CRH challenge in peripheral blood may give lower signal than pituitary effluent.
- Exclusively New Zealand Standardbred population for the anticipation study; may not replicate in JRA Thoroughbred stabling environments.

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `total_cortisol` — standard RIA cortisol (ng/ml) — training blood sample records — **DO NOT USE as standalone feature**; individual variation renders it uninformative for chronic stress detection; use only as part of free/total ratio if CBG is also available
- `cbg_binding_capacity` — equilibrium saturation binding (nmol/L) — specialised assay not routinely in JRA databases — strong chronic-stress indicator; reference value ~186–187 nmol/L at race; **data availability: very limited**
- `free_cortisol_fraction` — free / total cortisol ratio — derivable if both CBG and total cortisol known — reliable chronic stress indicator; elevated = "not coping" state
- `post_race_day_flag` — binary, 1 if horse was sampled within 24 h of previous race — race records (date comparison) — use to suppress cortisol-based features; morning-after cortisol suppression is normal, not pathological
- `days_since_last_race` — integer — race records — interacts with cortisol interpretation; suppression window ~1–3 days post-race
- `trainer_report_stress` — categorical (if available in comments/notes) — trainer/vet notes — proxy for the clinical overtraining signs (weight loss, infection, irritability); if standardised trainer observation codes exist in JRA records these could operationalise the "not coping" state
- `race_day_pre_cortisol` — cortisol measured hours before race at track — not standard in databases — heavily confounded by transport/novelty anticipation; **DO NOT USE without controlling for racetrack environment vs. stable environment**
- **Interaction:** `free_cortisol` × `days_since_last_race` — chronic stress signal is elevated free cortisol persistent beyond the normal 1–3-day post-race suppression window
