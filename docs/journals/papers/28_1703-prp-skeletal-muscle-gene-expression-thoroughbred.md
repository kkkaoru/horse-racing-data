## Abstract (verbatim)

> Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) therapy is promising for treating skeletal muscle injuries in human athletes by promoting muscle regeneration. It might also be useful for treating muscle injuries in equine athletes. In the present study, muscle regeneration induced by injection of PRP into intact muscle of Thoroughbred was investigated. Autologous PRP and saline were injected twice into intact left and right gluteus medius muscles of seven clinically healthy Thoroughbreds. Muscle samples were collected from the injection sites by needle biopsy at 2 and 7 days after PRP injection. Immunohistochemical staining to identify the types of myosin heavy chains (MHCs) and satellite cells was performed to compare morphological changes among intact (pre-injection), saline-, and PRP-injected muscles. The expression of marker genes related to muscle regeneration (MHC-I, MHC-II, and embryonic MHC [MHC-e]), satellite cell activity (CK, Pax7, MyoD, and myogenin), and proinflammatory and promyogenic cytokines (IL-6, IGF-1, and HGF) was analyzed and compared between saline- and PRP-injected muscles. There were no obvious morphological differences among the three treatments. There were no significant differences in gene expression associated with satellite cell activity between saline and PRP injection at 7 days after injection. MHC genes showed significantly higher expression levels with PRP than with saline, including MHC-e at 2 days and MHC-I at 7 days after injection. It is suggested that injection of PRP into intact skeletal muscle does not induce specific morphological changes, but upregulate the expression of genes related to muscle regeneration.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **C (exercise physiology/fitness)** and indirectly **D (genetics/pedigree)** via myosin heavy chain isoform genetics. The paper characterises **baseline MHC isoform composition** of Thoroughbred gluteus medius muscle and shows that PRP modulates MHC gene expression in intact muscle.

The MHC isoform ratios established here provide concrete baselines for JRA Thoroughbreds: Type I (slow-twitch, oxidative): 11.7–12.8%; Type IIa (fast-twitch oxidative): 44.6–49.0%; Type IIx (fast-twitch glycolytic): 39.5–43.5% at baseline. These ratios directly encode aerobic vs. anaerobic capacity: horses with higher MHC-I proportions have better stamina (favour longer distances), while higher MHC-IIx proportions favour sprint/anaerobic capacity (shorter distances). If genomic proxies for MHC composition are available (via MSTN-adjacent SNPs, especially BIEC2_417274 identified in Tozaki et al. 28_1726 as linked to muscle fiber type ratios), they could serve as distance-aptitude features.

The PRP intervention itself is not directly performance-predictive, but the gene expression biomarkers identified (MHC-e upregulation at 2 days, MHC-I upregulation at 7 days post-PRP) establish that MHC genes can be modulated post-treatment. If JRA horses receive PRP therapy (e.g., for muscle injury), subsequent races may show altered muscle composition — a potential confound.

The null result for satellite cell activation markers (Pax7, MyoD, myogenin, CK) with PRP in intact muscle confirms that PRP's muscle regeneration effect is primarily through MHC gene expression rather than inducing new fibre formation, which limits performance uplift from PRP treatment in healthy horses.

## Background & objective

PRP therapy is widely used in human and equine medicine for tendon and ligament injuries. PRP contains concentrated platelets (5–12× whole blood concentration); activated platelets release growth factors including TGF-β and PDGF from α-granules, promoting tissue regeneration. The hypothesis: PRP injection into intact Thoroughbred skeletal muscle would upregulate muscle regeneration genes (MHC-e, MHC-I) and activate satellite cell proliferation (Pax7+, MyoD+), as observed in rodent injury models.

The research gap: no published studies on PRP effects in equine skeletal muscle (as opposed to tendon/ligament).

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 7 clinically healthy Thoroughbreds (6 males, 1 female; 6–12 years of age; BW 435–493 kg). Approved by JRA Animal Welfare and Ethics Committee (authorization 2012-9).

**PRP preparation:** 100 mL whole blood from jugular vein (10% sodium citrate anticoagulant, ACD-A injection, Terumo BCT). Centrifuged twice: 400×g × 7 min at 4°C (remove RBCs), then 2,000×g × 7 min at 4°C (concentrate platelets). Final PRP volume: 10 × 1 mL aliquots. Stored at −30°C; single freeze-thaw cycle to activate platelets before injection.

**PRP characterisation:** Platelet concentration in whole blood: 5.4–13.3 × 10⁴/µL (mean 9.8 ± 2.8 × 10⁴/µL). Concentration in PRP: 62.6–85.0 × 10⁴/µL (mean 72.3 ± 8.2 × 10⁴/µL). Concentration factor: 5.4–12.1× (mean 7.9 ± 2.3×). Freeze-thawed PRP: PDGF-BB = 5.0 ± 1.6 ng/mL; TGF-β1 = 15.4 ± 6.6 ng/mL. PDGF-BB in PRP = 5.0 ± 0.7× serum; TGF-β1 in PRP = 3.7 ± 1.7× serum. No correlation among platelet count, PDGF-BB, and TGF-β1 concentrations.

**Injection protocol:** All procedures under medetomidine sedation (5 µg/kg IV). Right gluteus medius: 3 patches (2 × 2 cm, 5 cm apart) clipped and sterilised. Pre-injection needle biopsy from patch 1 (PRE sample). Then 4 mL PRP injected into patch 2 and 4 mL saline into patch 3 (or vice versa), 2–3 cm deep. Left side: same procedure 5 days later (biopsy 2 days post-injection = group 2D). Day 7 (right side): biopsy from both right-side sites (group 7D). Samples frozen in liquid N₂, stored at −80°C. Prophylactic antibiotic (MYCILLIN) given IM.

**Immunohistochemistry:** Serial 8-µm cryostat sections (−20°C) stained with MY-32 antibody (Sigma; reacts with MHC-IIa and MHC-IIx) and SC-71 antibody (DSHB; reacts with MHC-IIa). Fibre classification: Type I = MY-32(−), SC-71(−); Type IIa = MY-32(+), SC-71(+); Type IIx = MY-32(+), SC-71(−). Satellite cells identified by triple immunofluorescence: anti-Pax7 (mouse, DSHB) + anti-laminin L9393 (rabbit, Sigma) + DAPI. ~25 Type I fibres and ~50 IIa/IIx fibres counted per sample. Myonuclei/fibre, central nuclear ratio, myonuclear domain size, and satellite cells/fibre computed via ImageJ.

**RT-qPCR:** TRIzol RNA extraction; TURBO DNase treatment; ExscriptTM RT Reagent Kit (Takara); SYBR Green PCR in StepOne Real Time System (Applied Biosystems). 40 cycles at 95°C × 30 sec + 58°C × 1 min after initial denaturation 95°C × 10 min. GAPDH internal control. Target genes: MHC-I, MHC-II, MHC-e (embryonic), CK (creatine kinase), Pax7, MyoD, myogenin, IL-6, IGF-1, HGF. Results expressed as RQ (relative quantification) normalised to PRE = 1.00.

**Statistical analysis:** Morphological data by one-way ANOVA. mRNA expression data by Wilcoxon signed-rank test (PRP vs. saline within each timepoint). P < 0.05 significant. Software: Statcel3 (OMS Publishing).

### Gene expression (RT-qPCR, RQ normalised to PRE = 1.00; mean ± SE; n = 7)

**At 2 days post-injection:**

- MHC-e: PRP = **4.09 ± 1.54** vs. saline = 1.20 ± 0.18 (_P < 0.05_) — significant
- MHC-I: PRP = not significant
- MHC-II: not significant
- CK, Pax7, MyoD, myogenin: all low at 2D for both treatments (not significant)
- IL-6: higher trend in PRP but P>0.05 (large SEs)
- IGF-1, HGF: similar for both treatments

**At 7 days post-injection:**

- MHC-I: PRP = **1.78 ± 0.26** vs. saline = 1.33 ± 0.22 (_P < 0.05_) — significant
- HGF: PRP = **2.91 ± 1.03** vs. saline = 1.58 ± 0.29 (_P < 0.05_) — significant
- CK, Pax7, MyoD, myogenin: no significant difference between PRP and saline at 7D
- IL-6: no significant difference (though higher trend in PRP)
- IGF-1: no significant difference

### Summary of significant findings:

1. MHC-e expression 4.09× higher with PRP vs. saline at 2 days (P<0.05)
2. MHC-I expression 1.78/1.33 = 34% higher with PRP vs. saline at 7 days (P<0.05)
3. HGF expression 2.91/1.58 = 84% higher with PRP vs. saline at 7 days (P<0.05)
4. All satellite cell markers (Pax7, MyoD, myogenin, CK): non-significant

## Discussion & interpretation

PRP upregulated MHC-e (embryonic, indicating muscle regeneration initiation) at 2 days and MHC-I (slow-twitch) at 7 days in intact muscle. The authors propose this reflects activated satellite cells beginning to produce MHC variants — satellite cells are essential for MHC regeneration — though the satellite cell activation markers themselves (Pax7, MyoD, myogenin) were not significantly elevated, possibly because the effect size was too small for the 7-horse sample to detect.

The MHC-I upregulation (slow-twitch) at 7 days is biologically interesting: PRP may favour slow-twitch fibre regeneration in intact muscle, which could theoretically improve aerobic/stamina capacity if sustained. However, there was no morphological fibre-type shift, suggesting gene expression changes without structural remodelling at this early timepoint.

HGF (hepatocyte growth factor) was significantly elevated at 7 days with PRP. HGF is stored in platelet α-granules and promotes satellite cell activation; the elevated HGF may indicate ongoing muscle remodelling signalling without overt morphological change.

The null effect on satellite cell proliferation markers in intact muscle is consistent with other literature: PRP's regenerative advantage may be more pronounced in injured tissue where satellite cell activation is already triggered. In intact muscle, baseline turnover is low, limiting PRP's effect on new fibre formation.

## Limitations

- n = 7 horses; low statistical power for detecting small gene expression differences
- Only intact muscle studied; effects in injured muscle (clinically relevant scenario) were not assessed
- Study duration 7 days; longer timepoints might show additional morphological changes
- Single injection of 4 mL; clinical doses and protocols vary
- Mechanism of MHC-e and MHC-I upregulation without satellite cell marker changes remains unclear
- PRP composition (platelet count, growth factor concentration) varied substantially between horses (5–12× concentration factor); no correlation with gene expression outcomes

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `mhc_i_proportion_percent` — % of Type I (slow-twitch) fibres in gluteus medius biopsy — source: JRA ERI muscle biopsy records (research setting, not routine) — expected effect: higher MHC-I → better stamina → better finish in distance races (≥2000 m) — baseline value ~12%; not routinely available for prediction
- `mhc_iix_proportion_percent` — % of Type IIx fibres — source: biopsy — expected effect: higher MHC-IIx → better sprint → favours short-distance races (≤1200 m); baseline ~40% — not routinely available
- `mstn_snp_proxy` — genotype at BIEC2_417274 (chromosome 18 MSTN locus, see 28_1726) — source: JBBA/LRC genotyping — expected effect: MSTN C-allele carriers have altered muscle fiber type ratios (more sprint-type); linked to optimal race distance — available if SNP data provided
- `prp_treatment_flag` — binary: horse received PRP muscle injection within last N days — source: JRA veterinary records — expected effect: uncertain (gene expression changes but no morphological change at 7 days); potential mild positive effect on aerobic capacity if MHC-I upregulation persists — use as confounder control flag rather than performance predictor
- `hgf_serum` — hepatocyte growth factor in blood — source: blood panel (research only) — expected effect: elevated HGF may indicate ongoing muscle remodelling/repair; could flag suboptimal muscle state — not routinely measured
- **CAUTION:** MHC isoform ratios from biopsy are not available in public JRA race records; use MSTN genotype as the genomic proxy
- **CAUTION:** PRP treatment does not reliably enhance satellite cell proliferation in intact muscle; do not over-weight PRP treatment as a performance enhancer in model features
