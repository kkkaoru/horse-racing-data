## Abstract (verbatim)

> The purpose of this study was to compare some kinematic characteristics of horses of two different breeds, Dutch Warmblood (DWB) and Andalusian purebred (PRE), that shows differences in kinematics by simple visual inspection, above all in their forelimbs. Fourteen adult horses (5 DWB and 9 PRE) were recorded at the trot using a conventional videocamera (frame rate 1/25) from a lateral view. Basic stride parameters were calculated (speed, stride length, stride duration), joints angles of the right front limb (Maximum, Minimum, Angular Range of Motion), and the moments of maximal extension (PMAX) and flexion (PMIN), expressed as a percentage of the stride duration in each joint. Student's T tests were used to compare means of analyzed parameters between breeds. Velocities in both groups were similar (4.08 ± 0.25, 3.75 ± 0.51 m/s). Stride length, overtracking length and stride duration were lower in PRE than in DWB. The angular parameters that showed the greatest differences between breeds were those of the elbow, carpus and protraction-retraction angles: the minimum angles and the angular range of motion were greater in the elbow and carpus in PRE than in DWB horses.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

This paper belongs to **Feature family F (conformation/body-size/gait)**. It provides a rigorous kinematic comparison of forelimb movement patterns between two breeds at the trot, demonstrating that breed selection history produces measurable differences in stride length, stance/swing timing, and joint angular ranges of motion at identical speeds. The practical racing relevance is indirect but real.

Stride length is a primary determinant of speed (v = stride length × stride frequency). The DWB horses achieve 307 cm stride length vs. 247 cm for Andalusians — a 24% advantage — at nearly identical velocities. This "productive vs. elevated" movement dichotomy illustrates that biomechanical efficiency (horizontal progression vs. high knee action) is breed-encoded. For Thoroughbreds the same principle applies: horses with longer strides relative to body size tend to have higher maximum speeds. The paper provides normative values and methodology for gait-derived features, even though the measured breeds are not racehorses.

The finding that PRE horses have **greater elbow and carpal joint flexion** (elevated gait) corresponds to wasted vertical energy, while DWB's greater extension at take-off (higher joint angles) corresponds to efficient horizontal propulsion. In the Thoroughbred racing context, conformation traits visible in pre-race gait analysis could encode similar efficiency differences. Feature derivation from video gait analysis or wearable accelerometers would follow this paper's framework.

## Background & objective

Andalusian (PRE) horses have been selectively bred almost exclusively for elevated, "beautiful" gaits — primarily raised forelimb action — mainly used as saddle and farm animals. Dutch Warmblood (DWB) horses were refined from Gelderland and Groningen stock and subsequently crossed with Thoroughbreds; they are selected for functional characteristics (dressage, jumping). The two breeds are thus phenotypically at opposite ends of the "elevated vs. horizontal" spectrum. No prior paper had compared their gait kinematics using the same methodology. Objective: quantify forelimb kinematic differences between PRE and DWB at the trot.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 14 adult horses — 9 PRE from the VII Military Stud Farm, Córdoba, Spain; 5 DWB from the stables of the Veterinary Faculty, University of Utrecht, Netherlands. None were in a specific training programme.

**Recording:** Fixed camcorder (Hi8 for PRE; SVHS for DWB), 25 frames/s, placed 11 m perpendicular to track, zoom for 7–8 m wide field, shutter speed 1:250–1:10000. Time code reader-generator FOR-A TGR 2000 encoded 1/50 s time codes. Horses trotted at hand. Six trials per horse randomly selected. Markers placed on track for distance calibration; self-adhesive markers on anatomical landmarks: withers, tuberosity of scapula, caudal portion of greater tuberculum (humerus), lateral collateral ligament of elbow, lateral styloid process (radius), base of metacarpal III, lateral collateral ligament of fetlock, coronet of hoof.

**Digitisation:** Coordinates (x, y) of markers digitised frame-by-frame using SMVD v.1 software (custom spreadsheet, Microsoft Excel 4.0, Windows 3.1) with Movie Machine Pro digitiser card + MJPG compression card.

**Parameters measured:**

- Velocity (frames per known distance)
- Stride length (mean distance between successive contacts of four limbs)
- Overtracking length (distance between ipsilateral forelimb and hindlimb footfalls; positive = hind footfall ahead of front)
- Stride duration (time between successive forelimb ground contacts)
- Stance % and swing % of stride duration (right forelimb)
- Global swing duration %: time when no limb contacts ground
- Joint angles: scapula inclination, shoulder, elbow, carpus, fetlock, retraction-protraction angle — maximum, minimum, ARM (angular range of motion), timing of PMAX and PMIN (% of stride), angles at landing, take-off, max elevation

**Statistics:** Student's T test (SAS, 1982) after confirming no significant velocity difference between groups (p > 0.05).

**Summary of key breed differences:**

- PRE shows greater elbow flexion (minimum 79.3° vs. 96.6°, p < 0.001) and greater elbow ARM (71.3° vs. 57.8°, p < 0.01) — characteristic elevated gait
- PRE has greater carpal minimum angle and ARM — deeper knee flexion
- DWB shows higher take-off angles at elbow, carpus, and fetlock — more extension before lift-off
- Global swing duration CV: DWB 6.8% vs. PRE 29.2% — greater diagonal dissociation and interlimb coordination variability in PRE

## Discussion & interpretation

**Energetic interpretation:** When horses are free to choose speed, they prefer the speed minimising transport cost (Hoyt and Taylor 1981). At similar velocities, DWB horses achieve 60 cm more stride length per stride — this translates to the same speed with fewer (lower-frequency) strides. PRE horses use energy lifting the forelimbs ("elevated gait"), while DWB horses channel energy into horizontal progression. This is mechanistically equivalent to the "efficient galloper" vs. "high-knee action" distinction sometimes observed among Thoroughbred populations.

**Take-off angles:** Higher joint angles at take-off in DWB (except shoulder) indicate more full extension before the limb is lifted. This propulsive extension pattern is associated with efficient forward thrust. The finding that PRE joints are less extended at take-off but more flexed during swing aligns with the elevated-gait phenotype.

**Diagnostic utility for racehorse assessment:** The methodology (video + anatomical marker digitisation) is directly applicable to pre-race gait analysis. Stride length, stance %, and global swing % can be derived from modern inertial sensor rigs worn during trackwork. The key mechanistic ratio — horizontal progression per unit energy — would be captured by stride length per unit body weight or per stride frequency.

**Diagonal dissociation:** The high CV for global swing in PRE (29.2% vs. 6.8% in DWB) reflects greater temporal dissociation of diagonal fore–hind pair contact in Andalusians, a known breed characteristic. DWB have better interlimb coordination for forward movement; PRE have a more "dancy" and variable timing.

## Limitations

- Small sample: n = 5 DWB and n = 9 PRE; statistical power is limited for detecting moderate differences
- Trot at hand, not gallop; kinematic patterns at gallop (the relevant racing gait) may differ
- Different camera systems used (Hi8 vs. SVHS) for the two breeds; potential calibration differences
- Neither breed is a Thoroughbred; direct numeric transfer to racehorse gait analysis requires caution
- No performance outcome measured — the paper is purely descriptive biomechanics
- No body weight data reported, preventing normalisation of stride length
- 25 fps is insufficient to quantify diagonal dissociation precisely (250 fps required per Holmström et al. 1994)

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `stride_length_cm` — mean distance (cm) covered per stride cycle; available from modern GPS/accelerometer trackwork data; longer stride → faster for given frequency; expected effect: negative on finishing rank (longer = better); **derive from trackwork recordings if available**
- `stance_pct_rf` — right forelimb stance phase as % of stride; shorter stance → more time in air → typically faster; DWB 37.2% vs. PRE 42.5%; expected effect: negative on finishing rank (lower stance% = better)
- `overtracking_cm` — distance hind footfall overshoots front footfall (positive = more forward); DWB −3.7 cm vs. PRE −21.7 cm; proxy for hindlimb engagement and stride productivity; **positive overtracking expected in fit Thoroughbreds**
- `global_swing_pct` — % of stride when no limb contacts ground; higher = longer airborne phase; DWB 35.0% vs. PRE 20.2%; aerially more efficient movers should have higher values
- `breed_code` — categorical; Thoroughbred is distinct from both studied breeds but biomechanically closer to DWB (functional selection); encode breed for any mixed-breed databases
- `elbow_min_angle_deg` — minimum elbow flexion during swing; lower angle = more elevated gait = less efficient horizontal progression; **caution**: requires motion capture equipment, not in standard race records
- **Do NOT use** any of the angular parameters directly in a finishing-position model based on race records alone — they require kinematic measurement infrastructure not available in standard JRA/NAR data
