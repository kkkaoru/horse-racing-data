## Abstract (verbatim)

> Problems associated with the proximal metacarpal region, such as an osseous injury associated with tearing of Sharpey's fibers or an avulsion fracture of the origin of the suspensory ligament (OISL), are important causes of lameness in racehorses. In the present study, four Thoroughbred racehorses (age range, 2–4 years) were diagnosed as having forelimb OISL and assessed over time by using standing magnetic resonance imaging (sMRI). At the first sMRI examination, all horses had 3 characteristic findings, including low signal intensity within the trabecular bone of the third metacarpus on T1-weighted images, intermediate-to-high signal intensity surrounded by a hypointense rim on T2*-weighted images, and high signal intensity on fat-suppressed images. Following the sMRI examination, all horses received 50 mg of tiludronic acid by intravenous regional limb perfusion once weekly for 3 weeks. Attenuation of the high signal intensity on T2*-weighted and fat-suppressed images was observed on follow-up sMRI in 3 horses. Following rest and rehabilitation, these 3 horses successfully returned to racing. In contrast, the other horse that did not show attenuation of the high signal intensity failed to return to racing. To our knowledge, this is the first report of OISL in Thoroughbred racehorses assessed over time by sMRI under tiludronic acid treatment. Our findings support the use of sMRI for examining lameness originating from the proximal metacarpal region to refine the timing of returning to exercise based on follow-up examinations during the recuperation period.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **A (injury/soundness)**. Osseous injury at the origin of the suspensory ligament (OISL) is a specific, high-impact injury type at the proximal metacarpal region — a common site of performance-limiting lameness in Thoroughbred racehorses. The direct relevance to finishing-position prediction is through **injury history and recovery prognosis**: 3 of 4 horses with sMRI signal attenuation after tiludronate treatment successfully returned to racing (2 ran multiple subsequent races without reinjury), while the 1 horse without signal attenuation failed to return.

This establishes a **binary prognostic signal** — whether the injury has resolved (sMRI attenuation) vs. not — that predicts return-to-racing outcome. In the prediction model context: a horse with a prior OISL diagnosis and confirmed sMRI recovery is expected to perform at near-baseline; a horse with persistent signal (no attenuation) represents a retirement/scratch risk. Injury type, severity grade, and time-since-injury are the engineerable features.

The paper also establishes that sMRI is superior to radiography and ultrasonography for monitoring OISL healing: radiographic radiolucency and ultrasonographic surface irregularities persisted even after sMRI confirmed healing and clinical return to exercise. This means injury records based on radiography alone may underestimate actual recovery status.

## Background & objective

The proximal metacarpal region is a common lameness site in Thoroughbreds (Dyson 1991). OISL involves tearing of Sharpey's fibres or avulsion fracture of the suspensory ligament origin; hyperextension of the carpus is the proposed etiology. Diagnosis typically uses radiography (bone changes detectable in chronic/severe cases) and ultrasonography (limited by acoustic accessibility and operator variability at this site). Standing MRI (sMRI) under sedation enables superior visualisation of osseous and soft-tissue pathology with higher sensitivity than conventional imaging. Tiludronic acid (TA), a non-nitrogenous bisphosphonate, inhibits osteoclast-mediated bone resorption and suppresses inflammation; it is increasingly used in equine musculoskeletal disorders but its efficacy for OISL was previously unevaluated with serial sMRI follow-up.

## Materials & methods

**Case selection:** All horses presenting to the Ritto Training Center Racehorse Hospital, JRA, between 2015 and 2016 with forelimb lameness diagnosed as OISL by sMRI. Four Thoroughbreds (age range 2–4 years) included.

**Radiography:** Fujifilm FCR Speedia CS digital system. Projections: standing dorsopalmar, dorsolateral palmaromedial 45° oblique, dorsomedial palmarolateral 45° oblique.

**Ultrasonography:** Hitachi HI VISION Avius with 6–14 MHz linear probe. Palmar metacarpal region scanning.

**Standing MRI system:** 0.27-Tesla equine-dedicated Equine Limb Scanner (Hallmarq Veterinary Imaging, Guildford, UK). Sequences acquired in transverse and sagittal planes:

- T1-weighted GRE: TE 8 msec, TR 52 msec, flip angle 50°
- T2\*-weighted GRE: TE 13 msec, TR 68 msec, flip angle 28°
- STIR FSE (fat-suppressed): TE 22 msec, TR 2,536 msec, inversion time 120 msec

**Sedation:** Medetomidine (Domitor, Zenoaq), initial 5 µg/kg IV, then 0.2 µg/kg periodic top-ups.

**sMRI grading of osseous lesions:**

- Profound: intermediate-to-high signal surrounded by hypointense rim on T2\*; markedly high signal on fat-suppressed images
- Severe: intermediate-to-high signal surrounded by hypointense rim on T2\*; high signal on fat-suppressed images
- Moderate: low signal on T1; high signal on fat-suppressed images
- Mild: low signal on T1 only

**Tiludronate treatment:** 50 mg tiludronic acid (Tildren, CEVA) diluted in 40 mL saline; IV regional limb perfusion via 22-gauge butterfly catheter in cephalic vein; tourniquet applied and removed after 30 min. Administered once weekly × 3 consecutive weeks. Protocol approved by JRA Committee of Safe and Ethical Use of Medication; owner consent obtained.

**MRI review:** By 3 experienced clinicians (F.M., M.N., T.K.) using OsiriX DICOM viewer v3.9.2.

**Statistical analysis:** Descriptive case series; no formal statistical analysis due to n = 4.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

### Case 1 — 2-year-old Thoroughbred colt, right forelimb

**Presentation:** Acute-onset right foreleg lameness after exercise; swelling, heat, pain in palmar aspect of proximal metacarpus.

**Day 2 (radiography):** Linear radiolucent lesions at palmaromedial surface of proximal MC3 → incomplete/avulsion fracture classification.

**Day 3 (ultrasonography):** Hyperechogenic fragment on palmar cortex of MC3.

**Day 3 (sMRI):** T1 = low signal intensity in trabecular bone; T2\* = intermediate-to-high signal surrounded by hypointense rim; fat-suppressed = markedly high signal. Grade: **Profound**.

**TA treatment:** Days 3, 10, 17 (×3 sessions).

**Day 24 (2nd sMRI):** Decreased area of high signal on T2\* and fat-suppressed. Grade: **Severe**. No longer lame at walk; swelling/heat/pain reduced. Radiography/US: unchanged.

**Day 53 (3rd sMRI):** Further attenuation on T2\*; significant decrease in fat-suppressed signal. Grade: **Moderate**. Radiography still showed linear radiolucency; US showed surface irregularity. Swelling/heat resolved; slight pain on palpation.

**Day 87 (4th sMRI):** Confirmed attenuation on fat-suppressed. Grade: **Mild**. Still radiolucency on X-ray and surface irregularity on US. No lameness at trot; no palpation abnormalities.

**Rehabilitation:** Box rest 24 days → walking 30 min/day (day 24–53) → walking 60 min/day → ridden exercise from day 87 → full training (fast canter) on **day 184** → **returned to racing on day 242**. Ran 3 more races without reinjury.

### Case 2 — 4-year-old Thoroughbred colt, right forelimb

**Presentation:** Right foreleg lameness after exercise; swelling, marked pain.

**Day 1 (radiography):** Focal semicircular area of increased radiolucency at proximal MC3.

**Day 1 (sMRI):** T1 = low signal; T2\* = intermediate-to-high with hypointense rim; fat-suppressed = markedly high. Grade: **Profound**.

**TA treatment:** Days 2, 9, 16.

**Day 23 (2nd sMRI):** Decreased signal on T2\* and fat-suppressed. Grade: **Severe**. No lameness at walk; slight swelling/pain. Radiography unchanged.

**Day 85 (3rd sMRI):** Further attenuation on T2\*; significant decrease on fat-suppressed. Grade: **Moderate**. Lameness and pain resolved; mild increased radiolucency on X-ray.

**Rehabilitation:** Box rest 23 days → walking → ridden exercise → full training on **day 191** → **returned to racing on day 228**. Won 7th race after injury; no lameness for >1 year.

### Case 3 — 3-year-old Thoroughbred colt, left forelimb (post-race acute onset)

**Presentation:** Acute left foreleg lameness after turf race; swelling over palmar aspect of proximal metacarpus.

**Day 5 (radiography):** No abnormalities detected.

**Day 6 (sMRI after perineural anaesthesia confirmed proximal metacarpal origin):** T1 = focal area of low signal intensity; T2\* = focal intermediate-to-high surrounded by hypointense rim; fat-suppressed = diffusely high. Grade: **Severe**.

**TA treatment:** Days 6, 13, 20.

**Day 20 (2nd sMRI):** Attenuation of T2\* signal; high signal remained on fat-suppressed. Grade: **Moderate**. Swelling substantially reduced.

**Rehabilitation:** Box rest until day 20 → walking → ridden exercise day 90 → full training day 181 → first race day 280. Suffered left carpal fracture on day 280 → box rest → eventual return to racing on **day 441**. Owner retired colt after 1 race (not related to original OISL injury).

### Case 4 — 3-year-old Thoroughbred filly, right forelimb

**Day 2 (radiography):** Increased radiolucency at proximal MC3.

**Day 2 (sMRI):** T1 = hypointensity; T2\* = intermediate-to-high with hypointense rim; fat-suppressed = marked hyperintensity. Grade: **Profound**.

**TA treatment:** Days 2, 9, 16.

**Day 16 (2nd sMRI):** High signal on T2\* and fat-suppressed **unchanged**. Grade: **Profound** (no improvement). Increased radiolucency on radiography also unchanged.

**Outcome:** Filly retired from racing due to owner's financial reasons immediately after second sMRI. Career end.

## Discussion & interpretation

The three horses with sMRI signal attenuation (T2* and fat-suppressed signal decreasing over serial examinations) consistently returned to racing, while the one horse without attenuation (Case 4) did not. This suggests that sMRI signal attenuation is a reliable positive prognostic marker for OISL recovery. The authors propose that signal changes reflect resolution of bone marrow oedema/hyperemia: high signal on T2* surrounded by hypointense rim is thought to reflect phase effect cancellation (equal fat+water causing signal cancellation at the lesion edge), while high signal on fat-suppressed indicates fluid/oedema in trabecular bone. As oedema resolves, signals attenuate.

Radiography and ultrasonography lagged behind sMRI in detecting healing: radiolucencies and surface irregularities persisted on X-ray and US even after sMRI confirmed resolution and horses returned to full work. This confirms sMRI as the superior modality for OISL monitoring.

The mean resting period before return to racing was ~228–242 days for the two best-outcome cases, consistent with the published 3–6 month rehabilitation expectation for OISL. The rehabilitation protocol (box rest → walking → ridden exercise → full training) was guided by sMRI signal changes rather than symptom-driven progression, suggesting that serial imaging provides objective milestones.

## Limitations

- Case series of only 4 horses; no control group (no TA-untreated cases)
- Case 4 retired for financial reasons, not necessarily because TA would have been ineffective — cannot conclude TA failure; could be that the horse needed more time
- Unclear whether healing was accelerated by TA vs. natural resolution alone (no placebo arm)
- Short observation period; the sMRI follow-up was limited to 2–4 sessions per case
- All horses were at JRA Ritto Training Center; population limited to JRA Thoroughbreds in training (ages 2–4)
- OISL involving SL core lesions excluded (none detected in these cases); results may not apply to OISL with concurrent SL desmopathy

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `oisl_diagnosis_flag` — binary: horse has ever been diagnosed with OISL — source: JRA veterinary/injury records — expected effect: negative on performance in first ~6 months post-diagnosis; risk of reinjury elevated — encode as separate from general lameness history
- `days_since_oisl` — days elapsed since OISL diagnosis — source: injury records — expected effect: negative early post-diagnosis; recovers after ~200+ days if sMRI confirmed attenuation — use exponential decay encoding; 200-day threshold as approximate benchmark
- `oisl_smri_resolved` — binary: sMRI confirmed signal attenuation (grade ≤ Moderate after initial Severe/Profound) — source: JRA veterinary records — expected effect: strong positive for return-to-racing prognosis; use as recovery confirmation flag when available
- `proximal_metacarpal_lameness_history` — binary or count: prior forelimb proximal metacarpal lameness episodes — source: JRA race entry/health records — expected effect: negative history suggests vulnerability; count encodes chronicity — distinguish from other lameness sites
- `injury_grade_smri_initial` — ordinal (1=Mild, 2=Moderate, 3=Severe, 4=Profound) at initial sMRI — source: clinical records — expected effect: higher grade → longer recovery → higher probability of permanent retirement — not routinely available but highly informative
- `forelimb_lameness_flag` — binary: any forelimb lameness in last N races — source: JRA scratch records, veterinary reports — expected effect: negative; even mild lameness → degraded performance → worse finishing position — available as proxy from JRA race entry health checks
- `tiludronate_treatment_flag` — binary: horse received tiludronate treatment within last N days — source: JRA medication records — expected effect: neutral performance impact directly; flags existence of a musculoskeletal problem under treatment — use as confounder/risk flag
- **CAUTION:** radiographic and ultrasonographic findings alone are NOT reliable indicators of OISL healing — they lag sMRI by weeks; injury history from these modalities underestimates recovery status; do not conflate radiographic resolution with functional recovery
- **CAUTION:** OISL should be encoded separately from soft-tissue suspensory ligament desmitis (insertional desmopathy) — these are mechanistically and prognostically different injuries even though they share the same anatomical region
