## Abstract (verbatim)

> A study was made of the electrocardiograms of 16 Andalusian horses, with two controls, before and after three months' training, in order to assess the electrocardiographic changes taking place as a result of a defined training period. Five precordial leads were used, placing the electrodes in different locations around the thoracic cavity. The P wave and the QRS complex have shown some morphological modifications induced by training. Moreover, daily exercise gave rise to substantial variations in quantitative electrocardiographic parameters. There was a decrease in the duration of P, R and T waves, and in the voltage of P and R waves. A greater duration was recorded for the segments PQ, ST and TP, and for the intervals RR, PQ and TQ. The duration of intervals QRS and QT, however, was shorter. Finally, a greater number of changes related to training were observed by using the precordial leads than the semiorthogonal system.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **C (exercise-physiology/fitness)**, specifically cardiac adaptation to training. The resting heart rate (HR) dropped from ~43 beats/min to ~34 beats/min after 3 months of training (a ~21% reduction), and the RR interval correspondingly increased from ~1.43–1.46 s to ~1.78–1.84 s across all five precordial leads — these changes are among the most consistent and statistically significant findings in the study (Wilcoxon Z = 3.4–5.97, P<0.001 to P<0.001). This training-induced bradycardia is the cardiac conditioning hallmark, and resting HR is a validated fitness proxy.

The TP segment and TQ interval r² correlations with heart rate (lead 1D post-training: r²=0.772 for RR, r²=0.882 for TQ, r²=0.811 for TQ in Vj₀ pre-training: r²=0.863) are exceptionally high, confirming that the bulk of resting ECG interval variation is explained by HR. This means that if resting HR is available as a pre-race data point (e.g. from wearable monitors increasingly used in JRA stabling), it carries essentially the same information as the full ECG profile.

Shorter QRS (faster ventricular conduction, consistent with more developed Purkinje system after training) and shorter QT post-training indicate improved ventricular electrical efficiency — these are distinguishing markers of a well-conditioned vs. undertrained horse when the two could otherwise have similar resting HRs.

The study uses **Andalusian** horses (Spanish breed, not Thoroughbreds), so absolute parameter values are not directly transferable to JRA racehorses. However, the direction and relative magnitude of training-induced changes are expected to be consistent across breeds, since the underlying cardiac electrophysiology is shared.

## Background & objective

Standard limb lead ECG provides poor precision in horses because the heart lies perpendicular to the limb-to-body axis, creating near-right-angle misalignment between the QRS/T vectors and lead axes. Precordial (unipolar thoracic) leads have been shown to detect more changes than limb leads (Illera et al. 1987). A previous study by this group used semiorthogonal leads (I, aVF, Vj₀) to document some training-induced ECG changes in the same Andalusian horses (Muñoz et al. 1995, _J. Eq. Vet. Sci._ 15: 72–79). The present study adds five precordial leads to determine whether they detect additional training effects and provides a quantitative comparison.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 16 Andalusian horses (4 years old at start; Military Stud Center of Selection and Training, Jerez de la Frontera, Cádiz, Spain). Two controls.

**Training protocol:** 3 months, 60–80 minutes/day. Composition:

- Walking: 20–30 min
- Trotting: 20–25 min
- Galloping: 12–18 min
- Total distance: 5,300–6,400 m per session

**ECG recording:** Cardiostat-T portable electrocardiograph; 25 mm/s chart speed; 1 mm/mV sensitivity. Alligator clips on skin folds. 5-minute calm wait per animal to avoid emotional tachycardia; no tranquillisers. Duration: 30–45 seconds per recording.

**Five precordial leads:**

- 1D: right hemithorax, ~5 cm dorsal to ventral midline, ~5 cm caudal to olecranon
- 3D: cranial tip of right articulatio humeri
- 1I: left hemithorax, ~5 cm dorsal to ventral midline, ~5 cm caudal to olecranon
- 3I: cranial tip of left articulatio humeri
- Vj₀: spinal process of seventh thoracic vertebra

**Parameters measured per lead:** Heart rate; duration and voltage of P, Q, R, S, T waves; duration of PQ, ST, TP segments; duration of RR, PQ, QRS, QT, TQ intervals.

**Statistics:** Wilcoxon's non-parametric paired test; significance P=0.05. Pearson r² between heart rate and each ECG parameter.

### Morphological changes

- **P wave in lead 3I:** Proportion of simple negative waves increased from 40% pre-training to 92.86% post-training (at expense of bifid negative configuration, which went from 60% to near 0%)
- **P wave in Vj₀:** Polarity changed from simple negative to positive — indicating post-training dorsal displacement of atrial activation front
- **QRS in lead 3D:** Shifted from predominant qR to qr (reduced R voltage)
- **QRS in lead 3I:** Shifted from qR to R (Q wave near-elimination)
- **QRS in Vj₀:** qR configuration increased from 68.75% to 100%

### Correlation between heart rate and ECG parameters (Tables 5 and 6)

Highest r² values for association between HR and ECG parameters:

- Lead 1D post-training: RR r²=0.882, TQ r²=0.810, TP r²=0.772 (all P<0.001)
- Lead Vj₀ pre-training: RR r²=0.863, TQ r²=0.775 (P<0.001); post-training: RR r²=0.629, TQ r²=0.745
- Most wave duration/voltage parameters: low r² with HR (typically <0.3)

## Discussion & interpretation

The reduction in resting HR from ~43 to ~34 beats/min and corresponding increase in RR and TQ intervals are the dominant training effects. These are attributed to increased parasympathetic (acetylcholine-mediated sinoatrial) tone and/or reduced catecholamine sensitivity, consistent with endurance training adaptation in other species. The 3-month protocol (60–80 min/day, moderate intensity) is characteristic of endurance-type cardiovascular conditioning.

The shortened P, R, and T wave durations despite likely cardiac hypertrophy (not the expected finding for category I species where hypertrophy prolongs waves) are interpreted as evidence that in category II species (horse), training induces cardiac hypertrophy primarily through **proliferation of the cardiac conduction system** (Purkinje fibre network expansion), which accelerates electrical propagation and thus shortens activation times. This distinguishes training-induced equine cardiac adaptation from simple volume or pressure hypertrophy.

Reduced R wave voltage (decreased in 3D and 3I, P<0.01 and P<0.001) is attributed to the "Brody effect" — increased blood volume short-circuiting the longitudinal electrical components — rather than reduced cardiac mass.

The precordial lead system detected significantly more training-related changes than the semiorthogonal system used in the companion study, particularly changes in PQ segment, QRS duration, QT interval, and morphological P/T wave changes, confirming the superiority of precordial electrodes for cardiac fitness monitoring in horses.

## Limitations

- Andalusian horses (Spanish breed) not Thoroughbred racehorses; absolute ECG values are not directly transferable
- 4-year-old horses; Thoroughbred racehorses are typically 3–9 years at peak racing
- Training protocol represents endurance-type work (walking/trotting/galloping), not sprint racing preparation
- n=16 (plus 2 controls) — moderate sample; no male/female breakdown of ECG parameter data
- No post-detraining follow-up to confirm reversibility of changes
- Precordial lead positions (exact anatomical landmarks) may not be standardisable across different body sizes in practice

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `resting_HR_pre_race` — resting heart rate in beats/min measured at stable on race morning; primary non-invasive cardiac fitness indicator — expected effect: lower HR → better conditioning → positive for performance; ~34 beats/min is the trained range here vs ~43 untrained — data availability: available with wearable HR monitors (increasingly used at JRA stables)
- `RR_interval_pre_race` — reciprocal proxy for HR (1/HR in seconds); 1.80 s trained vs 1.43 s untrained in this study — data availability: same as resting HR
- `QRS_duration_pre_race` — shorter QRS indicates more developed Purkinje system (better cardiac conditioning); sensitive marker of training status — data availability: requires ECG device
- `QT_interval_pre_race` — shorter QT post-training indicates improved ventricular repolarisation efficiency — data availability: requires ECG device
- **HR variance note:** The TP segment and TQ interval explain 86–88% of HR variance in lead 1D post-training; these intervals are essentially HR proxies. When only HR is available, these do not add independent information.
- **Training interpretation:** A resting HR drop of ~9 beats/min over 3 months (consistent with Foreman et al. 1990, Thoroughbreds) signals effective cardiovascular conditioning — applicable to interpreting JRA/NAR horse fitness profiles over a racing season
