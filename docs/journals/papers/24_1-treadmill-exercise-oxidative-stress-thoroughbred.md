## Abstract (verbatim)

> The present study aimed to clarify changes of oxidative stress and antioxidative functions in treadmill-exercised Thoroughbred horses (n=5, 3 to 7 years old), using recently developed techniques for measurement of serum d-ROMs for oxidative stress, and BAP for antioxidative markers. Also, the effect of nasogastric administration of hydrogen-rich water (HW) or placebo water preceding the treadmill exercise on these parameters was examined. Each horse was subjected to a maximum level of treadmill exercise in which the horses were exhausted at an average speed of 13.2 ± 0.84 m/sec. Blood samples were taken 4 times, immediately before the intake of HW or placebo water at 30 min preceding the treadmill exercise, immediately before the exercise (pre-exercise), immediately after the exercise (post-exercise) and at 30 min following the exercise. In all horses, both d-ROMs and BAP values significantly increased at post-exercise. The increase in d-ROMs tended to be lower in the HW trial, as compared to the placebo trial at pre-exercise. The increase in BAP was considerable at approximately 150 % of the pre-exercise values in both the HW and placebo treatment trials. The BAP/d-ROMs ratio was significantly elevated at post-exercise in both treatment trials, while a significant elevation was also observed at pre-exercise in the HW trial. BAP, d-ROM, and the BAP/d-ROM ratio tended to decline at 30 min after the exercise, except BAP and BAP/d-ROMs in the placebo trial. These results demonstrate that the marked elevation of oxidative stress and antioxidative functions occurred simultaneously in the intensively exercised horses, and suggest a possibility that HW has some antioxidative efficacy.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family: **C — exercise-physiology / fitness**.

This paper establishes serum d-ROMs (reactive oxygen metabolites, unit: U.CARR) and BAP (biological antioxidant potential, unit: µmol/L) as measurable, exercise-responsive biomarkers in Thoroughbreds undergoing maximal treadmill exercise. Both markers surge acutely at peak exercise (d-ROMs P<0.005, BAP P<0.0001) and mostly recover within 30 minutes, characterising the acute oxidative-stress response profile. The BAP/d-ROMs ratio — reflecting the balance between antioxidant capacity and free-radical load — was the most discriminating single index.

For the prediction pipeline, these markers are not directly accessible from race-record data, but they validate three indirect feature families: (1) **days since last race** as a recovery/fatigue proxy (the 30-minute post-exercise BAP overshoot in the placebo group, remaining elevated while HW returned faster, suggests recovery completeness by race day matters); (2) **cumulative training history** as an antioxidant-capacity proxy (prior work, referenced here as Kinnunen et al. 2005, shows trained horses have higher resting ORAC/antioxidant capacity); and (3) **race-to-race interval** as a conditioning signal — horses raced very close together may carry residual oxidative load.

Reference ranges established by this paper: resting d-ROMs 148–179 U.CARR (pre-exercise, both groups); resting BAP 2,556–2,774 µmol/L; post-exhaustive exercise BAP ~3,834–4,254 µmol/L (150–153% of resting). The BAP/d-ROMs ratio is significantly higher in the HW trial overall (P=0.04 by two-way ANOVA), indicating modest but real antioxidative efficacy of hydrogen-rich water supplementation.

## Background & objective

Exercise training increases reactive oxygen species (ROS) through mitochondrial electron leakage, which can induce tissue damage, inflammation, and delayed muscle recovery when antioxidant capacity is exceeded. Thoroughbreds have a VO₂max up to 200 ml O₂/kg/min — over 3× the human value — making them an excellent high-oxidative-stress exercise model. While indirect ROS markers (lipid peroxidation, CK, glutathione) have been studied in horses, convenient automated assays for total ROS burden (d-ROMs) and total antioxidant potential (BAP) had not been validated in Thoroughbreds under controlled treadmill conditions. The secondary objective was to evaluate whether hydrogen-rich water (HW, molecular H₂ >1 ppm), which had shown antioxidant effects in rodents and human athletes, reduces exercise-induced oxidative stress in horses.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** n=5 Thoroughbred horses (2 mares, 3 geldings), aged 4–7 years, body weight 493.8 ± 26.6 kg (mean ± SD), belonging to the Equine Research Institute, JRA. Horses had been pasture-grazed for 2 months and specially trained for treadmill exercise prior to the study.

**Design:** Crossover, placebo-controlled, one-week washout. Trial 1 (placebo first), Trial 2 (HW). Each horse received both treatments in the same order.

**Hydrogen-rich water preparation:** High Density Hydrogen Water (Melodian Co., Tokyo); pH 6.82; H₂ concentration >1 ppm. Stored in sealed aluminium bags (10 L). 2 L administered by nasogastric catheter 30 min before treadmill exercise. Same volume of normal water for placebo.

**Exercise protocol:** Treadmill at 6% incline; stepwise speed increases every 2 min: 1.6 → 3.6 → 7 → 10 → 12 → 13 → 14 m/sec. Continued until exhaustion (horse unable to maintain position at front of treadmill). Mean exhaustion speed: 13.2 ± 0.84 m/sec.

**Blood sampling:** Jugular vein venipuncture, 10 mL per time point, at 4 time points:

1. 30 min before exercise (immediately before HW or placebo administration)
2. Pre-exercise (immediately before treadmill start)
3. Post-exercise (immediately after exhaustion)
4. 30 min post-exercise

**d-ROMs measurement:** Colorimetric method (FREE carpe diem analyser, WISMERLL, Tokyo); measures hydroperoxides reacting with N,N-diethyl-p-phenylenediamine; expressed in U.CARR (CARR units; 1 U.CARR = 0.08 mg/dL H₂O₂ equivalent). Method validated against ESR.

**BAP measurement:** Same analyser; thiocyanate colour reaction reflecting Fe³⁺→Fe²⁺ reduction potential; expressed in µmol/L; reflects total blood antioxidant capacity.

**Statistics:** Two-way repeated-measures ANOVA (substance × time). Dunnett test for post-exercise and 30 min post vs. pre-exercise. Wilcoxon signed-rank test for the 30-min pre-administration baseline comparison. P<0.05 significant.

**Background measurements:** Blood collected at 10:00 and 13:00 on a rest day from all 5 horses to establish diurnal background levels.

## Results (detailed — all numbers reproduced)

**Pre-administration baseline (30 min before exercise):**

- d-ROMs: 148.3 ± 15.3 U.CARR (placebo) vs. 152.8 ± 9.4 U.CARR (HW) — no significant difference (Wilcoxon, P not significant)
- BAP: 2,555.9 ± 92.5 µmol/L (placebo) vs. 2,774.1 ± 32.7 µmol/L (HW) — no significant difference

**d-ROMs time course:**

- Significant difference among time points in both placebo (P<0.005) and HW (P<0.005) trials
- Post-exercise vs. pre-exercise: significantly elevated in placebo (P<0.005) and HW (P<0.001)
- Pre-exercise (immediately before treadmill): 179 ± 12.1 U.CARR (placebo) vs. 148 ± 14.3 U.CARR (HW) — not significant but HW group was numerically lower
- At 30 min post-exercise: no significant change from pre-exercise in either group (d-ROMs recovery essentially complete in both)

**BAP time course:**

- Significant difference among time points in placebo (P<0.0001) and HW (P<0.0001)
- Post-exercise BAP increased to **150% of pre-exercise** (placebo) and **153% of pre-exercise** (HW)
- At post-exercise, significant increase from pre-exercise: placebo P<0.0001, HW P<0.0001
- At 30 min post-exercise: BAP remained significantly elevated in placebo (P<0.005) but returned toward pre-exercise in HW trial (P<0.0001 only at post-exercise, not significant at 30 min post)

**BAP/d-ROMs ratio:**

- Post-exercise: significantly elevated in both placebo (P<0.001) and HW (P<0.01)
- At 30 min post-exercise: still significantly elevated in placebo (P<0.001) and HW (P<0.05)
- Pre-exercise: significantly elevated in HW trial vs. placebo at the same time point
- **Two-way ANOVA overall: HW trial showed significantly higher BAP/d-ROMs ratio than placebo (P=0.04)**, with no significant interaction between substance and time (P=0.87)

**Summary of HW effect:** BAP/d-ROMs ratio was the only measure showing a significant overall difference between HW and placebo. Individual d-ROMs and BAP values showed trends but not statistically significant differences. HW facilitated faster recovery of BAP/d-ROMs toward baseline by 30 min post-exercise.

## Discussion & interpretation

Both markers (d-ROMs and BAP) rise simultaneously at peak exercise, consistent with prior equine studies reporting increased lipid hydroperoxides and antioxidant changes during high-intensity exercise (Mills et al., 1996; Chiaradia et al., 1998). The concurrent BAP surge is interpreted as a rapid, transient mobilisation of antioxidant defences — likely circulating antioxidants (glutathione, ascorbate, α-tocopherol, minerals) — against the exercise-induced ROS. The BAP recovery to pre-exercise levels within 30 minutes in the HW group, but not in the placebo group, suggests HW attenuates residual oxidative load in the early recovery window, likely through molecular hydrogen selectively quenching hydroxyl radicals (·OH) without affecting superoxide or hydrogen peroxide. The modest effect (significant only for the ratio, not individual markers) in a crossover of only n=5 horses is consistent with HW's mechanistic selectivity (H₂ targets the most harmful ·OH specifically). Prior human data (Aoki et al., 2012) also showed HW reduced blood lactate in athletes exercising at 75% VO₂max but did not affect d-ROMs or BAP — possibly because 75% VO₂max is insufficient to generate the oxidative burst seen at exhaustive intensities. Thoroughbreds at ~13.2 m/sec exhaustion represent a far higher absolute oxidative challenge.

## Limitations

- Very small sample size (n=5); crossover order was not randomised (placebo always first), introducing order effects.
- All horses were experienced treadmill-trained animals at the Equine Research Institute; resting d-ROMs and BAP values may differ from field-trained racehorses.
- Only one post-exercise time point (30 min); the duration of oxidative burden over 24–48 hr recovery is not characterised.
- d-ROMs measures total hydroperoxides, not specific ROS species; cannot distinguish lipid peroxidation from protein or DNA oxidation.
- No performance outcome (race result, time) is linked to these markers; the study is purely physiological.
- The crossover design with a 1-week washout may be insufficient if horses showed chronic cumulative oxidative load from the first exhaustive bout.

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `days_since_last_race` — integer days from previous race to current race — source: race history records — expected effect: negative correlation with residual oxidative load; shorter intervals (<7 days) may impair recovery (BAP/d-ROMs incomplete normalisation) → higher finishing position number (worse result) — high availability from JRA/NAR records
- `race_count_L30d` — number of races in the past 30 days — proxy for cumulative training/oxidative stress load — source: race history — expected: U-shaped effect (too few races = deconditioning; too many = accumulated fatigue)
- `training_era` — indicator for whether the horse is in the early-career conditioning phase (first 3 months of racing) vs. established — proxies for whether antioxidant capacity is fully trained up; resting BAP is higher in well-trained horses per referenced studies — source: debut date relative to race date
- `max_exercise_speed_proxy` — best recorded speed rating in recent races — proxy for VO₂max/conditioning level; higher speed → higher antioxidant capacity (training adaptation) — source: race speed records
- **BAP and d-ROMs themselves are NOT available from public JRA/NAR race records** — these are laboratory blood markers; do not use as direct features unless a proprietary blood data source is available.
