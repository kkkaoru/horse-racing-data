## Abstract (verbatim)

> The present study investigated the effects of caffeine on performance, cardiorespiratory function and plasma hormonal responses of exercising Thoroughbreds on a treadmill. Five Thoroughbreds completed an incremental graded exercise (IG) test and two endurance exercise tests at 80% and 105% of the maximal O2 uptake (VO2max), starting 1 hr after the intramuscular administration of either physiological saline or 2.5 mg/kg caffeine and continuing until exhaustion. The mean exercise time was not significantly affected by caffeine, but there were caffeine-induced inconsistent effects on the individual exercise times in the three exercise tests, which were related to its stimulant effects through the central nervous system (CNS). Caffeine tended to increase blood lactate (LA) and plasma catecholamines (CA) of adrenaline and noradrenaline as well as the cardiorespiratory variables such as O2 uptake, heart rate and packed cell volume during exercise. Caffeine significantly increased VO2max and the maximal levels in LA in the IG test. The maximal levels in plasma CA during the IG test were doubled by caffeine. Caffeine-mediated increases in the plasma CA significantly correlated with those in LA. Plasma ACTH levels reflected less exercise stress under caffeine during the endurance exercise test at 105%VO2max, in which caffeine increased the exercise times of three Thoroughbreds. Caffeine had no effect on the plasma cortisol responses to exercise. The present study suggests that caffeine improves exercise performance through actions on the CNS with the increase in cardiorespiratory function which is probably related to plasma CA responses during exercise.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **C (exercise-physiology / fitness)**. Caffeine is a prohibited doping agent in horse racing; this paper quantifies the physiological ceiling shifts that caffeine produces in Thoroughbreds — raising VO2max by +4.4% and peak blood lactate by +15.0%. For a finishing-position prediction model, this establishes a concrete magnitude benchmark: pharmacological stimulation of this class shifts aerobic ceiling by roughly 4–5% and anaerobic capacity by ~15%. It also clarifies that V200 and VLA4 (commonly used fitness indices) are both **distorted downward by caffeine** because the drug inflates HR and LA at any given speed — these indices should be treated with caution if training records cannot exclude drug exposure.

The finding that caffeine doubled peak plasma catecholamines (Ad ~12.3 → 23.2 ng/ml, NA ~10.0 → 20.4 ng/ml) while the CA–LA correlation structure remained intact reinforces blood lactate as a robust fitness proxy that tracks CA status even when the sympathoadrenal axis is pharmacologically amplified.

The inconsistent individual performance responses to caffeine (some horses improved, some worsened) were attributed to individual "disposition" (dull vs. nervous temperament). The nervous horse (Horse 4) was exhausted earlier on caffeine in all three tests, while the dull horse (Horse 1) improved. This documents temperament-based performance modulation as a real, measurable source of variation — relevant as a moderating feature if trainer behaviour or temperament ratings are available.

Plasma ACTH tended to decrease under caffeine in the 105% VO2max test (3 of 5 horses had longer exercise times), consistent with reduced subjective stress when performance improved. Cortisol was unaffected by caffeine in any test.

## Background & objective

Caffeine is traditionally believed to improve race performance in horses, but experimental equine data were extremely limited (only Fujii et al. 1972 and Sanford & Aitken 1975 in horses). Human literature showed ergogenic effects on endurance exercise (Graham & Spriet 1991) but not on high-intensity short-duration exercise (Tarnopolsky 1994). The authors designed a head-to-head crossover study using three exercise protocols spanning submaximal endurance (80% VO2max), maximal aerobic (IG incremental), and supramaximal (105% VO2max) intensities to determine whether caffeine's ergogenic mechanism is primarily CNS-mediated (behavioural) or cardiorespiratory.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 5 healthy trained Thoroughbreds (3 stallions, 2 mares), ages 4–5 years, weight 439–500 kg; left common carotid artery surgically relocated to subcutaneous position for arterial catheterisation.

**Catheters:** (1) arterial catheter in left common carotid for arterial blood and blood pressure; (2) right jugular venous catheter; (3) Swan-Ganz balloon-tipped pulmonary artery catheter (right jugular) for mixed venous blood sampling and blood temperature; (4) solid-state transducer-tipped catheter (Millar, Houston, TX) in left common carotid for continuous MAP.

**Drug:** Caffeine 2.5 mg/kg IM (caffeine-sodium benzoate injection, Pharmacopoeia of Japan, 10% caffeine); saline control IM; administered 1 hr before each test; randomised crossover; minimum 1-week washout. Dose determined from preliminary behavioural dose-response (2.5 vs. 5.0 mg/kg); 2.5 mg/kg produced moderate behavioural effect; 5.0 mg/kg produced excessive excitement.

**Exercise tests (three, each conducted under both caffeine and saline control):**

1. Incremental graded (IG): 5 min warm-up at 4.0 m/s (0% incline) + 5 min rest; then 10% incline, velocity steps 1.8 → 2.7 → 3.4 → 4.5 → 5.4 → 6.8 → 9.0 → 9.8 → 10.8 → 11.6 m/s (1 min each) until exhaustion.
2. 80% VO2max endurance: constant speed corresponding to 80% VO2max (7.3 ± 0.3 m/s, 10% incline) until exhaustion.
3. 105% VO2max endurance: constant speed at 105% VO2max (9.8 ± 0.2 m/s, 10% incline) until exhaustion.

**Measurements:** VO2 (bias-flow ventilatory system); HR (Polar Bandage-XL); SV [= CO/HR, CO = VO2/(CaO2 − CvO2)]; MAP (Millar catheter); O2 delivery (arterial O2 content × CO / 100); O2 extraction (CaO2 − CvO2)/CaO2 × 100; LA (YSI-1500); PCV (microhaematocrit); arterial and mixed-venous blood gases (ABL 510, Radiometer, 37°C, corrected to body temperature). Blood gases measured over last 15 sec of each time point.

**Performance indices (IG test only):** VO2max (plateau criterion); HRmax (plateau criterion); V200 (treadmill velocity at HR 200 beats/min, from regression); VLA4 (treadmill velocity at LA 4 mmol/L, from regression); LAmax and PCVmax (highest values during exercise/recovery).

**Plasma hormones:** Jugular venous blood at preadministration, 1, 3, 5 min during exercise, at exhaustion, 1, 5, 15, 60 min post-exercise. Ad and NA by HPLC-ECD; ACTH and cortisol by specific RIA.

**Statistics:** Two-way ANOVA (time × treatment) for repeated measures; log-transformation for skewed CA/ACTH; Wilcoxon paired signed-rank test or Student's paired t-test for individual timepoints; P < 0.05 significance.

### CA–LA correlation structure:

Under both saline and caffeine in the IG test, plasma Ad and NA responses significantly correlated with LA responses. The slope/regression structure of the CA–LA relationship was not changed by caffeine (Fig. 2, parallel regression lines for caffeine and saline, shifted upward on the CA axis).

## Discussion & interpretation

Caffeine's mechanism in horses is primarily CNS stimulant rather than cardiorespiratory, because: (1) mean exercise time was not significantly changed but individual responses were inconsistent and related to dispositional temperament; (2) the increase in VO2max (+4.4%) and LAmax (+15%) is consistent with caffeine-enhanced anaerobic metabolism (greater LA production → higher power output), and caffeine-mediated CA elevation → enhanced muscle glycogenolysis (β-adrenergic receptors). The V200 and VLA4 decreases (−9.6% and −9.7%) are artefactual: caffeine raises LA and HR at any given speed, so the submaximal speed required to reach these thresholds shifts left even though maximal performance improved.

The equine adrenal medulla secretes both Ad and NA (unlike humans where NA is predominantly neuronal spillover); caffeine appears to act specifically on the adrenal medulla, doubling both Ad and NA. The CA–LA correlation being maintained under caffeine confirms that the LA–CA coupling reflects metabolic acidosis driving reflex sympathetic outflow — a pharmacologically robust relationship. Plasma caffeine in horses reaches peak levels ~3 hr after IM administration; the 1-hr pre-exercise timing in this study captured a rising, not peak, plasma caffeine level.

## Limitations

- n = 5 horses; very small for detecting consistent performance effects. Individual effects dominate.
- Crossover design with ≥1-week washout; adequate for cardiorespiratory endpoints but caffeine tolerance/habituation effects across 6 tests (3 × 2 drugs) cannot be excluded.
- IM not oral administration; pharmacokinetics differ from traditional oral doping route.
- 2.5 mg/kg dose may not represent field doping concentrations; higher doses (5.0 mg/kg) caused excessive excitement that precluded testing.
- Only 3 exercise modalities tested; race-specific performance (track, field) not directly measured.
- No race performance data (finishing position, race time) linked to treadmill results.

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `v200` — velocity at HR 200 beats/min from regression (m/s) — JRA treadmill test records — standard fitness index; **DO NOT use raw V200 if drug exposure cannot be excluded** (caffeine deflates V200 by ~9.6%)
- `vla4` — velocity at blood lactate 4 mmol/L (m/s) — JRA treadmill test records — standard fitness index; same caffeine caveat (deflated by ~9.7%)
- `vo2max_rel` — relative maximal O2 uptake (ml/kg/min) — JRA treadmill records — most robust aerobic fitness index; caffeine raises by +4.4%; reference: Thoroughbreds ~160–167 in this study
- `la_max` — maximal blood lactate at exhaustion (mmol/L) — JRA treadmill records — anaerobic capacity indicator; caffeine raises by +15% (17.6 → 20.2 mmol/L); individual variation is high (range ~14–23 mmol/L under caffeine)
- `pcv_max` — peak PCV during exercise (%) — JRA treadmill records — splenic reserve proxy; caffeine trend upward (59 → 60% at exhaustion) but not significant
- `individual_temperament` — categorical disposition (dull/calm vs. nervous/excitable) — trainer assessment, not in standard databases — moderates CNS stimulant response; "nervous" horses may be more susceptible to performance degradation under stress/stimulants
- `ad_peak_exercise` — peak plasma adrenaline at exhaustion (ng/ml) — specialised assay — caffeine doubles it (12.3 → 23.2 ng/ml); strong CA–LA coupling means LA is a sufficient proxy; **data availability: very limited in standard databases**
- **Interaction cautions:** V200 and VLA4 are drug-sensitive; if training data predate modern anti-doping testing, treat with additional uncertainty. Absolute CA values and the doubling by caffeine should not be used as race-day features without controlling for drug-free status.
