## Abstract (verbatim)

> In recent years, there have been reports that uric acid has an antioxidative effect, producing allantoin in a non-enzymatic fashion by reacting with uric acid, as the level of active oxygen increases in the body. We analyzed the changes in the serum concentration of uric acid and allantoin in horses before and after exercise by subjecting horses to kinetic loads in exhaustive treadmill exercise. The uric acid value showed an increase until 60 min after the exhaustive exercise was completed, and declined after 120 min almost to the pre-exercise value. Concentration of allantoin increased after starting exercise, reached maximum values at 30–60 min, and then decreased. No significant difference in the allantoin-uric acid ratio was observed. This suggested that the increase in the allantoin level was closely associated with uric acid present before exercise as well as uric acid produced during exercise.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **C (exercise-physiology/fitness)**. This JRA-authored paper documents post-exercise oxidative-stress kinetics in Thoroughbreds using two blood biomarkers: serum uric acid and serum allantoin. Both are products of purine metabolism and reactive-oxygen-species (ROS) scavenging. Their post-exercise time course establishes quantitative reference points for exhaustive effort at the racing intensity level.

For finishing-position prediction, these markers are not routinely collected before races, but they are relevant in two ways. First, if a horse was sampled within the 30–120-minute post-exercise window (e.g., from a morning workout before a race day), elevated uric acid or allantoin could indicate incomplete recovery from recent hard work, serving as a proxy for residual oxidative stress. Second, the negative finding for lipid peroxidation (MDA/LPO) is informative: it establishes that uric acid/allantoin is a **more sensitive early marker** of oxidative response in horses than LPO, narrowing the candidate panel for any blood-based fitness monitoring programme.

The JRA Utsunomiya authorship ensures the experimental horses are of the same strain and training context as Japanese racehorses, making the time-course parameters directly applicable. The Laboratory of Racing Chemistry co-authorship (Kurosawa, Hasegawa) indicates this work was conducted with drug-testing laboratory infrastructure, which is the same institutional framework that produces pre-race blood panels in JRA racing.

## Background & objective

Uric acid is the end-product of purine catabolism in horses; it has recognised antioxidant activity by reacting non-enzymatically with reactive oxygen species to produce allantoin. Strenuous exercise accelerates conversion of xanthine dehydrogenase to xanthine oxidase, generating superoxide (O₂⁻) and hydroxyl radical (OH⁻), which in turn react with uric acid. Prior human studies (Hellsten et al. 1997) had shown allantoin rising earlier than uric acid after short-duration ergometer exercise. No published equine-specific data on the uric acid/allantoin time-course after exhaustive treadmill exercise existed. The study aimed to characterise serum uric acid, allantoin, and lipid peroxidation (MDA) in Thoroughbreds subjected to exhaustive treadmill exercise, and to determine whether uric acid present before exercise or newly produced uric acid was the dominant source of allantoin production.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 4 Thoroughbreds.

**Preliminary testing:** Incremental load applied to determine maximum heart rate (HRmax), then treadmill speeds corresponding to 80% HRmax (80% HRmax-V) and 105% HRmax (105% HRmax-V) established for each individual horse.

**Exercise protocol:**

1. Warm-up: 5 minutes at 4 m/s, 0% slope
2. Rest: 5 minutes standing
3. Main phase 1: 3 minutes at 80% HRmax-V, 10% slope
4. Main phase 2: exercise shifted to 105% HRmax-V within 10 seconds, continued until exhaustion (horse-specific endpoint)
5. Recovery: 15 minutes walking at 1.0 m/s, 0% slope

**Blood sampling:** Jugular venepuncture at:

- Before exercise (pre-exercise baseline)
- Immediately after exercise
- 5 min post-exercise
- 15 min post-exercise
- 30 min post-exercise
- 60 min post-exercise
- 120 min post-exercise

Serum separated, stored at −80°C until measurement.

**Analytical methods:**

- Serum uric acid: uricase-peroxidase method (Wako Pure Chemical Industries kit)
- Serum allantoin: modified Young-Conway method (Young & Conway 1942; Matsumoto et al. 1994); protein and lipids removed with chloroform before colorimetric determination
- Serum lipid peroxidation (LPO): LPO-586 method (BIOXYTECH, France); bimolecular chromogenic reagent combines with MDA (malondialdehyde), coloured product read at 586 nm

**Statistics:** Mean ± SD; Student's t-test, significance level P < 0.05.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

The paper reports results as time-course graphs (Figs. 1–4) and text; exact numerical values for means and SDs are not tabulated in text form but are readable from graphical representation. Key quantitative findings from text:

**Serum uric acid (Fig. 1):**

- Started rising immediately after exercise was completed
- Continued to increase until **60 min post-exercise** (peak)
- Decreased at **120 min post-exercise**, approaching pre-exercise value
- No numerical mean ± SD reported in text for the graph values

**Serum allantoin (Fig. 2):**

- Level started to rise immediately after the completion of exercise
- Continued to increase until **60 min post-exercise** (peak)
- **Significant increase from pre-exercise level at 30 min and 60 min post-exercise** (by Student's t-test, P < 0.05)
- Decreased at 120 min post-exercise, approaching pre-exercise value

**Allantoin/uric acid ratio (Fig. 3):**

- Ratio increased slightly after exercise
- **No significant change from pre-exercise level** at any time point (P > 0.05)
- Interpretation: the proportional relationship between the two metabolites was maintained, indicating both pre-existing uric acid and newly generated uric acid contributed similarly to allantoin production

**Serum LPO / MDA (Fig. 4):**

- Level increased slightly immediately after exercise
- **No significant change from pre-exercise level** at any time point (P > 0.05)
- Interpretation: lipid peroxidation was not significantly elevated by this level of exhaustive exercise; uric acid/allantoin is a more sensitive early oxidative-stress marker

**Mechanistic interpretation from text:** During strenuous exercise, accumulation of blood in active muscles plus ischemia of other organs, followed by blood reflow after exercise, converts xanthine dehydrogenase to xanthine oxidase, producing superoxide and hydroxyl radicals. Uric acid scavenges these radicals, producing allantoin. The constant allantoin/uric acid ratio means the system is stoichiometrically balanced — the antioxidant pool mobilises in proportion to oxidative stress, without net depletion of uric acid relative to allantoin production.

**Comparison to human data:** Hellsten et al. (1997) found allantoin rising earlier than uric acid in human skeletal muscle during short ergometer exercise; this study in horses finds simultaneous rises beginning immediately post-exercise for both. The difference may reflect species differences in sampling site (serum vs. muscle) or exercise protocol length.

## Discussion & interpretation

The authors conclude that exhaustive treadmill exercise in Thoroughbreds stimulates the antioxidative uric acid pathway in proportion to exercise intensity, with both pre-existing uric acid and newly synthesised uric acid contributing to allantoin formation. The stable allantoin/uric acid ratio throughout the time course supports the interpretation that there is no net depletion of the uric acid antioxidant reserve — the body replenishes uric acid from purine catabolism at roughly the same rate as it is consumed for radical scavenging.

The absence of significant LPO elevation differentiates this from the pattern seen in pathological oxidative stress (e.g., rheumatism patients showing elevated allantoin — Grootveld & Halliwell 1978; Wilson's disease — Ogihara et al. 1995), where allantoin rises without proportional uric acid replenishment. In healthy Thoroughbreds performing exhaustive exercise, the antioxidant system is upregulated but not overwhelmed.

## Limitations

- Extremely small sample size (n = 4); no statistical power for group comparisons
- No individual horse data reported; only group mean described
- Blood sampling via jugular venepuncture at 7 time points; no arterial or mixed venous blood
- No measures of actual exercise speed at 80% or 105% HRmax — these speeds are horse-specific and not reported; the absolute intensity cannot be compared across horses
- No lactate or pH measurement, preventing direct integration with the EOF literature (docids 10_3,4_61, 10_3,4_67, 11_3_51)
- Figures are the primary data; precise mean ± SD values not given in text

- `serum_uric_acid_60min` — serum uric acid measured at 60 min post-maximal-effort workout — source: post-workout blood panel from JRA/NAR veterinary infrastructure — expected effect: elevated values indicate exhaustive effort was performed within prior 2 hours; could flag incomplete recovery if race is imminent — data availability: NOT in standard race records; research/veterinary context only
- `serum_allantoin_30_60min` — serum allantoin at 30–60 min post-exercise — same context as above — the only time-points with statistically significant elevation (P < 0.05); peak oxidative-stress indicator window
- `allantoin_uric_acid_ratio` — the ratio itself: if significantly elevated (unlike this paper's finding), it would indicate pathological oxidative stress beyond normal antioxidant capacity — as a feature: an **abnormally high ratio** (ratio ≫ baseline) could flag pathological oxidative burden not seen in healthy Thoroughbreds performing normal work
- CAUTION: LPO/MDA was not significantly elevated; do NOT use MDA as an early-stage oxidative-stress marker in horses — uric acid/allantoin is more sensitive at this exercise intensity
- `time_since_last_hard_workout_hours` — derivation: from training log, hours since last recorded maximal gallop — expected effect: horse sampled/raced within 30–120 min of peak uric acid/allantoin window may show residual oxidative burden; useful interaction with `pre_race_uric_acid` if that data ever becomes available
- Key negative result to internalise: **lipid peroxidation (MDA) is not elevated by exhaustive exercise in healthy Thoroughbreds** — do not use MDA-based oxidative-stress features if collected from racing-context blood panels
