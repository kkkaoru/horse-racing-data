## Abstract (verbatim)

> The purpose of this study was to create a lower forelimb model of the Thoroughbred horse for measuring the force in the superficial and deep digital flexor tendons (SDFT and DDFT), and the suspensory ligament (SL) during a trot. The mass, centers of gravity, and inertial moments in the metacarpus, pastern, and hoof segments were measured in 4 Thoroughbred horses. The moment arms of the SDFT, DDFT, and SL in the metacarpophalangeal (fetlock) and distal interphalangeal (coffin) joints were measured in 7 Thoroughbred horses. The relationship between the fetlock joint angle and the force in the SL was assessed in 3 limbs of 2 Thoroughbred horses. The forces in the SDFT, DDFT, and SL during a trot were also measured in 7 Thoroughbred horses. The mass of the 3 segments, and the moment arms of the SDFT and DDFT in the fetlock joint of the Thoroughbred horses were smaller than those of the Warmblood horses, whereas the other values were almost the same in the 2 types. The calculated force in the SDFT with this Thoroughbred model reached a peak (4,615 N) at 39.3% of the stance phase, whereas that in the DDFT reached a peak (5,076 N) at 51.2% of the stance phase. The force in the SL reached a peak (11,957 N) at 49.4% of the stance phase. This lower forelimb model of the Thoroughbred can be applied to studying the effects of different shoe types and change of hoof angle for the flexor tendon and SL forces.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **A (injury/soundness — musculoskeletal biomechanics)**.

This paper provides the first Thoroughbred-specific biomechanical model for quantifying forelimb tendon and ligament loading during locomotion. The key finding — that the suspensory ligament (SL) peak force (11,957 N) is approximately 2.6× the SDFT peak force (4,615 N) and approximately 2.4× the DDFT peak force (5,076 N) during trotting — directly quantifies relative injury severity. SL rupture would involve much greater peak force than SDFT rupture, justifying different risk weights in an injury-history feature.

For the finishing-position model: injury type flags for SDFT, DDFT, and SL should not be collapsed into a single "tendon/ligament injury" category. The force magnitudes differ by a factor of ~2–2.5× and the clinical prognosis and return-to-racing timelines also differ materially. The Thoroughbred-specific model values (segment masses, moment arms) are smaller than Warmblood values — reinforcing that injury risk coefficients from Warmblood or pony studies should not be transferred to JRA Thoroughbred data.

The paper also provides the first validated non-invasive method (inverse dynamics, using Thoroughbred-specific anatomical parameters) for estimating tendon forces, applicable to future instrumented shoe studies at JRA training centres.

## Background & objective

Forelimb flexor tendon and ligament injuries (especially SDFT, DDFT, SL) are common in athletic horses; overload and repeated loading are assumed causes. Previous tendon force measurements used invasive implanted sensors (low durability, single-use, damages tissue) or non-invasive ultrasonic methods (SDFT only, not DDFT or SL). Inverse dynamics models existed for Warmbloods and ponies but not Thoroughbreds, which differ in body mass and configuration. The objective was to build a Thoroughbred-specific lower forelimb model and compute SDFT, DDFT, and SL forces during trotting.

## Materials & methods

**Three-part study:**

**Part 1 — Moment arm measurement:**

- Sample: 7 left forelimbs from necropsy specimens, Thoroughbreds (5 males, 2 females; M = 460 ± 22 kg; age = 6.6 ± 2.1 years); cut at mid-radius, frozen at −20°C.
- Sectioned in median plane; photographed at fetlock and coffin joints; moment arms measured using ImageJ 1.43u at ~130 pixels/cm resolution.
- Joint center of rotation determined by fitting a circle to each joint's arc of motion.
- Moment arm = minimum distance from joint center to SDFT, DDFT, or SL.
- Statistical test: Pearson correlation between moment arm and body mass M (P < 0.05 threshold; JMP 9.0.3).

**Part 2 — SL angle-force relationship:**

- Sample: 3 forelimbs from 2 male Thoroughbreds (M = 445 and 515 kg; ages 6 and 8 years); cut at distal radius; frozen then thawed overnight.
- Skin, SDFT, DDFT removed. Two markers glued to palmar SL surface ~3 cm apart for video extensometer. Three markers (1 cm diameter) on lateral surface for fetlock joint angle measurement.
- Hoof fixed to horizontal plate on universal testing machine (AG-IS 100kN, Shimadzu, Kyoto); limb loaded to 2.0 kN and unloaded to 0.2 kN, 10 cycles each at 35 or 70 mm/min head speed.
- SL strain measured by video extensometer DVE-201 (Shimadzu) at 100 Hz; fetlock joint angle by high-speed video HSV-500C3 (nac Image Technology, Tokyo) at 250 Hz; synchronised by LED electronic flash.
- SL strain–force relationship: after fetlock angle–strain test, SL (+ sesamoid bone + distal sesamoidean ligament) removed; loaded to 4.0 kN and unloaded to 0.3 kN, 10 cycles at 100 or 200 mm/min.

**Part 3 — In vivo force calculation during trot:**

- Sample: 7 Thoroughbreds (3 males, 2 females, 2 geldings; M = 520 ± 24 kg; ages 3–7 years). 1 horse participated twice (left and right limb at different sessions); data from 8 limbs total.
- All confirmed non-lame; no shoes during experiment.
- 4 markers (1 cm diameter) on hoof and joint centers; positions confirmed by radiography.
- 2D motion analysis: single camera HSV-500C3 (nac), ~5 pixels/cm resolution.
- Ground reaction force (GRF): DPM-612B force plate + EFP-396ASA21 (Kyowa Electronic Instruments, Tokyo).
- Trot speed: ~3 m/s (comfortable trot for each horse).
- Sampling: 250 Hz for both kinematic and GRF; synchronised.
- Low-pass filter: 2nd order, 15 Hz Butterworth; applied to kinematic data.
- Stance phase defined: GRF ≥ 200 N = ground contact.
- SL force = 0 at initial ground contact (assumption; SL unstrained when fetlock angle at impact defined as zero).
- Forces calculated using Thoroughbred model from Part 1–2 (mean values, not regression predictions).
- Software: BIMUTUS (Kissei Comtec, Nagano) for peak values and normalization to %stance.

**Part 4 — Segment inertial properties:**

- Sample: 4 left forelimbs from necropsy Thoroughbreds (3 males, 1 female; M = 447 ± 82 kg; age = 5.3 ± 1.3 years); cut at distal radius; frozen at −20°C.
- Segments: metacarpus (proximal carpal–fetlock center), pastern (fetlock center–coffin center), hoof (coffin center–sole).
- Proximal interphalangeal joint assumed fixed.
- Center of gravity and inertial moments measured by oscillation method with aluminum fixation (2.634 kg); oscillation times for 20 complete oscillations around 6 axes; laser displacement meter (LB-01 + LB-60 and KZ-U3, Keyence, Osaka) at 1,000 Hz.

DDFT force: peaks at mid-stance (51.2%); shows a secondary rise near 85% of stance (Fig. 2 in paper).

SL force: rises with stance phase, peaks at 49.4%, consistent with maximum fetlock extension at mid-stance.

## Discussion & interpretation

The Thoroughbred-specific model is necessary because segment masses and SDFT/DDFT moment arms at the fetlock are smaller in Thoroughbreds than in Warmbloods, while other anatomical values (SL moment arm, segment CoM positions, limb length proportions) are similar. Using Warmblood parameters would overestimate segment inertia and therefore overestimate tendon forces in Thoroughbreds.

The SL peak force (11,957 N) being approximately 2.6× SDFT (4,615 N) is consistent with the SL bearing the primary role of supporting fetlock hyperextension against the ground reaction force at mid-stance. SDFT serves an energy-storage/return function peaking earlier; DDFT provides energy at push-off. The force hierarchy SL > DDFT > SDFT supports epidemiological findings that SL injuries, while less commonly reported than SDFT injuries, result in higher peak loading per structural cross-section.

Authors note that the Thoroughbred model can be applied to study shoe type and hoof angle effects — highly relevant for JRA track and training surface management research.

The SDFT negative force in the final 30% of stance is a modelling artefact: when the coffin joint hyperextends during late push-off, the navicular ligaments generate part of the coffin joint moment, meaning the standard two-structure model (SDFT + DDFT) overestimates DDFT and underestimates SDFT. This finding is consistent with direct implanted sensor measurements in Thoroughbreds (cited in paper).

## Limitations

- All force calculations from necropsy specimens; in vivo geometry during racing gallop will differ from trot geometry used here — forces at gallop are substantially higher (SDFT forces at gallop estimated at 3–5× trot values in other species).
- n=4 horses for inertial properties; n=7 for moment arms; n=8 limbs for force calculations — small samples.
- 2D motion analysis (single camera) cannot capture out-of-plane mediolateral forces.
- Skin movement artifact not corrected (accepted as small in distal limb).
- Proximal interphalangeal (pastern) joint assumed fixed — introduces some error, though authors note this has small effects.
- No repeatability data (within-horse test-retest); model parameters estimated from mean values.
- Horses trotted barefoot; shoeing alters hoof geometry and GRF distribution, affecting calculated tendon forces.

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `injury_type_sdft` — binary: SDFT tendinopathy in clinical history — derivation: JRA veterinary records — expected effect: positive on scratch/DNF risk; recovery time 12–18 months — data availability: restricted to JRA internal
- `injury_type_ddft` — binary: DDFT injury — derivation: JRA veterinary records — expected effect: positive; DDFT peak force (5,076 N) > SDFT (4,615 N) at mid-stance; clinically different prognosis — do NOT collapse with SDFT injury
- `injury_type_sl` — binary: suspensory ligament injury — derivation: JRA veterinary records — expected effect: strongest negative; SL peak force (11,957 N) ≈ 2.6× SDFT; SL injuries are often career-ending
- `injury_severity_weight` — numeric risk weight: SL=3.0, DDFT=1.1, SDFT=1.0 (proportional to peak force ratios) — derivation: force magnitudes from this paper — expected effect: rank-orders injury type by severity for composite injury risk score
- `breed_specific_note` — Thoroughbred biomechanical parameters differ from Warmbloods; if cross-breed modelling, do NOT use Warmblood injury incidence rates as Thoroughbred priors; segment masses at same body weight are smaller (metacarpus: Thoroughbred 1.435 kg vs. Warmblood approximately 20–30% heavier segments for similar M)
