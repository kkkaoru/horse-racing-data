## Abstract (verbatim)

> Plasma adrenaline (Ad), noradrenaline (NA), adrenocorticotropin (ACTH) and cortisol responses to exhaustive incremental treadmill exercise were investigated in seven Thoroughbreds. Blood lactate (LA), heart rate (HR) and oxygen uptake (VO2) were measured and their correlations with plasma hormone levels were examined. Although the magnitude of plasma Ad, NA and ACTH response to the exercise was great, that of cortisol was very slight. Plasma Ad and NA levels reached peaks at exhaustion in all Thoroughbreds. On the other hand, plasma ACTH levels reached peaks at a different time in each Thoroughbred, followed by a 10–15 min delayed peak of plasma cortisol levels. The maximal levels of plasma Ad, NA, ACTH and cortisol were significantly 300, 150, 70 and 1.8 fold higher, respectively, than the pre-exercise levels. Plasma Ad, NA and ACTH during exercise were highly and significantly correlated to each other. The noticeable increase in plasma Ad levels during exercise was characteristic of Thoroughbreds. Plasma Ad, NA and ACTH responses during exercise were significantly correlated with treadmill velocity, blood LA, HR and VO2 in an exponential manner. The threshold for blood LA was significantly lower than the threshold for Ad, NA and ACTH. In conclusion, the present data on plasma CA and ACTH responses to exercise suggest that the sympathoadrenal axis and the pituitary gland are highly activated by exercise in Thoroughbreds. In addition, it is suggested that plasma CA and ACTH levels as well as LA and HR may be useful indicators for estimating exercise stress and physical fitness in the Thoroughbred.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **C (exercise-physiology / fitness)**. This paper establishes exponential correlations between plasma catecholamines (Ad, NA), ACTH, and the practically accessible proxies blood lactate (LA) and heart rate (HR) during exhaustive incremental treadmill exercise in seven trained Thoroughbreds. The finding that Ad, NA, and ACTH responses all correlate with velocity, LA, HR, and VO2 at r > 0.9 per individual validates LA and HR as surrogate fitness features for the full sympathoadrenal response — highly relevant because LA and HR can be derived from standardised treadmill tests recorded in JRA research databases.

The threshold framework (LT < Ad-T ≈ NA-T ≈ ACTH-T, corresponding to 71.3% vs. ~77% of VO2peak) provides a physiological rationale for distinguishing aerobic from anaerobic fitness states. Horses whose LA threshold occurs at a higher fraction of VO2max have a superior aerobic base — a feature derivable from serial treadmill data. The marked individual variation in CA response at a given velocity suggests that horse-level heterogeneity in sympathoadrenal reactivity is a genuine dimension of performance, separate from the mean group response.

Cortisol, by contrast, rose only 1.8-fold versus 70-fold for ACTH, confirming that cortisol is a poor real-time exercise-intensity indicator. Any pipeline feature based on post-exercise cortisol should account for the 10–15 min lag between ACTH peak and cortisol peak. In JRA/NAR race records the nearest practical equivalents are training-session blood samples (LA, PCV), HR monitors, and treadmill test indices (V200, VLA4).

The paper is specific to adult Thoroughbreds (ages 4–5), so threshold values and fold-changes apply to horses in established training rather than yearlings or unbroken horses.

## Background & objective

Prior human literature established that plasma CA increases exponentially with exercise intensity and correlates with LA, HR, and VO2. In horses, Snow et al. (1992) showed much larger CA responses than in humans, but the correlation structure between CA and cardiorespiratory variables had not been quantified. ACTH responses to exercise in horses were also unmeasured. The study aimed to characterise Ad, NA, ACTH, and cortisol kinetics during exhaustive incremental treadmill exercise and to determine whether the CA-LA-HR-VO2 correlation structure observed in humans holds for Thoroughbreds.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 7 healthy trained Thoroughbreds, ages 4–5 years, mean weight 450 ± 11 kg; trained on treadmill 5 days/week for 1 month prior to testing.

**Instrumentation:** High-speed equine treadmill (Mustang-2200, Kagra, Switzerland); open-flow respirometry for VO2; Polar HR monitor (Bandage-XL); 14-gauge jugular vein catheter.

**Exercise protocol:** 5 min warm-up at 4 m/s on 0% incline, 5 min rest; treadmill inclined to 10%; incremental steps (1 min each) at 1.8, 2.7, 3.4, 4.5, 5.4, 6.8, 9.0, 9.8, 10.8, 11.6 m/s until exhaustion (horse cannot maintain position). All horses completed the 9.0 m/s step; maximum velocity at exhaustion = 10.5 ± 0.3 m/s. Total incremental exercise duration ~9 min.

**Blood sampling:** Jugular catheter; samples at rest (pre), immediately before warm-up (WP), and after each speed step; recovery at 1 (A1), 5 (A5), 15 (A15), 60 (A60), 120 (A120) min post-exercise.

**Assays:** HPLC-ECD for Ad and NA (detection limit 10 pg/ml, intra-assay CV < 5%); specific RIA for cortisol (limit 2 ng/ml, CV 7.7%) and ACTH (limit 5 pg/ml, CV 4.7%); YSI-1500 for blood lactate; microhaematocrit for PCV.

**Thresholds (LT, Ad-T, NA-T, ACTH-T):** Identified as the VO2 corresponding to the individual breakpoint of log-log transformed curves using two-segmental linear regression (Beaver et al. 1985 method).

**Statistics:** One-way ANOVA with repeated measures; logarithmic transformation for skewed CA/ACTH data; Tukey post-hoc; Student's paired t-tests for threshold comparisons and peak vs. pre-exercise comparisons.

**Correlation structure:** Ad, NA, and ACTH all correlated exponentially (P < 0.001) with treadmill velocity, blood LA, HR, and VO2. Individual r values were > 0.9 in almost all cases; pooled r values were lower (< 0.9) due to marked inter-individual variation. Notably, CA–LA r > 0.97 in each individual (substantially higher than CA–HR or CA–VO2). CA and ACTH abruptly increased at HR ~200 beats/min and VO2 ~150 ml/kg/min, consistent with the aerobic-anaerobic transition. Cortisol showed no significant exponential correlation with velocity, LA, HR, or VO2.

## Discussion & interpretation

The extreme CA responses in Thoroughbreds (Ad ~300×, NA ~150×) far exceed those in humans and reflect a greater activation of the adrenal medulla. The authors note Ad and NA behaved similarly (unlike humans where NA > Ad) and attribute this to equine adrenal medullary NA secretion in addition to sympathetic neuronal spillover. The CA–LA exponential correlation (r > 0.97 per individual) supports the hypothesis that muscle pH reduction from lactate accumulation drives reflex sympathetic outflow (Schneider et al. 1992). The ACTH–LA correlation and the ACTH-T > LT finding support lactate production as an activator of the pituitary-adrenal axis. The cortisol system is constrained — the adrenocortical production capacity appears to limit cortisol despite 70× ACTH rise, or ACTH-like immunoreactive fragments with reduced bioactivity may be measured. Persson (1983) identified HR 200 beats/min as an aerobic power index; the abrupt CA/ACTH rise at this threshold in the present study provides a neuroendocrine basis for that index. Individual CA variation among horses was notable, raising the possibility that sympathoadrenal reactivity heterogeneity contributes to performance differences.

## Limitations

- n = 7 horses, no statistical power for subgroup analyses.
- All horses 4–5 years, single training center — generalisation to yearlings, older horses, and Standardbreds requires caution.
- No performance (race result) data linked to CA values; individual CA response-performance correlation is speculative.
- Pre-exercise VO2 not measured (mask attached only on treadmill), so % VO2peak calculations use treadmill peak.
- Jugular venous (not arterial) CA measured; may slightly underestimate arterial levels.

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `blood_lactate_peak` — peak LA during standardised treadmill test (mmol/L) — JRA treadmill test records — higher peak LA indicates anaerobic capacity; expected positive correlation with finishing position at longer distances
- `heart_rate_peak` — peak HR during test (beats/min) — JRA treadmill test records — less discriminative than LA but accessible; positively correlated with fitness
- `pcv_peak` — peak PCV (%) during test — JRA treadmill test records — proxy for splenic reserve and O2 delivery capacity; higher is better
- `lactate_threshold_vo2pct` — LT as % of VO2peak — derivable from serial LA-speed curves — higher % indicates better aerobic base; use relative form to control for body size
- `vo2peak_rel` — VO2max relative (ml/kg/min) — JRA treadmill test records — strongest single aerobic fitness index; reference ranges: pre-breaking yearling ~135, trained adult ~163
- `ca_threshold_gap` — (Ad-T or NA-T VO2) − (LT VO2) — if serial CA data available — larger gap = greater aerobic-anaerobic differential; potentially correlates with race stamina
- **DO NOT USE** cortisol alone as an exercise-intensity feature — 1.8-fold increase is too small and too delayed to be informative; confounded by chronic stress and circadian rhythm
- **Interaction:** `vo2peak_rel` × `distance` — aerobic capacity benefit should be amplified at longer distances; sprint performance less dependent on VO2max ceiling
