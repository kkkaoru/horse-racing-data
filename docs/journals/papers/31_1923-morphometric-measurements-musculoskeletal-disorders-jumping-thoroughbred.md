## Abstract (verbatim)

> There is limited knowledge about causes of musculoskeletal injury in jumping Thoroughbred horses. The objective of this study was to describe the relationships between musculoskeletal disorders and linear and angular limb measurements acquired from photographs of horses with markers at specific reference points. The diagnosed musculoskeletal disorders in either fore or hind limbs were flexor tendonitis, suspensory ligaments desmitis, and osteoarthritis of the distal intertarsal and tarso-metatarsal and carpometacarpal joints. Lengths and angles in 17 clinically normal jumping Thoroughbred horses and 34 horses with musculoskeletal problems were measured. Horses with musculoskeletal disorders had significantly shorter neck, shoulder, and pelvis lengths (P<0.05), significantly longer arm and forearm front lengths (P<0.05), and significantly lower front shoulder, elbow, and hind fetlock joint angles (P<0.05). In conclusion, this study describes significant relationships between linear and angular morphometric measurements and musculoskeletal disorders in jumping Thoroughbred horses. These data could possibly provide indicators for better selection of jumpers with less risk of developing orthopedic disorders.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

This paper belongs to feature family **F (conformation/body-size/gait)** with direct downstream relevance to **A (injury/soundness)**. Conformation measurements that predict musculoskeletal disorder risk in Thoroughbreds can inform a lameness-probability prior that adjusts expected finishing-position distributions — horses more likely to be lame are more likely to miss starts (scratch risk) or underperform when competing with sub-clinical pain.

Specific conformation profiles associated with elevated musculoskeletal disorder risk in this study: **shorter neck + shorter shoulder + shorter pelvis + longer arm front + longer forearm front + lower front shoulder angle + lower front elbow angle + lower lateral hind fetlock angle**. The mechanism is a shifted centre of gravity (forward) with asymmetric load distribution leading to forelimb flexor/tendon overload.

The study uses jumping Thoroughbreds rather than flat racehorses. The biomechanical demands differ (jump landing vs. high-speed gallop), but many conformation risk factors overlap (Anderson et al. 2004, EVJ 36:571, cited here, provides the flat-racehorse precedent). Transferability to JRA/NAR flat racehorses is plausible for the skeletal measurements (neck, shoulder, pelvis, arm, forearm proportions) but uncertain for joint angles optimised for jumping.

## Background & objective

Musculoskeletal diseases are the main cause of withdrawal from equestrian sports. Show jumping performance is linked to conformation (Pretorius 2003), and various studies have examined conformation–lameness relationships in Thoroughbred racehorses (Anderson et al. 2004), sport horses (Murray et al. 2006), and warmbloods. However, no prior study had specifically quantified linear and angular morphometric measurements in relation to musculoskeletal disorders in jumping Thoroughbreds. The study aimed to fill this gap using standardised digital photography with anatomical markers and AutoCAD software for objective measurement.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 51 jumping Thoroughbred horses at the Armed Forces Equestrian Club, Egypt:

- 17 clinically sound controls: no lameness on physical exam; no abnormalities on digital radiography or ultrasonography
- 34 lame horses: confirmed musculoskeletal disorders by physical exam + radiography + ultrasound
- Age range: 5–15 years; weight range: 450–600 kg
- All horses received identical management and competed in the same number of jumping courses per year

**Photography protocol:** Horses haltered, standing squarely on flat horizontal surface. Left lateral and frontal views. Camera positioned at midpoint of lateral thoracic wall; frontal view centred between forelimb shoulder points, 2 m from horse. Background contrast colour matching. Skin markers at 16 anatomical reference points (per Anderson et al. 2004 and Senna et al. 2015 methods).

**Measurements using AutoCAD 2013 v19 (Autodesk):**

- Lengths: neck, back, shoulder (scapula), arm front, arm lateral, forearm front, forearm lateral, fore cannon lateral, fore cannon front, pelvis, thigh (femur), gaskin (tibia), hind cannon lateral, shoulder lateral, shoulder front
- Angles (12 total): front shoulder joint, front elbow joint, front carpus joint, front forefetlock joint, lateral shoulder joint, lateral elbow joint, lateral carpus joint, lateral forefetlock joint, lateral hip joint, lateral stifle joint, lateral tarsal joint, lateral hind fetlock joint

**Diagnostic imaging:**

- Digital radiography: CR-IR 357, Fujifilm, Tokyo (lateromedial and dorsopalmar/dorsoplantar views)
- Ultrasonography: SSA-320A Toshiba Just Vision, 8 MHz linear transducer (palmar/plantar of fore and hind cannons and phalangeal regions)
- Lameness examination performed by second author (YE)

**Statistical analysis:** ANOVA and independent-samples t-test (IBM SPSS Statistics v20). Kolmogorov-Smirnov normality test (all P > 0.05 = normal distribution). Data expressed as mean ± SD. P < 0.05 considered significant.

## Discussion & interpretation

Longer arm front and forearm front combined with smaller front shoulder and elbow angles indicate a "steep shoulder" conformation: a common finding in jumping Thoroughbreds with disorders (McIlwraith et al. 2003). The steep shoulder shifts weight forward and increases concussive impact loads on the distal forelimb structures (flexor tendons, suspensory ligaments, carpal/fetlock joints). The front-view vs. lateral-view discrepancy in arm and forearm measurements reflects that lateral arm/forearm length relates to shoulder/elbow inclination (lateral stance), while front measurements relate to chest width and elbow tying (tied-in vs. tied-out elbows), affecting biomechanics differently.

Short neck is mechanistically linked to balancing difficulty, causing stumbling and increased forelimb concussion, potentially promoting degenerative joint disease (Thomas 2005 cited). Long neck is reportedly more desirable in jumping horses for balance over fences (Hölmstrom 2001 cited).

Short pelvis reduces the length of muscular attachment to thigh and gaskin, diminishing hindquarter propulsive power — particularly important for jumping but also relevant for acceleration in flat racing.

The large lateral hind fetlock angle difference (8.1° lower in disorder horses: 144.7° vs. 152.8°) indicates more acute fetlock angulation (caudally), which increases peak compressive forces on the hind fetlock joint and predisposes to osteoarthritis of the distal hock and fetlock joints.

Tarsal angle was not significantly different, despite prior studies reporting associations. The authors note that previous tarsal angle thresholds for osteoarthritis predisposition were <155° (small) and >165° (large) in racing horses, and >140° predisposing to distal intertarsal/tarsometatarsal OA in performance horses; the non-significant result in this jumping Thoroughbred population may reflect sample heterogeneity.

## Limitations

- **Small sample (n=51)** with 17 controls vs. 34 cases; lame group is heterogeneous (three pathology types pooled)
- **Population**: Egyptian military-equestrian jumping Thoroughbreds; different use, training regime, footing, and selection criteria from JRA/NAR flat racehorses
- **Cross-sectional design**: cannot determine whether conformation caused disorder or disorder caused secondary postural changes (particularly for angle measurements)
- **Photographic method**: skin-marker measurements have intra- and inter-observer error; AutoCAD measurement is objective but depends on marker placement accuracy
- **No blinding**: same examiner performed conformation measurements and presumably contributed to lameness evaluation; potential for confirmation bias
- **p-values only, no effect sizes reported** (Cohen's d, OR, etc.); the absolute differences (e.g., 8.1° hind fetlock angle) are the most clinically interpretable numbers
- **Jumping vs. flat racing**: forelimb impact pattern at landing differs substantially from galloping; the critical forelimb features (fetlock hyperextension, tendon loading) overlap but are not identical

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `neck_length_cm` — shorter neck predicts MSK disorders; source: stud-book or JRA registration conformation data (if available); expected sign: negative association with soundness/finishing position; rough derivation: photograph-based or physical measurement at registration
- `shoulder_scapula_length_cm` — shorter shoulder (scapula) associated with disorders; same source; negative soundness association
- `pelvis_length_cm` — shorter pelvis associated with disorders, reduced hindquarter power; negative on finishing position (compounded for acceleration-dependent short races)
- `arm_front_length_cm` — longer arm (humerus, front view) associated with disorders; positive disorder risk
- `forearm_front_length_cm` — longer forearm (radius, front view) associated with disorders
- `front_shoulder_angle_deg` — lower angle = steeper shoulder = disorder risk; threshold: disorder mean 73.8° vs. normal 75.2°
- `front_elbow_angle_deg` — lower angle = disorder risk; disorder mean 167.5° vs. normal 169.8°
- `hind_fetlock_lateral_angle_deg` — lower (more acute) = disorder risk; disorder mean 144.7° vs. normal 152.8°; largest absolute difference of all significant variables
- `steep_shoulder_composite` — interaction: arm_front_length_cm > 32 AND forearm_front_length_cm > 48 AND front_shoulder_angle_deg < 74; flags high-risk conformation profile
- **Data availability**: conformation measurements are not typically in public JRA race result data; if available, they would be at horse registration/stud book level; most useful as cross-sectional injury-risk priors
- **Do NOT use tarsal angle** as a disorder predictor from this study — non-significant in this population; rely on other sources
