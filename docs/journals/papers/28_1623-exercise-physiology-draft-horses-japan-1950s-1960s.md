## Abstract (verbatim)

> Although the total number of horses raised in Japan dramatically decreased after World War II, because draft horses were still used for farm work in paddy fields and on farms during the period of the 1950s and 1960s, a performance test for selecting better draft horses was needed. In order to determine the most suitable size of draft horses for Japanese farm conditions, the working power of horses weighing from 185 to 622 kg was evaluated by performing an endurance test, several kinds of working power tests, and maximum pulling power tests. Oxygen consumption during draft exercise was measured by the Douglas bag method in order to evaluate effects of draft workload under the conditions of different types of work (14- and 18-cm plow depths, cultivator, and tillage), traction methods (shoulder traction, shoulder-trunk traction, and chest-trunk traction), walking speeds (40, 60, 80, 100, and 120 m/min), and depths of water (0, 18, 36, and 54 cm) on energy expenditure. The relationship between energy consumption and pulse rate during exercise was also evaluated. A study of a performance test for draft horses was conducted to establish a new approach for evaluating draft horse performance using heart rate as an index. For this study, a beat meter for measuring heart rate was developed, and experimental protocols were used to evaluate the relationship between heart rate and workload. Although the research results obtained from these studies do not have particular relevance in the current day, these studies are valuable for understanding the history of equine exercise physiology in Japan.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

This paper is a historical review (feature family **C — exercise physiology/fitness** and **F — body size**). Although the subjects are draft horses, the physiological principles it establishes underpin modern Thoroughbred performance modelling in several concrete ways.

The most direct connection is the **body weight → locomotion power** relationship. Across 185–622 kg draft horses, the correlation between body weight and maximum pulling power was r = 0.98, and between body weight and packing (carrying) ability r = 0.91. These high correlations reflect biomechanical allometry: heavier horses have longer stride lengths and therefore higher absolute speed at a given stride frequency. In JRA, race-day body weight (馬体重) is publicly recorded and already used as a raw feature; this review validates that the effect is real and physiologically grounded.

The second key contribution is the **HR ↔ VO₂ (energy expenditure) relationship**. Tatsumi et al. measured VO₂ and pulse rate in the same exercise bouts and found r = 0.83 between VO₂ and pulse rate, r = 0.78 between relative metabolic rate and pulse rate. This is the empirical foundation for using resting HR and the V200 index (running velocity at HR = 200 beat/min) as fitness proxies in Thoroughbreds — a methodology extended by Ohmura & Jones (28_1717) and widely used in JRA training monitoring.

The **optimal walking speed** finding (minimum energy cost at 80 m/min; both slower and faster speeds increase cost per unit distance) provides the conceptual basis for efficiency-based stride analysis in racehorses.

The **Ban-ei** section documents that heavy draft horses pulling 650–750 kg produce blood lactate of 14.8–15.5 mmol/L — essentially the same as Thoroughbred flat-racing levels — suggesting the anaerobic threshold workload is intensity-dependent, not breed-dependent.

## Background & objective

After World War II, Japan still had ~400,000 horses (mostly draft) for farm work. Selection criteria for draft horses were urgently needed. The review synthesises studies from three institutions (Nasu Branch, Tohoku Experiment Station, Tokyo University) conducted 1949–1965 covering body size–work power, energy expenditure during various draft tasks, and performance testing using HR as the index. The goal was to identify optimal horse size and to develop a practical HR-based field test.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects (across reviewed studies):**

- 3 Korean ponies (~190 kg BW), 3 Hokkaido ponies (~300 kg), crossbred horses ~500 kg; range 185–622 kg across all studies
- 13 horses (306–652 kg) for deep plowing requirements
- 3 Percherons (avg 650 kg), 3 crossbreds (500–600 kg), 3 trotters (340–440 kg), 3 Hokkaido ponies (300 kg) for cross-breed comparison
- 22 horses (BWs not all specified) for HR performance test (trained vs. untrained)
- 15 horses (530–650 kg) and 22 horses (649–750 kg) for heavy-load HR linearity tests
- 24 horses (530–725 kg) for light-load HR linearity tests
- 2 crossbred horses (482 and 542 kg) for water-depth experiment
- 6–7 crossbred horses (400–550 kg) for walking-speed energy experiments

**Endurance tests:** Carts drawn 8–24 km/day for 3 successive days at varying loads (1/9 to 1/5 of BW).

**Working power tests:** Plowing, cultivating, harrowing, drawing carts, packing, riding for 4-hr bouts.

**Maximum pulling power tests:** Drawing carts/wagons with heavy loads over 600 m.

**VO₂ measurement:** Douglas bag method with face mask; samples collected for 5–6 min during exercise and 6–10 min recovery; gas analyzed for O₂ fraction.

**HR measurement:** Custom beat meter (Nomura, 1964) counting QRS complexes; later a radiotelemetry ECG system. HR measured every 15 sec at 50, 150, and 250 m marks during 300-m exercise and every 30 sec during 3-min recovery.

**Performance test protocol:** 300-m straight course; walking speed 70 m/min; draft workload ~1/4 BW reference; at least 3 workload levels. Workload index = (draft workload × distance) ÷ (BW × time). Horses classified A/B/C from regression of total HR on workload.

**Statistical analysis:** Correlation coefficients (r) and regression lines; performance classification from ±1 SD limits around regression line.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

### Body size and work power (Ishizaki et al., data range 185–622 kg)

- Maximum pulling power vs. BW: r = 0.98 (almost perfectly proportional to BW)
- Maximum packing (carrying) ability vs. BW: r = 0.91 (less proportional; lighter horses pack heavier loads disproportionally)
- Area plowed (paddy) vs. BW: r = 0.92
- Stride frequency: lower in heavier horses; stride length: longer in heavier horses
- Minimum horse size for deep plowing (18–20 cm): ≥498 kg BW, ≥148 cm height, ≥177 cm chest circumference, ≥19.9 cm cannon circumference, ≥156 cm body length
- Crossbred working capacity per kg BW (from Tohoku/Fuchimkai): trotters highest > Percherons > Hokkaido ponies > crossbreds
- Korean ponies (~190 kg) could plow 720 m² paddy in ~3.5 hr (11-cm depth, draft workload 42–45 kg, mean draft ~1/5 BW)
- Korean ponies: endurance test at 20 kg draft (1/9 BW) → 24 km/day for 3 days; at 40 kg draft → 8 km/day
- Korean ponies: packing 80 kg load → 16 km in 4 hr

**Walking speed and energy cost (6 crossbred horses, 400–500 kg BW, speeds 40–120 m/min):**

- Minimum energy cost per kg BW and per distance traveled: at 80 m/min
- Total energy consumption at 80 m/min was lowest; values at 60 and 100 m/min were lower than at 40 and 120 m/min
- Relative metabolic rate and energy per minute increased linearly with speed

**Water depth experiment (2 crossbreds, 482 and 542 kg BW, speed 60 m/min, depths 0–54 cm):**

- Total energy expenditure and net energy expenditure increased linearly with water depth
- Net energy expenditure at 54 cm was nearly twice that at 18 cm
- Relative metabolic rate at 54 cm depth ≈ 3× resting ≈ same as walking at 100 m/min on flat ground with no load

**Traction method comparison (shoulder-trunk, chest-trunk, shoulder):**

- Draft workload, work rate, and net efficiency similar among 3 methods
- Shoulder traction: slightly higher calories used and higher relative metabolic rate (though not significantly different)
- Conclusion: method choice depends on horse size and pulling weight combination

### Heart rate as workload index (Nomura 1964)

- VO₂ vs. pulse rate: r = 0.83
- Relative metabolic rate vs. pulse rate: r = 0.78
- Both relationships validated post-exercise pulse rate as a proxy for VO₂

**HR performance test linearity:**

- Trained horses had consistently lower total HR (exercise + recovery) for same workload than untrained horses
- Relationship between total HR and workload was linear across individual horses (Fig. 7)
- With heavy workloads (draft workload exceeding 1/3 BW, i.e., workload index >450): slope became steeper — non-linear above threshold
- With light workloads (draft workload <1/5 BW, index <250): slope also became steeper
- Recommended performance test workload: between 1/5 and 1/4 of BW

**Ban-ei draft horses (Arakawa 2008, cited in review):**

- 4 Percherons + 3 Bretons pulling 350–750 kg on 300-m flat sand
- V200 (velocity at HR = 200 beat/min) decreased as pulling weight increased (Fig. 10)
- Blood lactate 3 min post-exercise at 650 kg: 14.8 mmol/L; at 750 kg: 15.5 mmol/L
- These values are comparable to Thoroughbred flat-racing lactate levels

## Discussion & interpretation

The key insight is that HR encodes metabolic rate reliably across a wide range of horse sizes and workloads (r = 0.83 with VO₂), validating HR-based fitness testing as a practical alternative to direct VO₂ measurement. This underpins modern use of V200 and V4 (velocity at lactate 4 mmol/L) in Thoroughbred training programmes.

Body weight is nearly perfectly predictive of maximum pulling power (r = 0.98) but less so for carrying ability (r = 0.91), reflecting a biomechanical difference: pulling uses whole-body force while carrying imposes differential hoof-ground pressure. In racehorses, stride length scales with height and BW, so larger horses are faster at identical relative fitness.

The minimum energy cost at 80 m/min walking corresponds to a "preferred natural frequency" of the locomotor system — resonance between pendulum-like limb swing and ground reaction force. The same principle applies to Thoroughbred galloping: there is an optimal pace per stride frequency, and deviations (over-striding or under-striding) increase metabolic cost.

The Bang-ei data show that even draft breeds can reach thoroughbred-like lactate concentrations at high intensities, demonstrating the universality of anaerobic threshold physiology across equine breeds.

## Limitations

- All subjects are draft horses (185–622 kg), not Thoroughbreds (400–600 kg); transferability of absolute values to racehorses is limited
- VO₂ measured post-exercise (expired gas collection during walking, or 5–6 min exercise sample), not continuously during gallop; Douglas bag method is indirect
- Small sample sizes in most experiments (n = 2–13 horses per study)
- Historical studies from 1949–1965 using equipment and protocols that predate modern respirometry and ECG telemetry
- No statistical power analysis; many conclusions from descriptive regression only
- No control for training status in most body-size experiments

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `race_day_body_weight` — 馬体重 recorded at each JRA race — source: JRA official race data — expected effect: positive up to ~500 kg, potential negative for extreme BW; nonlinear — include as feature; consider delta from last race (`bw_change`)
- `bw_change` — body weight difference from previous race — source: JRA race data — expected effect: negative (weight loss may signal stress/overtraining); also positive weight gain from poor conditioning — encode as signed delta; large deviations (>10 kg) as flag feature
- `stride_length_proxy` — cannot be computed directly; height (withers) × 0.85 approximation — source: JBBA stud book — expected effect: positive correlation with speed at given effort — if unavailable, BW is decent proxy (r~0.92 with area plowed as a biomechanical surrogate)
- `fitness_hr_residual` — if pre-race HR data were available, residual vs. age-expected HR (Ohmura equation Y=48.2X^−0.129) — source: would require wearable sensor data, not in public JRA records — expected effect: below-expected HR → better fitness → better finish — note: NOT currently available in JRA public data
- `v200_proxy` — training velocity at HR = 200 beat/min — source: training facility data (JRA ERI) — expected effect: higher V200 → better aerobic base → better finish position in stamina races — not in public race records; would require training data integration
- **CAUTION:** body weight alone is a blunt proxy for fitness; horses can be heavy from fat or heavy from muscle — use alongside BW change and age to contextualise
