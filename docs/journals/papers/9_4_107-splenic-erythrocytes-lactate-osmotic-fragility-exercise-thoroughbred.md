## Abstract (verbatim)

> Changes in the packed cell volume (PCV), blood lactate concentration [La–] and osmotic fragility of circulating erythrocytes were determined in three horses subjected to treadmill exercises or treated with epinephrine. The osmotic fragility was taken as the haemolysis rate (HL) in 0.56% NaCl. Previous warm-up significantly decreased HL by 24%, and cantering at 8 m/s for 8 min (10% incline) increased HL by 92%; subsequent cool-down significantly decreased the rate by 23% to the initial resting level, but thereafter the rate gradually increased again, returning to the initial resting level. In contrast, continuous trotting at 5 m/s for 15 min (0% incline) decreased the HL by 50% to the initial resting level. Intravenous injection of epinephrine had little effect on the HL. Although the exercises in combination with the injection significantly increased PCV and [La–], these changes in the experiments were not correlated with the HL, but the correlation coefficient for PCV and [La–] and HL during cantering was positive: r=0.875 and 0.958, P<0.01, respectively. These results suggest that: 1) the release of red cells from the spleen into the circulation has no effect on changes in the osmotic fragility of erythrocytes during exercise; 2) heavy exercise increases the osmotic fragility, but 3) light exercise decreases it.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **C (exercise-physiology / fitness)**. This paper characterises the splenic red-cell mobilisation mechanism (the Thoroughbred's unique performance-enabling adaptation) and its relationship to blood lactate and erythrocyte physiology. The key finding — that PCV rises ~45–58% during exercise and that this is uncoupled from the fragility increase during anaerobic canter — clarifies the role of PCV as a fitness proxy.

For the model: the r = 0.958 (P < 0.01) correlation between blood lactate and erythrocyte osmotic fragility (haemolysis rate) during heavy canter, and r = 0.875 for PCV vs. haemolysis rate during the same phase, confirm that PCV and blood lactate are tightly coupled indicators of exercise intensity during anaerobic work. Both are practical fitness proxies in JRA/NAR treadmill test records. The critical mechanistic insight is that splenic contraction (high PCV via epinephrine alone) does **not** increase red cell fragility — the fragility increase is driven by metabolic acidosis (low blood pH, high lactate) during anaerobic exercise. This separates warm-up PCV rise (beneficial, protective) from heavy exercise PCV rise (accompanied by fragility increase and potential haemolysis risk).

The warm-up finding has a practical race-day implication: horses that perform adequate light warm-up before a race enter the race with reduced erythrocyte fragility (−24% HL after warm-up), protecting O2-carrying capacity. Poor warm-up quality could be a risk feature for performance.

The VLA4 = 4 mmol/L threshold is confirmed as physiologically meaningful: heavy exercise (LA > 4 mmol/L) increased fragility; light exercise (LA < 2 mmol/L) decreased it. This aligns with VLA4 as a standard fitness threshold in equine exercise physiology.

## Background & objective

Sports anaemia in horses was attributed to increased erythrocyte osmotic fragility during exercise. Boucher et al. (1985) suggested osmotically sensitive splenic red cells released during exercise cause fragility increase. Smith et al. (1989) found the opposite (decreased fragility after exercise). Hanzawa et al. (1995) showed warm-up reduces fragility and heavy incremental exercise increases it. The present study aimed to disambiguate: is the fragility change caused by (a) splenic red cell release per se, (b) metabolic acidosis from lactate, or (c) both? Epinephrine injection was used to induce splenic contraction without exercise-induced acidosis.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 3 clinically healthy male Thoroughbreds — two 2-year-olds and one 8-year-old; mean weight ± SEM: 455 ± 13 kg. All conditioned on treadmill (Mustang-2200, Kagra, Switzerland) 5 days/week for 1 month before each test period, including 2 min of submaximal exercise at 7–10 m/s.

**Three test protocols (all three horses completed all three):**

1. **Heavy exercise test:**
   - Warm-up: 2 min walk at 2 m/s + 5 min trot at 4 m/s + 5 min walk at 2 m/s (0% incline)
   - Main exercise: 8 min canter at 8 m/s (10% incline) — causes sudden lactate accumulation
   - Cool-down: 20 min trot at 2 m/s (0% incline)

2. **Light exercise test:**
   - Continuous trot at 5 m/s for 15 min (0% incline) — causes little lactate change

3. **Epinephrine treatment test:**
   - IV injection of epinephrine at 2.5 µg/kg body weight — previously validated dose for satisfactory splenic contraction (Persson et al. 1973)

**Blood sampling:** Jugular venepuncture; heparinised tubes; immediately chilled at 4°C; initial resting sample taken 30 min before experiment start; samples at predetermined intervals during/after exercise and post-epinephrine; all analyses within 2 hr.

**Measurements:**

- PCV: microhaematocrit technique (splenic contraction index)
- Blood lactate [La–]: YSI-1500 Sport automated analyser
- Osmotic fragility: haemolysis rate (HL, %) in 0.56% NaCl — packed cells washed 3× with ice-cold Dulbecco's PBS (pH 7.4, 279 mOsm/kg), centrifuged at 1,450g for 10 min; haemolysis measured as absorbance at 540 nm

**Statistics:** One-way ANOVA; Scott & Knott's cluster analysis for post-hoc multiple comparisons (P < 0.05); simple Pearson correlation for PCV–HL and [La–]–HL relationships.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

### Resting baseline values (no significant differences between tests)

| Variable   | Heavy exercise rest | Light exercise rest | Epinephrine rest |
| ---------- | ------------------- | ------------------- | ---------------- |
| PCV (%)    | 34.8 ± 0.53         | 34.3 ± 1.1          | 35.8 ± 0.86      |
| [La–] (mM) | 0.54 ± 0.25         | 0.62 ± 0.25         | 0.42 ± 0.17      |
| HL (%)     | 45.2 ± 3.2          | 46 ± 4.0            | 53 ± 5.0         |

### PCV responses

**Heavy exercise test:**

- Warm-up: PCV significantly increased >**45%** (from resting ~35%)
- Canter (8 m/s): PCV significantly increased >**58%**
- Cool-down: gradually decreased; returned to initial resting level

**Light exercise test:**

- Peak PCV from 3rd to final minute of trotting: ~**53%** (continued at peak through end of exercise); gradually decreased over 45 min post-exercise; returned to resting

**Epinephrine test:**

- 1 min post-injection: PCV significantly increased; peak at 3–5 min post-injection: >**50%**; gradually decreased 5–11 min; returned to resting

### Lactate [La–] responses

**Heavy exercise test:**

- Warm-up: [La–] slightly decreased
- Canter: significantly increased, >**4 mM** (causes sudden accumulation)
- Cool-down: gradually decreased

**Light exercise test:**

- 1st min: briefly increased; then decreased from 1st–7th min; increased again until exercise stopped; peak value **< 2 mM**; gradually decreased post-exercise; returned to resting

**Epinephrine test:**

- 5 min post-injection: significantly increased; peak value ~**0.9 mM** at 15–30 min; decreased 30–50 min; returned to resting

### Haemolysis rate (HL) responses

**Heavy exercise test:**

- Warm-up: HL significantly **decreased** (less than **40%**), i.e., −**24%** from resting
- Canter: HL significantly **increased** (more than **80%**), i.e., +**92%** from resting ~45%
- Cool-down: significantly decreased (less than **40%**), i.e., −**23%** from resting
- After cool-down: gradually increased over 80 min, then returned to initial resting level

**Light exercise test:**

- 2nd–7th min of trotting: HL significantly **decreased** (to ~**20%**), i.e., −**50%** from resting
- Continued low until 5 min after exercise stopped
- Gradually increased over 100 min; remained near initial resting level

**Epinephrine test:**

- Slight increase and decrease oscillations during 1st–15th min; no significant change; returned to initial resting level. **No significant effect on HL.**

Key interpretations:

- During heavy canter: PCV and [La–] both **positively** correlated with HL — higher PCV and higher LA accompany higher fragility.
- During warm-up: PCV negatively correlated with HL — PCV rises (splenic contraction) while HL falls (fragility decreases); these are dissociated.
- In epinephrine test: PCV rises ~50% (splenic contraction) but HL is **not** correlated — confirming that splenic red cell release per se does not increase fragility.
- Light exercise: PCV rise negatively correlated with HL — beneficial splenic mobilisation without acidosis; [La–] not correlated with HL (lactate stays low, not driving fragility).

## Discussion & interpretation

The key mechanistic finding is that splenic contraction (high PCV) alone — demonstrated by the epinephrine experiment — does not increase erythrocyte osmotic fragility. The fragility increase during heavy canter is caused by metabolic acidosis (high blood LA → lower pH → erythrocyte membrane injury). Two possible mechanisms: (1) lysolecithin released from the spleen into circulation during strong splenic contraction injures erythrocyte membranes; (2) lactate-driven decrease in blood pH directly injures erythrocyte membranes. The authors favour pH as primary, citing evidence that temperature and pH decreases increase osmotic fragility in vitro (Hiro 1982). Light exercise increases blood pH (ventilatory alkalinisation) and temperature in a way that reduces fragility. The decrease in HL during cool-down is attributed to selective haemolysis of the most fragile cells during the canter bout — those cells are lost, and the surviving cells are more resistant.

During warm-up: the epinephrine-like splenic contraction releases red cells; these are not intrinsically fragile (epinephrine experiment shows no HL change), and the mild aerobic exercise simultaneously decreases pH and increases blood temperature slightly — net effect is reduced fragility. The protective effect of warm-up on erythrocyte integrity is confirmed by prior data from splenectomised horses (Hanzawa et al. 1995) showing the same warm-up HL decrease — independent of splenic cells.

## Limitations

- n = 3 horses only; very small sample, high individual variation probable.
- Two 2-year-olds and one 8-year-old — age heterogeneity; 8-year-old may differ in baseline PCV and splenic function.
- All male horses; sex effect on splenic contraction or erythrocyte fragility not assessed.
- Fixed haemolysis threshold (0.56% NaCl) is a single-concentration assay; full osmotic fragility curve (0.2–1.0% NaCl) would give more information.
- HL measured in vitro after cold storage (up to 2 hr on ice); may not perfectly reflect in vivo fragility.
- The mechanism for the post-canter HL increase after cool-down (gradual return to resting HL over 80 min) is not fully explained — possibly methaemoglobin formation and Heinz body accumulation.

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `pcv_peak` — peak PCV during standardised treadmill exercise (%) — JRA treadmill test records — reflects splenic reserve and O2 delivery capacity; reference: ~53–58% at canter, ~35% at rest; higher peak PCV → better O2 transport
- `pcv_resting` — resting PCV (%) — routine blood work — baseline haematological status; resting ~35%; lower than normal may indicate poor health or sports anaemia
- `blood_lactate_heavy` — blood lactate during heavy canter segment (mmol/L) — JRA treadmill records — r = 0.958 vs. HL during canter; > 4 mmol/L = anaerobic threshold crossed; correlates with fragility and anaerobic capacity
- `warm_up_quality_flag` — binary, adequate pre-race warm-up performed — not in standard databases (race-day practice varies); if derivable from race morning workouts, could capture the −24% HL protective effect
- `pcv_change_exercise` — PCV(peak) − PCV(rest) = absolute splenic contribution (%) — treadmill records — larger splenic reserve → more O2 delivery margin; not the same as PCV–HL correlation (splenic release alone does not cause fragility)
- **DO NOT** confound PCV with fragility: high PCV alone (warm-up or epinephrine) is not a risk; high PCV combined with high LA (anaerobic canter) is associated with elevated fragility and potential sports anaemia
- `vla4` — velocity at [La–] = 4 mmol/L — treadmill test — confirmed as meaningful aerobic/anaerobic boundary: below VLA4, fragility decreases; above it, fragility increases; higher VLA4 = better aerobic fitness
