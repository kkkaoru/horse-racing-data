## Abstract (verbatim)

> The oxidant and antioxidant equilibrium is known to play an important role in equine medicine and equine exercise physiology. There are abundant findings in this field; however, not many studies have been conducted for reference ranges of oxidative stress biomarkers in horses. This study was conducted to determine the reference values of reactive oxygen metabolites (d-ROMs) and biological antioxidant potential (BAP) using blood samples from 372 (191 males, 181 females) Thoroughbred racehorse aged 2 to 5 (3.43 ± 1.10 (mean ± SD)) years old. There were obvious gender differences in oxidative biomarkers, and growth/age-related changes were observed especially in females. Gender and age must be considered when interpreting obtained oxidative stress biomarkers for diagnosis of disease or fitness alterations in Thoroughbred racehorses.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

This paper belongs to feature family **C (exercise physiology/fitness)**. It establishes the most comprehensive sex- and age-stratified reference ranges for reactive oxygen metabolites (d-ROMs), biological antioxidant potential (BAP), and the oxidative stress index (OSI = d-ROMs/BAP × 100) in JRA Thoroughbred racehorses — sampled at JRA Miho Training Center quarantine.

For the finishing-position pipeline, oxidative stress status is relevant as a **health condition indicator** with the following logic: horses under excessive training load, recovering from disease, or in sub-optimal health show elevated d-ROMs and/or depressed BAP. When OSI deviates upward from age-sex reference range at the time of training centre entry (or between races), that horse is at elevated risk of underperformance or scratch. The sex and age effects documented here are **essential for correct normalisation**:

- Females have significantly higher d-ROMs (+12.8 U.CARR; P<0.001) and lower BAP (−138 µmol/l; P<0.001) than males, resulting in lower OSI (16.1 vs. 18.3; P<0.001). A raw d-ROM value of 160 U.CARR is normal for a female 2yo but above the male mean — without sex-stratified normalisation, females would systematically appear "stressed" relative to males.
- Age effects differ by sex: females show the highest d-ROMs at ages 2–3, declining at 4–5; males show no significant age trend. This means an older mare with d-ROMs of 170 U.CARR is less "abnormal" than a 2yo filly with the same value.

These reference ranges also calibrate other blood-based features from this literature (CK, AST from 27_1604; post-exercise inflammatory markers from 27_1616) in the context of background oxidative status.

## Background & objective

Oxidant-antioxidant disequilibrium (oxidative stress, OS) is associated with diverse equine diseases: recurrent airway obstruction, exercise-induced pulmonary haemorrhage, equine grass sickness, motor neuron disease, pituitary pars intermedia dysfunction, endometritis, osteoarthritis, white muscle disease, post-anaesthetic myopathy, degenerative joint disease, and Rhodococcus equi pneumonia. The effect of exercise on oxidant-antioxidant balance has been studied for >20 years in horses.

Two validated biomarkers are now considered gold standards:

- **d-ROMs (reactive oxygen metabolites):** colorimetric method; hydroperoxide reacts with N,N-diethyl-p-phenylenediamine to form a chromogen radical cation [A-NH₂⁻]⁺. Units: U.CARR (Carratelli units). Validated by comparison with ESR direct measurement of unpaired electrons.
- **BAP (biological antioxidant potential):** thiocyanate colorimetric method reflecting Fe³⁺ → Fe²⁺ reduction potency (includes uric acid, ascorbic acid, proteins, α-tocopherol, bilirubin). Units: µmol/l. Validated vs. hydroxyl radical scavenging activity by ESR.
- **OSI = d-ROMs / BAP × 100** (arbitrary units). Reflects the ratio of oxidant load to antioxidant capacity.

Both measured using FREE carpediem free radical analyser (Wismerll, Tokyo). At time of this study, only a few small-population reference studies (Celi et al. n=15; Po et al. n=10 foals) existed; this study is the largest (n=372) and first age-sex-stratified reference for JRA Thoroughbreds.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 372 clinically healthy Thoroughbred racehorses: 191 males, 181 females. Age 2–5 years, mean 3.43 ± 1.10 years (males 47.4 ± 12.0 months; females 47.0 ± 12.1 months). No significant difference in age or transport category distribution between sexes (χ² test NS).

**Collection protocol:** Blood collected at quarantine on entry to Miho Training Center, JRA, January–December 2011. All horses transported via horse van; categorised by transport duration:
| Category | Duration | Distance | n males | n females |
|----------|----------|----------|---------|---------|
| Short | ~1 hr | ~30 km | 97 (50.8%) | 96 (53.0%) |
| Medium | ~4 hr | ~350 km | 55 (28.8%) | 44 (24.3%) |
| Long | ~12–20 hr | ~690 km | 39 (20.4%) | 41 (22.7%) |

Rest period before sampling: ≥3 hr for short transport; ≥7 hr for medium/long transport. Body temperature measured before blood draw; horses with T ≥38.5°C or abnormal clinical findings excluded. Blood by jugular venipuncture into heparinised Vacutainers; plasma by centrifugation (1,500 × g, 10 min); stored at −80°C.

**Statistical methods:**

- Sex comparison: unpaired t-test
- Age comparison within sex: one-way ANOVA (post-hoc comparisons; specific pair annotations in Table 3)
- Transport time interaction: two-way ANOVA (age × transport time; main effects and interaction)
- Reference ranges: mean ± 2SD (normal distribution); median with 10th–90th percentile for OSI (non-normal distribution)
- Software: JMP v11.0.0 (SAS Institute)

### Transport time effects

Two-way ANOVA revealed a **slight tendency for BAP to rise as transportation time increased** (significant main effect of transport time on BAP). No significant interaction between age and transport time for any marker. Authors note this as a potential confound when using BAP as a condition indicator.

### Comparison with prior studies

- Po et al. 2013 (n=10 Thoroughbred foals): d-ROMs 286 ± 24 U.CARR; BAP 3,403 ± 124 µmol/l; OSI 8.4 ± 0.5. No sex differences discussed (small n).
- Celi et al. 2010 (n=15 horses, 10 geldings + 5 mares): d-ROMs 358 ± 37 U.CARR; BAP 2,533 ± 66 µmol/l. No age/sex stratification.

The JRA Thoroughbred racehorses in this study show markedly lower d-ROMs and higher BAP than the Celi/Po foal values — consistent with age, training state, and population differences.

## Discussion & interpretation

The key finding — that females show higher d-ROMs and lower BAP than males — is consistent with sex differences in oxidative stress reported in humans, bovine tissue, and Ukrainian Warmblood sport horses (Andriichuk et al. 2014). Proposed mechanisms include: (1) regulation of antioxidative enzymes by sex steroid hormones (Pajović & Saicić 2008); (2) differences in antioxidant defence of XX vs. XY cells (Marino et al. 2011); (3) possible differences in emotional/temperament character (females described as more "pessimistic" per Momozawa et al. 2007 — cited but not a primary explanation).

The age trajectory in females (d-ROMs highest at 2–3yo, declining at 4yo; BAP declining at 4–5yo; OSI peaking at 4yo) is complex and not fully explained. The authors speculate that hormonal changes during sexual maturation and training adaptation interact. The lack of age effects in males suggests a more stable oxidative status in male Thoroughbreds across the 2–5 year age range.

The slight BAP increase with longer transportation time is a methodological confound: longer transport = more standing exercise → slight increase in antioxidant mobilisation? Authors recommend standardised rest periods (used in this study: ≥7 hr for long transport) to minimise this effect.

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional design: one measurement per horse at quarantine entry; no within-horse longitudinal data.
- All samples taken at Miho Training Center quarantine (eastern Japan); horses from Ritto (western Japan) or other regions may differ.
- Samples collected over one calendar year (2011 only); seasonal effects not reported.
- No outcome linkage: d-ROMs/BAP not linked to subsequent race performance or injury.
- Transport time was categorised as short/medium/long based on approximate origin distance; actual vehicle time not recorded precisely.
- Some horses may have had recent vaccinations or minor health events not captured by clinical screening, which could affect oxidative markers.

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `d_roms_u_carr` — continuous (raw) — source: JRA Miho quarantine bloodwork — expected effect: above age-sex reference range → elevated disease/overtraining burden → negative predictor of performance; below reference range → lower metabolic stress
- `bap_umol_l` — continuous (raw) — source: same — expected effect: below age-sex reference range → reduced antioxidant defence → higher OS; negative predictor of performance
- `osi_index` — d-ROMs / BAP × 100 — derived — expected effect: OSI > upper reference range → health/fitness concern; non-linear relationship with performance (moderate OS = hormesis, very high = risk)
- `d_roms_sex_age_zscore` — standardised: (individual − age-sex mean) / SD — derived from Table 3 values — expected effect: positive z-score indicates elevated oxidative burden relative to same-age, same-sex peers; more actionable than raw value
- `bap_sex_age_zscore` — analogous z-score for BAP — derived — expected effect: negative z-score (below-average antioxidant capacity) → elevated OS risk
- `transport_time_category` — categorical: short / medium / long — source: training centre records — use as a **covariate / confounder** when interpreting BAP, NOT as a feature in finishing-position models
- Interaction: `d_roms_zscore × age × sex` — female age 2–3yo shows highest absolute d-ROMs; 4yo females show relative decline; males stable — age-sex interaction required for correct normalisation
- `os_above_reference_flag` — binary: d-ROMs > mean+2SD for age-sex group — derived — expected effect: high-specificity flag for elevated stress state; positive predictor of scratch or sub-par performance in near-term races
- **Do NOT use** raw d-ROMs values across sexes without age-sex normalisation; females will systematically appear more stressed than males at comparable health levels
