## Abstract (verbatim)

> Knowledge about changes in the osmotic fragility of erythrocytes during exercise in athletic horses which is introduced in this review can be summarized as follows; 1) Changes in the osmotic fragility of equine erythrocytes during exercise are an important indicator of intravascular haemolysis. 2) The sensitivity of the regulatory volume is decreased by a K-Cl cotransporter, lactate and/or pH susceptibility, and the osmotic fragility of erythroid membrane due to exercise stress increases with the repeated accumulation of red cells in the spleen. 3) The increase in blood flow and the release of erythrocytes from the spleen into the circulation have little effect on the changes in the osmotic fragility of erythrocytes during exercise. 4) Increases in blood pH and temperature caused by aerobic exercises cause osmotic resistance in erythrocytes. 5) The decrease in pH and increase in blood lactate and peroxide caused by anaerobic exercises promotes the osmotic fragility of erythrocytes. 6) The osmotic fragility of erythrocytes could be detected by the modification, degeneration, decomposition and oxidation-reduction in cell membrane lipids and proteins, and cell volume regulation, changes which follow pH and temperature changes during exercise. 7) The K-Cl cotransporter which controls the intracellular ionic homeostasis and regulatory volume decrease has a functional interrelation with the fragility of erythrocytes. The osmotic fragility of erythrocytes in athletic horses is therefore an indicator of the effects of training and/or athletic performance and stress.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **C (exercise-physiology/fitness)**. This review synthesises a programme of Thoroughbred-specific research by the Tokyo University of Agriculture group (principally the companion papers docids 9:107, 10_3,4_61, 10_3,4_67) into a unified picture of erythrocyte osmotic fragility (EOF) as a validated blood marker of training adaptation and aerobic/anaerobic stress in racehorses.

The core finding for prediction is that EOF at a given speed has opposite signs depending on exercise intensity: aerobic work (≤9 m/s) decreases EOF (improving osmotic resistance — a marker of good aerobic fitness), while anaerobic work (≥13 m/s) increases EOF (indicating membrane damage and intravascular haemolysis). Blood pH is the single strongest correlate of EOF (r = −0.85 to −0.88, P < 0.01 in arterial and venous blood respectively). The combination of pH and temperature gives r = 0.93 (P < 0.01). This places EOF mechanistically upstream of EIPH, exertional rhabdomyolysis (tying-up), and orthopaedic injury — all performance-limiting conditions.

For a finishing-position pipeline, this review validates the use of pre-race blood pH, lactate, haematocrit, and PCV as fitness/form features, and links these to the physiological mechanism of oxygen-transport capacity. It also establishes that horses trained predominantly aerobically show progressive EOF decrease during aerobic work — meaning resting EOF level after a training phase is itself an indicator of aerobic adaptation.

The review further clarifies that: **splenic RBC release per se has little direct effect on resting EOF**, but **repeated splenic storage cycles progressively degrade RBC membrane quality** — this mechanistic detail explains why horses that have been intensively raced without adequate recovery show degraded haematological profiles.

## Background & objective

Conflicting prior reports existed: Boucher et al. reported exercise increased EOF in Thoroughbreds via splenic release of fragile echinocytes; Smith et al. reported EOF decreased. This review by Hanzawa & Watanabe synthesises approximately 78 cited references and their own programme of treadmill and field experiments to resolve the discrepancy. The resolution is that exercise intensity determines the direction of EOF change: light/aerobic → EOF decreases; heavy/anaerobic → EOF increases. Splenic release is incidental rather than causal.

## Materials & methods

Narrative review synthesising the following primary data sources (approximately 78 references):

- Hanzawa et al. series: treadmill experiments at various intensities in Thoroughbreds, n typically 4–8 per study
- Sakurai et al. (1967): Thoroughbred field exercises at 6.7, 11.1, 12.5 m/s
- Geor et al. (1994): incremental treadmill in Thoroughbreds
- Smith et al. (1989): brief high-speed treadmill in Quarterhorses and Thoroughbreds
- Own unpublished data on splenectomised Thoroughbreds and lactate dose-response

Key measurements across studies: EOF expressed as haemolysis rate (HL₀.₅₆) in 0.56% NaCl hypotonic solution; haematocrit (PCV); MCV; MCHC; blood pH; PCO₂; HCO₃⁻; blood lactate; temperature; plasma haemoglobin and haptoglobin; K-Cl cotransport activity.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

**Exercise intensity and EOF (Table 1 from paper — comparison across research groups):**

| Source                                 | Exercise level | MCV      | MCHC      | Echinocytes | EOF      |
| -------------------------------------- | -------------- | -------- | --------- | ----------- | -------- |
| Boucher et al. refs 4–6, 45            | High           | Decrease | Increase  | Increase    | Increase |
| Geor et al. / Smith et al. refs 18, 68 | High           | Increase | Decrease  | No change   | Decrease |
| Hanzawa et al. refs 25–29              | Low            | Decrease | No change | No change   | Decrease |
| Hanzawa et al. refs 25–29              | High           | Increase | Decrease  | No change   | Increase |

**Resolution of discrepancy:** The direction of EOF change depends on exercise intensity. Aerobic exercise (≤9 m/s field sprint, or warm-up at 4 m/s / trot at 5 m/s) decreases EOF; anaerobic exercise (≥13 m/s or ≥15 m/s field sprint for 800 m, or cantering/galloping at 3–4 incremental speeds to fatigue) increases EOF.

**Sequential treadmill protocol results (Hanzawa et al. [27]):**

- Warm-up at 2 m/s → 4 m/s → 2 m/s (0% incline): HL₀.₅₆ significantly decreased
- Cantering at 8 m/s for 8 min (10% incline): HL₀.₅₆ significantly increased
- Cooling down at 4–2 m/s for 20 min (0% incline): HL₀.₅₆ significantly decreased, then gradually returned toward initial resting level
- Continuous trotting at 5 m/s for 15 min (0% incline): HL₀.₅₆ significantly decreased

**Incremental exercise with three 5-min periods at 80–120% VLA4 (Hanzawa et al. [26]):** During rest periods between bouts, HL₀.₅₆ was decreased by exercise; HL₀.₅₆ increased with increasing speed and had an upward tendency as exercise intensified.

**pH as the dominant correlate of EOF (Hanzawa et al. [23]):**

- 10 physiological variables examined simultaneously against HL₀.₅₆ during treadmill exercise in Thoroughbreds (exercise at 30% or 105% VO₂max to fatigue)
- Simple correlation: r = −0.85 (arterial blood, P < 0.01) and r = −0.88 (mixed venous blood, P < 0.01)
- Multiple regression: PCO₂, HCO₃⁻, blood pH, and temperature all showed significant correlations
- Multi-correlation coefficient of pH + temperature combined: r = 0.93 (arterial) and r = 0.92 (venous), both P < 0.01
- In vitro: correlation between medium pH and HL₀.₅₆: r = −0.93 (P < 0.01) (Hanzawa et al. [22])

**Splenic RBC fragility vs. circulating RBC fragility:**

- Splenic RBC haemolysis rate: 81 ± 8.9% HL (from 10_3,4_61 data cited in this review)
- This is significantly higher than circulating erythrocytes before splenectomy
- After splenectomy, circulating RBC HL decreased significantly by month 2 and remained lower through month 8
- Intravenous adrenaline injection (2.5 µg/kg body weight): significantly increased haematocrit in peripheral blood, but had **little effect** on HL₀.₅₆ — confirming splenic release per se is not the cause of changed fragility

**Lactate dose-response (unpublished data cited in review):**

- In normal horses: HL₀.₅₆ increased in concentration-dependent fashion when lactate was used for cell treatment in range 0.15625–10 mM
- In splenectomised horses: HL₀.₅₆ also increased concentration-dependently, but was significantly lower than in normal horses at all lactate concentrations

**Aerobic vs. anaerobic training groups (Hanzawa et al. [24]):**

- Group Aer and Group Ana tested at field sprints (800 m on sand track)
- VLA4 in Group Ana was slightly higher than Group Aer
- Group Ana showed progressive EOF decrease during aerobic exercise — interpreted as better aerobic adaptation
- Group Aer: increase in blood lactate during aerobic work not associated with EOF changes

**Summary table (Table 2 from paper):**

| Variable          | Aerobic exercise  | Anaerobic exercise |
| ----------------- | ----------------- | ------------------ |
| Cell volume       | Decrease          | Increase           |
| Density           | Decrease          | Increase           |
| Echinocyte        | Small increase    | No change          |
| Blood pH          | No change or rise | Fall               |
| Blood temperature | Rise              | Rise               |
| Fragility (EOF)   | Decrease          | Increase           |

**Intravascular haemolysis marker:** Plasma haemoglobin bound to haptoglobin increased in parallel with EOF increase after maximal exercise on a sand track, single high-intensity exercise, and three repetitive loads of incremental exercise on a treadmill — confirming EOF is a clinical indicator of intravascular haemolysis during exercise.

**K-Cl cotransporter findings:**

- Horse RBCs have high intracellular K⁺ and a high-capacity K-Cl cotransporter sensitive to cell volume, pH, and PO₂
- Lower external pH and/or osmotic pressure → cotransporter stimulated → volume decrease → denser cells
- Higher external pH and/or osmotic pressure → cotransporter inhibited → volume increase
- Increase in cell density associated with progressive increase in K-Cl cotransport activity AND decrease in HL₀.₅₆ — cotransporter provides a partial buffer against exercise-induced swelling and fragility

**Polycythaemia in fully trained racehorses:** Cited literature indicates polycythaemia (higher circulating RBC mass) is more common than anaemia in fully trained racehorses, associated with lower resting fragility (better O₂ transport).

## Discussion & interpretation

The review establishes a mechanistic hierarchy: exercise intensity → blood pH/temperature change → EOF change. Aerobic exercise raises pH (via hyperventilation-driven CO₂ washout) and temperature, shrinking erythrocytes and increasing membrane stability. Anaerobic exercise lowers pH (via lactate and CO₂ accumulation), swells erythrocytes via KCl cotransporter inhibition, and damages membrane lipids via oxidative stress (hydrogen peroxide, malondialdehyde depleting alpha-tocopherol, ascorbate, reduced glutathione, uric acid).

The splenic role is indirect: the spleen does not cause acute fragility changes during exercise by releasing cells, but **repeated storage-and-release cycles progressively increase membrane fragility** by depleting ATP, lowering intracellular pH, and altering membrane lipids in stored cells.

The clinical link to EIPH, tying-up, and orthopaedic disease via echinocytosis (ATP-depleted cells) from the spleen is a hypothesis proposed by Boucher; the authors regard the evidence as indirect. However, the echinocytosis–EIPH hypothesis is clinically relevant as a scratch-risk model even under uncertainty.

Haemolysis with exercise is described as having an **active, beneficial function** — it rejuvenates the erythrocyte pool by removing old cells and triggering production of young, more oxygen-efficient cells. Sports anaemia is actually uncommon in well-trained racehorses; polycythaemia is more typical.

## Limitations

- Review mixes species (human, dog, rat, horse); species-specific findings are clearly labelled but interpretation requires care
- Primary studies typically n = 4–8 Thoroughbreds; replication in larger cohorts not available at time of writing
- EOF measurement (HL₀.₅₆) requires specialised laboratory equipment; not available from routine JRA/NAR veterinary records
- The aerobic/anaerobic threshold definition (9–13 m/s) varies by individual horse fitness; the threshold is not a fixed species constant
- Unpublished data are cited for some critical findings (lactate dose-response in splenectomised horses)

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `pre_race_blood_pH` — blood pH at rest or 30 min before race — source: JRA/NAR veterinary panel data if available — expected effect: higher pH → lower EOF → better aerobic condition → positive for performance; directly supported by r = −0.85/−0.88 with EOF — data availability: NOT in standard race records; would require direct sampling programme
- `pre_race_PCV` — packed cell volume (%) — source: veterinary panel or post-race blood draw — expected effect: higher PCV → more O₂-carrying capacity → positive for performance; interaction with distance and surface — data availability: same limitation as pH
- `pre_race_blood_lactate` — resting or warm-up blood lactate (mmol/L) — source: veterinary panel — expected effect: lower resting lactate → better aerobic base → positive for performance — data availability: not standard
- `training_intensity_proxy` — categorical: predominantly aerobic (trot/canter-based) vs. anaerobic (high-speed gallop-based) recent training, if derivable from workout logs — expected effect: aerobic-trained horses expected to show lower EOF and better form — data availability: indirectly from JRA training records
- `plasma_haptoglobin_bound_Hb` — indicator of intravascular haemolysis post-race — source: post-race blood panel — expected effect: high values indicate recent heavy anaerobic effort and possible membrane damage; useful for recovery-state modelling — data availability: research only
- CAUTION: EOF itself (HL₀.₅₆) is not derivable from any standard race or training record; all features above require dedicated blood-sampling programmes. The value of this review is establishing the mechanistic rationale for using pH/PCV/lactate when such data become available.
- `consecutive_high_intensity_races` — count of recent races run at anaerobic pace (approximated by fast pace times) — derivation: from race times and distance; horses finishing in front third at high pace → anaerobic effort → cumulative RBC membrane degradation — effect: increasing count → rising scratch risk and form regression
