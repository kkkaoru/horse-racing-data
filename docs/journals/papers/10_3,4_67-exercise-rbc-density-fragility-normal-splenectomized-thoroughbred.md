## Abstract (verbatim)

> Changes in cell density and osmotic fragility of erythrocytes during exercise were determined in 3 control (Group C) and 3 splenectomized (Group S) horses that performed an incremental exercise test on a treadmill until the point of fatigue. Venous blood samples were drawn before the start of the treadmill program, and immediately after the warm-up and the incremental exercise test. After the incremental exercise in Group C, red cells in both groups were swelled by washing with PBS regardless of exercise. Warm-up did not change the density of fractionated red cells that were separated by the method of Murphy in both groups of horses. In contrast, incremental exercise significantly increased the density of cells in Group C but not in Group S. The osmotic fragility of fractionated red cells was significantly lower in Group S than in Group C horses regardless of exercise. Warm-up significantly decreased the fragility, but incremental exercise significantly increased it in all fractionated erythrocytes from both groups. There are two possible explanations for the above phenomena: 1) Erythrocytes which are released from the spleen by strong contraction during heavy exercise show a stronger tendency to regulatory volume decrease which shows osmotic sensitivity, but not during light exercise; and/or 2) The sensitivity of the regulatory volume decreases and membrane fragility versus exercise stress increases with the repeated accumulation of erythrocytes in the spleen.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **C (exercise-physiology/fitness)**. This companion paper to docid 10_3,4_61 uses the same 6-horse splenectomy cohort but extends the analysis to erythrocyte density fractionation, providing a more granular picture of how the spleen selectively mobilises higher-density, physiologically more active RBCs during maximal exercise in Thoroughbreds.

The key findings for prediction are:

1. **Intact horses show 14% higher peak running speed** (10.3 vs. 8.8 m/s) than splenectomised horses — confirming O₂ transport capacity as a primary performance gate (consistent with docid 10_3,4_61).
2. **Intact horses' EC, PCV, and Hb are 30–40% higher post-exercise** than splenectomised (EC 14.2 vs. 10.8 × 10⁶/µl; PCV 56.1 vs. 39.7%; Hb 20.8 vs. 17.9 g/dl), providing quantitative thresholds for what constitutes adequate O₂-carrying capacity.
3. **Splenectomised horses run slower but show higher maximum HR** (219.7 vs. 203.7 bpm) — heart rate at a given speed is inversely related to aerobic capacity; a horse using a higher % of its cardiac output at a given speed is less fit.
4. **Blood lactate was similar between groups** (8.7 ± 0.9 vs. 10.1 ± 0.7 mmol/L at last incremental step) — again confirming lactate alone does not differentiate capacity.

These findings directly validate the use of PCV, EC (erythrocyte count), and Hb concentration as fitness-related performance features, and establish that heart rate at a given speed is a more sensitive capacity indicator than lactate.

## Background & objective

The companion paper (10_3,4_61) had shown that splenectomy reduces peak speed and abolishes exercise-induced PCV increase, but did not examine the quality (density distribution) of the RBCs mobilised by the spleen. Earlier work (Hanzawa et al. 1995) had found heavy exercise increased erythrocyte density in normal but not splenectomised horses. Geor et al. (1994) reported the opposite (less dense cells after exercise), attributed to measurement methodology differences. This paper aims to resolve the discrepancy and to characterise which density fraction of splenic RBCs is preferentially mobilised during maximal exercise.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** Same 6 Thoroughbreds as docid 10_3,4_61 (5 two-year-olds, 1 five-year-old; 3 males, 1 gelding, 2 females; bodyweight 456 ± 15 kg). Group C: 3 sham-operated (18th rib removal only). Group S: 3 splenectomised.

**Acclimation:** Treadmill 5 days/week for 1 month before exercise tests; 15 min exercise at 7% incline including 1–2 min submaximal running at 7–10 m/s.

**Exercise protocol:**

1. Warm-up: 4 min at 4 m/s (0% incline)
2. Rest: 5 min (0% incline)
3. Walk: 4 min at 1.5 m/s
4. Trot: 2 min at 4.4 m/s
5. Canter/gallop: incremental speeds to peak speed at 7% incline
   - Group C: 1.5 min at 80% PS, 1 min at 90% PS, 1 min at 100% PS (total 3 steps)
   - Group S: 1.5 min at 70% PS, 1 min at 80% PS, 1 min at 90% PS, 1 min at 100% PS (total 4 steps)
   - Speed increased by 5% PS every minute until fatigue
   - Individual peak speed (PS) determined in preliminary test (horse-specific)

**Blood sampling:** Jugular venepuncture at 30 min pre-treadmill (rest), within 30 sec of warm-up end, within 30 sec of incremental exercise end.

**Analytical methods:**

- Erythrocyte count (EC): Toma-Zeiss blood cell counting plate
- PCV: microhaematocrit centrifuge
- Haemoglobin (Hb): cyanmethaemoglobin method
- MCV, MCH, MCHC: calculated from EC, PCV, Hb
- MCVrc and MCHCrc (of washed cells): blood centrifuged at 1450g for 10 min, plasma and buffy coat discarded, packed cells washed 3 times with ice-cold Dulbecco's PBS (pH 7.4, 279 mOsm/kg)
- Erythrocyte density fractionation: Murphy method — erythrocyte resuspension (8 ml packed cells + 2 ml autologous plasma) centrifuged at 20,000g for 1 hr at 30°C; packed mass divided into 6 equal fractions from top (fraction 1) to bottom (fraction 6) by careful aspiration; each fraction washed 3× with ice-cold PBS
- Erythrocyte density (ED, g/ml): continuous Percoll gradient (75.6% Percoll solution, pH 7.5; modified Wolowyk's method described in Hanzawa et al. [9])
- Osmotic fragility (HL): haemolysis rate in 0.56% NaCl

**Statistics:** Splenectomy effects: Student's t-test. Pre/post-wash differences in MCV/MCHC: paired t-test. Exercise effects and fraction differences: one-way and two-way ANOVA; multiple comparisons by LSD (P < 0.05).

**Erythrocyte density (ED) fractions (Fig. 1):**

- Both groups: increasing fraction number (1 top → 6 bottom) associated with progressively increasing ED
- Warm-up: **no change in ED of any fraction in either group**
- Incremental exercise: **ED significantly increased in Group C**, particularly in fractions 3–6; **no change in Group S**
- After exercise: ED in Group S **significantly lower** than Group C, especially fractions 4–6

**MCHCrc of density fractions (Fig. 2):**

- Increasing fraction number → progressive increase in MCHCrc in both groups
- Warm-up: **no change in MCHCrc of any fraction in either group**
- Incremental exercise: MCHCrc significantly increased in fractions 3–6 of Group C; no change in Group S
- After exercise: MCHCrc in Group S significantly lower than Group C in fractions 4–6

**Osmotic fragility (HL) of density fractions (Fig. 3):**

- Increasing fraction number → progressive **decrease** in HL (denser cells are more osmotically resistant) — both groups
- HL of **all fractions significantly lower in Group S than Group C** regardless of exercise level
- Both groups: warm-up → HL significantly decreased in all fractions; incremental exercise → HL significantly increased in all fractions
- **Increases in HL from resting level caused by incremental exercise in Group C were greater than those of Group S** in all fractions
- "These increases in HL with exercise were greater than the differences in HL among the fractions" — exercise effect size exceeded density-fraction effect size

**PBS washing effects:**

- After incremental exercise in Group C: erythrocytes did **not** swell on washing (MCVrc not elevated above pre-wash MCV after exercise in Group C)
- In Group S: erythrocytes consistently swelled after washing regardless of exercise condition
- Interpretation: RBCs released from spleen during exercise in Group C are not swollen; in Group S, the remaining circulating cells are susceptible to osmotic swelling

**Mechanistic interpretations:**

- The larger cells released from the spleen into circulation in Group C during exercise (as indicated by increased ED and MCHCrc in fractions 3–6 post-exercise) show a stronger tendency toward regulatory volume decrease — they are osmotically sensitive
- Two non-exclusive mechanisms:
  1. Splenic-pooled RBCs deplete ATP, lower intracellular pH, alter membrane lipids → on release, show heightened sensitivity to osmotic challenge
  2. Sensitivity of regulatory volume decrease is permanently increased by repeated accumulation in spleen

## Discussion & interpretation

The central discrepancy with Geor et al. (1994) — who reported less dense cells after exercise — is attributed to methodological differences: Geor used blood adjusted to a fixed PCV of 30% with autologous plasma and measured MCV in glutaraldehyde-fixed cells, whereas this study measured ED directly by Percoll gradient in unadjusted blood. This paper's finding (denser cells post-exercise in intact horses) is consistent with the hypothesis that denser splenic cells are released during maximal effort.

The Group S data show that without splenic RBC delivery, EC and PCV barely change during exercise, and despite this, lactate profiles are identical — confirming that lactate concentration at fatigue is determined by exercise intensity (% of capacity) rather than absolute O₂ delivery capacity. A horse can reach peak lactate concentrations at lower absolute speeds if its O₂ ceiling is lower.

The clinical implication is that any condition that impairs splenic function (chronic splenic disease, dehydration-induced reduced splenic responsiveness, or pharmacological splenic inhibition) would reduce O₂-carrying capacity to exercise and directly cap peak running speed — with a ~14% performance ceiling reduction based on this experiment.

## Limitations

- n = 6 (3 per group); same limitations as docid 10_3,4_61
- Density measurement by Murphy method at 20,000g/1hr/30°C; different from other density-gradient methods (arabinogalactan, Percoll step gradient) used in the Geor et al. comparison study — methodology is a source of discrepancy
- PBS washing at lower osmotic pressure than plasma (279 vs. ~290 mOsm/kg) causes cell swelling artefact; the MCVrc/MCHCrc values are affected by this, though the authors account for this in their analysis
- Operations performed at least 2 months before exercise tests — any acute post-splenectomy compensation is not captured
- All horses young (2–5 years old); splenic function and RBC mobilisation capacity may differ in older horses

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `post_exercise_PCV` — PCV immediately post-gallop (within 30 sec) — source: veterinary sampling after trial run — expected effect: Group C equivalent horses (intact, fit spleen) should reach 50–56% PCV; values below 45% suggest impaired splenic response — data availability: research/veterinary context; not standard race records
- `post_exercise_EC` — erythrocyte count post-exercise — derivation: as above — expected magnitude in fit intact horses: ~14 × 10⁶/µl; in splenectomised or compromised horses: ~10–11 × 10⁶/µl — effect: directly proportional to peak running speed based on this experiment
- `post_exercise_Hb` — haemoglobin concentration post-exercise — expected range: 20–21 g/dl in intact fit horses at peak effort; 17–18 g/dl in splenectomised — data availability: as PCV
- `HR_at_PS` — heart rate at individual peak speed in trial runs — derivation: Polar-type HR monitor during workout — expected effect: ~204 bpm in intact fit Thoroughbreds; 220 bpm or higher suggests reduced O₂-delivery capacity (compensation); lower bpm at same speed indicates better fitness — data availability: JRA monitors HR in some training contexts
- `resting_PCV_trend` — trend across serial resting PCV measurements in training — expected effect: rising trend → improving erythropoiesis and fitness; declining trend → overtraining, illness — data availability: veterinary periodic testing
- `erythrocyte_density_fraction_ratio` — ratio of dense fractions (3–6) to light fractions (1–2) from Murphy centrifugation — derivation: specialised laboratory analysis — expected effect: after exercise, intact horse should show increased dense fraction; absence suggests impaired splenic RBC quality — data availability: research only; not feasible for routine screening
- CAUTION: `blood_lactate_at_fatigue` cannot be used alone as a performance measure — identical lactate at fatigue is compatible with very different peak speeds (8.8 vs. 10.3 m/s in this study). Always pair lactate with the speed at which it was measured.
- Interaction term: `age × post_exercise_PCV` — younger horses (2yo) may have less fully developed splenic reserve than older horses; the 5-year-old in Group C likely had the highest absolute splenic contribution
