## Abstract (verbatim)

> An interesting aspect of athlete horse management is connected with the effects of the profuse sweating required to maintain body temperature. The various aspects related to thermoregulation are discussed, with special reference to water and electrolyte losses. The effects of the sporadic or systematic administration of rehydrating supplements were examined in four trotters aged 3–4 years used in competitions. The horses performed three exercise tests simulating a 2,000 m race. At the time of the second and third test, the control diet (Test 1) also included a rehydrating supplement which subjects had been receiving for one day (Test 2) or 12 days (Test 3). Subjects were weighed (at rest, after the race and 24 hr later) and blood samples were collected (at rest, immediately after the race; 20 min after the race, and 24 hr thereafter). Changes in weight and in blood parameters (pH, packed cell volume (PCV), lactic acid, Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻, lactate dehydrogenase (LDH), aspartate aminotransferase (AST), glutathione peroxidase (GSH-Px), antioxidant power (AOP) indicate a favourable effect of rehydrating supplements on muscle condition, especially when administered systematically.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family: **C (exercise-physiology / fitness) and E (environment / management / nutrition)**. This study documents the blood-based exercise performance and recovery markers — lactic acid, PCV, Na⁺/K⁺/Cl⁻, LDH, AST, antioxidant power, and GSH-Px — measured before, during, and after simulated 2,000 m trot races in Italian Standardbred trotters. While the sample is very small (n=4 horses) and the species is standardbred trotters rather than JRA Thoroughbred/NAR gallopers, the biochemical markers described are generalisable performance-physiology indicators.

Specific features motivated: (1) **blood lactate** — slower clearance (elevated AE₂ at 24 h in Test 1) signals fatigue/poor aerobic metabolism; (2) **plasma Na⁺/Cl⁻ dysregulation at rest** — in Test 1 without supplementation, resting Na⁺ (158.25 mmol/l) and Cl⁻ (115.75 mmol/l) significantly exceeded normal ranges, suggesting pre-existing electrolyte imbalance that correlates with worse performance and muscle recovery; (3) **LDH and AST** — rise attenuated and delayed in systematic supplementation (Test 3), indicating reduced muscle damage; (4) **GSH-Px and AOP** — antioxidant markers that reflect cumulative training-load and recovery quality.

Interactions with pipeline features: `days_since_last_race` (recovery interval affects electrolyte balance), `race_distance` (2,000 m trot = ~2 min submaximal; 1,200 m sprint may show different pattern), `season / temperature` (study conducted at 25°C in September; heat stress amplifies electrolyte losses), 馬体重変化 (body weight loss ~2% per race reflects dehydration state).

Limitations for direct application: n=4, standardbred trotters not Thoroughbreds, Italian private stable not JRA/NAR, 2,000 m trot not 1,200/1,600/2,400 m gallop. The marker patterns are indicative rather than definitive for Japanese racing.

## Background & objective

Horses lose 6–10 l of sweat per 1,600–3,200 m race, and up to 35–40 l in a 5-h endurance race. Sweat is rich in Na⁺, Cl⁻, and K⁺ (isosmotic unlike human sweat), so fluid losses cause electrolyte depletion, not just dehydration. A dehydration of ≥2% body weight impairs performance in humans; similar effects are expected in horses. Under dehydration, both muscle perfusion and skin cooling deteriorate. Electrolyte deficiency impairs muscle cell function. The voluntary water intake mechanism is weakened by plasma hypotonicity: horses drink only ~60% of water lost. Competition trotters in Italy are occasionally given electrolyte-containing rehydrating supplements, but systematic evidence of benefit was lacking. The study aimed to quantify the effect of sporadic (1-day) vs. systematic (12-day) supplementation on blood markers of exercise performance, muscle damage, and oxidative stress.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 4 standardbred horses (2 male, 2 female; body weight 412–450 kg; age 3–4 years); belonging to Terre Reggiane private stable, Reggio Emilia, Italy. All clinically normal, in active racing activities, trained 3–4 times/week with paddock time on other days. Study conducted September, ambient temperature 24–25°C. Approved by Bologna University Faculty Ethics Committee.

**Control diet:** grass hay 5.5 ± 0.5 kg + 2.5 kg complete feed + 4.5 kg flaked oats/barley/carob mix = 11.5 kg DM, 1.2 kg crude protein, 125 MJ DE. Na 16 g/d, K 110 g/d, Cl 37 g/d. Fed 3 times/day; water ad libitum.

**Electrolyte supplement (Test 2 & 3):** Commercial compound at 2 kg/100 kg feed, supplying: vitamin C 400 mg/head/day, vitamin E 200 mg/head/day, Na 4.0 g/head/day (as NaCl + Na phosphate), K 5.2 g/head/day (as KCl), Cl 8.6 g/head/day.

**Experimental design:**

- **Test 1 (Day 1):** Control diet only
- **Test 2 (Day 6):** Control diet + supplement for 1 day (from Day 5)
- **Test 3 (Day 16):** Control diet + supplement for 12 days (from Day 5 through Day 16)

**Exercise test:** Warm-up: 10 min trotting at 250 m/min + 10 min rest. Then simulated 2,000 m trot race on field track; speed recorded by Bauman-Haldi Equus Puls system. All test days: sun, 25°C, dry track.

**Body weight:** At rest before warm-up (Wt₁), after race (Wt₂), 24 h later (Wt₃).

**Blood sampling schedule:**

- AR: at rest before warm-up
- AE₀: immediately after race
- AE₁: 20 min after race
- AE₂: 24 h after race

**Blood measurements:**

- pH (portable pH-meter, whole blood, immediate)
- PCV (capillary centrifugation, 12,000 rpm)
- GSH-Px (RANSEL, UV, 37°C, Paglia and Valentine method; U/ml PCV; lysate prepared immediately)
- Total protein, AST, LDH (Du Pont Dimension system, 37°C)
- AOP, antioxidant power (PAO kit — Cu²⁺ reduction to Cu⁺; MED.DIA)
- Electrolytes Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻ (Olympus ion-selective AU400)
- Lactic acid (Roche Diagnostics kit, plasma from silicon-coated tubes + EDTA fluoride)

**Statistical methods:** ANCOVA for body weight; ANOVA (GLM) with model: y = µ + Animal + Test + Time + interactions; Animal and Animal×interactions not significant, removed from final model. Lactic acid: one-way ANOVA for Test factor; time separately to prevent physiological AE₀ spike from masking Test effect. Resting electrolyte values: one-way ANOVA for Diet effect. Correlations: simple regression (y = a + bx) of AST/LDH on GSH-Px/AOP.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

**Exercise intensity (same across all tests):**

- Maximum speed reached: 880 m/min
- Mean ± SD race speed: 745.6 ± 41.6 m/min across three tests
- Heart rate mean ± SD: 218.0 ± 8.5 bpm (no significant Test effect)

**Body weight (Table 1):**

| Test   | BW before test (kg, mean ± SD) | BW loss (%)  | BW recovery at 24 h (%) |
| ------ | ------------------------------ | ------------ | ----------------------- |
| Test 1 | 431.25 ± 11.08                 | −1.80 ± 0.51 | 0.061 ± 0.122 ᵃ         |
| Test 2 | 432.75 ± 15.39                 | −2.21 ± 1.11 | 0.126 ± 0.614 ᵃᵇ        |
| Test 3 | 422.50 ± 8.60                  | −2.18 ± 0.62 | 1.095 ± 0.927 ᵇ         |

Different letters in BW recovery column: significant difference (p < 0.05). BW loss (absolute 6–14 kg) not significantly different across tests.

**pH:** Significant decrease immediately post-exercise (Sampling time effect p < 0.001) across all tests; Test 2 slightly higher mean (7.75 vs. 7.70). No significant Test effect.

**PCV:** Significant increase at AE₀ and AE₁ (p < 0.001) due to hemoconcentration and splenic contraction. Test 2 PCV values numerically higher (50.00 vs. 48.87 Test 1 vs. 47.12 Test 3), not significant overall; Test 1 vs. Test 2 significant at p < 0.05 by post-hoc Student t.

**Lactic acid (Fig. 3):**

- AE₀ values: higher than prior reports but similar to Assenza et al. (1992) in trotters
- Test 3 AE₀ lower (trend), suggesting better O₂ transport with systematic supplementation (lower PCV → better blood viscosity)
- AE₂ (24 h): significantly greater (p < 0.01) for Test 1 vs. Tests 2 and 3, indicating slower lactate clearance without supplementation

**AOP (antioxidant power):** Sampling time effect significant (p < 0.05). Test 2 shows adequate antioxidant defence immediately post-exercise; Test 3 shows slower but more sustained resistance to oxidative stress.

**GSH-Px:** Test 1 shows decrease from AR to AE₂. Tests 2 and 3 show stable trend — better limitation of ROS-induced damage.

**LDH and AST:** No significant differences in ANOVA; however, Test 3 shows more modest and delayed LDH increase vs. Tests 1 and 2; AST in Test 3 decreases at AE₀ (opposite to Tests 1 and 2), then rises later — indicating reduced acute muscle damage.

## Discussion & interpretation

Despite similar body weight losses across all tests (~2% per race, absolute 6–14 kg), recovery at 24 h was significantly better in Test 3 (1.095 ± 0.927% BW recovery vs. 0.061 ± 0.122% in Test 1, p < 0.05), indicating that systematic supplementation improved rehydration, likely via greater voluntary water intake stimulated by salt (Coenen et al. 1995). The control diet's sodium (158 mmol/l) and chloride (115 mmol/l) at rest exceeded normal athletic-horse ranges — suggesting an inherent electrolyte imbalance in unsupplemented horses that hampered recovery. The supplemented horses (Tests 2 and 3) had significantly lower resting Na⁺ and Cl⁻, within normal ranges, reflecting better daily electrolyte regulation. The 12-day systematic supplementation (Test 3) showed superiority over sporadic 1-day supplementation (Test 2) across most markers, supporting the need for chronic management rather than acute administration.

## Limitations

- n = 4 horses — extremely small; individual variation likely hid many statistical significances.
- Standardbred trotters (Italy), not Thoroughbred gallopers (Japan). Sweat composition, aerobic/anaerobic balance, training workload, and racing speed profiles differ substantially.
- Only one-day and twelve-day comparisons; the optimal supplementation duration is unknown.
- 2,000 m trot at 745 m/min (≈12.4 m/s) vs. JRA 1,200 m sprint at 15.9 m/s (Mukai et al. 2007 [18_4_153]); different energetics.
- Diet composition not representative of JRA/NAR racing Thoroughbred diets.
- 25°C ambient; Japanese summer racing can reach higher temperatures with humidity — stronger heat stress and electrolyte loss expected.
- No finishing-position outcome data; only physiological markers.
- Lactic acid measured from plasma (silicon-coated tubes), not whole blood — creates 30% higher values than whole-blood method (see also 18_4_153).

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `post_race_lactate_clearance` — derived: lactate at AE₁ or AE₂ minus AE₀; slower clearance (Test 1 pattern) indicates poor aerobic recovery; proxy: time between races (days_since_last_race can serve as a crude recovery-window proxy) — source: blood tests (not standard) — expected effect: negative for next-race performance
- `plasma_na_cl_balance` — resting Na⁺ and Cl⁻; elevated resting values (>155 mmol/l Na⁺ in this study) signal electrolyte dysregulation and poor recovery state — source: veterinary blood records — availability: research only for JRA
- `body_weight_delta_24h` — percentage body weight recovery at 24 h post-race; proxy for rehydration efficiency — source: 馬体重 records at next day's weigh-in — availability: possible if daily weigh-in data exists; simpler proxy: `馬体重_change` between last race and next entry
- `ast_ldh_muscle_damage_index` — composite of AST and LDH; AST–LDH r = 0.54, p < 0.01; if available, elevated post-race AST/LDH indicates muscle damage and predicts performance degradation in next race — availability: research blood tests
- `antioxidant_status` (AOP, GSH-Px) — theoretical feature; in practice not available from standard databases; note that vitamin C/E supplementation improves AOP/GSH-Px stability (Test 3)
- **Caution:** n = 4 standardbred trotters; all quantitative thresholds from this paper should not be applied directly to JRA/NAR Thoroughbred models without validation; use as qualitative feature motivation only
