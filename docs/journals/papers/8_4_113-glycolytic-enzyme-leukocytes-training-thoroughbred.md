## Abstract (verbatim)

> Glycolytic enzyme activities and expression of glucokinase (GK) were investigated in leukocytes (WBC) from Thoroughbreds, racehorses undergoing continuous training exercise and riding horses. Glycolytic enzyme activities in WBC from racehorses were higher than those from riding horses. Especially activities of GK, rate-limiting enzyme of glycolysis, were significantly higher in WBC from racehorses than in those from riding horses. Analysis of reverse transcription-polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) with specific primer for GK showed that expression of mRNA of GK was remarkably increased in WBC from racehorses. This suggested that activities and gene expression of some kinds of glycolytic enzymes including GK in WBC are possibly a good indicator for assessing glucose metabolism in Thoroughbreds undergoing training exercise.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

This paper belongs to **Feature family C (exercise-physiology/fitness)**. It demonstrates that racehorses undergoing continuous training have approximately 2× higher glucokinase (GK) activity in white blood cells (WBC/leukocytes) compared to riding horses, with matching upregulation of GK mRNA. GK is the rate-limiting enzyme of glycolysis (hexokinase IV), and its upregulation in blood cells reflects a system-wide increase in glucose flux associated with sustained high-intensity training.

The practical modelling relevance is that WBC glycolytic enzyme activity — specifically GK — is a training-load-dependent metabolic biomarker accessible from routine blood draws (no tissue biopsy required). If pre-race blood biochemistry panels include enzyme activity assays (possible in research-grade testing but not standard JRA/NAR commercial panels), GK activity in WBC could serve as a metabolic fitness indicator for racehorses.

The authors explicitly state that "the relationship between glycolytic enzyme in tissues and racing performance is not known" and that further work is needed. This paper is therefore a candidate feature-discovery study, not a validated predictor. It establishes the biochemical rationale and magnitude of the training signal (GK activity: 8.4 ± 2.4 vs. 4.1 ± 1.2 nmol/min/mg; ~2× difference, p < 0.05).

The study uses NAR Urawa racehorses (flat, NAR category), making it directly applicable to NAR race data — a notable feature given that most exercise physiology studies in this collection use JRA Thoroughbreds.

## Background & objective

Glucose is the primary energy source for mammalian cells. In horses, blood glucose and plasma insulin concentrations are stable throughout the day (Arai et al. 1996), but glucose utilisation at the cellular level varies with training status. Continuous training increases glucose utilisation in animal tissues generally (Ezaki et al. 1990). In Thoroughbred erythrocytes (RBC), glucose transport and glycolytic activity are higher under continuous training than in untrained horses (Arai et al. 1994). However, skeletal muscle and liver biopsies are difficult to obtain from racehorses.

Leukocytes (WBC) — primarily lymphocytes — can be isolated from routine venous blood samples by gradient centrifugation. If WBC glycolytic enzyme activities mirror those in skeletal muscle or liver, they provide a non-invasive window into whole-body glucose metabolism. Glucokinase (GK, hexokinase IV) is expressed in liver and pancreatic β-cells and is the rate-limiting enzyme of glycolysis; its expression is regulated by insulin and metabolic state (Magnuson et al. 1989; Printz et al. 1993). Objective: measure cytosolic glycolytic enzyme activities and GK mRNA expression in WBC from racehorses vs. riding horses to test whether training load is reflected in WBC metabolic phenotype.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:**

- Racehorses: 12 Thoroughbreds (4–8 years old; 5 female, 7 male) kept at Kaminoda Training Center, Urawa Race Course, National Association of Racing (NAR)
- Riding horses: 10 Thoroughbreds (5–16 years old; 2 female, 8 gelding) at the Equestrian Club, St. Paul's University

**Training regime (racehorses):** Every 9 of 10 days: 3 days fast galloping at 13–17 m/s for 1,000–1,200 m + 6 days slow work at 6–8 m/s for 1,500–2,000 m (including 2 hr warm-up/cool-down). Riding horses: light equestrian activities only.

**Blood collection:** Jugular vein, heparinised tubes, 14:00–15:00, at rest, unfasting, no excitement/forcible restraint. Plasma recovered at 4°C, stored at −20°C.

**WBC isolation:** Gradient centrifugation using SEPARATE-L (lymphocyte isolating solution; Muto Pure Chemicals, Tokyo); washed twice with PBS pH 7.2. Result: WBC fraction containing mainly lymphocytes.

**Enzyme assay:** Cytosol fraction prepared from washed WBC; stored at −80°C. Enzyme activities measured at 25°C by published spectrophotometric methods (cited references). Activities expressed as nmol substrate degraded/min/mg protein. Protein concentration by Bradford (1976) method.

- Hexokinase I, II, III (HK): Vinuela et al. 1963 method
- Glucokinase (GK, hexokinase IV): same method as HK
- Pyruvate kinase (PK): Hess and Wieker 1974
- Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD): Bergmeyer et al. 1974
- Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH): Kaloustian et al. 1969

**RT-PCR for GK mRNA:** Total RNA isolated with RNeasy Total RNA Kit (Qiagen). Primers targeting glucose- and ATP-binding domains of GK: positions 162–736 of rat liver GK cDNA (Andreone et al. 1989). Sense: 5'-GTCGAGCAGATCCTGGCAG-3'; Antisense: 5'-ACTGTGTCGTTCACCATTGCC-3'. Expected product: 574 bp. RT reaction: 1 µg RNA, AMV reverse transcriptase (TaKaRa RNA PCR Kit AMV); 30°C 10 min → 55°C 30 min → 99°C 5 min → 5°C 5 min. PCR: 30 cycles (94°C 30 s, 60°C 30 s, 72°C 90 s); 2% agarose gel; ethidium bromide.

**Other assays:** Blood glucose by glucose-oxidase method (Huggett and Nixon 1957); plasma insulin by micro ELISA sandwich method (Arai et al. 1989). Statistics: mean ± SD; Student's t-test for between-group comparisons.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

**Blood glucose and plasma insulin (Table 1):**

| Parameter              | Racehorses (n=12) | Riding horses (n=10) | Significance |
| ---------------------- | ----------------- | -------------------- | ------------ |
| Blood glucose (mg/dl)  | 64 ± 11           | 62 ± 12              | ns           |
| Plasma insulin (µU/ml) | 16 ± 6            | 14 ± 7               | ns           |

No significant difference in glucose or insulin between groups. (Note: these values are lower than previously reported in horses [Arai et al. 1996], attributed to ~1 hr pre-feeding sampling window in the afternoon.)

**Cytosolic enzyme activities in WBC (Table 1):**

| Enzyme                                   | Racehorses (n=12) nmol/min/mg | Riding horses (n=10) nmol/min/mg | Significance |
| ---------------------------------------- | ----------------------------- | -------------------------------- | ------------ |
| HK (hexokinase I/II/III)                 | 1.6 ± 0.6                     | 0.9 ± 0.3                        | ns (trend)   |
| **GK (glucokinase)**                     | **8.4 ± 2.4**                 | **4.1 ± 1.2**                    | **p < 0.05** |
| PK (pyruvate kinase)                     | 53 ± 16                       | 34 ± 12                          | ns (trend)   |
| G6PD (glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase) | 11 ± 4                        | 10 ± 3                           | ns           |
| LDH (lactate dehydrogenase)              | 196 ± 46                      | 136 ± 34                         | ns (trend)   |

All cytosolic enzyme activities were numerically higher in racehorses. GK was the only enzyme with a statistically significant difference (p < 0.05; ~2.05× higher in racehorses).

**RT-PCR GK mRNA (Fig. 1):**

- A 574 bp fragment (containing glucose- and ATP-binding domain of GK family) was detected in PCR products from both racehorses and riding horses
- Racehorse with high GK activity (10.8 nmol/min/mg): 574 bp band stained strongly with ethidium bromide
- Riding horse with low GK activity (2.8 nmol/min/mg): 574 bp band was "obscure" (faint, barely visible)
- Staining intensity (band density) appeared proportional to GK enzyme activity
- The 574 bp fragment contains putative amino acid residues for glucose- and ATP-binding, highly conserved in hexokinase family across man and rat
- Same fragment also detected in dog and mouse liver; absent from cattle liver (unpublished data); only detected in tissues with GK activity

## Discussion & interpretation

**Training-load signal:** The ~2× higher GK activity in WBC from continuously trained racehorses vs. lightly exercised riding horses demonstrates that whole-body metabolic adaptation to sustained high-intensity training is reflected in blood cells. This aligns with the prior finding (Arai et al. 1994) that RBC glucose transport and glycolytic enzyme activity are also elevated in trained Thoroughbreds. Both findings suggest a systemic metabolic reprogramming toward higher glucose flux under sustained training.

**mRNA–activity correlation:** RT-PCR results confirmed that increased GK protein activity (8.4 vs. 4.1 nmol/min/mg) corresponds to increased GK mRNA abundance, indicating transcriptional upregulation rather than post-translational modification or enzyme stabilisation. This validates GK mRNA as a more direct regulatory indicator.

**GK specificity:** GK is the rate-limiting enzyme of glycolysis specifically in the liver and pancreatic β-cells at high glucose concentrations; its expression in WBC lymphocytes is consistent with the metabolic "spillover" of training adaptation into blood cells. The 574 bp RT-PCR fragment spans the glucose- and ATP-binding domain of the hexokinase family (conserved region). The authors note that additional fragments (other than 574 bp) may represent related hexokinase family members; sequencing would be needed to confirm specificity.

**Glucose/insulin paradox:** Despite higher GK activity in racehorses, resting blood glucose and plasma insulin were not different between groups. This is consistent with Arai et al. (1996) and suggests that resting homeostatic control is maintained at the same set-point regardless of training level; it is the enzyme capacity and metabolic response to glucose that differs, not the baseline glucose concentration.

**Performance link — still open:** The authors explicitly state that "the relationship between glycolytic enzyme in tissues and racing performance is not known." GK activity is a marker of training load (racehorses vs. riding horses) but it has not been shown to discriminate among racehorses of different performance levels. This is the critical limitation for direct use as a finishing-position feature.

**Comparison group coarseness:** Racehorses (continuous training, 3 fast gallop days/10) vs. riding horses (light activity) represents a large training-load difference. Within the racehorse group, GK activity ranged from the individual cited (10.8 nmol/min/mg) down toward the mean (8.4 nmol/min/mg) — a 29% range. Whether GK activity predicts differences within racehorse performance tiers is untested.

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional design; cannot determine causality (training → higher GK) vs. selection (higher-GK animals chosen for racing) — though Arai et al. 1994 longitudinal study in 2-year-olds supports causality
- Coarse comparison (racehorses vs. riding horses); no within-racehorse performance data
- n = 12 racehorses and 10 riding horses; marginal statistical power; only GK reached p < 0.05
- Primer designed from rat liver GK cDNA; equine GK sequence was not available; cross-species amplification assumed based on conserved domain
- WBC fraction is mainly lymphocytes; exact cell-type composition not characterised; different lymphocyte/monocyte ratios between groups could confound
- All blood taken at 14:00–15:00 unfasting — consistent sampling window reduces time-of-day bias, but pre-feeding state may underestimate insulin-sensitive GK activity compared with post-feeding
- NAR Urawa racehorses (flat, lower-tier than JRA); direct extrapolation to JRA Thoroughbreds should be done with caution (though breeds are the same)

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `gk_activity_wbc_nmol_min_mg` — glucokinase activity in WBC cytosol (nmol/min/mg protein); racehorses 8.4 ± 2.4 vs. riding 4.1 ± 1.2; **~2× training-load signal**; not available in routine JRA/NAR race databases; if research-grade blood assay data exist, use this; expected effect: higher GK → more intensively trained → negative on finishing rank (better performance); **data availability**: specialised research settings only
- `ldh_wbc_nmol_min_mg` — lactate dehydrogenase activity in WBC; racehorses 196 ± 46 vs. riding 136 ± 34; numerically higher but not significant; correlated with GK (same training-load signal); LDH is more routinely measured in clinical biochemistry panels than GK — **check if WBC-specific LDH is available in JRA panel data** vs. serum LDH (which is different)
- `pk_wbc_nmol_min_mg` — pyruvate kinase in WBC; racehorses 53 ± 16 vs. riding 34 ± 12; complements GK in glycolytic capacity signal
- `training_intensity_proxy` — if WBC enzyme data unavailable, use a composite of trackwork speed + frequency as a proxy for GK activity tier; fast gallop (13–17 m/s) × 3 days per 10 is the regime that produces GK = 8.4 nmol/min/mg
- `days_continuous_training` — number of consecutive training days before race; longer = higher cumulative glucose utilisation upregulation; **derivable from JRA/NAR trackwork records**
- **Caution**: GK mRNA by RT-PCR is a research technique not applicable to routine race-day monitoring; use enzyme activity (not mRNA) if any blood assay data are available
- **Caution**: Blood glucose and plasma insulin are NOT good training-load features from this paper — they were identical between trained and untrained groups. Do not use resting blood glucose as a performance predictor.
- **Interaction**: `gk_activity_wbc × distance_m` — glycolytic capacity matters most for short-distance sprint races where anaerobic glucose flux is rate-limiting; less relevant for long-distance aerobic events dominated by fat oxidation
