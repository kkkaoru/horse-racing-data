## Abstract (verbatim)

> Ligand-dependent nuclear receptor compressor-like (LCORL) encodes a transcription factor, and its polymorphisms are associated with measures of skeletal frame size and adult height in several species. Recently, the single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) BIEC2-808543 located upstream of LCORL was identified as a genetic diagnostic marker associated with withers height in Thoroughbreds. In this study, 322 Thoroughbreds-in-training were genotyped for BIEC2-808543 to evaluate the association between genotype and body composition traits, including body weight, withers height, the ratio of body weight to withers height, chest circumference, and cannon circumference. Of these, withers height and cannon circumference were significantly associated with LCORL genotypes throughout almost the entire training period in males and females. Animals with a C/T genotype had higher withers height (maximum differences of 1.8 cm and 2.1 cm in males and females, respectively) and cannon circumference (maximum differences of 0.65 cm and 0.48 cm in males and females, respectively) compared with animals with a T/T genotype. These results suggested that the regulation of LCORL expression influences the skeletal frame size in Thoroughbreds and thus, indirectly affects the body weight. Although LCORL and BIEC2-808543 would be useful for selective breeding in Thoroughbreds, the production of genetically modified animals and gene doping based on genetic information should be prohibited in order to maintain racing integrity.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

This paper belongs to feature family **D (genetics/pedigree)** and **F (conformation/body-size)**. LCORL (ligand-dependent nuclear receptor corepressor-like) on Equus caballus autosome 3 (ECA3) is one of the most strongly validated skeletal-size loci in horses, originally discovered in multi-breed GWAS studies and now confirmed in Thoroughbreds under real training conditions. The SNP BIEC2-808543 predicts both withers height and cannon circumference throughout training — two traits directly relevant to the finishing-position pipeline:

1. **Withers height** is a frame-size proxy that correlates with stride length. Larger horses (C/T genotype: +1.8–2.1 cm) have longer strides per unit speed and may show different performance profiles at different distances.

2. **Cannon circumference** is a bone-strength proxy (larger bone circumference = greater cross-sectional moment of inertia = greater resistance to bending and torsional fracture). The authors explicitly note that cannon circumference is a likely determinant of PSB fracture susceptibility (cross-referencing 27_1514). C/T genotype males: +0.65 cm cannon circumference; C/T females: +0.48 cm.

3. **LCORL + MSTN combined genetic profile:** LCORL affects skeletal frame; MSTN (myostatin) affects muscle mass. The same research group (Tozaki et al. 2011) previously demonstrated that MSTN C/C genotype produces higher body weight and skeletal muscle mass. A two-locus genotype combining LCORL (frame) × MSTN (muscle) can define four body-composition profiles relevant to race class suitability, distance preference, and weight-carrying ability.

4. **Sex specificity:** LCORL's effect on body weight is significant in males (Sep–Nov, Feb) but not females; cannon circumference effect is significant in both sexes throughout training. Sex must be interacted with LCORL genotype in any feature that uses genetic data.

Practically, LCORL genotype is not available in standard race databases, but the horse-level measurement of withers height and cannon circumference (if recorded at sales or training centres) serves as a functional equivalent — these phenotypic measurements capture the downstream effect of LCORL and other skeletal-size loci.

## Background & objective

Current Thoroughbreds have substantially larger body size than their 18th-century ancestors (Darley Arabian withers height ~152 cm; current average ~160.5 cm). LCORL was mapped on ECA3 and the SNP BIEC2-808543 upstream of LCORL was associated with withers height in multiple horse breeds (Makvandi-Nejad et al. 2012 showed four loci explain 83% of size variation in horses; BIEC2-808543 confirmed in Signer-Hasler et al. 2012 across breeds including Thoroughbreds). This study extends prior findings to: (1) Thoroughbreds specifically; (2) during active training (not just at a single time point); (3) additional body composition traits beyond withers height, particularly cannon circumference and body weight–to–withers-height ratio (a proxy for muscle mass relative to frame size); (4) sex-stratified analysis.

Hypothesis: LCORL expression influences skeletal frame size (withers height, cannon circumference) but not muscle mass (body weight:withers height ratio) in Thoroughbreds during training.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 322 Thoroughbred racehorses (165 males, 167 females). Born January–June 2009–2014; majority born March–April. All purchased at commercial livestock auctions at 1 year of age; introduced to Hidaka Training and Research Center (JRA) by end of September; initial training October; full-scale training November through April.

**Body composition measurement:** Performed monthly from September (age ~18 months) to March (7 months total: Sep, Oct, Nov, Dec, Jan, Feb, Mar). Measurements: body weight (kg), withers height (cm), chest circumference (cm), cannon circumference (cm). Ratio of body weight to withers height (kg·cm⁻¹) defined as "muscle content" proxy (skeletal muscle >55% of total body mass in Thoroughbreds).

**SNP genotyping:** Blood collected from each animal, stored at −40°C. Genomic DNA extracted using MFX-2000 MagExtractor System (Toyobo, Osaka). Genotyping via TaqMan SNP Genotyping Assay with 7500 Fast Real-Time PCR System (Applied Biosystems).

Primer and probe sequences:
| Component | Sequence |
|-----------|---------|
| F-primer | CCAAATTTGCCTGGCTAGAGA |
| R-primer | TGTTCCCTGTGATTCTGCCTTT |
| MGB_VIC_C probe | CATTCCAGCTTATTTCTGTA |
| MGB_FAM_T probe | CATTCCAGTTTATTTCTGTAC |

**Analysis subpopulations:** To remove pedigree-based and age-based systematic errors, full- and half-siblings and horses born in January, February, May, or June were excluded. Final subpopulations:

- Growth trend analysis: 59 males, 48 females
- Genotype association analysis: 67 males (C/T: 13, T/T: 54), 55 females (C/T: 11, T/T: 44)

**Statistical method:** Student's t-test comparing C/T vs. T/T genotypes separately for each sex and time point. Double-blind design. IBM SPSS Statistics 19.

## Discussion & interpretation

The paper confirms that BIEC2-808543 near LCORL is the first reported genetic marker significantly associated with cannon circumference in Thoroughbreds — extending prior withers-height findings (Makvandi-Nejad et al. 2012; Signer-Hasler et al. 2012) to a second skeletal size trait relevant to fracture risk.

Key mechanism: BIEC2-808543 polymorphism disrupts a putative binding site of transcription factor TFIID, suggesting that the influence on skeletal frame is caused by regulation of LCORL expression (Metzger et al. 2013 confirmed LCORL expression levels correlate with body size in horses).

The absence of BW/WH ratio differences confirms that LCORL affects **skeletal frame only, not muscle mass** — consistent with its role as a frame-size gene rather than a muscle-hypertrophy gene (contrast with MSTN). This distinction matters for predicting race performance: LCORL primarily predicts frame size and fracture resistance, not power output or speed.

The discussion proposes a combined LCORL × MSTN genotype profile for personalised breeding and training management:

- Male, C/C at LCORL, C/C at MSTN: large frame + heavy muscle = heavy body
- Female, T/T at LCORL, T/T at MSTN: small frame + light muscle = light body
  The authors caution against gene doping using CRISPR/Cas9 or AAV vectors to modify these loci.

## Limitations

- n=322 total (n=67 males and n=55 females in association analysis after sibling/birth-month exclusions); C/T group is small (13 males, 11 females), limiting statistical power.
- C/C genotype not evaluable (frequency 0–1%); effect at homozygous advantageous genotype can only be estimated by extrapolation from one C/C female.
- Cross-sectional genotype measurement; longitudinal training effects on genotype expression not isolated from training effects.
- No direct link between LCORL genotype and race performance (speed, finishing position, earnings); the performance pathway is mediated through body composition traits.
- Population limited to horses introduced to JRA Hidaka Training and Research Center; sale-selected horses are already filtered for quality, limiting generalisability.

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `lcorl_genotype_biec2_808543` — categorical: C/T vs. T/T (C/C negligible) — source: LCORL genotyping (Laboratory of Racing Chemistry data if accessible) — expected effect: C/T → larger skeletal frame (+1.8–2.1 cm WH, +0.48–0.65 cm CC); indirect positive effect on stride length (distance events); positive effect on fracture resistance
- `withers_height_cm` — continuous — source: JRA training centre body composition records (measured monthly from age 18 months) or JBBA sales data — expected effect: taller horses may have longer strides, potential advantage at longer distances; interaction with race distance
- `cannon_circumference_cm` — continuous — source: JRA training centre records — expected effect: larger cannon = stronger bone = lower PSB fracture risk; negative predictor of injury scratch
- `mstn_genotype` — categorical (C/C vs. C/T vs. T/T) — source: Tozaki et al. companion MSTN genotyping — expected effect: C/C → higher muscle mass → more speed/power; interacts with LCORL
- `lcorl_mstn_combined_score` — composite: C-allele count at LCORL + C-allele count at MSTN → range 0–4 — source: genotype data — expected effect: higher combined score → larger, more muscular horse; may interact with distance (positive for sprint, negative for stamina)
- Interaction: `lcorl_genotype × sex` — BW effect is sex-specific (males significant, females not); cannon circumference effect is significant in both sexes
- `body_weight_to_withers_height_ratio` — continuous (kg/cm) — source: training centre records — expected effect: proxy for muscle content; values 2.87–3.03 in this study; higher ratio = more muscular relative to frame size → speed/power indicator; NOT associated with LCORL genotype (pure muscle proxy)
- **Do NOT use** genotype features as inputs without available genotype data; use phenotypic proxies (WH, cannon CC, BW, BW/WH) when genotype data is unavailable
