## Abstract (verbatim)

> Since the first recording of electrocardiograms (ECGs) of a horse in Japan was carried out in 1944, studies on ECGs have been performed intensively. During the early stages of research from the 1950s to 1960s, leads to use for ECG recording were evaluated using several different approaches including unipolar leads, bipolar limb leads, and bipolar chest leads. Based on these studies, the AB lead, which is oriented along the long axis of the heart, became the standard reference method in Japan. Electrodes of the AB lead are placed on the upper 1/4th point along a straight line between the withers and the left shoulder blade (base: B), and 10 cm posterior to the left olecranon (apex: A). The incidence of equine arrhythmias among racehorses has been surveyed, and details of the electrocardiographic characteristics of several arrhythmias have been investigated. In particular, atrial fibrillation (AF) has been extensively studied, and papers have reported findings such as that paroxysmal AF occurs during racing and described electrocardiographic changes that occur at the onset of AF during exercise. Development of a radiotelemetry system for ECG recording enabled the first recording of equine ECGs during galloping in 1964, the detection of arrhythmias, and calculation of heart rate during exercise. Studies on comparative and developmental changes of ECGs have described characteristics of the equine ECGs. Future research on changes in cardiac function, including autonomic function, that occur with aging may lead to new developments in equine electrocardiography and contribute to improving the health and welfare of the horse.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **C (exercise-physiology/fitness)** and **A (injury/soundness — cardiac)**.

Atrial fibrillation (AF) occurring during a race is the most clinically important arrhythmia for JRA race outcomes. Amada and Kurita (1975) documented 5 horses that developed paroxysmal AF during races and finished well behind the leaders; all returned to sinus rhythm within 23 hours without medication. If a horse's AF history is available from veterinary records, it is a strong negative prior for next-race performance and a meaningful scratch-risk predictor. The paper also establishes that premature atrial contractions (PACs) immediately precede AF onset during exercise — AF onset was triggered when R-R interval shortened to 0.16–0.20 seconds during galloping — making PAC history an indirect signal.

Exercise ECG via radiotelemetry gives heart rate (HR) during gallop as a validated aerobic fitness proxy. The review documents HR rising from ~45 bpm at rest to 150 bpm at extended canter and exceeding 200 bpm at 100 m sprint-gallop. The study of 124 horses found premature ventricular contractions (PVCs) during exercise in 51.6% of horses (64/124), with 45/64 appearing in the 3-minute post-gallop cool-down — relevant as a mild risk signal rather than a major performance limiter. Heart-rate variability (HRV) power spectral analysis was established as an autonomic function measure; training, drug, and transport effects on HRV have been quantified.

Age-related cardiac autonomic changes are flagged as a priority for future research: breeding stallions >10 years old are known to show cardiac sudden deaths during mating/exercise, suggesting an age × cardiac fitness interaction term is physiologically justified in any long-term model.

Engineering note: direct ECG or HR telemetry data are not available in JRA race records, but a horse's race-scratch history combined with the AF prevalence statistics from this review (2 AF cases in 214 horses = 0.9% of a mixed racehorse population at a given examination; see Table 1) gives a base-rate prior for cardiac-related underperformance.

## Background & objective

Narrative review of 70+ years (1944–2014) of Japanese equine electrocardiography research, from the first horse ECG (Yasuda et al., 1944) through lead standardisation, arrhythmia prevalence surveys, exercise ECG development, and developmental/comparative ECG studies. Objective: synthesise the history and identify future directions, particularly aging-related cardiac changes.

## Materials & methods

Narrative review; no new primary data collected. Primary studies cited include:

- **Lead standardisation**: Multiple unipolar and bipolar lead systems evaluated from 1944–1966; the AB lead (base B at upper 1/4 of withers-to-left-shoulder-blade line; apex A at 10 cm posterior to left olecranon) adopted as Japanese standard in 1966.
- **Arrhythmia prevalence survey (Amada and Senta 1964b)**: n=214 horses (121 two-year-olds, 66 adult racehorses ≥3 yr, 27 riding horses ≥3 yr); AB lead; direct heat stylus cardiograph; 48 cases (22.4%) abnormal. Extended to n=250: 65 cases (26.0%) abnormal.
- **Exercise ECG**: radiotelemetry first reported in Japan by Nomura et al. 1964; 20 MHz carrier wave → 5 km+ range system → portable cassette Holter → digital Holter systems.
- **Exercise ECG survey**: 124 horses examined using portable Holter; PVC incidence and timing recorded.
- **AF onset during exercise**: Hiraga and Kubo described 3 episodes in which AF onset was captured on ECG; R-R interval at AF onset: 0.20 s, 0.16 s, 0.19 s.
- **HRV autonomic analysis**: Kuwahara et al. established power spectral analysis for horses; subsequent studies on training effects, drugs, transport, hot-spring immersion.
- **Developmental ECG**: AB lead ECGs from 1 week to 12 months post-birth; fetal ECGs from 190 days gestation; twin pregnancy case.

When extended to 250 horses: 65 cases (26.0%); AV block incidence among abnormal ECGs rose from 39.6% (19/48) to 55.4% (36/65).

**Paroxysmal AF during racing (Amada and Kurita 1975):** 5 horses confirmed AF during races; all returned to sinus rhythm spontaneously within <23 hours. PACs observed in 2/5 recovering horses.

**AF onset ECG (Hiraga and Kubo, 3 episodes):** R-R intervals at AF onset were 0.20 s, 0.16 s, and 0.19 s during exercise. Common pre-cursor: series of PAC-like premature contractions just before onset.

**ECG waveform parameters (AS lead, Amada and Senta 1964a):**

| P wave amplitude  | 0.30 mV (avg)            | 0.34 mV (avg)            |
| P wave duration   | 0.13 s (avg)             | 0.15 s (avg)             |
| S wave amplitude  | 2.40 mV (avg)            | 3.24 mV (avg)            |
| QRS duration      | 0.11 s (avg)             | 0.12 s (avg)             |

**Exercise ECG survey (portable Holter, n=124 horses):** PVCs in 64/124 horses (51.6%); 45/64 occurred in cooling-down phase within 3 min after galloping; 36/64 within 1 min after galloping.

**Gallop HR (Nomura et al. 1964):** resting ~45 bpm; extended canter ~150 bpm; 100 m sprint >200 bpm; at HR=200 bpm, T-wave and P-wave fusion observed on ECG.

**Fetal HR (Matsuda et al., 10 mares from 6 months gestation):** Baseline fetal HR decreased in a logarithmic manner with gestational advance; maternal HR increased over same period; frequency and magnitude of fetal HR acceleration increased in latter gestation.

**Stallion HR during mating (Hatazoe et al., n=10 Thoroughbred stallions, Holter):** Maximum HR during mating: 168.9 ± 14.2 bpm; PVCs observed in 3/10 stallions.

**Cardiac sudden death (Kiryu et al., 1 case):** 440 s of ECG captured before sudden death in 2-year-old Thoroughbred; R-on-T phenomenon after pair of PVCs triggered ventricular fibrillation.

**Chronic AF (Shinzan, longest-lived Japanese Thoroughbred, 35 yr 3 months):** AF first detected December 1994; still present in final Holter in May 1996; died July 13, 1996.

**Paroxysmal AF in newborn foals (Machida et al., n=20 foals):** 3/20 (15%) showed paroxysmal AF; vagal tone, atrial stretch, and hypoxia proposed as triggers.

## Discussion & interpretation

The review traces the evolution of Japanese equine ECG research: from confusion over lead systems in the 1940s–1960s to the adoption of a single standard (AB lead) and the development of radiotelemetry enabling real-time exercise ECG. The AB lead was adopted over the more theoretically ideal AS lead (which more closely follows the cardiac long axis) for practical convenience.

The most clinically important finding is paroxysmal AF during racing, first documented in 1975. The review establishes that AF does not always follow a recognisable ECG precursor pattern except for the finding that PACs precede AF onset in some cases and that AF onset correlates with very short R-R intervals (<0.20 s) during exercise. AF terminates spontaneously in many cases, which complicates pre-race screening.

The prevalence of PVCs during post-exercise cool-down (51.6% of horses) is presented as a common finding that may not indicate pathology, in contrast to AF which has direct performance consequences. This distinction is relevant to modelling: PVC history should not be weighted as heavily as AF history.

The review points out that age-related cardiac changes in Thoroughbreds remain understudied, identifying autonomic HRV analysis as the most promising tool. The establishment of HRV norms for training, transport and drug effects provides a background against which deviations could be interpreted as illness or fitness loss.

## Limitations

- No new primary data; reliance on cited studies with varying methodologies, lead systems, and horse populations.
- Arrhythmia prevalence data span a 70-year period during which diagnostic sensitivity improved, making period-to-period comparisons unreliable.
- Post-race ECG surveys (AF during racing) may underestimate prevalence because horses that finished normally were not examined.
- Fetal ECG and developmental ECG findings are based on small samples (n=10 mares, n=20 foals).
- No quantitative meta-analysis; all results are narrative summaries.

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `af_history_flag` — binary: horse has documented AF history in veterinary records — derivation: JRA health records — expected effect: strong negative on finishing position, strong positive on scratch probability — data availability: likely restricted to JRA internal records, not in public race results
- `pac_flag` — binary: premature atrial contractions documented in exercise ECG — derivation: exercise Holter records — expected effect: moderate positive scratch risk (AF precursor) — data availability: restricted
- `pvc_flag` — binary: PVCs in cool-down ECG — derivation: exercise Holter — expected effect: small if any; most common finding (51.6%) suggests weak signal — likely confounded with training load
- `cardiac_scratch_prior` — float: base-rate probability of cardiac-related underperformance, using AF prevalence ~0.9% in surveyed population as prior — derivation: population estimate, not individual — expected effect: weak calibration signal for base rate
- `age_cardiac_interaction` — float: age × recent_performance_drop interaction — biological basis: cardiac autonomic aging documented; stallions >10 yr flagged as high-risk — expected effect: older horses with sudden performance drops may have cardiac cause
