## Abstract (verbatim)

> We tested the hypothesis that frequency of intense exercise during training would affect the likelihood of bucked shins complex developing in young horses. The relative prevalence of bucked shins were examined in two groups of Anglo-Arabian horses that trainined by different training programs. One group (6 horses) exercised once per week at maximal speed, the other group (7 horses) exercised twice per week at maximal speed. They ran on similar track surface conditions on the same track. Although the group exercised twice per week at maximal speed had a prevalence, there was no significant difference in the incidence of bucked shins between the two groups. It suggest that the number of maximum gallop than the duration of intense exercise. Pathomorphogenetically, there was suggestion that focal radiolucent areas, indicating focal remodeling regions consisting of a focal conglomerate of irregular-sized osteons, at the site of the middle layer in the cortical bone beneath periosteal bony proliferation might play a role as a precursor lesion for the occurrence of otitis of the third metacarpal bone (McIII).

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **F (conformation/body-size/gait) and G (statistical-modelling)** — specifically lameness and musculoskeletal injury risk in young horses.

Bucked shin complex (periostitis of the third metacarpal bone, McIII) is the leading cause of exercise-induced lameness in 2-year-old Thoroughbreds in JRA training, with a cited 66% incidence during the first 8 months of training (JRA Annual Report on Racehorse Hygiene, 1999). This paper quantifies how exercise frequency (number of maximal-speed gallops) relates to onset timing and identifies a focal cortical bone remodelling lesion as a likely precursor. The key finding for finishing-position prediction is that **clinical signs appear after only 2–3 maximal gallops**, meaning a horse entering a race during or just after the high-risk window has materially elevated scratch/lameness risk.

For a finishing-position model, this creates a direct engineered feature: **high-intensity session count in last 4–6 weeks**, particularly the number of maximal gallops logged. The study confirms this is a more discriminating signal than cumulative distance alone. Because onset occurs at the beginning of fast work after a progressive base-training phase, the first 5 weeks of gallop work represent a particularly high-risk window. The JRA authorship and Anglo-Arabian experimental cohort make the findings directly applicable to Japanese racing.

Track hardness (impact deceleration in G) interacts with exercise frequency: the study site had a mean 56.70 ± 13.81 G on sand 8–9 cm deep, which the authors note is lower than JRA racetracks, so the injury-rate numbers reported here are a lower bound for harder surfaces.

## Background & objective

Bucked shin complex is particularly common in 2-year-olds during their first year of fast-track training on hard ground. Complex aetiology involves fast work on hard ground and skeletal immaturity. Prior work had examined track surface effects (Moyer et al. 1991) but not exercise-frequency effects. The pathological process underlying the syndrome was also not well understood. The study hypothesised that exercise intensity (specifically the frequency of maximal-speed gallops) relates to bucked shin onset, and aimed to characterise the structural bone changes involved.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 13 Anglo-Arabian female horses aged 2 years 3 months to 2 years 5 months (mean 2.36 ± 0.07 years); physical examination normal in all horses. Divided into three groups:

- Group A (n = 6): one maximal-speed gallop per week
- Group B (n = 7): two maximal-speed gallops per week
- Group C (n = 2): stall-rested controls (no fast work), aged 2 years 5 months

**Training protocol (identical between A and B except gallop frequency):**

1. Months 1–2: walk, trot, slow canter
2. Weeks 1–6 post-base: canter at 8.3 m/s for 1,000 m, then 10 m/s for 1,200–1,800 m
3. Weeks 1–5 of fast gallop phase (progressive distance): 200 m → 400 m → 600 m → 800 m → 800 m at maximal speed

**Track:** dirt course, sand cushion 8–9 cm thick; same track throughout.

**Track hardness measurement:** self-propelled racetrack hardness measurement system (Oikawa et al. 2000); mean impact deceleration = 56.70 ± 13.81 G (n = 24). Authors note JRA racetracks have higher values.

**Clinical endpoint:** bucked shin syndrome diagnosed by shortened anterior stride, frequent lead changes, changed training enthusiasm, reluctance to accelerate, plus heat and swelling of dorsal metacarpal region, pain on digital palpation.

**Pathology:** affected horses immediately euthanised; 5-mm horizontal bone slices cut from mid-diaphysis of each McIII. Soft radiography (40 kVp, 5 mA, 80 s). Polyester resin embedding; ground sections 20–30 µm. Micro-radiography (38 kVp, 5 mA, 20 min). Histology: Cole's haematoxylin and eosin. All unaffected horses also euthanised at end of experiment.

**Statistics:** chi-squared test for group comparisons of prevalence.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

**Incidence of bucked shins:**

| Group       | Horses affected | %   | Limbs affected | %   |
| ----------- | --------------- | --- | -------------- | --- |
| A (1×/week) | 3/6             | 50% | 5/12           | 42% |
| B (2×/week) | 5/7             | 71% | 7/14           | 50% |
| C (control) | 0/2             | 0%  | 0/4            | 0%  |

Note: within Group A, 33% (1/3) of affected horses had unilateral bucked shins; within Group B, 60% (3/5) had unilateral bucked shins. There was **no statistically significant difference** between Groups A and B (chi-squared test). The authors note prevalence in this study is comparable to Moyer et al. (1991).

**Individual case data (Table 1):**

- A1: right limb affected; A2: not affected; A3: not affected; A4: right+left; A5: right+left; A6: not affected
- B7: right+left; B8: left; B9: not affected; B10: right+left; B11: not affected; B12: right; B13: left

**Onset timing:** Clinical signs first observed after the beginning of the fast gallop exercise period. Signs tended to appear after 2 or 3 high-speed gallops. Because Group B horses galloped twice per week, they accumulated 2–3 gallops faster in calendar time, so clinical onset occurred earlier in the training programme for Group B — but the number of gallop exposures to onset was similar across groups.

**Track hardness:** Mean 56.70 ± 13.81 G (n = 24). Authors explicitly note these values are lower than JRA racetracks.

**Gross pathology (affected horses):** Distinct congestion and oedema of surrounding periosteum and subcutaneous tissue at dorsal McIII.

**Micro-radiographic and histological findings (affected horses):**

- Periosteal new bone formation on the outer circumferential lamella of the dorsal aspect
- Focal radiolucent area beneath the periosteal proliferation at the middle cortical layer of the dorsal aspect — interpreted as focal remodelling region consisting of a focal conglomerate of irregular-sized osteons
- Endosteal bony proliferation on the inner circumferential lamella of the dorsal aspect
- Thin lines or fissures crossing the focal remodelling region occasionally visible in cortical bone

**Critical finding regarding precursor lesion:** The same focal remodelling architecture (focal conglomerate of irregular-sized osteons in the middle layer) was **also observed in the contra-lateral unaffected limbs** of affected horses. It was **absent in control (Group C) horses**. This indicates focal remodelling is a **precursor lesion** that develops before clinical signs, representing compensatory adaptive hyperplasia or healing of fatigue microdamage.

**Histology of affected periosteum:** Subcutaneous oedema with vascular mixed microthrombosis at the site of periosteal bony proliferation.

## Discussion & interpretation

The authors conclude that exercise intensity measured as number of maximal gallops (rather than duration or total distance of intense work) is the more relevant training-load feature for bucked shin risk. McIII imposes dorsal cortical loading at high speed through bending and compressive forces; repeated loading causes progressive loss of stiffness, decrease in yield strength, and increase in permanent deformation (Carter & Hayes 1976, 1977). The dorsal McIII region is mechanically weaker than other cortical regions (Martin et al. 1996), so fatigue microdamage accumulates faster under repeated compressive stress.

The focal remodelling zone is interpreted as a precursor: once this zone is established in the cortex, additional high-speed loading can trigger the full clinical syndrome via circulating disturbance (vascular thrombosis and congestion) at the periosteal proliferation site. The fact that contra-lateral clinically unaffected limbs in affected horses already show this precursor lesion suggests that when a horse develops bucked shins in one limb, the other limb is already at high risk.

The authors note the 66% JRA epidemiological incidence rate (from the JRA 1999 Annual Report on Racehorse Hygiene) as the reference population base rate for Japanese racing conditions.

## Limitations

- Small sample size: n = 13 total (6, 7, 2 per group); statistical power to detect differences in prevalence is low
- Single breed (Anglo-Arabian females only); Thoroughbreds may differ
- Track hardness in this study (56.70 G) is lower than JRA racetracks, so risk thresholds may not transfer directly
- Only frequency of maximal gallops was varied; speed within "maximal" category was not standardised precisely
- No quantitative measurement of bone mineral density or cortical thickness
- Controls were stall-rested rather than exercised at sub-maximal load, limiting interpretation of whether any fast work is harmful

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `max_gallop_count_4w` — number of recorded maximal-speed workouts in the last 4 weeks — derivation: training log data if available from JRA/NAR records — expected effect: positive (higher count → higher lameness risk for 2yo horses) — data availability: JRA training records potentially available; not in standard race entries
- `max_gallop_count_6w` — same over 6-week window — more closely matches the 5-week fast-gallop phase described in the training protocol — effect and availability as above
- `early_career_flag` — binary: horse is in first 8 months of JRA training (approximated by age ≤ 2y 9m and ≤ N starts) — base rate 66% incidence makes this cohort a qualitatively distinct risk group
- `age_at_first_start` — continuous (years/months) — derivation: from JRA race record birth date and first race date — expected effect: younger first-start horses may have greater skeletal immaturity and higher bucked-shin risk
- `track_hardness_G` — mean impact deceleration on race day track (G units) — derivation: JRA publishes track condition codes; harder tracks (高速馬場) correlate with higher G values — interaction term: `max_gallop_count_4w × track_hardness_G`
- `contra_lateral_precursor_flag` — CAUTION: this would require histological data; not derivable from standard race records; do NOT attempt to proxy this from race performance data
- Interaction: `early_career_flag × max_gallop_count_4w` — captures the compounding of skeletal immaturity and high training load
