## Abstract (verbatim)

> Supramaximal exercise while inspiring different O2 gases may induce different responses in cardiopulmonary function at the same relative and/or absolute exercise intensity. The purpose of this study was to compare the effects of supramaximal exercise in hypoxia, normoxia and hyperoxia on cardiopulmonary function in Thoroughbred horses. Using a crossover design, five well-trained horses were made to run up a 6% grade on a treadmill at supramaximal speeds sustainable for approximately 110 sec (approximately 115% VO2max) while breathing normoxic gas (NO, 21% O2) or hypoxic gas (LO, 15.3% O2) in random order. Horses also ran at the same speed, incline and run time as in NO while breathing hyperoxic gas (HONO, 28.8% O2) and as in LO while breathing normoxic gas (NOLO). Runs were on different days, and cardiopulmonary variables were analyzed with repeated-measures ANOVA and the Holm-Šidák method for pairwise comparisons. Supramaximal speeds differed significantly between NO and LO (14.0 ± 0.5 [SD] m/sec vs. 12.6 ± 0.5 m/sec), but run times to exhaustion did not (112 ± 17 sec vs. 103 ± 14 sec). The VO2max in NO was higher than that in LO (165 ± 11 vs. 120 ± 15 ml (min×kg)), as was the arterial oxygen tension (66 ± 5 vs. 45 ± 2 Torr). Oxygen consumption was increased in HONO and NOLO compared with the values in NO and LO, respectively. Supramaximal exercise in hypoxia induces more severe hypoxemia and decreases VO2max compared with normoxia at the same relative intensity. Conversely, supramaximal exercise in hyperoxia alleviates hypoxemia and increases VO2 compared with normoxia at the same absolute intensity.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

This paper belongs to feature family **C (exercise-physiology/fitness)**. It quantifies the cardiopulmonary ceiling of Thoroughbred aerobic capacity under controlled oxygen conditions, establishing VO2max, arterial oxygen saturation (SaO2), cardiac output, plasma lactate, and running speed as the key fitness parameters that determine race-day performance potential.

The core finding is that VO2max (165 ± 11 ml/min/kg under normoxia) is limited primarily by pulmonary diffusion capacity and SaO2 — not by cardiac output or haemoglobin concentration, which remain constant across conditions. This means that horses that improve VO2max via training (especially hypoxic training) do so through pulmonary adaptation, not cardiovascular enlargement. For finishing-position modelling:

1. **Post-race plasma lactate** is the most accessible fitness marker: lower lactate at the same speed indicates higher aerobic contribution and better VO2max-relative fitness. Lactate decreases significantly when O2 delivery is improved (hyperoxia condition), confirming lactate as an inverse proxy for aerobic fitness.
2. **Heart rate (HR) at maximal exercise** is approximately invariant (~207 bpm) across all conditions — HR alone cannot distinguish aerobic capacity differences between horses at maximal effort. This challenges HR-based training load metrics as fitness proxies at the high end of intensity.
3. **Hypoxic training history**: prior work by the same JRA group (Ohmura et al. 2017 JES 28:41) showed that 3 weeks of hypoxic training increased VO2max in well-trained horses plateaued on normoxic training; this paper explains the mechanism (more severe hypoxemia → greater stimulus for aerobic adaptation).
4. The absolute VO2max figures (120–194 ml/min/kg across conditions) provide a reference frame for estimating aerobic capacity of JRA-level horses from training monitoring data.

## Background & objective

Hypoxic training is used to enhance athletic performance in humans and horses. JRA's own group (Ohmura et al. 2017) previously demonstrated that 3 weeks of hypoxic (15% O2) training increased VO2max in 5 well-trained Thoroughbreds that had plateaued on normoxic training. The specific cardiopulmonary stimulus driving this adaptation was unclear. Thoroughbreds are known to suffer exercise-induced arterial hypoxemia (EIAH) under normoxic conditions due to diffusion limitation (PaO2 < 75 Torr during maximal exercise).

This study tested two hypotheses:

1. Hypoxic supramaximal exercise induces more severe hypoxemia than normoxic exercise at the same relative intensity → confirmed
2. Hyperoxic supramaximal exercise alleviates hypoxemia and increases VO2 at the same absolute intensity → confirmed

These confirm that cardiopulmonary responses during supramaximal exercise are primarily driven by pulmonary O2 diffusion capacity, with cardiac output as a fixed ceiling.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 5 well-trained Thoroughbreds (2 females, 3 geldings), mean body weight 490 ± 38 kg SD, mean age 5.2 ± 0.5 years, from the Equine Research Institute herd. All horses had undergone preliminary surgery to move the carotid artery from the carotid sheath to a subcutaneous location to facilitate arterial catheterization.

**Treadmill:** Mustang 2200, Ansorix Systems AG, Switzerland. 6% grade throughout. Warm-up: 2 min walk (1.7 m/s) + 3 min trot (4 m/s). Then 2 min each at 1.7, 4.0, and 7.0 m/s before the test run.

**Experimental conditions (4 runs per horse, crossover, once per week):**

| Condition                  | Gas           | FIO2        | Speed          | Run time   |
| -------------------------- | ------------- | ----------- | -------------- | ---------- |
| NO (normoxic all-out)      | Normal air    | 21.0%       | 14.0 ± 0.5 m/s | 112 ± 17 s |
| LO (hypoxic all-out)       | Hypoxic gas   | 15.4 ± 0.3% | 12.6 ± 0.5 m/s | 103 ± 14 s |
| HONO (hyperoxic, NO speed) | Hyperoxic gas | 28.8 ± 0.7% | 14.0 ± 0.5 m/s | 112 ± 17 s |
| NOLO (normoxic, LO speed)  | Normal air    | 21.0%       | 12.6 ± 0.5 m/s | 103 ± 14 s |

- Speed for NO and LO set to produce exhaustion at ~110 s (≈115% VO2max relative intensity)
- HONO and NOLO ran at the same speed as NO and LO but with different gas
- Gas mixing: mass flow controller (Model DPM-3, Kofloc, Tokyo) + compressed N2 or O2 via gas manifold; O2 monitored continuously (METS-900 O2 analyzer)

**Instrumentation:**

- Heart rate: Polar S810 chest electrode ECG monitor
- Arterial catheter: 18-gauge × 6.4 cm Teflon (Surflo, Terumo) in carotid artery
- Swan-Ganz catheter: 8 Fr × 9 cm introducer via jugular vein; tip in pulmonary artery (position confirmed by pulmonary artery pressure waveform via Statham P23d transducer)
- Gas mask: T-shaped lightweight PVC mask with rubber diaphragm over muzzle; overhead pulley/elastic cord support
- VO2 measurement: N2-dilution technique (modified for non-20.95% inspired O2, Fedak et al. 1981)
- VCO2 measurement: CO2-addition technique (Jones et al. 1989)
- Gas analyzers: dried with CaSO4; O2 analyzed after NaOH CO2 removal; A/D recording with DI-720-USB DATAQ hardware + WinDaq Pro+ software
- Blood gas: ABL800 FLEX analyzer (Radiometer) for PaO2, PaCO2, PvO2; ABL80 FLEX CO-OX for SaO2 and [Hb]
- Cardiac output: Fick principle from Swan-Ganz thermodilution; stroke volume = CO/HR
- O2 delivery = CaO2 × cardiac output
- avdiff = CaO2 − CvO2
- Plasma lactate: Biosen C-Line Glucose and Lactate analyzer (EKF-diagnostic GmbH, Barleben, Germany)
- PCV: centrifugation 12,000 rpm (KH120A, Kubota)

**Statistics:** Repeated-measures ANOVA; Holm-Šidák post-hoc pairwise comparisons (P ≤ 0.05). SigmaStat 3.5 (Systat Software).

**Ethics:** Approved by Animal Welfare and Ethics Committee of the Equine Research Institute.

**Key quantitative findings:**

_VO2max and speed:_

- Maximal speed: NO (14.0 ± 0.5 m/s) significantly faster than LO (12.6 ± 0.5 m/s); P significant
- Run times not significantly different: NO 112 ± 17 s vs. LO 103 ± 14 s
- VO2max: NO (165 ± 11 ml/min/kg) >> LO (120 ± 15 ml/min/kg); 27% reduction in hypoxia

- Heart rate: 203–207 beat/min (all conditions)
- Cardiac output: 684–737 ml/min/kg (all conditions, ns)
- Stroke volume: 3.36–3.53 ml/kg (all conditions, ns)
- PCV: 60.1–61.3% (all conditions, ns)
- [Hb]: 22.9–23.4 g/dl (all conditions, ns)

_O2 transport (significantly different):_

- SaO2 ranks: HONO (93.7%) >> NOLO (83.0%) > NO (77.9%) >> LO (59.1%); all 4 significantly different from each other
- PaO2: HONO (118.2 Torr) >> NO/NOLO (~66–68 Torr) >> LO (45.3 Torr)
- CaO2: HONO (29.7 ml/dl) > NO/NOLO (~25–26 ml/dl) >> LO (18.7 ml/dl)

_Plasma lactate:_

- LO and NO: 21.6 ± 3.8 vs. 20.1 ± 1.9 mmol/l (not significantly different from each other)
- NOLO: 15.1 ± 3.3 mmol/l (significantly less than LO)
- HONO: 15.8 ± 2.0 mmol/l (significantly less than NO)
- Interpretation: when O2 delivery is improved at the same speed (NOLO > LO; HONO > NO), aerobic contribution increases and lactate decreases; when running at maximum relative intensity (LO all-out vs. NO all-out), lactate accumulation is equal because anaerobic contribution is approximately equal for equal exhaustion durations

## Discussion & interpretation

The key mechanistic finding is that Thoroughbred VO2max is limited by pulmonary O2 diffusion (SaO2 falls to 78% in normoxia and 59% in hypoxia), not by cardiac output or haemoglobin concentration (both invariant across conditions). Hypoxic breathing exacerbates this diffusion limitation, reducing SaO2 further and constraining O2 delivery; hyperoxic breathing alleviates it, raising SaO2 to 93.7% and allowing more aerobic ATP production per unit time.

The equal lactate concentrations at exhaustion in LO vs. NO (both ~20–22 mmol/l) despite different speeds and VO2max values reflect that the anaerobic contribution to a ~110-s maximal effort is approximately equal when run to exhaustion, regardless of oxygen environment. The difference is in the aerobic share of total energy expenditure — higher in normoxia (VO2 = 165 vs. 120 ml/min/kg), lower in hypoxia.

The decrease in lactate when running at the same absolute speed under better-oxygenated conditions (NOLO < LO; HONO < NO) demonstrates the direct aerobic capacity benefit: at fixed power output, less anaerobic glycolysis is needed when O2 delivery is higher. This is precisely the mechanism by which VO2max improvement from hypoxic training translates to lower lactate at race speeds.

HR-invariance is particularly important for practical implications: maximal HR of ~207 bpm is a physiological ceiling for Thoroughbreds that is not shifted by oxygen environment. This means that HR-based training monitoring cannot distinguish horses with different VO2max values at maximal effort; lactate-based fitness testing is more sensitive.

The authors also note that even at HONO (93.7% SaO2), there is potential room for further VO2 improvement with higher O2 concentrations (hyperoxia 50% in humans increases maximal power output), suggesting Thoroughbreds could theoretically run faster still with higher inspired O2 — confirming that normal atmospheric O2 does not fully saturate their aerobic potential.

## Limitations

- **n = 5** horses, all from the JRA Equine Research Institute herd; well-trained, older (mean 5.2 yr), geldings/mares — not a representative sample of race-day competition horses
- Horses had undergone carotid artery surgery (moved subcutaneously); minor procedural modification
- Crossover design with 4 conditions (weekly runs) introduces fatigue/adaptation ordering effects despite randomization of NO/LO order; HONO and NOLO always matched to NO and LO speeds respectively (not fully randomized)
- Treadmill at 6% grade differs from flat racing conditions; treadmill speed–power relationship does not directly map to racecourse speed
- Swan-Ganz thermodilution cardiac output measurement is invasive and not available in a field/racing context; limits direct translation to non-invasive monitoring
- No direct training-intervention arm: this study characterises acute responses, not the training adaptation effect

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `post_race_blood_lactate_mmol_l` — if JRA training/veterinary records include post-exercise blood lactate, values significantly below 20 mmol/l after a maximal effort indicate higher aerobic fitness (VO2max-relative); source: clinical records (not standard race results); expected sign: negative association with finishing position (lower lactate = fitter = better finisher)
- `training_altitude_flag` — horses trained in hypoxic environments (altitude or hypoxic treadmill facilities) may have higher VO2max than normoxia-trained peers; source: trainer/stable records; expected sign: positive for performance at all distances
- `training_intensity_fatigue_score` — number of high-intensity works in preceding 14 days; excessive loading without recovery depresses aerobic capacity temporarily; source: training records with speed/distance data
- `max_hr_bpm` — maximal HR ~207 bpm is approximately fixed across fitness levels in Thoroughbreds; HR-based training load metrics (heart rate × duration) may be misleading as fitness proxies at the high end; cautionary feature — do NOT use max HR alone as a fitness discriminator
- `vo2max_estimate_ml_min_kg` — if treadmill test data available (e.g., from JRA Sports Science Division), direct VO2max is the strongest predictor of aerobic performance; reference: 165 ± 11 ml/min/kg for well-trained JRA horses under normoxia; source: not in public race data
- **Caution:** All physiological variables from this study require laboratory measurement; the direct features are not available in standard JRA race entry/result data; use as mechanistic motivation for surrogate features (rest intervals, workload indices, post-race recovery time)
