## Abstract (verbatim)

> A bicompartment model has been proposed to describe the distribution of lactate between the active space—muscles—and passive space —plasma and red blood cells—. This transport could be conditioned by the possible existence of a membrane-carrier, blood pH, the concentration of several hormones, the haematocrit value and the haemoglobin concentration. The purpose of this study was to determine the influence exercised by some haematological parameters in the lactate transport between plasma and red blood cells. Twelve male 4-year-old Andalusian horses were subjected to an exercise test, composed of 5 bouts at 4, 5, 6, 7 and 8 m/s, over a distance of 1,000 m at each level. The horses were given a 3 min. rest between each bout. Venous blood samples were collected at rest, on finishing each level of exercise and at 6, 10, 15 and 30 min of an active recuperation. In blood, pH, RBC, PCV, Hb, MCV, MCH and MCHC were determined. Lactate concentrations were analyzed in plasma and hemolysed blood. Other indices, such as VLA4 (Velocity at a lactate concentration of 4 mmol/l), Rtd (Rate of lactate disappearance) and ΔLA (Increase in lactate concentrations) were calculated. The highest lactate concentrations were found inside the erythrocytes. The lower values corresponded to plasma, whilst the mean concentrations were observed in hemolysed blood. These differences were only significant at rest and at the two initial levels of exercise. As a consequence, there were found significant differences between VLA4, Rtd and ΔLA determined in the three media. A positive correlation between blood lactate, PCV, Hb and RBC and a negative correlation to pH were observed. Plasma and erythrocyte lactate showed a negative correlation to pH. Finally, intraerythrocyte lactate was positively correlated to MCH and MCHC.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

This paper belongs to **Feature family C (exercise-physiology/fitness)**. It characterises blood lactate dynamics and fitness indices (VLA4, Rtd, ΔLA) across three measurement media — plasma (PLA), whole/hemolysed blood (BLA), and intraerythrocyte (RBCLA) — during an incremental exercise test and a 30-minute active recovery in non-racing horses. The central finding for modelling is that **VLA4 differs enormously depending on the measurement medium**: PVLA4 = 6.09 m/s vs. BVLA4 = 4.31 m/s. Any pre-race blood lactate feature in a racing database must therefore carry a flag for measurement type or the values will be inconsistent across labs.

VLA4 (velocity at 4 mmol/l blood lactate) is the most widely used aerobic-fitness proxy in horse racing physiology (Persson 1983; Harkins et al. 1993) and correlates directly with racing ability in Thoroughbreds. The haematological co-variates — PCV, Hb, RBC — are available from routine pre-race blood panels and positively correlate with blood lactate accumulation during exercise (r = 0.37, 0.33, 0.30 respectively), partly because splenic contraction releases additional RBC that expand the lactate sink. These values plausibly encode training-load-dependent conditioning state.

The large inter-individual variance (SD of BVLA4 = 2.09 m/s, nearly half the mean) underscores that horse-level fixed effects dominate population averages for lactate-based features. A feature derived from population means is likely noisy; a per-horse deviation from that horse's own prior races would be more informative.

The study uses Andalusian horses (not Thoroughbreds), which limits direct quantitative transfer. However, the qualitative relationships between haematological variables and lactate transport are mechanistically grounded in membrane-carrier biology and apply across breeds.

## Background & objective

Freund and Gendry (1978) proposed a bicompartmental lactate model: muscles are the "active space" and plasma + RBC form the "passive space." Lactate transport across the erythrocyte membrane is mediated by a saturable monocarboxylate carrier that co-transports a proton (Dubinsky and Racker 1978; Roth and Brooks 1990). At physiological pH, lactic acid (pK 3.86) exists mainly as a lactate anion. Transport is influenced by blood pH, PCV/Hb (contact surface between plasma and cells), hormones (e.g., adrenaline stimulates sarcolemmal transport), and the Donnan equilibrium with haemoglobin. High inter-individual variation in RBCLA reported by Rainger et al. (1995) and Pöso et al. (1995) was unexplained; the authors hypothesised that PCV, Hb and other haematological parameters modulate this variance. Objective: quantify those relationships in 12 Andalusian horses under a standardised incremental exercise test.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 12 male 4-year-old Andalusian horses, Training and Selection Centre, Military Stud, Jerez de la Frontera (Cádiz), clinically healthy, same management conditions.

**Exercise protocol:** Semi-elliptical sandy track, 1,000 m long. Five bouts at 4, 5, 6, 7, 8 m/s, 1,000 m each, 3-minute rest between bouts. Velocity verified by cassette-player timing signals every 100 m (riders confirmed timing vs. chronometer, difference p ≤ 0.01). Active recuperation for 30 minutes after the final bout.

**Blood sampling:** External jugular vein puncture at rest, at end of each velocity bout, and at 6, 10, 15, 30 minutes of recuperation. Split into heparin-lithium tube (for plasma, centrifuged immediately) and EDTA-3K tube (haematological variables + BLA; 1 ml taken within 5 hr + 2 drops Hemolisian lysing agent). All stored refrigerated; analysis within 12 hr.

**Measurements:**

- RBC, PCV, Hb, MCV, MCH, MCHC: Sysmex-800 automated cell counter
- Plasma lactate (PLA) and hemolysed blood lactate (BLA): Analox P-LM5 Champion
- Intraerythrocyte lactate (RBCLA) calculated: RBCLA = [BLA − (1 − PCV) × PLA] / PCV

**Derived indices:**

- PVLA4, BVLA4, RBCVLA4: velocity at which lactate = 4 mmol/l in each medium
- PRtd, BRtd, RBCRtd: rate of lactate disappearance = (LA8 − LA30)/30 (mmol/l/min)
- ΔLA = LA8 − LArest (mmol/l) in each medium

**Statistics:** Basic descriptive statistics (mean, SD, range). ANOVA to compare lactate concentrations across three compartments. Pearson correlations between lactate in each medium and haematological variables, with exercise stage as the weight variable (p < 0.05 significance level).

## Discussion & interpretation

**Why RBCLA > BLA at rest:** Contrary to most equine studies (where PLA > BLA), the authors attribute the opposite finding to their sample handling procedure — plasma was separated immediately but whole blood remained refrigerated, allowing continued glycolysis in RBC to generate lactate before analysis.

**Saturation at high intensity:** At 7–8 m/s PLA exceeds BLA, indicating that the monocarboxylate carrier is saturated and net flow reverses; lactate can no longer enter RBC fast enough to maintain RBCLA > PLA. This aligns with non-ionic diffusion becoming negligible at these concentrations (Roth and Brooks 1990).

**VLA4 medium dependence:** The 1.77 m/s difference between PVLA4 and BVLA4 is practically large. The authors argue plasma VLA4 better reflects muscle lactate production (less influenced by RBC uptake kinetics), while whole-blood VLA4 better mirrors turnover during prolonged exercise. Cross-lab comparison of VLA4 without specifying medium is unreliable.

**PCV and RBC positive correlation with BLA:** Interpreted as an indirect effect of splenic RBC release: exercise-triggered splenic contraction releases RBC, increasing PCV, which increases the plasma–RBC contact surface and facilitates lactate entry into cells — indirectly elevating BLA. The correlation with BLA (r = 0.37) is modest because the effect is indirect.

**High SD in RBCLA and RBCRtd:** Attributed to inter-individual differences in monocarboxylate carrier density or activity — not explainable by RBC size (MCH/MCHC correlations are very low). The authors recommend calculating individual BLA/PLA conversion factors rather than using a population equation.

**Comparison with prior breeds:** Rubio et al. (1995) showed Andalusian horses have lower PCV and Hb than Anglo-Arab horses during exercise, and slower lactate recovery, perhaps linked to differences in muscle fibre composition (Muñoz et al. 1996).

## Limitations

- Sample size is small (n = 12, and n = 9 for RBCLA due to undetectable values in some horses).
- Andalusian horses are not racehorses; absolute VLA4 values are lower than Thoroughbred norms (~7–8 m/s at PVLA4 in trained Thoroughbreds).
- Blood handling procedure (delayed centrifugation for BLA) introduces artefactual RBC glycolysis, making resting RBCLA higher than in vivo reality.
- Exercise performed on a sandy track (not treadmill), with velocity controlled by cassette timing — some velocity variance possible.
- No direct racing performance outcomes measured; VLA4 association with race results is inferred from other literature (Harkins et al. 1993).
- Only males studied; sex effects on lactate distribution unknown.

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `vla4_plasma_ms` — velocity (m/s) at which plasma lactate reaches 4 mmol/l; most comparable across labs; fitness proxy; **derive from trackwork blood sampling logs if available**; expected effect: higher VLA4 → better finishing position (negative coefficient on finishing rank)
- `vla4_blood_ms` — velocity at 4 mmol/l in whole/hemolysed blood; always lower than PVLA4 by ~1.5–2 m/s in this breed; use only if medium is consistently documented; **do NOT mix with PVLA4**
- `pcv_prerace_pct` — packed cell volume %; available in standard JRA/NAR blood panels; positive correlation with blood lactate accumulation (r = 0.37); higher PCV at rest suggests more available splenic reserve → proxy for fit conditioning state
- `hb_prerace_gdl` — haemoglobin concentration (g/dl); similar mechanistic interpretation to PCV; r with RBCLA = 0.89 (strongest correlate)
- `rbc_prerace_m6mm3` — RBC count; weaker signal (r = 0.30 with BLA); redundant with PCV/Hb but adds marginal independent information
- `blood_ph_prerace` — blood pH at rest; negative correlation with all lactate measures (pH −0.71 vs. PLA); resting acidosis → impaired lactate clearance; **caution**: resting pH is highly sensitive to sample timing and technique
- `rtd_plasma` — rate of plasma lactate disappearance post-exercise (mmol/l/min); encodes recovery speed; **individual-horse calibration needed**; interaction with race distance (longer races tax recovery more)
- `delta_pla` — increase in plasma lactate from rest to peak (mmol/l); anaerobic capacity proxy; individual variation large (4.9–16.9 mmol/l)
- **Interaction term**: `pcv_prerace_pct × distance_m` — PCV effect may be magnified over longer distances where aerobic ceiling matters more
- **Do NOT use** RBCLA as a direct feature — its measurement requires immediate heparinised centrifugation and a Hemolisian lysing step; it is calculated, not directly measured, and lab artefacts strongly affect it
