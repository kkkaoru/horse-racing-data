## Abstract (verbatim)

> Bone mineral density (BMD) is one of the indications of the strength and health. BMD measured by quantitative computed tomography (QCT) was compared with that measured by dual energy X-ray absorptiometry (DXA) and radiographic bone aluminum equivalence (RBAE). Limbs were removed from horses that had been euthanized for reasons not associated with this study. Sixteen limbs (left and right metacarpals and metatarsals) from 4 horses were used to compare BMD as measured by QCT with those measured by DXA and RBAE. There was a strong correlation between BMD values measured by QCT and those measured by DXA (R2=0.85); correlation was also observed between values obtained by QCT and those obtained by RBAE (R2=0.61). To investigate changes in BMD with age, 37 right metacarpal bones, including 7 from horses euthanized because of fracture were examined by QCT. The BMD value of samples from horses dramatically increased until 2 years of age and then plateaued, a pattern similar to the growth curve. The BMD values of bone samples from horses euthanized because of fracture were within the population range, and samples of morbid fracture were not included. The relationship between BMD and age provides a reference for further quantitative studies of bone development and remodeling. Quantitative measurement of BMD using QCT may have great potential for the evaluation of bone biology for breeding and rearing management.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **A (injury/soundness — skeletal)**. The paper establishes that metacarpal BMD in Thoroughbreds rises rapidly until age 2 and then plateaus. This developmental arc has direct implications for scratch/DNF risk in young horses: rigorous race training beginning before BMD plateau (before ~age 2) increases bucked-shin and fatigue-fracture risk. A horse that enters training unusually early or has a high race-count before age 3 carries a higher cumulative skeletal stress load.

A critical nuance: the 7 horses euthanised for fracture (femur, pelvis, sternum) all had metacarpal BMD within the normal population range. This means BMD alone is not a simple fracture discriminator — geometry, accumulated fatigue cycles, and loading pattern matter. For the model, this implies that naive age-at-first-race or early-training-density features should be used cautiously: they proxy skeletal immaturity risk but are not perfectly correlated with catastrophic fracture outcomes.

The paper also establishes that BMD correlates strongly with bone circumference (QCT R²=0.82, DXA R²=0.96, RBAE R²=0.86), which means any available conformation measurement of cannon bone circumference (a standard yearling sale measurement) can serve as a BMD proxy. QCT is the most accurate method but requires anaesthesia; RBAE (radiographic, field-portable) cannot distinguish 1-, 12-, and 14-year-old bone and is inferior for individual-level screening.

## Background & objective

In horses, musculoskeletal problems are a major economic liability. Low BMD increases incidence of orthopaedic problems; bucked shins are more common in Thoroughbreds than Standardbreds because Thoroughbreds begin more intensive early training. Prior equine BMD studies used ultrasonography, X-ray, single-photon absorptiometry, DXA, and CT but did not establish a QCT reference for Japanese Thoroughbreds. The study aimed to: (1) validate QCT against DXA and RBAE; (2) characterise age-BMD relationship in Thoroughbreds to create a quantitative reference for rearing management.

## Materials & methods

**Experiment 1 (QCT vs. DXA vs. RBAE):**

- Sample: 16 limbs (left and right metacarpals and metatarsals) from 4 Thoroughbred horses: ages 1-day-old male, 1-year-old male, 12-year-old female, 14-year-old female.
- Euthanised for reasons unrelated to this study; protocol approved by Obihiro University Animal Experiment and Welfare Committee.
- QCT: Asteion Super 4 (Toshiba, Tokyo); 5-mm slices; B-MAS200 phantom (Fujirebio, Tokyo) for calibration; ROIs at nutrition foramen for whole bone (cortex + center), dorsal cortex, and center; expressed as hydroxyapatite density.
- DXA: Discovery unit (Hologic, Bedford, MA); human femoral neck parameter settings; at nutrition foramen level.
- RBAE: portable X-ray unit TRB9020H (Mikasa, Tokyo); 70 kV/1.5 mAs; aluminum step wedge (1 mm/step, 20 steps); digital radiography AeroDR (Konica Minolta, Tokyo); calibration line from aluminum step densities.

**Experiment 2 (age-BMD relationship):**

- Sample: 37 right metacarpal bones from 37 Thoroughbred horses, ages 0–28 years; 12 males, 25 females; includes 7 from horses euthanised for fracture (femur, pelvis, sternum).
- All measured by QCT using same protocol as Experiment 1.

**Statistics:** Mann-Whitney U test for group comparisons; Pearson correlation coefficient (R²) for method-to-method and BMD-to-circumference relationships; P < 0.05 threshold. Software: Statcel 3 (OMS Publishing) for Excel.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

**Cortex regional BMD comparison (QCT, Experiment 1):**

- Palmar/plantar cortex BMD significantly lower than dorsal, lateral, and medial cortex (P < 0.05); no significant differences among dorsal, lateral, and medial cortex.

**Method comparison (Experiment 1, n=16 limbs):**

| Comparison   | ROI           | R²   | P         |
| ------------ | ------------- | ---- | --------- |
| QCT vs. DXA  | Whole bone    | 0.85 | <0.01     |
| QCT vs. DXA  | Dorsal cortex | 0.85 | <0.01     |
| QCT vs. DXA  | Center        | 0.07 | 0.36 (NS) |
| QCT vs. RBAE | Whole bone    | 0.61 | <0.01     |

RBAE could not distinguish 14-year, 12-year, and 1-year-old bone (all gave similar RBAE values); QCT and DXA both showed decreasing BMD order: 14 yr > 12 yr > 1 yr > 1 day (consistent with growth pattern).

**Hindlimb vs. forelimb BMD:**

- Metatarsal BMD significantly higher than metacarpal when measured by RBAE (P < 0.05).
- No significant forelimb/hindlimb difference when measured by QCT or DXA.
- Authors attribute the RBAE discrepancy to RBAE being strongly affected by bone circumference (metatarsal is larger), which inflates RBAE values.

**BMD vs. bone circumference correlations (across all methods):**

| Method | R²   |
| ------ | ---- |
| QCT    | 0.82 |
| DXA    | 0.96 |
| RBAE   | 0.86 |

QCT has lowest R² with circumference, meaning it is least confounded by bone size — this is a methodological advantage.

**Age-BMD curve (QCT, Experiment 2, n=37 horses, ages 0–28 yr):**

- BMD increased dramatically until age 2 years, then plateaued.
- Pattern mirrors the equine growth curve.
- All 7 bones from fracture-euthanised horses (femur, pelvis, sternum fractures) had BMD within the population range — none were obvious low-BMD outliers.
- No significant sex difference in age-BMD trajectory (female horses did not show a dramatic decline with age, unlike postmenopausal women).

**QCT methodological detail:**

- Coefficient of variation of whole-bone QCT within raw data: <2% — confirms good reproducibility.
- Tube voltage effect (80, 120, 135 kV) tested preliminarily: no significant difference in BMD value, so phantom calibration allows consistent measurement across different CT units and settings.
- Left-right symmetric: no significant difference between left and right metacarpal BMD across all methods.

## Discussion & interpretation

QCT is established as the reference standard for equine BMD because: (1) it is least confounded by bone size (lowest BMD-circumference R²); (2) it distinguishes 1-year, 12-year, and 14-year-old bone (RBAE cannot); (3) its coefficient of variation is <2%. DXA is simpler but not portable and requires anaesthesia in horses; RBAE is field-portable but unreliable for individual-level BMD assessment.

The age-2 BMD plateau mirrors known skeletal maturation in horses: epiphyseal closure progresses from distal to proximal, with the most proximal (cervical vertebra) closing at age 4–5 years. The metacarpal BMD plateau at age 2 coincides with the end of rapid appendicular bone growth and explains why horses that begin rigorous race training before age 2 have elevated bucked-shin incidence.

The finding that fracture-euthanised horses had normal metacarpal BMD range suggests catastrophic fractures are not primarily BMD failures — they reflect accumulated fatigue damage, geometric stress concentrations, or a single extreme loading event. This is consistent with epidemiological data showing most catastrophic fractures occur on race day, not in training.

The palmar cortex having significantly lower BMD than dorsal/lateral/medial cortex is relevant because palmar cortex is the tension side of the metacarpal during gallop loading, and palmar cortical fatigue failure is a mechanism for condylar fractures.

The absence of age-related BMD decline in mares (unlike human postmenopausal osteoporosis) is attributed to continuous reproductive cycling throughout life, preventing estrogen withdrawal.

Bell et al. (cited) reported that stalling horses for months before yearling sales (to improve coat condition) reduces BMD, creating a training-onset bone-strength penalty. This supports number-of-stall-days before first race as a potential risk feature.

## Limitations

- Experiment 1 used only 4 horses at 4 age points (1 day, 1 year, 12, 14 years) — too few for population-level normative data; Experiment 2 n=37 provides a reference but is still small given the 0–28-year age range.
- Excised limb specimens — no in vivo loading; BMD in active racehorses may differ (exercise increases BMD by 3–18% in published equine treadmill studies).
- Fracture-euthanised horses were a mare and a service horse with "excellent racing records" — not representative of the BMD range in fracture cases in active racehorses.
- No morbid fracture (fracture as cause of death) cases included — the study cannot exclude that morbid-fracture horses would show lower BMD than population average.
- No serum hormonal measurements possible from excised specimens; cannot test estrogen/PTH effects.

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `races_before_age_3` — count of races run before 3rd birthday — derivation: race history records — expected effect: positive on orthopaedic injury/scratch risk (excessive early skeletal loading before BMD plateau) — data availability: available in JRA records
- `debut_age_months` — age in months at first race — derivation: (first_race_date − birth_date) / 30.44 — expected effect: earlier debut → higher bucked-shin risk — available in JRA records
- `cannon_bone_circumference` — cm, measured at yearling sale if available — derivation: JBBA sale records — expected effect: positive on BMD and bone strength (R²=0.82–0.96 with BMD); proxy for skeletal robustness — data availability: JBBA yearling catalogue data (not always in race records)
- `stall_rest_days_before_debut` — days spent in restricted exercise before first race — derivation: training record — expected effect: positive on BMD deficit/injury risk (stalling reduces BMD) — data availability: likely not in public race records
- `injury_history_bone` — flag for documented bucked shin, sesamoiditis, splint, or stress fracture — derivation: JRA veterinary records — expected effect: strong positive on future scratch risk — data availability: restricted to JRA internal records
- Caution: do NOT use metacarpal BMD as a fracture predictor without acknowledging that fracture horses had normal-range BMD in this study; BMD-derived features should be framed as injury-risk (orthopaedic problems leading to missed races) rather than catastrophic-fracture risk.
