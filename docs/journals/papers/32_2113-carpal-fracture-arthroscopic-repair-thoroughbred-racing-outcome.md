## Abstract (verbatim)

> An 8-month-old thoroughbred colt presented with sudden onset right forelimb lameness. A radiographic series of the right carpus was performed, and it revealed a slab fracture of the fourth carpal bone and fracture of the proximal part of the third metacarpal bone. Arthroscopically guided repair of the slab fracture of the fourth carpal bone with a 3.5 mm cortex screw and lag screw fixation of the fracture of the proximal part of the third metacarpal bone were performed. The horse started to race at 32 months old and started in 65 races over three years without any trouble associated with the right carpus.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

This paper belongs to feature family **A (injury/soundness — return-to-racing prognosis)**. It documents the first confirmed racing return after arthroscopic lag-screw repair of a fourth carpal bone slab fracture, with the horse subsequently competing in 65 races over three years (5 wins) with no recurrence.

Carpal bone slab fractures are a well-documented injury type in racehorses. For finishing-position prediction, a horse's injury history (fracture type, carpal bone affected, surgical modality, months since surgery) is a clinically meaningful feature group for several reasons:

1. Return-to-racing probability differs by fracture type and treatment: open arthrotomy for C4 slab fractures historically yielded 0/5 successful returns (Auer et al. 1986); arthroscopic repair of C3 slab fractures yields 42–63% return; this case adds the first positive outcome for arthroscopic C4 repair.
2. Post-surgical performance trajectory: the horse had a competitive career (65 starts, 5 wins) despite early injury at 8 months, suggesting that surgically treated carpal fractures with good joint congruity restoration do not systematically depress finishing position.
3. Career interruption length: from injury (8 months old) to first race (32 months old) = 24 months post-surgery. Horses with carpal fracture history and >18-month gap between last start and current entry may have successfully recovered rather than declining.

The paper is a single-case report; all statistics are contextual (from the referenced literature), not derived from a controlled cohort. Direct inferential value is limited but the qualitative clinical information is useful for coding injury history variables.

## Background & objective

Slab fractures of the carpal bones most commonly involve the third carpal bone (C3); fourth carpal bone (C4) slab fractures are uncommon. Prior attempts to treat C4 slab fractures by open arthrotomy (screw fixation or otherwise) resulted in poor outcomes: Auer et al. (1986) reported that none of 5 horses with arthrotomy-treated C4 or combined C4/intermediate carpal slab fractures returned to intended use. A single prior case of sagittal C4 slab fracture treated by arthrotomy in a Standardbred foal had no follow-up beyond 8 months (Field & Zaruby 1994). Arthroscopic repair has become the standard for C3 slab fractures (Richardson 1986 technique), allowing accurate articular surface reconstruction; the authors hypothesised that the same technique could be adapted for C4 fractures.

## Materials & methods

**Subject:** Single 8-month-old, 256 kg, Thoroughbred colt; Hidaka, Hokkaido (Kushima breeding region). Presented with sudden-onset right forelimb lameness; unable to bear weight.

**Physical examination on admission (day after onset):** AAEP grade 4/5 lameness; flexion of carpus resented; entire carpus region swollen; no external wounds.

**Imaging:** Dorsomedial-palmarolateral oblique radiographic view confirmed slab fracture of fourth carpal bone and fracture of proximal part of the third metacarpal bone.

**Anaesthesia protocol:** Preoperative dihydrostreptomycin sulfate 12.5 mg/kg IM and benzylpenicillin procaine 10,000 IU/kg IM; sedation with medetomidine 6 µg/kg IV + butorphanol 0.01 mg/kg IV; induction with ketamine 2.2 mg/kg IV + GGE 500 mg/head IV. Squeeze gate used. Dorsal recumbency, right limb extended.

**Surgical procedure:**

- Dorsomedial arthroscopic portal into middle carpal joint
- Chip fractures of C3 and intermediate carpal bone removed
- Carpal flexion adjusted to ~50° (smaller than standard 70° for C3 arthroscopy) to visualise C4 fracture line
- Glide hole drilled to fracture plane with 3.5 mm drill; thread hole drilled into C4 body; countersink created
- Single 32 mm × 3.5 mm cortex screw inserted as lag screw; compression confirmed arthroscopically
- Proximal third metacarpal bone articular fracture: additional 3.5 mm cortex screw in lag fashion
- Skin portals closed with 2-0 Poliglecaprone 25; padded dressing applied

**Post-operative management:**

- Procaine penicillin 10,000 IU/kg IM once daily for 8 days
- Stall confinement: 2 months
- Limited paddock exercise: months 3–7
- Large pasture: from 8 months post-surgery
- Serial radiographs: progressive healing, no osteomyelitis signs

**Follow-up:** Racing career tracked via stable records; 3 years of post-first-race follow-up.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

**Immediate post-operative recovery:**

- Could bear weight on forelimb on day 1 post-surgery
- Sound at walk: post-operative day 7
- Sound at trot: 3 months post-surgery
- No osteomyelitis on serial radiographs over 5 months

**Return to training and racing timeline:**

- 8 months post-surgery: sound during free exercise in large pasture
- 32 months of age = first race (approximately 24 months post-surgery, given injury at 8 months)
- Racing career: 65 starts over 3 years, 5 wins
- No right forelimb lameness throughout 3-year racing career

**Surgical technique adaptation:** Flexion angle of 50° (vs. standard 70° for C3 arthroscopy) improved arthroscopic visualisation of the C4 fracture line because C4 fracture lies more caudal than the typical C3 fracture location.

## Discussion & interpretation

The authors conclude that the Richardson (1986) arthroscopic technique for C3 slab fractures can be adapted to C4 slab fractures by adjusting the carpal flexion angle. The case demonstrates that early surgical intervention for C4 slab fractures results in progressive radiographic healing and a competitive racing career, contrasting with the uniformly poor outcomes following arthrotomy in the historical case series.

The injury likely resulted from an abnormal concentration of force in the right carpus during pasture activity (colt found with acute-onset lameness in paddock), rather than cumulative high-speed exercise fatigue (which would be the typical mechanism in older racehorses). The foal's 256 kg body weight at 8 months and rapid growth may have created transient biomechanical vulnerability of the carpal bones.

The metacarpal articular fracture is itself unusual (articular fractures of the dorsoproximolateral aspect of the MC3 bone are not previously reported in Thoroughbreds), and the displaced nature of the fragment justified internal fixation. The functional outcome in the present case was superior to the mixed results in the Standardbred literature for similar metacarpal fractures (one horse retired due to continued lameness; one raced 7 months post-surgery).

## Limitations

- Single-case report: no statistical inference possible; prognosis for C4 slab fracture cannot be generalised from n = 1
- No comparison group (other C4 slab fractures managed differently)
- Concurrent fracture (proximal MC3) complicates attribution of outcomes to the C4 repair specifically
- Follow-up limited to 3 years; long-term carpal osteoarthritis trajectory unknown
- The horse was a foal at injury (256 kg, 8 months), and skeletal immaturity may have contributed to both injury susceptibility and healing capacity; results may not generalise to adult racehorses

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `carpal_fracture_history` — binary flag for any carpal bone slab fracture in career history; source: injury/surgery records (where available from JRA veterinary data); expected directional effect: modest negative on finishing position in the immediate post-return period, neutral to positive once 12+ months post-surgery
- `carpal_bone_affected` — categorical: C3 vs. C4 vs. other; C4 with arthroscopic repair has better-than-historical prognosis (this case), but C3 has the larger published evidence base; available only if injury detail records exist
- `months_since_fracture_surgery` — continuous; return-to-racing for C3 arthroscopic: ~43–63% within variable timeframes; for C4 (this case): 24 months; injury-induced absence of 12–24 months followed by a race start is a positive prognostic signal relative to 0% arthrotomy return rate; source: race gap + surgery date
- `racing_gap_months` — gap between last race start and current entry; a gap of >18 months following a carpal injury flag may indicate surgical recovery (better prognosis) vs. age-related decline (worse); interaction with age
- **Caution:** Injury history features require veterinary records not universally available in public JRA race data; proxy via anomalous race-start gaps combined with body-weight changes
