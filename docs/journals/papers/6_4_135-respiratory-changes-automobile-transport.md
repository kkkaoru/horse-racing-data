## Abstract (verbatim)

> Transportation is generally recognized as a factor that raises the incidence of respiratory disorders in horses. However, their relationship to changes in respiratory function of horses during transportation have yet to be clarified in many respects. Furthermore, there is as yet no report concerning changes in arterial blood gas partial pressure and other parameters that reflect pulmonary ventilatory state in transported horses. In view of this, we conducted this study to clarify the relationship of transport with ventilatory function by investigating changes in respiratory pattern. Respiratory rate increased markedly as transport started (19.0±1.2/min. → 42.4±8.2/min.), remained increased during the course of transport, and diminished close to a pre-transport level while at a stop. Arterial blood gases were not affected by respiratory rate, and did not reveal any significant change except a mild hyperventilatory state seen in febrile horses during transport.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family: **B (respiratory)** and **E (environment/transport stress)**.

This study directly measures the respiratory cost of truck transportation in young Thoroughbreds, documenting a 2.2-fold increase in respiratory rate (19.0 → 42.4/min) that persists throughout transit and abates only when the vehicle stops. For finishing-position prediction, this establishes the physiological mechanism behind "shipping fever" and transport-related performance degradation: sustained tachypnoea constitutes a large increase in respiratory workload, enhancing airway exposure to dust and pathogens, while simultaneously indicating sympathetic activation that disrupts homeostatic recovery before racing.

A concrete feature implication: travel duration (hours in transit) to race venue and time elapsed from arrival to race start are proxy variables for the cumulative respiratory burden and sympathetic recovery gap. Horses transported for 41 hours (Group A) or 60 hours (Group B) would each have spent essentially all of those hours at ~42 breaths/min rather than their resting 9.3/min. The lack of arterial blood gas change in afebrile horses means the elevated respiratory rate is dead-space ventilation (anatomical dead-space expansion), not compensatory hypoxic ventilation — the effective gas exchange is not impaired acutely, but the upper airway clearance and mucosal defences are. This is consistent with respiratory disease appearing 16–25 hours into transit (fever onset time for animals No. 2 and No. 6).

Combined with companion epidemiological paper 6_1_25 (Oikawa & Kusunose) showing increased disease incidence with transport duration, this paper provides the mechanistic link: sustained tachypnoea drives excess deposition of noxious particles and dust, inhibits mucosal clearance, and in horses that become febrile the dead-space ventilation transitions to true hyperventilation with mild respiratory alkalosis (decreasing PaCO2, increasing pHa).

## Background & objective

Transportation is a known risk factor for equine respiratory disorders [ref 9]. Several mechanisms have been proposed: increased dust and lowered relative humidity in the vehicle [refs 8, 9], pre-existing respiratory tract inflammation [refs 9, 10], sympathetic nervous system activation causing adrenocortical steroid hypersecretion, plasma sodium and glucose elevation, and plasma aldosterone reduction [refs 1, 11]. Transport has also been shown to suppress pulmonary immune function [refs 2, 5]. Despite this epidemiological evidence, no prior study had directly measured intratracheal pressure and arterial blood gas parameters continuously during transport.

Two specific aims: (1) to characterise the change in respiratory pattern (rate, depth via intratracheal pressure) during transport, and (2) to determine whether the altered respiratory pattern affects pulmonary ventilatory function as reflected by arterial blood gases (PaO2, PaCO2, pHa).

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** Five clinically healthy young Thoroughbreds (2 colts, 3 fillies; age 27–29 months old) from the Equine Research Institute, Japan Racing Association. Arranged in two groups with different transport durations (Table 1):

- Group A: 3 horses (Nos. 1, 2, 3); total 41 hours of transit; seven serial 5-hour transport sessions each with a 30-min stop (water supply) and a 1-hour rest between sessions
- Group B: 3 horses (Nos. 4, 5, 6); total 60 hours of transit; ten serial sessions of the same 5-hour pattern
- Note: one filly appeared in both groups (horses No. 3 and No. 5 are the same animal)
- All three horses in each group faced forward in the truck

**Rectal temperatures (Table 1):** Animal No. 2 (Group A, female): maximum 39.2°C (febrile, onset 16 h into transport). Animal No. 6 (Group B, male): maximum 40.4°C (febrile, onset 25 h into transport). Remaining animals remained afebrile (max 38.2–38.3°C).

**Respiratory pattern measurement (intratracheal pressure, P_IT):**

- Two horses in Group A (Nos. 1, 2) and two in Group B (Nos. 4, 6) were instrumented
- Upper 1/3 of the ventral neck shaved and locally anaesthetised with 1 mL of 2% lidocaine (Xylocaine; Fujisawa Pharmaceutical, Japan)
- A 7F balloon catheter (Thermodilution Catheter; Baxter Ltd., Japan) introduced into the tracheal lumen via an inserted 10-gauge I.V. indwelling needle (Angiocut; Nippon Becton Dickinson, Japan), taped to the neck
- Pressure transducer: Lifekit DX-360 (NEC San-ei Instruments); amplifier: Propaq 106 (NEC San-ei); recorder: Propaq Printer 001–0007 (NEC San-ei)
- Waves above baseline = expirations; waves below baseline = inspirations (Fig. 1)
- Respiratory rate = number of negative pressure swings in P_IT per minute

**Blood gas measurement:**

- Subcutaneous carotid arterial loop prepared in 5 horses (3 in Group A, 3 in Group B)
- Blood sampled approximately every 6 hours
- Measured: pHa, PaO2, PaCO2, base excess, HCO3−, SaO2 using blood gas analyser (ABL-510; Radiometer Inc., Copenhagen); temperature corrections applied using rectal temperature

**Environmental measurements:** Ambient temperature and relative humidity within the vehicle measured bihourly with a thermohygrometer (Table 2):

- Group A: max temperature 28.6°C, min 22.3°C; max relative humidity 85.0%, min 58.3%
- Group B: max temperature 34.8°C (6.2°C higher than Group A), min 27.7°C; max relative humidity 79.8%, min 63.1%

**Statistical presentation:** Data as mean ± standard deviation.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

**Respiratory rate — core findings (Fig. 2):**

| Condition                                      | Mean respiratory rate (breaths/min) |
| ---------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Pre-transport (at rest in stall)               | 9.3 ± 1.0                           |
| At a stop (vehicle stationary, engine running) | 19.0 ± 1.2                          |
| During transit (vehicle moving)                | 42.4 ± 8.2                          |

- The respiratory rate increased markedly as transport started and remained elevated throughout the entire transit period.
- At each stop the rate diminished close to the stop-level (19.0/min), not to the pre-transport stall level.
- The pattern was repeated consistently for each 5-hour session.
- The mean respiratory rate during transit was higher by 12.7/min in Group B (Nos. 4, 6; mean ~55/min) than in Group A (Nos. 1, 2; mean ~42/min); Group B also experienced higher ambient temperature (+6.2°C), suggesting temperature contributed to the higher rate in Group B.
- In febrile horses (Nos. 2 and 6), the respiratory rate during transit increased by about 30–50% above the rate seen in these same animals in their afebrile phase.
- The rate tended to diminish slightly with progressing transit time (adaptation to vehicle joggling), but remained substantially elevated throughout.

**Note on engine start vs. transport start (Fig. 1 tracings):**
The P_IT tracings show that respiratory rate increased slightly as the engine was started, and increased markedly at the actual start of forward movement. The increase is therefore predominantly motion-related rather than engine-noise related.

**Arterial blood gases (Fig. 3):**

_Afebrile horses (Nos. 1, 3, 4, 5 — presented as mean ± SD):_

- pHa: essentially stable throughout 0–60 hours of transport; mean ~7.40–7.42 with no systematic trend
- PaCO2: stable ~40–47 mmHg throughout; no significant trend
- PaO2: stable ~90–110 mmHg throughout; no significant change

_Febrile horse No. 6 (Group B, onset 25 h):_

- After 35 hours of transport: tendency for PaCO2 to decrease (toward ~38 mmHg) and pHa to increase (toward ~7.43–7.44), indicating mild respiratory alkalosis/hyperventilatory state
- These changes were not statistically significant across the group but represent a clinically meaningful individual-level shift

_No significant changes noted for base excess, HCO3−, or SaO2 in either febrile or afebrile horses._

**Physical interpretation (authors' mechanistic conclusion):**
The marked tachypnoea during transit is NOT associated with altered blood gas composition in afebrile horses. Therefore the increased respiratory rate represents primarily anatomical dead-space ventilation — the increase in breathing frequency does not enhance alveolar gas exchange but does increase the volume of air passing over the upper respiratory tract. This increases deposition of airborne particles and noxious gases onto the mucosa and inhibits upper airway clearance mechanisms.

## Discussion & interpretation

The paper confirms empirical knowledge that horses breathe faster during truck transport. The mechanistic driver remains unclear but is attributed to vehicle vibration perceived via proprioceptors in muscles, tendons, and joints (reflex tachypnoea [ref 4]), plus emotional/CNS excitation. The vehicle's vertical acceleration during transit (0.7G in the plus x-axis direction, 0.8G in the minus direction; mean vertical acceleration 0.2G in each direction, exceeding human "allowable limits" of 0.2G [ref 7]) supports proprioceptive stimulation as the primary mechanism.

The fact that the rate at a stop (19.0/min) — while the engine continued running — was roughly double the stall rest rate (9.3/min), but far below the moving transit rate (42.4/min), confirms that engine vibration plays a role but vehicle motion is the dominant driver.

The intergroup difference (Group B ~12.7/min higher) is attributed at least partly to the higher ambient temperature in Group B (max 34.8°C vs. 28.6°C), affecting heat exchange in the respiratory tract.

The finding that arterial blood gases were unchanged in afebrile horses despite the 2.2× respiratory rate increase is mechanistically important: increased respiratory rate during transport reflects dead-space ventilation, not true hyperventilation with CO2 washout. The tachypnoea therefore constitutes a severe increase in the physical churning of potentially contaminated air over the airway mucosa without providing compensatory benefit to gas exchange. This explains why transport predisposes to respiratory disease without causing acute hypoxia.

In febrile horses, the slight decrease in PaCO2 and increase in pHa at 35+ hours indicates that fever-associated true hyperventilation begins only after respiratory disease has been established — a lag of ~16–25 hours from transport start.

## Limitations

- Very small sample: n=5 horses (6 transport sessions, one horse appearing in both groups), all 27–29 months old from a single institution.
- Intratracheal pressure measurement was technically demanding and achieved in only 4 of 5 horses (2 per group); horse No. 3/No. 5 did not have respiratory measurement due to equipment limitations.
- No controls (horses transported but with the truck stationary, or horses exercised at equivalent respiratory rates) to fully isolate transport-specific effects on mucosal defences.
- The study design (7 or 10 serial 5-hour sessions with 30-min water stops) may not represent typical single-destination transport durations to race venues.
- Ambient dust and particle measurements were not taken, leaving the hypothesised mechanism of increased noxious particle deposition unquantified.

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `transport_duration_hours` — total hours in vehicle transit from origin stable to race venue — source: JRA/NAR race entry records (origin stable location × race venue) — expected effect: positive association with respiratory disease risk, negative association with next-race finishing position; likely non-linear (little effect below ~4 h, sharper above ~12 h)
- `hours_since_arrival` — time elapsed from arrival at race venue to race start — source: race-day schedule data — expected effect: longer recovery time → better performance; horses arriving day-before vs. same-day represent a meaningful binary
- `transport_ambient_temp` — estimated temperature in vehicle during transit (season × route) — source: weather/date/departure-time — expected effect: higher temperature → higher respiratory rate during transit (+12.7/min per 6°C difference observed); interaction with transport duration
- `febrile_within_transport` — binary indicator: horse developed fever ≥38.5°C during or just after transport — source: veterinary records — expected effect: strong predictor of DNF/scratch/poor finish; fever onset ~16–25 h into transit in 2/5 horses (40% incidence in this study)
- `respiratory_rate_elevation_proxy` — composite = transport_hours × (1 + temp_excess_above_25C / 10) — no direct data source; must be computed — expected effect: proxy for total dead-space ventilation load during transport
- **DO NOT USE** arterial blood gas features (PaO2, PaCO2, pHa) as proxies for transport stress in afebrile horses — blood gases are stable despite severe tachypnoea; the stress pathway is mucosal exposure, not hypoxia
