## Abstract (verbatim)

> Seven horses were exercised five days a week for 12 months at different gaits on a treadmill, and eight horses were pastured freely in a paddock without any training programs. They were 60 days of age at the beginning of the experiment. We compared the number of C cells in the thyroid, ultrastructure of chief cells in the parathyroid and radiography of the third metatarsal bone of the training group with the control group. In the training group, the number of C cells was not increased and chief cells were not in a phase of secretion. In addition, the bone mineral content and the cortical area did not show high values. We considered that an additional exercise used in this study had suppressive effects on the thyroid and parathyroid. Our results also suggest an additional and/or intense exercise in early stage of life (foal to weanling) may have an adverse effect on bone growth and metabolism.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family: **A (injury/soundness) with sub-theme of early-career training load and bone-endocrine interaction**. This paper demonstrates that intensive treadmill training begun at 60 days of age suppresses calcitonin C-cell secretion (significantly; p<0.05) and parathyroid hormone (PTH) activity (ultrastructurally, not statistically), without the expected increase in third metatarsal bone mineral content (BMC) or cortical area (p>0.05; directionally lower in training group: 3.13 vs 3.35 mmAl). The primary practical implication is that early-life over-training does not strengthen bone; it may actually compromise bone quality by dysregulating the hormonal axis that should be actively promoting mineralisation during the foal growth phase.

For a finishing-position model, the paper contributes mechanistic support for two feature directions:

1. **Age-at-first-start and early training intensity as fracture/scratch risk predictors.** Horses that enter race training at a very young age (age 2 in Japan, equivalent to ~18–20 months of formal training) without an adequate pasture-based preparatory period may have compromised bone quality due to the same endocrine suppression pathway shown here. The combination `age_at_first_start` + `career_starts_at_age_2` captures cumulative early-load proxy.

2. **Asymmetric bone adaptation (dorsolateral cortex thickening) as a running-style signal.** The paper shows that early treadmill training causes asymmetric osteogenesis — the dorsolateral cortex of the third metatarsal expands preferentially. This mirrors the "bucked shin" (dorsal periostitis) pattern in 2-year-old racehorses. Horses with dorsolateral remodelling are adapting to high-speed galloping loads; its presence could serve as a health screening signal in veterinary examination records.

Limitation for direct modelling: no performance outcome is measured; the paper documents a physiology–bone relationship under controlled conditions in very young foals, not in race-age horses. The connection to race performance is biologically plausible but indirect.

## Background & objective

Fractures in Thoroughbred racehorses are known to arise from pathological changes in bone (not just sudden mechanical overload). Calcitonin (from thyroid C cells) suppresses osteoclast activity and prevents calcium release from bone; PTH (from parathyroid chief cells) promotes bone remodelling and calcium metabolism. Both are critical during the rapid growth phase from birth to weanling (~60–180 days). Weanling-to-yearling studies (Bell et al. 2001, Raub et al. 1989) generally found exercise increased bone mineral content, but no prior study examined horses starting at age < 100 days. The hypothesis was that early-life treadmill exercise would alter C-cell and parathyroid chief-cell activity along with third metatarsal bone morphology.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 15 Thoroughbreds (no history of musculoskeletal or endocrine disease); 60 days old at start. Randomly divided: training group (cases 1–7; 5 females, 2 males) and control group (cases 8–15; 4 females, 4 males). Mean body weight: birth 56.1 ± 6.4 kg (training) / 53.7 ± 4.8 kg (control); start of study 154.3 ± 12.3 / 153.0 ± 17.1 kg; end 264.6 ± 15.1 / 252.4 ± 15.6 kg (no significant difference between groups at start or end, p>0.05).

**Training protocol (Mustang-2200, Kagra treadmill; flat):**

| Period (months) | Walk (m/s, sec) | Trot (m/s, sec) | Canter/Gallop (m/s, sec) |
| --------------- | --------------- | --------------- | ------------------------ |
| 0–1             | 1.6, 45         | 2.6, 180        | 5.0, 75                  |
| 2–3             | 1.6, 45         | 2.6, 180        | 6.0, 75                  |
| 4–5             | 1.6, 45         | 3.3, 180        | 7.0, 75                  |
| 6–7             | 1.6, 45         | 3.3, 240        | 9.0, 75                  |
| 8–9             | 1.6, 45         | 3.3, 240        | 10.0–10.5, 75            |
| 11–12           | 1.6, 45         | 3.3, 240        | 11.0, 75                 |

Total daily treadmill distance: 915–1,652 m/day (increased with growth), 5 days/week.

Both groups also pastured in same area (11,141 m²) for 4 hr/day. Pasture movement was visually monitored at 10 m intervals. Control group pastured freely; no treadmill.

Net daily canter in training group ≈ 2× control group throughout experiment.

**Nutrition:** All horses received concentrate twice daily; water and hay ad libitum. Daily nutrient requirements calculated per Japanese Feeding Standard for Horses (JRA/ERI, 1998).

**Euthanasia:** Intravenous barbiturate overdose at end of experiment (12 months = ~13–14 months of age).

**Thyroid C-cell quantification:**

- Annular sections at maximum surface area; 10% neutral buffered formalin fixation; paraffin-embedded; 4 µm sections; H&E stain
- Immunohistochemistry: rabbit polyclonal anti-human calcitonin antibody (ZYMED, 1:50 in PBS); HISTOFINE SIMPLESTAIN MAX PO detection; DAB visualisation; haematoxylin counterstain
- C cells counted per mm² in 7 fields/horse (method of Yoshikawa et al. 2001); mean cells/mm² per horse
- Statistics: Mann-Whitney analysis

**Parathyroid ultrastructure:**

- Chief cells fixed in 2% glutaraldehyde (pH 7.4, 0.05 M cacodylate buffer), postfixed in 1% OsO₄, dehydrated, embedded in Epon 812
- Ultrathin sections stained with uranyl acetate and Sato's lead citrate; examined by Hitachi H-7000 TEM
- Assessment: relative development of granular endoplasmic reticulum (synthesis stage), Golgi complexes (packaging/secretion stage), secretory granule location (synthesising vs secreting morphology)

**Third metatarsal bone radiography:**

- Left or right third metatarsal removed; soft tissue dissected; 3-mm transverse slabs at mid-diaphysis
- Radiography: Fuji sftexfilm FG, 39 kV, 5 mA, 2 min; aluminum step wedge (20 steps, 1–20 mm) exposed simultaneously
- Scanned (Scanmaker 9600XL); pixel values converted to aluminum equivalent (mmAl) using NIH Image
- Cortical area (CA) = π(X₁·Y₁ − X₂·Y₂)/4 (where X₁, Y₁ = lateromedial and dorsoplantar bone diameters; X₂, Y₂ = medullary diameters)
- Statistics: Mann-Whitney analysis

Training group had significantly fewer C cells — indicating suppressed calcitonin secretory activity.

**Parathyroid chief-cell ultrastructure:**

Training group: chief cells oval to polygonal; plasma membranes with many folds; granular endoplasmic reticulum cisternae abundant (randomly distributed or aggregated at apical poles); secretory granules distributed throughout cytoplasm (diameter 180–320 nm); Golgi complexes poorly developed. **Interpretation: synthesising phase, not secreting.**

Control group: chief cells more tortuous outlines, complicated interdigitations; Golgi complexes well developed; large vacuolar bodies near plasma membrane (exocytosis); secretory granules (diameter 140–280 nm) located near plasma membrane. **Interpretation: actively secreting phase.**

## Discussion & interpretation

The findings contradict the expectation that exercise increases bone mineral content, which is observed in weanling-to-yearling studies (Bell et al. 2001, Raub et al. 1989). The authors explain this discrepancy by the extreme youth of subjects (60 days old, vs 3–12 months in prior studies) and the fact that control-group canter distance (~600 m/day pasture) was closer to prior studies' "exercise" groups than the treadmill distance (~900–1,500 m/day canter equivalent). Thus, comparing treadmill to paddock at this age is comparing excessive exercise to the natural optimum, not active to sedentary.

The suppressed C-cell count in trained horses indicates calcitonin secretion is reduced or down-regulated, possibly because high-speed running-induced mechanical signals transiently lower serum calcium, making calcitonin less necessary. Alternatively, the metabolic cost of intense exercise may deplete systemic resources needed for thyroid follicular cell activity.

The parathyroid finding (chief cells in synthesising, not secreting, phase) suggests PTH secretion is also impaired. Since PTH is essential for bone remodelling and calcium mobilisation during growth, its suppression during intensive early training may explain why BMC was not increased.

The dorsolateral cortex osteogenesis (asymmetric) reflects bone adaptation to the specific loading pattern of treadmill galloping — consistent with McCarthy and Jeffcott (1992) and Sherman et al. (1995) on third metacarpal bone remodelling. Boston and Nunamaker (2000) showed that training in a mode resembling racing prevents bucked shins, suggesting that proper bone adaptation to racing loads does require some high-speed exercise, but only at the appropriate developmental age.

Barnevelt and von Weeren (1999) found T3 compact bone density lower in trained vs pastured Dutch Warmblood foals, supporting the current conclusion that over-training at a very young age compromises systemic bone density.

## Limitations

- Very small sample (n=7 training, n=8 control). No statistical power for bone measurements; trends may be real but non-significant due to underpowering.
- Only third metatarsal bone measured; authors note that third metacarpal is the standard systemic indicator and they could not examine it (front vs hind limb limitation).
- Training vs control differed not just in treadmill but also in net canter distance (2× higher in training group counting both treadmill and pasture); confounding not fully eliminated.
- Breed not fully specified (Thoroughbred) but all from JRA Equine Research Institute breeding; management and nutritional conditions controlled.
- No race performance outcome measured; entirely pre-racing age group.

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `age_at_first_race_start` — age in days or months when horse had first recorded official race start — source: JRA/NAR race records — early first start (≤24 months Japan equivalent) signals potentially compromised bone quality via endocrine suppression pathway; expected positive association with injury risk (scratch, non-completion) in subsequent starts
- `career_starts_at_2yo` — count of race starts before turning 3 (Japanese racing age) — source: race records — proxy for early training load accumulation; positive correlation with C-cell elevation in fracture-prone young horses (Yoshikawa et al. 2002)
- `career_starts_at_2yo_and_3yo` — combined count for ages 2–3 — broader early-career load proxy
- `layup_flag_before_age_4` — binary flag for any recorded rest period (layup) during first 2 years of racing career — may indicate early injury/bone stress; captures post-injury degradation signal
- `days_since_last_race` — gap between previous race and current race — captures rest/recovery periods; very long gaps at early career age may reflect bone-related layups
- `early_career_race_frequency` — starts per month during first 12 months of racing — high frequency → higher cumulative skeletal load → higher fracture risk
- **Caution:** The endocrine pathway (calcitonin/PTH suppression) is not directly observable from standard race records. The feature engineering relies on race frequency and timing as proxies for the underlying skeletal load.
