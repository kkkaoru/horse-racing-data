## Abstract (verbatim)

> To clarify the effects of free amino acid types on the change in the physiological character of equine erythrocytes with in vivo aging, the cell density, mean cell haemoglobin concentration (MCHC), acetylcholinesterase activity, osmotic fragility, alanine influx and reduced glutathione (GSH) content of the erythrocytes which were fractionated by the method of Murphy were compared among three types of horses which were classified according to the basic amino acid-positive (BA⁺) and -negative (BA⁻), and GSH-low (GSH-L) and -high (GSH-H): type [BA⁺:GSH-L] (I), [BA⁺:GSH-H] (II) and [BA⁻:GSH-H] (III) of erythrocytes. Except two of the type III animals, the cell density, MCHC, acetylcholinesterase activity and osmotic fragility of each cell population which was separated into six fractions based on density did not differ significantly among the three cell types. The increase in cell density was associated with a progressive increase in MCHC, and with the decrease in acetylcholinesterase activity and osmotic fragility. The osmotic fragilities of the above two type IIIs were much higher than those of other horses, and did not differ among the six cell fractions. The alanine influx and GSH content of each fractionated cell showed significant differences among the three cell types. The ranking for alanine influx was type I < II < III, and that for GSH content was type I < II = III. Increase in cell density was associated with a progressive decrease in the alanine influx of both types II and III, but not type I, and a decrease in GSH content in all three types of cells. These experimental results suggest that an increase in equine erythroid density is a function of their in vivo aging, regardless of the three cell types defined by BA and GSH systems. Moreover, the variation in alanine influx and GSH content in erythrocytes among the three cell types might take place before the release of mature red cells from the bone marrow into the circulation.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **C (exercise-physiology/fitness)**, specifically haematological oxygen-carrying capacity. The study characterises density-fraction-based aging of equine erythrocytes and establishes that MCHC (mean corpuscular haemoglobin concentration) increases and acetylcholinesterase activity decreases with erythrocyte density (age), confirming these as RBC aging biomarkers. Crucially, it also identifies that two horses among the type III category showed **anomalously high osmotic fragility** that did not vary among density fractions — indicating a horse-level constitutional predisposition to RBC fragility independent of cell age, which would reduce oxygen-carrying reserve during exercise.

For the prediction pipeline, the primary feature implication is that **between-horse variation in RBC physiology** (amino acid transport type / GSH level) is a stable individual characteristic determined before RBCs even enter circulation. If blood-test data (MCHC, RBC count, haematocrit) are available pre-race, they encode meaningful individual differences in oxygen-carrying capacity. The finding that osmotic fragility decreases with increasing RBC density (age) in most horses means that a horse with a high proportion of young (less dense) cells (indicating recent RBC turnover or recovery from anaemia/bleeding) has more fragile, potentially more oxygen-efficient cells, whereas cells in the heaviest fractions are more concentrated (MCHC higher) but also older.

The three erythrocyte types (I, II, III) are defined by:

- Type I: BA⁺, GSH-L — no amino acid transporter; low GSH
- Type II: BA⁺, GSH-H — low-affinity transporter; high GSH
- Type III: BA⁻, GSH-H — high-affinity transporter; highest alanine influx; high GSH

Type is fixed per individual. Type III shows the highest alanine transport (amino acid supply for RBC metabolism) but includes the anomalously fragile outlier horses. This type classification — if linked to JBBA-recorded blood group systems — could in principle serve as a genetic risk flag.

## Background & objective

RBC density increases with in vivo aging in multiple species because of cell volume decrease (MCV ↓) and MCHC increase due to cellular shrinkage. Density-fractionation by centrifugation (Murphy 1973 method) separates young (low density, fraction 1) to old (high density, fraction 6) RBCs. In sheep, loss of GSH with aging causes Heinz bodies and increased osmotic fragility; whether the same relationship holds in horses, and whether it is modified by the BA/GSH amino acid type, was unknown. The objective was to characterise multiple erythroid aging parameters across density fractions in 12 Thoroughbreds representing the three amino acid types.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 12 adult healthy Thoroughbred horses from the Horsemanship Club, Tokyo University of Agriculture. Classification by BA/GSH systems (prior work by Hanzawa 1995):

- Type I [BA⁺:GSH-L]: n=3 (mare 7yr, gelding 6yr, gelding 8yr)
- Type II [BA⁺:GSH-H]: n=3 (gelding 6yr, gelding 7yr, gelding 9yr)
- Type III [BA⁻:GSH-H]: n=6 (mare 6yr, gelding 6yr, gelding 6yr, gelding 7yr, gelding 7yr, gelding 8yr)

**Blood collection:** Jugular venipuncture, heparinised tubes, immediately chilled at 4°C. Centrifuged 1,450G × 10 min at 4°C (swing-out rotor R300S-11, Sakuma), plasma removed, buffy coat aspirated, washed ×3 with ice-cold PBS (pH 7.4).

**Density fractionation (Murphy method):** 8 ml packed cells + 2 ml self-plasma in polyallomer tube; centrifuged 20,000G × 1 hr at 30°C (angle head rotor 50A-7); then 1,450G × 10 min at 4°C; pellet collected into 6 equal fractions from top (fraction 1, youngest) to bottom (fraction 6, oldest); each washed ×3 with ice-cold PBS.

**Measurements per fraction:**

- **Cell density:** 75.6% Percoll continuous density gradient (pH 7.5) per Hanzawa et al. 1995
- **MCHC:** cyanmethaemoglobin method + fraction calculation per Fisher et al. 1986
- **Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) activity:** Chow and Islam 1970 colorimetric method
- **Osmotic fragility:** haemolysis rate (HL, %) against 0.56% NaCl solution
- **Alanine influx:** cold start/cold stop method (Harvey and Ellory 1989) using [¹⁴C]-alanine tracer (Amersham)
- **GSH content:** alloxan chromogen specific for reduced glutathione (Patterson and Lanzarow 1955)

**Statistics:** Scott and Knott's cluster analysis for between-type comparisons; significance P<0.05.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

### Cell density (Fig. 1)

Progressive increase from fraction 1 (top, lightest/youngest) to fraction 6 (bottom, densest/oldest) in all three types. Density ~1.114–1.119 g/ml range (exact values from graphical data). **No significant difference among the three types** (P>0.2) at any fraction.

### MCHC (Fig. 2)

Progressive increase with increasing fraction number in all three types. **No significant difference among types** (P>0.2). MCHC increases ~linearly from fraction 1 to fraction 6 (consistent with cell shrinkage + Hb concentration increasing with aging).

### Acetylcholinesterase activity (Fig. 3)

Progressive **decrease** with increasing fraction number in all three types. **No significant difference among types** (P>0.2).

### Osmotic fragility — HL at 0.56% NaCl (Fig. 4)

Progressive **decrease** in HL with increasing fraction number in all three types — meaning **denser (older) cells are less osmotically fragile** (more resistant to hypotonic lysis) in most horses. **No significant difference among fractions for 10 of 12 horses.** However, 2 type III horses showed HL much higher than all other horses, and these two horses' HL **did not differ among the six density fractions** — indicating a constitutional, age-independent membrane fragility anomaly.

### Alanine influx (Fig. 5)

**Significant differences among all three types in all six fractions (P<0.05):**
| Type | Relative ranking (all fractions) |
|------|--------------------------------|
| Type I [BA⁺:GSH-L] | Lowest alanine influx |
| Type II [BA⁺:GSH-H] | Intermediate |
| Type III [BA⁻:GSH-H] | Highest alanine influx |

Direction with density/age:

- Type I: **no change** in alanine influx across fractions (transporter absent)
- Type II and III: **progressive decrease** in alanine influx from fraction 1 to 6 (transporters present but activity declines with cell aging)

### GSH content (Fig. 6)

**Significant differences among types:**

- Type I GSH significantly lower than types II and III at all fractions (P<0.05)
- Types II and III: no significant difference in fractions 1–5 (P>0.1); fraction 6 from type II lower than fraction 6 from type III (P<0.05)

Direction with density/age: **progressive decrease** in GSH content from fraction 1 to 6 in all three types.

## Discussion & interpretation

The convergent pattern — density increase with MCHC increase, AChE decrease, osmotic fragility decrease — confirms that equine RBC density is a reliable in vivo aging marker regardless of amino acid type. This contradicts findings in some other species (humans, sheep) where osmotic fragility increases with RBC age. The authors propose that K⁺-Cl⁻ co-transporter activation during aging causes KCl efflux and cell dehydration, reducing osmotic fragility; this is consistent with known equine erythrocyte volume regulation mechanisms (Gibson et al. 1993).

The anomalous type III horses with uniformly high HL across fractions — without differences in MCHC or AChE from other type III animals — suggest a structural membrane protein abnormality (possibly protein 4.2, calmodulin binding, or lipid composition variants) rather than age-related oxidative damage. GSH level alone does not explain the fragility, since other type III horses with similar GSH showed normal fragility patterns.

The bone-marrow origin of type differences: since equine reticulocytes are absent from peripheral blood (anaemic horses do not release reticulocytes), final RBC maturation occurs in the bone marrow. The alanine transporter and GSH level differences are already present in the youngest (least dense) fractions, supporting the conclusion that type differences are determined during intramedullary maturation before release into circulation.

## Limitations

- n=12 horses (3, 3, 6 per type); the two anomalous type III horses introduce large within-type variance
- Only horses from one university herd (Horsemanship Club, Tokyo Univ. Agriculture); not racing-trained Thoroughbreds
- Blood samples taken at rest; exercise-related changes in RBC density distribution and fragility are not captured
- Density fractionation is a crude age proxy — precise cell age in days is not determined
- No sex-by-type or age-by-type breakdown of key parameters (though types were defined a priori)

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `MCHC_pre_race` — mean corpuscular haemoglobin concentration from blood test; higher MCHC → denser (older) average RBC population → lower osmotic fragility (more robust cells) → positive for oxygen-carrying stability — data availability: available if blood tests are conducted pre-race (not standard in public JRA records)
- `erythrocyte_osmotic_fragility_type` — binary flag for constitutionally fragile RBC type (equivalent to anomalous type III) — data availability: requires specific haematological testing; not in standard records; use as a research flag only
- `GSH_content_relative` — higher GSH → greater antioxidant protection; inter-horse differences are stable and determined pre-birth — data availability: not in standard records
- `amino_acid_type` (I/II/III by BA/GSH) — stable individual characteristic; type III highest alanine influx — data availability: requires specialised typing; not in standard race records
- **Practical summary for pipeline:** Without direct blood-test access, standard haematological features (PCV/haematocrit, RBC count, Hb) from racing databases can proxy for overall RBC fitness; this paper provides biological grounding for why between-horse haematological variation is real and meaningful, not just measurement noise
