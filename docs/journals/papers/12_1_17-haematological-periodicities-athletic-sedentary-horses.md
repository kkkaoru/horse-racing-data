## Abstract (verbatim)

> The fundamental aim of chronobiological analysis methods in the field of sport physiology in the horse is to establish the time interval in which the organism gives the best performance. Since it is well known that numerous physiological parameters (electrocardiographic, haemogasanalytic and haemodynamic) vary during physical exercise and influence athletic potential considerably, we studied the periodic variations in the following haematological parameters at a different frequency in 12 horses (6 athletes and 6 sedentary): erythrocytes (RBC), total leukocyte count (WBC), haemoglobin (Hb), packed cell volume (PCV), platelets (PLT), mean corpuscular volume (MCV), mean corpuscular haemoglobin (MCH) and mean corpuscular haemoglobin concentration (MCHC). Some of the parameters studied showed periodicity at different frequencies both in athletic and sedentary horses: in athletic horses - infradian (RBC, PCV), circadian (RBC, WBC, PCV), ultradian (RBC, WBC, Hb); in sedentary horses- infradian (RBC, Hb, PLT), circadian (RBC, PCV), ultradian (RBC, Hb). The results obtained, therefore, can suggest that physical exercise is an exogenous synchronizer which modulates the periodicity of the haematopoietic system and of the functions of the organs involved in athletic performance.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

This paper belongs to feature family **C (exercise-physiology/fitness)** with important implications for **G (statistical-modelling)**. It demonstrates that blood fitness markers (RBC, WBC, Hb, PCV) in athletic horses exhibit distinct rhythms at infradian (~hours), circadian (~24 h), and ultradian (~monthly) frequencies that differ from sedentary horses, with physical exercise acting as an exogenous synchronizer of the haematopoietic system. The key modelling implications are:

First, if pre-race blood samples are available, the time of day at which they were taken must be recorded as a covariate, because haematological values fluctuate by 5–10% across the day. For example, RBC circadian acrophase (peak) in athletic horses is ~14:04, and PCV circadian acrophase is ~05:04 (nocturnal). A blood sample taken at 05:00 would show higher PCV than one taken at 14:00. Without time-of-day correction, comparisons across horses sampled at different times would be confounded.

Second, the ultradian (monthly) rhythms in athletic horses show RBC and WBC acrophases at day 23 of the month, and Hb at day 17. This means there is a ~23-day optimal-fitness window within a training month when haematological capacity is at its cyclic peak. Training blocks and race scheduling that align with these peaks could theoretically improve performance, though this has not been directly tested.

Third, the key qualitative difference between athletic and sedentary horses is the presence of WBC circadian and ultradian periodicity in athletes only. Exercise modulates leukocyte pool dynamics via the hypothalamo-hypophyseal axis. The authors specifically note that WBC shows ultradian periodicity only in athletes (acrophase day 23). Since WBC rhythmicity reflects immunological training adaptation, a horse in appropriate athletic conditioning should show measurable WBC periodicity. Persistent WBC elevation (neutrophilia) without normal cyclic rhythm could signal inflammatory stress or overtraining.

## Background & objective

Chronobiological research in human sports physiology has identified time windows for optimal performance by characterising rhythmic variations in blood and cardiovascular parameters. Despite substantial literature on equine haematology, chronohaematological studies in athletic vs. sedentary horses were lacking. The study aimed to define and compare the rhythmic variations at infradian (0.5–20 hr period), circadian (20–28 hr period), and ultradian (25–35 day period) frequencies in eight haematological parameters in trained vs. untrained horses, using the cosinor statistical method. The authors hypothesised that physical exercise acts as a synchronizer of haematopoietic periodicity analogous to light-dark cycles for circadian rhythms.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 12 adult female Sella Italiana horses from a single stud farm; not pregnant.

- Group 1 (Athletic): 6 horses, ~8 years old, following a pre-agonistic show-jumping training programme
- Group 2 (Sedentary): 6 horses, ~10 years old, remaining in boxes except for a few hours/day in paddock

**Pre-experimental period:** 30 days in naturally lit boxes; fed hay and oats at 07:00, 12:00, and 18:00. Both groups under identical environmental conditions (light, climate, photoperiod, diet).

**Athletic training programme (Group 1):** Three 1-hour sessions/week (16:30–17:30): 15 min walk + 10 min gallop (warm-up) + 25 min trot + 2 jumps + 5 min walk. Additionally, twice/week at the same hour: simulated competition on a 400 m course with 10 vertical fences (1.0–1.10 m high) + double fence, followed by 5 min walk.

**Blood sampling:** Jugular vein; EDTA-evacuated tubes. Three temporal series (times chosen to be far from agonistic activity):

- Infradian: every 2 hr from 08:00 to 20:00 on one day (June 9, 1998); 7 time points
- Circadian: every 4 hr from 04:00 to 24:00 on one day (September 9, 1998); 6 time points
- Ultradian: every 5 days over 30 days (March 1998); 6 time points

**Parameters measured:** RBC (×10⁶/mm³), WBC (×10³/mm³), Hb (g/dl), PCV (%), PLT (×10⁹/l), MCV (fl), MCH (pg), MCHC (g/dl). All measured by electronic cell counter immediately after sampling.

**Statistical analysis:** Data expressed as group means at each time point (intragroup variance not significant). Cosinor method applied: trigonometric model fitting by least-squares; yields Mesor (rhythm-adjusted mean), Amplitude (half-extent of rhythmic change), Acrophase (φ, timing of crest relative to reference time, with 95% CI). Null hypothesis for amplitude tested by F-test; if rejected, rhythm confirmed. Reference time for circadian: local midnight; for infradian and ultradian: start of measurement period.

- WBC showed periodicity ONLY in athletic horses (circadian acrophase 03:12; ultradian acrophase 23rd March): exercise modifies leukocyte pool via hypothalamo-hypophyseal axis
- Hb ultradian Mesor: athletic horses 14.10 g/dl vs. sedentary 12.60 g/dl — ~12% higher Hb in athletes, reflecting training-induced erythropoiesis
- RBC circadian amplitude: athletic 0.26 (7.73 Mesor) vs. sedentary 0.13 (7.00 Mesor) — both diurnal acrophases (athletic 14:04; sedentary 14:40); photoperiod dominates RBC circadian rhythm in both groups

- PLT showed infradian periodicity only in sedentary horses (acrophase 10:10); exercise appears to suppress or alter platelet rhythmicity
- MCV, MCH, MCHC: no significant periodicity in either group

## Discussion & interpretation

Physical exercise acts as an exogenous synchronizer of haematopoietic rhythms in horses, analogous to light-dark cycles for sleep. The mechanism involves: (1) exercise-induced plasma catecholamine + ACTH/cortisol surge activating the hypothalamo-hypophyseal-suprarenal axis; (2) catecholamines mobilising splenic erythrocytes and lymphocytes; (3) ACTH/cortisol stimulating neutrophil production; (4) haemoglobin concentration increases (exercise-trained horses Hb Mesor 14.10 g/dl vs. sedentary 12.60 g/dl). WBC increases 10–30% depending on exercise intensity and duration, but red cell indices (RBC, Hb, PCV) increase more dramatically (primarily via splenic erythrocyte release and haemoconcentration from fluid shifts).

The fact that WBC periodicity appears only in athletic horses is particularly informative: it reflects that the training-induced rhythmic stimulation of the hypothalamo-hypophyseal-adrenal axis creates regular leukocyte pool dynamics that sedentary horses lack. An athlete's blood shows WBC nocturnal circadian peak (03:12) and late-month ultradian peak (day 23) — both absent in sedentary animals.

The higher Hb in athletes (14.10 vs. 12.60 g/dl at ultradian Mesor) is a consistent training adaptation, with athletes peaking mid-month. The authors note this creates a practical opportunity: if training and competition sessions are scheduled when RBC/Hb/PCV are at their cyclic peak (mid-month for ultradian, ~14:00 for circadian), performance should be better. However, this has not been directly tested in racehorses.

## Limitations

- n=6 per group (total n=12); small for chronobiological analysis where individual variation is substantial
- All female Sella Italiana horses (~8–10 years old) from a single stud farm; not Thoroughbred racehorses; results may not generalise to JRA/NAR racing
- Three temporal series measured on different calendar dates (March, June, September 1998); seasonal effects confounded with the chronobiological patterns
- "Athletic" group followed show-jumping programme (3×/week, 1 hr); this is much lower intensity than Thoroughbred race training
- Blood sampling times were chosen to be "far from agonistic activity" — so measurements reflect baseline rhythms, not race-day blood states
- The cosinor method assumes sinusoidal rhythms; actual biological rhythms may be asymmetric or multi-harmonic

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `blood_sample_time_of_day` — hour of day at blood sampling (decimal, e.g., 14.07 = 14:04) — if blood data available — correction covariate for haematological features; RBC peaks ~14:04 in athletes, PCV peaks ~05:04; without this covariate, cross-horse comparisons are systematically biased
- `PCV_pre_race` — packed cell volume (%) at blood screening — veterinary records — higher PCV → more O2 transport → better aerobic performance; athletic Mesor 34.13% (circadian) vs. sedentary 32.67%; note: PCV changes dramatically with exercise (splenic release can push to 57%, see 13_3_89)
- `Hb_pre_race` — haemoglobin (g/dl) — same source — athletic ultradian Mesor 14.10 g/dl vs. sedentary 12.60 g/dl; ~12% higher in trained horses
- `RBC_pre_race` — red blood cell count (×10⁶/mm³) — same source — athletic circadian Mesor 7.73 vs. sedentary 7.00; ~10% higher in athletes
- `WBC_pre_race` — white blood cell count (×10³/mm³) — same source — WBC periodicity only in athletes; persistently high WBC outside cyclic pattern may indicate inflammation/illness; expected negative association with performance if elevated above athletic baseline
- `days_in_training_cycle` — day within current training cycle (1–30) — training schedule records — ultradian acrophase at day 23 for RBC and WBC in athletes; proxy for optimal blood fitness window if horse follows regular monthly training pattern
- `race_time_of_day` — hour of race start — JRA race records — circadian RBC acrophase 14:04 means afternoon races may coincide with peak aerobic blood state; interaction with blood collection time; small but potentially real effect
- **Caution:** These blood-derived features are not in standard JRA/NAR race records. They require access to training-centre blood screening data. Without this access, indirect proxies (training frequency, days since last race, recent race times) are the available alternatives.
