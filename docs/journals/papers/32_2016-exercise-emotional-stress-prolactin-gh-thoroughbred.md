## Abstract (verbatim)

> The secretion of prolactin and growth hormone in response to exercise and emotional stresses was investigated in Thoroughbreds. Two experiments were performed: one with loading of only exercise stress and one with simultaneous loading of exercise and emotional stresses. Exercise stress was loaded in 4 steps using a treadmill for horses: pre-exercise period (5 min), walking period (6.5 min), galloping period (3 min), and cooling down period (10 min). Emotional stress was loaded by showing a loud video of an audience at a racetrack during the walking period. The results clearly demonstrated that exercise stress rapidly increased the secretion of prolactin and growth hormone and that secretion of them persisted for a specific period after the exercise. In addition, emotional stress promoted prolactin secretion.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

This paper belongs to feature family **C (exercise-physiology/fitness)** and has secondary relevance to **E (environment/stress reactivity)**. The study characterises prolactin and GH response kinetics during treadmill exercise at race-simulating intensity (110% VHRmax to exhaustion) in JRA-affiliated Thoroughbreds. GH peaked at ~85× resting level during the galloping phase and remained elevated 40 min post-exercise, establishing a biochemical signature of maximal-effort exercise intensity.

Emotional stress (racetrack-atmosphere noise/video at 85 dB) selectively amplified prolactin secretion, indicating that racetrack environmental context modulates the endocrine system independently of physical effort. Horses that are more reactive to crowd/noise stimuli may show greater endocrine perturbation on race day, which can feed into a latent "environmental excitability" feature (e.g., first race at a new venue, large attendance events).

GH promotes muscle protein anabolism and repair; prolactin activates immune pathways (interferon production, NK cells, phagocytosis). Chronic under-recovery (persistently elevated GH/prolactin baseline) could foreshadow performance decline — relevant when modelling short rest intervals between races. Prolactin also attenuates subsequent stress responses in the brain (anxiolytic effect via paraventricular hypothalamic nucleus), which may explain why experienced racehorses show attenuated debut-race disadvantage.

Practical limits: n = 4 males only, single training centre; cannot directly transfer quantitative thresholds to population-level modelling without larger validation data.

## Background & objective

Racehorses experience multiple stress sources beyond physical training: transport, crowd noise, pre-race atmosphere. Gastric ulcer incidence is high in Thoroughbreds, and chronic stress suppresses immunity. Prior work (Thompson et al. 1994 J. Anim. Sci.) compared only pre- vs. post-exercise hormone levels without fine temporal resolution. The present study aimed to document the detailed time course of prolactin and GH secretion during a simulated race using a treadmill, and to test whether emotional stress (racetrack-mimicking stimuli) further alters secretion on top of exercise stress. The JRA/TUAT collaboration used horses from JRA's own Equine Research Institute herd, making the exercise conditions directly relevant to JRA race training.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 4 male Thoroughbreds, age 4.1 ± 0.2 yr, body weight 466 ± 6 kg. Four weeks of treadmill familiarisation (Mustang 2000, Kagra, Switzerland) before the experiment.

**Experimental design:** Two within-subject crossover experiments one week apart with the same 4 horses.

- ES group (exercise stress only)
- EES group (exercise + emotional stress simultaneously)

**Treadmill protocol (4 steps):**

1. Pre-exercise: 5 min trot/gallop warm-up
2. Walking: 6.5 min walk
3. Galloping: 3 min at 110% VHRmax (speed at 110% of maximum heart rate from HR–speed regression) to exhaustion
4. Cool-down: 10 min walk

**Emotional stress stimuli (EES group only, during the 6.5 min walking period):**

- Acoustic: fanfare and cheering from horse races played at 85 dB
- Visual: video of an audience projected onto a screen
- Stimuli chosen based on prior Hada et al. 2003 (J Neuroendocrinol) novelty-stress experiment

**Blood collection:** Jugular vein catheter (14-G Angiocath, Becton Dickinson) placed ≥2 hr before experiment. Samples at: rest, on entering treadmill room, pre/post novel stimulation, during gallop (at 30, 60, 90, 120, 150, 180 sec), post-exercise (1, 5, 10, 20, 30, 40 min).

**Hormone assays:**

- Prolactin: RIA using rat anti-serum AFP-261987 (equine PRL) and AFP-8794B standard; intra-assay CV 7.1%, inter-assay CV 9.8%
- GH: RIA using anti-porcine GH AFP10318545 and equine GH AFP-5022 standard; intra-assay CV 5.0%, inter-assay CV 12.0%
- Both kits provided by National Hormone and Pituitary Program, NIDDK, NIH

**Statistics:** Mean ± SEM. Student's t-test (F-test for variance equality); Welch correction when variance non-uniform. P < 0.05 considered significant.

**Ethics:** Approved by Animal Welfare and Ethics Committee of the Equine Research Institute, JRA.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

**Emotional stress alone (during 6.5 min walking, EES vs ES):**

- Prolactin: significantly elevated in EES group during walking period (P < 0.05)
- GH: no significant elevation from emotional stress alone

**Exercise stress: prolactin time course (both groups):**

- Rose immediately after initiation of pre-exercise period
- Continued rising without decrease during the walking period
- ES group: peaked at 120 sec after galloping period initiation
- EES group: peaked earlier, at 60 sec after galloping period initiation
- Galloping-phase prolactin level tended to be higher in EES than ES (not stated as statistically significant — described as "tended to be higher")
- Both groups: prolactin still above resting level at 40 min post-exercise completion

**Exercise stress: GH time course (both groups):**

- Rose throughout pre-exercise, walking, and galloping periods in both ES and EES
- No significant difference between ES and EES for GH at any time point
- Resting GH: 1.4 ± 0.7 ng/mL (ES group)
- Peak GH (at 150 sec after galloping period initiation): 118.8 ± 22.1 ng/mL (ES group)
- Ratio peak/resting: approximately 85-fold increase
- Still above resting level at 40 min post-exercise completion in both groups

## Discussion & interpretation

The authors conclude that racehorses approaching a race "are aware of the atmosphere and noise of the audience at the racetrack" — the endocrine data support that emotional/environmental stimuli at racetracks elicit physiologically meaningful hormonal responses even before physical exertion begins.

The selective effect of emotional stress on prolactin but not GH is mechanistically attributed to suppression of dopamine (a prolactin suppressor) by elevated β-endorphins in the hypothalamus. GH secretion, by contrast, is driven primarily by physical workload via the GHRH axis and is not sensitive to emotional stimuli at sub-exhaustion intensity.

Prolactin acts as an anti-stress hormone: it inhibits stress-induced gastric ulceration (relevant given high ulcer incidence in Thoroughbreds) and exerts anxiolytic effects by attenuating neuronal activation in the paraventricular hypothalamic nucleus during acute stress. The immune-activating roles (helper T-cell interferon, NK cells, macrophage phagocytosis, lymphocyte proliferation, antibody production) suggest that well-managed pituitary hormone response post-exercise is protective against exercise-induced immunosuppression.

GH promotes protein anabolism and IGF-I production in the liver; both are necessary for muscle repair after high-intensity exercise. The 85-fold peak GH response establishes that supramaximal treadmill exercise is a potent GH stimulus in horses, consistent with human sports physiology data.

Individual variability in stress reactions was explicitly noted by the authors as a concern ("the grade of stress reactions markedly varies among individual horses"), motivating future work with larger samples.

## Limitations

- **Very small n = 4**, all male, from a single training centre (Equine Research Institute, JRA Tochigi) — effect sizes should be treated as directional indicators only
- Within-subject crossover (same 4 horses, ES first then EES) introduces potential order/habituation effects
- No statistical significance reported for the EES vs ES galloping-phase prolactin difference (described as "tended to be higher"); GH differences between groups were not significant at any time point
- Treadmill exercise (constant speed, controlled environment) differs from actual race conditions (variable pace, ground surface, pre-race procedures, competitor interaction)
- No mares or fillies included; prolactin physiology differs by sex
- No measurement of cortisol, ACTH, β-endorphin, or catecholamines in parallel, limiting mechanistic inference

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `emotional_stress_reactivity` — individual horse's prolactin peak amplitude per unit emotional stimulus; not directly observable from race records but potentially proxied by performance variance across venue familiarity (first race at a given racecourse vs. repeat visits); expected positive association with performance drop in novel/large-crowd settings
- `inter_race_rest_days` — GH and prolactin remain elevated 40 min post-exercise; with very short rest intervals (< 2 weeks), incomplete hormonal normalisation may indicate physiological under-recovery; source: race date differencing from race records; expected negative effect on finishing position when rest < 7–10 days (non-linear)
- `training_intensity_proxy` — if JRA training records include gallop furlongs per session or top speed, these could proxy VO2max-related fitness; GH/prolactin data confirm that supramaximal gallop is the threshold for peak anabolic hormone release
- `gastric_ulcer_risk_flag` — horses with high emotional stress reactivity and short rest intervals may have chronically elevated prolactin/GH that, when overwhelmed, predicts gastric ulcer incidence and associated performance decline; derivable from medication or vet-scratch records
- **Caution:** All features derived from this paper are biologically motivated but not directly measurable from standard JRA race records; use as prior-belief motivators rather than directly engineered features
