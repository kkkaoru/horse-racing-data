## Abstract (verbatim)

> Measurements of gas exchange while breathing gases of different O2 concentrations are useful in respiratory and exercise physiology. High bias flows required in flow-through indirect calorimetry systems for large animals like exercising horses necessitate the use of inconveniently large reservoirs of mixed gases for making such measurements and can limit the amount of equilibration time that is adequate for steady-state measurements. We obviated the need to use a pre-mixed reservoir of gas in a semi-open flow-through indirect calorimeter by dynamically mixing gases and verified the theoretical accuracy and utility of making such measurements using the mass-balance N2-dilution method. We evaluated the accuracy of the technique at different inspired oxygen fractions by measuring exercising oxygen consumption (V̇O2) at two fully aerobic submaximal exercise intensities in Thoroughbred horses. Horses exercised at 24% and 50% maximum oxygen consumption (V̇O2max) of each horse while breathing different O2 concentrations (19.5%, 21% and 25% O2). The N2-dilution technique was used to calculate V̇O2. Repeated-measures ANOVA was used to tested for differences in V̇O2 between different inspired O2 concentrations. The specific V̇O2 of the horses trotting at 24%V̇O2max and cantering at 50%V̇O2max were not significantly different among the three different inspired oxygen fractions. These findings demonstrate that reliable measurements of V̇O2 can be obtained at various inspired oxygen fractions using dynamic gas mixing and the N2-dilution technique to calibrate semi-open-circuit gas flow systems.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

This is a methodology paper validating the N2-dilution technique for VO2 measurement under altered FIO2 conditions in treadmill-trained Thoroughbreds. Its direct relevance to finishing-position prediction is moderate: it does not report on race outcomes or individual VO2max variation as a predictor. However, it provides three pieces of value:

2. **Lactate threshold as a VO2max confirmation criterion:** the authors use plasma lactate accumulation rate exceeding 8 mM/min as part of their VO2max confirmation criteria. The connection to finishing position is: high individual VO2max → high aerobic capacity → better performance at longer distances where oxidative metabolism predominates. A horse's VO2max relative to field competition average is the gold-standard aerobic-capacity feature but requires treadmill testing data not present in standard race databases.

## Background & objective

Measuring VO2 under altered FIO2 is important for studying oxygen transport limitations and altitude physiology. For large animals like horses, flow-through (open-circuit) systems with high bias flows (≈7,500 l/min) are necessary, but providing large volumes of pre-mixed gas is impractical. The N2-dilution technique (Fedak et al. 1981) calibrates the system by injecting a known flow of N2 to displace O2, without needing to measure total bias flow directly. This study extends the technique to verify it remains accurate when FIO2 deviates from normoxia (0.195, 0.21, 0.25), which was theoretically asserted but never rigorously validated for equine-scale systems.

## Materials & methods

**Ethics:** Protocol approved by UC Davis Animal Use and Care Committee.

**Subjects:** 6 Thoroughbred horses (3 geldings, 2 males, 1 female; all 3-year-olds; body weight 490 ± 31 kg SD). Trained on a treadmill (Mustang 2200, Graber AG, Fahrwangen, Switzerland) for 17 weeks; maximum training speed up to 16 m/sec.

**VO2max determination:** normoxic VO2max established for each horse prior to altered FIO2 experiments. Criteria: no increase in V̇O2 with increasing speed; RER >1.0; plasma lactate accumulation rate >8 mM/min. Confirmed specific V̇O2max = 2.59 ± 0.10 ml/(kg×sec).

**Open-circuit system:**

- 25-hp turbine drawing bias flow ≈7,500 l/min (ATP)
- 25-cm diameter PVC tubing for bias flow
- Dual-channel O2 analyzer: S-3/A II (Ametek Inc.); simultaneous upstream (FIO2) and downstream (FĒO2) readings
- Gas drying: CaSO4 (Drierite) for both; NaOH (Ascarite) for downstream CO2 removal
- Data recorded: DI-720-USB, WinDaq Pro+ (DATAQ Instruments)
- Mask pressure drop: <4 cm H2O at experimental bias flows

**Experimental design:** randomised cross-over design; each horse exercised once at each of three FIO2 conditions (19.52 ± 0.03%, 20.95 ± 0.00%, 25.11 ± 0.07% O2) at two intensities (24% and 50% V̇O2max). Total of 6 horses × 3 FIO2 × 2 intensities = 36 measurements.

**Warm-up protocol:** walk 1.5 m/sec for 3 min → trot 4 m/sec for 3 min → walk 1.5 m/sec for 3 min (total 9 min); horses breathed experimental gas mixture throughout warm-up to equilibrate.

**Exercise intensities:**

- 24% V̇O2max = trot 4 m/sec
- 50% V̇O2max = canter 8 m/sec
- Both intensities confirmed to be fully aerobic (no anaerobic contribution assumed)

**Treadmill room conditions:** temperature 20–25°C, relative humidity 20–60%.

**Calibration:** N2 metered into bias flow at mask; calibration factor = V̇N2* / ΔN2* (l N2 min⁻¹ per 1% O2 deflection). Calibrations performed at 15%, 21%, and 30% FIO2 even though horses only ran at 19.5%, 21%, and 25%.

**VO2 calculation equation:**
V̇O2 = V̇N2* × (ΔO2 / ΔN2*) × FIO2 / (1 – FĒO2)

**Statistics:** repeated-measures ANOVA; P<0.05 significant; data mean ± SD; power analysis: magnitudes detectable with 90% probability at P=0.05 level calculated for non-significant comparisons.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

### VO2max (established prior to altered FIO2 experiments)

- Specific V̇O2max = **2.59 ± 0.10 ml/(kg×sec)**
- Based on 6 horses, 3-year-olds, 490 ± 31 kg, 17 weeks treadmill training

### Specific V̇O2 at 24% V̇O2max and 50% V̇O2max across three FIO2 (Table 1)

| Exercise intensity           | FIO2 = 19.5% | FIO2 = 21%  | FIO2 = 25%              | P (ANOVA) |
| ---------------------------- | ------------ | ----------- | ----------------------- | --------- |
| 24% V̇O2max (trot, 4 m/sec)   | 0.59 ± 0.28  | 0.57 ± 0.08 | 0.68 ± 0.21 ml/(kg×sec) | NS        |
| 50% V̇O2max (canter, 8 m/sec) | 1.42 ± 0.35  | 1.43 ± 0.24 | 1.44 ± 0.19 ml/(kg×sec) | NS        |

Neither intensity showed significant differences in V̇O2 across FIO2 conditions.

### Calibration factors

- Calibration factor (l N2 STPD min⁻¹ per 1% O2 change) was calculated at 15%, 21%, and 30% FIO2 for the equine system.
- The factor varies linearly with FIO2: at FIO2=0.16, 1 l N2/min displaces 160 ml O2/min; at FIO2=0.26, 1 l N2/min displaces 260 ml O2/min.
- Fig. 2 in paper shows calibration factors across FIO2 range 0.15–0.30 for the equine system; relationship is linear.

### Error sources quantified

- Error from assuming RER=1.0 (rather than true RER): 0.1% error per 0.1 unit RER deviation when FIO2−FĒO2 = 0.01; in this study FIO2−FĒO2 typically ranged 0.002–0.006, so RER-related error was much less than 0.1%.
- Maximum error from not correcting for H2O vapor production (V̇H2O): <1% given bias flow ≈10× minute ventilation.
- He leak test: undetectable He downstream, confirming mask integrity.
- No systematic bias: if calculation were biased, hyperoxia V̇O2 would be overestimated and hypoxia underestimated, but no such directional asymmetry was observed.

## Discussion & interpretation

The N2-dilution technique proved unbiased across FIO2 from 0.19 to 0.25 in this equine semi-open system. The key equation (Eq. 1) is a modified form of Fedak et al. (1981) Eq. 11b, incorporating FIO2 as a variable, which was never formally validated at altered FIO2 before this study. The practical advantage over closed-system or flow-meter-based approaches is that it avoids the need to accurately measure large bias flows (6,000–10,000 l/min) or to provide large volumes of pre-mixed gas — only N2 calibration flow (≈300–500 l STPD/min) needs accurate measurement, which is achievable with electronic mass flowmeters.

The technique enables future research on altitude effects in horses (relevant to racecourses at altitude) and on response to hypoxic training protocols. JRA racecourses are at sea level, limiting direct application of the altitude-specific findings.

## Limitations

- n=6; single institution; only 3-year-old, treadmill-trained horses. VO2max reference value is not population-representative.
- Methods paper; no race outcome data, no performance prediction.
- Only submaximal intensities (24% and 50% V̇O2max) tested at altered FIO2; confirmation that N2-dilution remains accurate at VO2max under altered FIO2 was not tested.
- RER and H2O corrections omitted; small but finite error sources.
- The study does not link individual VO2max variation to actual race performance outcomes.

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `specific_vo2max` — individual horse treadmill VO2max (ml/kg/sec); reference value 2.59 ± 0.10 for 3-year-olds at JRA level. Inter-individual variation is a known aerobic-capacity predictor. Source: JRA Equine Research Institute treadmill records (not public). Expected effect: higher VO2max → better performance at longer, aerobically demanding distances.
- `lactate_accumulation_rate_treadmill` — plasma lactate accumulation rate at VO2max test (mM/min); threshold >8 mM/min confirms VO2max. Can proxy aerobic power margin. Source: JRA ERI treadmill records.
- `treadmill_vo2_at_race_pace` — V̇O2 measured at speed corresponding to expected race pace; if below VO2max, horse is in aerobic zone; if approaching VO2max, anaerobic contribution needed. Requires linking treadmill speed-VO2 relationship to expected race pace.
- **Altitude/FIO2 interaction:** not relevant for JRA (sea level venues) but potentially relevant for Ban-ei (Obihiro, Hokkaido; low altitude) or hypothetical JRA overseas races.
- **Caution:** treadmill VO2 data are internal research data at JRA ERI, not in standard public race databases. Practical use requires data access agreement.
- **Do NOT extrapolate:** the reported VO2max (2.59 ml/kg/sec) is for 17-week treadmill-trained 3-year-olds; horses earlier or later in their career, or differently trained, will show different values. Cross-horse comparison requires contemporaneous measurement under standardised conditions.
