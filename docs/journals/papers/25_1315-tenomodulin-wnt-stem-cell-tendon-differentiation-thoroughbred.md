## Abstract (verbatim)

> Tenomodulin has been recognized as a biomarker for tendon differentiation, and its gene expression is regulated by several transcription factors including Scleraxis and Mohawk. In this study, we found a novel regulatory mechanism of tenomodulin expression. Equine bone marrow-derived mesenchymal stem cells (BMSCs) in monolayer culture showed a low mRNA level of tenomodulin in comparison with the level in the tendon. When cultured in collagen gel containing a glycogen synthase kinase-3 (GSK-3) inhibitor (BIO), expression of tenomodulin in BMSCs increased up to the level in the tendon. Participation of GSK-3 in its gene expression was further demonstrated by a gene silencing experiment with small interference RNA corresponding to GSK-3, suggesting that Wnt/β-catenin signaling mediated expression of tenomodulin. These results were confirmed by nuclear translocation of β-catenin in BIO-treated BMSCs cultured in collagen gel. Under this culture condition, expression of tenomodulin-related transcription factors including Scleraxis and Mohawk was not affected, suggesting that Wnt/β-catenin signaling was independent from these transcription factors. Additionally, BIO strongly enhanced expression of type XIV collagen in collagen-embedded BMSCs up to the level in the tendon, and other tendon-related extracellular matrix components such as decorin and fibromodulin were also upregulated. Taken together, these results indicated that activation of Wnt/β-catenin signaling could induce differentiation of BMSCs into tenomodulin-expressing tendon cells in collagen gel.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **A (injury/soundness — soft tissue/tendon repair)**.

This is a mechanistic in vitro study, not a clinical or epidemiological paper, so its connection to race prediction is indirect. However, it is directly relevant to understanding why SDFT tendinopathy in Thoroughbreds is chronic, recurrence-prone, and career-limiting — which in turn justifies the use of SDFT injury history as a strong scratch/DNF risk feature in the model.

The central finding is that Wnt/β-catenin signalling — not the canonical Scleraxis/Mohawk transcription factor pathway — drives tenomodulin expression (a marker of genuine tendon cell differentiation) in equine BMSCs. This implies that the clinical approach of implanting BMSCs into the SDFT for regenerative repair is only effective if the cells are differentiated into true tenomodulin-expressing tendon cells, which requires the Wnt pathway to be active. In vivo, inadequate Wnt activation during BMSC-based repair could result in fibrous scar tissue (type XII collagen-rich, tenomodulin-poor) rather than true tendon tissue, explaining the high recurrence rate of SDFT tendinopathy after BMSC treatment.

For the model: horses with a history of SDFT injury and BMSC treatment are not at zero recurrence risk; the biological basis for persistent vulnerability is established here. This supports treating SDFT injury history as a persistently elevated risk flag rather than a single-episode recoverable event.

## Background & objective

SDFT tendinopathy (equine tendonitis) is common in racehorses due to excessive mechanical loading and is known to be intractable and recurrence-prone. Autologous BMSC implantation into the injured SDFT has been developed clinically (Kasashima et al., 2011). BMSCs can differentiate into adipocytes, osteocytes, and chondrocytes, but the mechanism of differentiation into tenocytes is not fully understood. Tenomodulin is the best biomarker of tendon differentiation; its expression is regulated by Scleraxis and Mohawk in developmental systems, but external signals that directly upregulate tendon-related differentiation markers in adult BMSCs are unknown.

Objective: Identify external signalling factors that drive tenomodulin expression in equine BMSCs, specifically whether Wnt/β-catenin signalling (via GSK-3 inhibition) can induce tenomodulin and tendon ECM gene expression.

## Materials & methods

**Cell source:** BMSCs established from 8 Thoroughbred racehorses aged 3–5 years (aspiration from sternum per Kasashima et al. 2011). Pluripotency confirmed by tri-lineage differentiation (osteocyte, adipocyte, chondrocyte). Used at population doubling level 4.

**Culture conditions:**

- Monolayer: standard DMEM + 20% FBS + penicillin-streptomycin-neomycin, 5% CO₂, 37°C.
- Collagen gel: 5 × 10⁵ cells in 500 µl growth medium + 50 µl 10× DMEM + 450 µl 1% collagen solution (Wako); polymerised in 24-well plates; cultured 7 days.

**Signal inhibitors tested:** Cytochalasin D (5 µM), genistein (100 µM; tyrosine kinase inhibitor), U0126 (1 µM; MEK1 inhibitor), BIO (10 µM; GSK-3 inhibitor — activates Wnt/β-catenin).

**Gene silencing:** siRNA targeting GSK-3α/β (Cell Signaling Technology) transfected into BMSCs before collagen gel culture; effect confirmed at 72 hr.

**Outcome measurements:**

- qRT-PCR for: tenomodulin (tnmd), Scleraxis (Scx), Mohawk (Mkx), NKD1 (Wnt downstream target), Col1a2, Col3a1, Col12a1, Col14a1, decorin, fibromodulin, lumican, tenascin-C, GAPDH (reference). Primer sequences listed in Table 1 of paper; annealing temperatures 45.6–59.6°C; amplicon sizes 91–215 bp.
- Reference tissue: native SDF tendon from 2 adult (3-year-old) male Thoroughbreds; powdered under liquid nitrogen, RNA extracted.
- Immunofluorescence: anti-β-catenin polyclonal antibody (Sigma-Aldrich) + TRITC secondary antibody + DAPI; LSM710 confocal laser-scanning microscope (Carl Zeiss).

**Statistics:** Mean ± SD of representative experiment in triplicate; Mann-Whitney U test for group comparisons; P < 0.05 significant.

Key tendon differentiation markers Col14a1, decorin, and fibromodulin are extremely low in monolayer BMSCs.

**Effect of BIO (GSK-3 inhibitor) vs. other signal inhibitors on tenomodulin expression in collagen gel:**

- BIO: tenomodulin mRNA upregulated approximately 3-fold within 72 hr versus control (P<0.05, Fig. 1a).
- Cytochalasin D, genistein, U0126: no effect on tenomodulin mRNA (NS).
- BIO also upregulated NKD1 (Wnt downstream target) confirming Wnt pathway activation (Fig. 1b, P<0.05).

**GSK-3 siRNA knockdown (Fig. 2):**

- GSK-3α/β siRNA knockdown in BMSCs in collagen gel (without BIO) produced tenomodulin upregulation similar to BIO treatment.
- Both BIO-treated and GSK-3 knockdown cells showed tenomodulin mRNA ~3-fold above control at 72 hr (P<0.05 vs. non-treated; different superscript letters on bar charts).
- Confirms GSK-3 inhibition (by either pharmacological or genetic means) drives tenomodulin expression via Wnt/β-catenin.

**β-catenin nuclear translocation (Fig. 3, immunofluorescence):**

- Control cells: β-catenin localised in cytoplasm (cytoplasmic staining, fibroblastic cell shape with collagen assemblies observed around cells).
- BIO-treated cells: β-catenin nuclear translocation confirmed; cells showed large round shape with very thin cytoplasm (changed morphology).

**Effect of culture condition (monolayer vs. collagen gel) × BIO on ECM components (Fig. 4, qRT-PCR):**

- Tenomodulin: slightly enhanced in collagen gel vs. monolayer (without BIO); strongly enhanced in collagen gel + BIO. BIO in monolayer culture showed no effect.
- Scleraxis: not affected by BIO in either culture condition.
- Mohawk: not affected by BIO in either culture condition.
- Col14a1: monolayer BMSCs relative value <1/100 of tendon; BIO in collagen gel raised Col14a1 to tendon level (upregulated ~160-fold from monolayer baseline; P<0.05). BIO in monolayer: no effect.
- Decorin: BIO in collagen gel raised decorin ~3–4-fold vs. monolayer (P<0.05). BIO in monolayer: no effect.
- Fibromodulin: BIO in collagen gel raised fibromodulin ~3–4-fold vs. monolayer (P<0.05). BIO in monolayer: no effect.

**Summary of upregulation magnitude by BIO in collagen gel:**

- Col14a1 relative to GAPDH: upregulated ~160-fold vs. monolayer; reached tendon level.
- Decorin: upregulated ~7-fold vs. monolayer (not fully reaching tendon level of ~42× higher).
- Tenomodulin: upregulated ~2-fold vs. monolayer.

Collagen gel culture itself (without BIO) slightly upregulated tenomodulin; authors attribute this to the collagen matrix stimulating β-catenin stabilisation even without pharmacological intervention (consistent with human BMSC collagen lattice studies showing nuclear β-catenin increase).

## Discussion & interpretation

The finding that Wnt/β-catenin acts independently of Scleraxis and Mohawk is novel. Prior work established that Scleraxis-null mice lack tenomodulin expression on embryonic day 16.5, and Mohawk-null mice show reduced tenomodulin in embryonic tendons. The BIO experiment shows that neither of these transcription factors changes with BIO treatment in equine BMSCs, yet tenomodulin is upregulated — demonstrating a parallel, adult-stage Wnt-mediated pathway.

The requirement for collagen gel (3D matrix) for BIO's effect suggests that ECM mechanical/structural cues cooperate with Wnt signalling. This is consistent with the hypothesis that during natural SDFT repair, the collagen-rich environment of the healing tendon provides the matrix signal that, if sufficient Wnt activity is present, drives BMSC → tenocyte differentiation. Without adequate Wnt activation, BMSCs in the tendon repair environment may default to a fibroblast-like scar phenotype (high Col3a1 and Col12a1, low Col14a1 and tenomodulin), explaining the biomechanical inferiority of repaired tendons.

The Col14a1 upregulation to tendon levels by BIO is particularly notable because Col14a1 (type XIV collagen, a FACIT collagen) regulates collagen fibril assembly and diameter; reduced Col14a1 in scar repair tissue is consistent with the disorganised collagen fibril structure observed histologically in repaired SDFTs.

Decorin and fibromodulin (small leucine-rich proteoglycans) regulate fibril diameter and assembly; their upregulation by Wnt/BIO (to ~3–4× monolayer but not yet tendon level) suggests partial but incomplete tendon ECM recapitulation, consistent with the clinical observation that BMSC-repaired tendons are mechanically stronger than scar but weaker than native tendon.

## Limitations

- In vitro only; no in vivo validation that BIO-primed BMSCs, when injected into an injured SDFT, produce better repair than unprimed BMSCs.
- n=8 horses for BMSC derivation; n=2 horses for reference tendon RNA — small samples with pooled tendon tissue.
- BIO concentration (10 µM) is pharmacological; endogenous Wnt ligand levels in injured tendon environment are unknown.
- Collagen gel culture is artificial; the 3D matrix may not recapitulate the mechanical and biochemical milieu of an injured SDFT in vivo.
- Population doubling level 4 — BMSCs may have been partially culture-adapted; earlier passage cells might respond differently.
- The qRT-PCR validation by sequencing of amplified fragments confirms primer specificity but not protein-level expression or function.
