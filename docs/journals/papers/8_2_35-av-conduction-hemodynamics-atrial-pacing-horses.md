## Abstract (verbatim)

> The relationship between the atrioventricular (AV) conduction ratio and systolic blood pressure or right atrial pressure during atrial pacing was investigated before and after pharmacological autonomic nervous blockade (PAB) in horses. The increase in the pacing rate decreased the AV conduction ratio. Systolic blood pressure increased when the AV conduction ratio became low, but the relevancy of the both stopped being recognized after PAB. Also right atrial pressure decreased with a decrease in the AV conduction ratio irrespective of the presence of PAB. These findings indicate that the onset mechanism of dropped beats involves an interaction among atrial rate, AV conduction, autonomic nervous system, and hemodynamics in horses with a second degree AV block.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **B (respiratory)** and cardiac performance, specifically cardiac arrhythmia as a performance risk. Second-degree AV block (dropped beats) is the most common arrhythmia in resting horses and is typically vagally mediated and benign at rest, but its persistence or transition during exercise can impair cardiac output and racing performance. This paper mechanistically characterises the dropped-beat phenomenon and quantifies the hemodynamic consequences — a systolic blood pressure rise via the baroceptor reflex — providing the physiological basis for understanding when AV block transitions from benign to performance-limiting.

For a finishing-position model, the direct link is indirect: horses with persistent high-frequency dropped beats or those diagnosed with true structural AV block have reduced effective cardiac output per unit time, reducing O2 delivery during racing. The companion ECG paper (8_2_39) provides clinical context. The mechanism described here (autonomic-mediated SBP rise when AV ratio drops) implies that horses with high baseline sympathetic tone may experience different hemodynamic consequences from dropped beats than parasympathetically dominant horses — a source of individual variation in cardiac performance under exercise stress.

The data also document baseline hemodynamic values in normal 2-year-old Thoroughbreds before and after full autonomic blockade (atropine + propranolol), providing physiological reference values.

## Background & objective

Watsabaugh et al. (1964) reported that systolic pressure gradually increased with dropped beats in horses with 2nd-degree AV block. The authors' prior work (Yamaya et al. 1993) demonstrated diurnal variation in dropped-beat frequency and that pacing can induce dropped beats even after autonomic blockade (Yamaya et al. 1997). The present study aimed to elucidate the onset mechanism of 2nd-degree AV block by simultaneously measuring atrial rate (via pacing), AV conduction ratio (QRS/P), systolic blood pressure (SBP), and right atrial pressure (RAP) before and after full pharmacological autonomic blockade, to separate the autonomic-dependent from autonomic-independent hemodynamic effects.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 6 clinically normal 2-year-old Thoroughbreds; all had undergone left carotid loop displacement (for blood pressure access); no arrhythmia at rest before experiment.

**Instrumentation:**

- Pacing catheter: 7Fr bipolar electrode catheter inserted via left jugular vein; tip placed in higher right atrium; stimulation: 2.0 msec duration, 9 volts output.
- RAP: micro-tip catheter pressure transducer (Millar Instruments) in centre of right atrium via same approach.
- SBP: 18-gauge catheter in left carotid loop connected to blood pressure transducer (Nihon Koden).
- ECG: A-B lead recorded simultaneously at 25 mm/sec.

**Pacing protocol:** Pacing rate from slightly above intrinsic atrial rate (starting at 50/min) to 180/min in 10/min increments; 1 min at each rate; 1 min resting interval between rates. After full data collection, pharmacological autonomic blockade (PAB) applied: atropine sulfate 0.04 mg/kg IV + propranolol hydrochloride 0.3 mg/kg IV. Complete protocol repeated after PAB.

**Measurements:**

- AV conduction ratio: number of QRS complexes / number of P waves, calculated from ECG during pacing
- SBP: measured from peak of carotid blood pressure wave; average of 10 sec before cessation of pacing
- RAP: measured from peak of RAP wave; average of 10 sec before cessation of pacing
- ΔSBP = SBP during pacing − SBP before pacing
- ΔRAP = RAP during pacing − RAP before pacing

**Statistics:** Pearson correlation coefficients between AV conduction ratio and ΔSBP, and between AV conduction ratio and ΔRAP; significance at P < 0.01.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

**Baseline hemodynamics before PAB (means ± SD, n = 6):**
| Variable | Before PAB |
|----------|-----------|
| Atrial rate (AR, beats/min) | 43 ± 5 |
| Systolic blood pressure (SBP, mmHg) | 132.5 ± 5.7 |
| Right atrial pressure (RAP, mmHg) | 4.8 ± 0.8 |

**Baseline hemodynamics after PAB (means ± SD, n = 6):**
| Variable | After PAB |
|----------|----------|
| Atrial rate (AR, beats/min) | 93 ± 9 |
| Systolic blood pressure (SBP, mmHg) | 173.2 ± 4.6 |
| Right atrial pressure (RAP, mmHg) | 3.1 ± 2.2 |

Atropine + propranolol raised AR from 43 to 93 beats/min (vagal tone removal); raised SBP from 132.5 to 173.2 mmHg (sympathetic dominance after β-blockade removed vagal withdrawal effect?); slightly reduced RAP.

**AV conduction ratio during pacing:**

- Before PAB: AV conduction ratio decreased gradually (linearly) with increasing pacing rate.
- After PAB: AV conduction ratio also decreased linearly with pacing rate, but was **higher at any given pacing rate** compared to before PAB. (Vagal tone removal by atropine reduced AV nodal refractoriness, allowing more beats to conduct.)

**AV conduction ratio vs. ΔSBP:**

- **Before PAB:** Significant **negative** correlation (P < 0.01): lower AV conduction ratio (more dropped beats) → higher ΔSBP (SBP rose when beats were dropped).
- **After PAB:** Correlation **not significant** (no longer present).

**AV conduction ratio vs. ΔRAP:**

- **Before PAB:** Significant **positive** correlation (P < 0.01): lower AV conduction ratio → lower RAP (RAP decreased with dropped beats).
- **After PAB:** Still significant **positive** correlation (P < 0.01): relationship persisted after autonomic blockade.

**Giant "a" waves in RAP:** Observed in RAP traces when dropped beats occurred. These arise from atrial contraction against a closed AV valve when the ventricle fails to respond to a P wave — the same phenomenon seen in clinical 2nd-degree AV block in horses (Brown & Holmes 1978 cited).

**Summary of mechanism (from Discussion):**

1. Mismatch between AR and AV conduction → dropped beat
2. Dropped beat → decreased RAP (ventricular filling reduced)
3. Decreased RAP → inhibited sinoatrial automaticity (Ito et al. 1988 mechanism) OR increased sinoatrial automaticity (Paulay et al. 1971 mechanism depending on direction)
4. Dropped beats → decreased effective cardiac output → SBP rises via arterial baroceptor reflex → enhanced sympathetic activity
5. Enhanced sympathetic activity dominates after dropped beats → SBP rise
6. This sympathetic-mediated SBP rise is abolished after PAB — confirming autonomic mediation

## Discussion & interpretation

The paper demonstrates that the 2nd-degree AV block mechanism in horses is a self-reinforcing cycle: AR–AV mismatch → dropped beat → RAP decreases → altered sinoatrial automaticity (the AR/AV mismatch may be transiently corrected, then re-established) → SBP rises via baroceptors → vagal tone enhanced against baroceptor-driven SBP rise → further AV node refractoriness → more dropped beats. The RAP–AV conduction correlation survives PAB (autonomic-independent), suggesting direct mechanical coupling between atrial filling pressure and AV nodal function. The SBP–AV conduction correlation is autonomic-dependent (abolished by PAB), confirming it requires sympathetic-vagal interaction.

The clinical relevance: horses with high resting vagal tone (athletic hearts) are more prone to 2nd-degree AV block at rest; this is generally benign. However, if the cycle becomes persistent during exercise (when vagal tone should be suppressed), the dropped-beat-SBP feedback loop could impair cardiac output. Horses where the AV block persists into warm-up or early exercise stages have reduced effective HR (and therefore cardiac output) during that window.

## Limitations

- n = 6 horses, acute invasive experiment; highly controlled conditions may not reflect natural resting or exercise states.
- Fixed-rate atrial pacing is an artificial induction of dropped beats; natural 2nd-degree AV block in horses involves spontaneous sinus rhythm variation.
- Left carotid loop displacement surgery required — not applicable to routine clinical horses.
- Only 2-year-old horses; AV nodal function changes with age and training.
- Hemodynamics measured after dropped beats were already established, not during the transition — temporal dynamics of cycle initiation not fully captured.
- No performance (race) data linked to arrhythmia parameters.

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `av_block_2nd_degree_flag` — binary, 1 if clinical record documents 2nd-degree AV block — JRA/NAR veterinary screening records — mild AV block at rest is common and typically benign; persistence into exercise is performance-limiting
- `dropped_beat_frequency_rest` — ECG-derived dropped beat rate at rest (per minute) — clinical ECG records — high frequency (e.g., >2/min at rest) may indicate elevated vagal tone or early cardiac disease; needs context (age, fitness level)
- `av_block_exercise_persistence_flag` — binary, 1 if AV block documented during or immediately post-exercise — clinical records — stronger predictor of cardiac output impairment and underperformance than resting AV block
- `resting_sbp` — resting systolic blood pressure (mmHg) — clinical records — reference ~132 mmHg before PAB in 2-year-olds; elevated resting SBP may indicate sympathetic activation or cardiac disease
- `resting_rap` — resting right atrial pressure (mmHg) — clinical records — reference ~4.8 mmHg; not routinely measured outside research settings
- **Interaction:** `av_block_flag` × `race_distance` — cardiac output impairment from dropped beats should disproportionately affect endurance performance at longer distances vs. short sprints
- **Interaction:** `av_block_flag` × `age` — 2nd-degree AV block most common in young athletic horses due to high vagal tone; in older horses, structural atrial changes more likely (see 8_2_39 companion paper)
- **Clinical data availability:** JRA/NAR veterinary withdrawal records may include cardiac arrhythmia codes; linking these to race entries would operationalise the cardiac risk feature without direct ECG data
