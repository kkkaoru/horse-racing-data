## Abstract (verbatim)

> It is important to know the effects of the inclination of a slope on the activity of each muscle, because training by running on a sloped track is commonly used for Thoroughbred racehorses. The effects of incline (from −6 to +6%) on the forelimbs and hind limbs during walking and trotting on a treadmill were evaluated by an integrated electromyogram (iEMG). The muscle activities in the forelimbs (5 horses) and hind limbs (4 horses) were measured separately. Two stainless steel wires were inserted into each of the brachiocephalicus (Bc), biceps brachii (BB), splenius (Sp), and pectoralis descendens (PD) in the forelimb experiment and into the longissimus dorsi (LD), vastus lateralis (VL), gluteus medius (GM), and biceps femoris (BF) in the hind limb experiment. The EMG recordings were taken at a sampling rate of 1,000 Hz. At a walk, the iEMG values for the forelimb were not significantly different under any of the inclinations. In the hind limb, the iEMG values for the GM and BF significantly decreased as the inclination decreased. At a trot, the iEMG values for the Bc in the forelimb significantly decreased as the inclination of the treadmill decreased. In the hind limb, the iEMG values for the LD, GM, and BF significantly decreased as the inclination decreased. Uphill exercise increased the iEMG values for the Bc, LD, GM, and BF, while downhill exercise resulted in little increase in the iEMG values. It was concluded that the effects of inclination on the muscle activities were larger for the uphill exercises, and for the hind limb muscles compared with the forelimb muscles.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

Feature family **C (exercise-physiology/fitness — training environment)**.

This paper establishes that uphill training is the dominant stimulus for propulsive hindlimb muscle conditioning in Thoroughbreds, while downhill training produces negligible additional muscle activation. The JRA standard training track slope is 3–3.5%, and this study tested ±3% and ±6% (representing the range from standard uphill to standard downhill). The finding that gluteus medius (GM), biceps femoris (BF), and longissimus dorsi (LD) iEMG values are substantially higher at +6% vs. flat, while there is no meaningful increase on negative slopes, means:

1. Horses trained at JRA centres with steeper uphill tracks (e.g., if Miho vs. Ritto training centre slope profiles differ) receive differential hindlimb propulsive muscle conditioning.
2. The propulsive muscles (GM, BF) are the primary drivers of race pace and acceleration; horses with superior hindlimb conditioning from slope training should, in theory, have higher finishing speed capacity.
3. Downhill training does not compensate: it reduces stride time slightly at −6% but does not increase muscle activation — making it a cardiovascular rather than a strength training stimulus.

For the model, `training_centre` (Miho vs. Ritto vs. other JRA centres) could serve as a proxy for track gradient profile, capturing differential conditioning. The study also quantifies that walking and trotting account for 60–80 min and 5–15 min respectively of a 90–120 min JRA daily training session, so walk-level inclination effects (none for forelimb; significant for GM/BF) also contribute to cumulative daily conditioning.

## Background & objective

Thoroughbred racehorses at JRA training centres complete 90–120 min of daily exercise: walking (60–80 min) and trotting (5–15 min) constitute the majority. Uphill training is commonly used; the JRA standard slope is 3–3.5%. Only one prior study (Crook et al. 2010) reported downhill effects on hindlimb muscles; forelimb and systematic ±gradient comparisons for Thoroughbreds at both walk and trot were not available. Objective: evaluate iEMG responses of forelimb and hindlimb key muscles across 5 gradients (−6%, −3%, 0%, +3%, +6%) at walk (1.6 m/s) and trot (3.5 m/s).

## Materials & methods

**Animals:**

- Forelimb experiment: 5 gelding Thoroughbreds, ages 5–7 years, body weight 468–535 kg.
- Hindlimb experiment: 4 gelding Thoroughbreds, ages 5–7 years, body weight 468–544 kg.
- Both groups familiar with running on upward and downward inclined treadmills.
- Note: animal ages overlap; likely some horses participated in both experiments.

**EMG electrodes:** Teflon-coated stainless steel wire (A-M Systems Inc., Carlsborg, WA); 1 cm tip bared and bent back + twisted around 23G × 6 cm needle shaft. Inserted intramuscularly per muscle:

_Forelimb experiment:_

- Brachiocephalicus (Bc): middle point of muscle belly, depth 20 mm
- Biceps brachii (BB): middle point of muscle belly, depth 50 mm
- Splenius (Sp): level of 2nd cervical vertebra, depth 20 mm
- Pectoralis descendens (PD): middle point of muscle belly, depth 20 mm

_Hindlimb experiment:_

- Longissimus dorsi (LD): level of 1st lumbar vertebra, depth 50 mm
- Biceps femoris (BF): upper third of muscle belly, depth 60 mm
- Vastus lateralis (VL): middle point of muscle belly, depth 80 mm
- Gluteus medius (GM): middle point of muscle belly, depth 40 mm

Bipolar electrodes; 3 cm inter-electrode distance. EMG amplifier: Type 271 (NEC San-ei Sokki Inc., Tokyo); frequency response 32–500 Hz.

**Hoof strain gauges:** N22-FA-10-120-11-VS3 (NEC San-ei Instruments); attached to dorsal midline of fore or hind hoof; connected to dynamic strain measuring instruments DP-612B (Kyowa Electronic Instruments, Tokyo). Used to detect hoof contact for stride timing.

**Data acquisition:** WINDAQ software (DATAQ, Akron, OH); 1,000 Hz sampling rate for both EMG and strain gauge. Left and right side muscles recorded; one side selected based on signal quality.

**Treadmill:** SÄTO AB (Knivsta, Sweden). Five gradients: −6%, −3%, 0%, +3%, +6%. Sequence randomised between subjects. Each condition ~1 min duration.

**Speeds:** Walk at 1.6 m/s; Trot at 3.5 m/s.

**Signal processing:** Software BIMUTUS (Kissei Comtec Co., Nagano); high-pass Butterworth filter 10 Hz; low-pass Butterworth filter 40 Hz. iEMG = integrated EMG per stride for each muscle; averaged over 5 consecutive strides per condition.

**Statistics:** Repeated measures ANOVA (R 3.0.2, within-subject design, compensates for inter-horse variability). Pairwise Ryan test applied post-hoc where ANOVA showed P < 0.05.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

**Stride time and duty factor:**

- Forelimb experiment: stride time and duty factor at walk and trot were NOT significantly different under any inclination.
- Hindlimb experiment (walk): stride time at −6% was significantly shorter than at 0% and +3%.
  - Mean difference: −0.035 s at −6% vs. 0% (approximately −3.3% shorter).
- Hindlimb experiment (trot): stride time at −6% was significantly shorter than at 0%, +3%, and +6%.
  - Mean difference: −0.024 s at −6% vs. +3% (approximately −3.5% shorter).
- Duty factor at walk and trot: NOT significantly different under any inclination in either experiment.

**Forelimb iEMG at WALK (Fig. 2, left panel; n=Bc 5, BB 3, Sp 4, PD 5):**

- Bc: NS across all inclinations.
- BB: NS (n=3; low statistical power noted).
- Sp: NS.
- PD: NS.
- All forelimb muscles: no significant inclination effect at walk.

**Hindlimb iEMG at WALK (Fig. 2, right panel; n=LD 3, VL 3, GM 4, BF 4):**

- LD: NS across all inclinations.
- VL: NS across all inclinations.
- GM: Significantly lower at −6%, −3%, 0%, +3% than at +6%. All inclinations other than +6% had significantly lower iEMG than at +6%. (Exact n=4.)
- BF: Significantly decreased as inclination decreased; significant difference between each consecutive inclination pair EXCEPT between −6% and −3% (NS). I.e.: −6% ≈ −3% < 0% < +3% < +6% (P<0.05 for all other adjacent pairs).

**Forelimb iEMG at TROT (Fig. 3, left panel; n=Bc 4, BB 2, Sp 3, PD 5):**

- Bc: Significantly lower at −6%, −3%, and 0% than at +6% (P<0.05). Not specified for +3% vs. +6%.
- BB: Statistical comparison not performed (only 2 horses; insufficient power).
- Sp: NS across all inclinations.
- PD: NS across all inclinations.

**Hindlimb iEMG at TROT (Fig. 3, right panel; n=LD 3, VL 4, GM 4, BF 4):**

- VL: NS across all inclinations.
- LD: −6% and −3% significantly lower than +3% and +6%; flat (0%) not significantly different from +3% or +6% in the reported comparisons.
- GM: −6% and −3% significantly lower than +6%; intermediate inclinations not individually specified beyond this.
- BF: All inclinations other than +6% were significantly lower than +6% (i.e., +3%, 0%, −3%, −6% all < +6% at P<0.05).

**Oxygen consumption context (cited in discussion, from Hiraga et al. 1995 and Wickler et al. 2000/2006):**

- O₂ consumption approximately +250% on 10% upslope vs. level trotting.
- O₂ consumption approximately −50% on −10% downslope vs. level trotting.
- The iEMG increases on upslope (Bc, GM, BF, LD) reflect increased muscle energy demand consistent with O₂ increase.

## Discussion & interpretation

**Forelimb — uphill:** Bc (brachiocephalicus) protracts the forelimb; its iEMG increases uphill because gravity retards forward limb swing. Conversely, downhill gravity assists protraction, reducing Bc recruitment. BB (biceps brachii, antigravity) was not significantly different at any inclination, despite the decrease in forelimb VGRF reported on upslope in other studies — the authors suggest their ±6% range may be insufficient to reach the threshold for BB response.

**Hindlimb — uphill:** GM and BF produce propulsive force; their iEMG tracks the propulsive demand, which increases with inclination. LD (longissimus dorsi, back-bending) responded to inclination at trot but not walk; authors interpret this as greater back movement at trot than walk making trunk stabilisation more inclination-sensitive at trot. VL (vastus lateralis, antigravity quadriceps) showed no significant inclination effect despite expectations from VGRF theory; authors note ±6% may be below the VL activation threshold.

**Downhill finding:** On negative slopes, iEMG values for active muscles (Bc, GM, BF) did not increase above flat-ground values — they decreased. This means downhill training provides no additional muscle strengthening stimulus for these muscles; it only slightly shortens stride time. The asymmetry between uphill (strong muscle activation increase) and downhill (no increase) has implications for program design: equal distance of uphill and downhill training is NOT equivalent to double the uphill.

**Training implications:** New forelimb-specific training methods are needed — the paper notes that no downhill or flat condition provided significant forelimb muscle strengthening beyond that seen at +6%. Uphill is the key conditioning stimulus for the muscles driving race performance (GM, BF for propulsion; LD for trunk stability). The standard JRA 3–3.5% uphill training slope falls between the 0% and +6% test conditions, likely producing intermediate muscle activation effects.

## Limitations

- Small n: forelimb n=5, hindlimb n=4; some muscles had only n=3 or 2 observations due to electrode failure, reducing statistical power.
- Speeds tested: walk (1.6 m/s) and trot (3.5 m/s) only; race gallop is 16–17 m/s. The inclination-EMG relationship at gallop is unknown and likely qualitatively different (motor unit recruitment patterns change with speed).
- Inclination range ±6% around the JRA standard of 3–3.5%; more extreme slopes (±10%, as in some human studies showing VO₂ responses) were not tested.
- All geldings; results may not apply to entire JRA racehorse population (intact males, fillies may differ in muscle mass distribution).
- Electrode placement precision: fine-wire intramuscular EMG can vary between sessions; calibration with maximal voluntary contraction not performed (would require surgery in horses).
- No data on whether chronic uphill training at 3–3.5% produces measurable muscle hypertrophy or performance gains (this is a single acute session study).

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `training_centre` — categorical: Miho / Ritto / Niihari / other JRA training centre — derivation: horse training registration in JRA records — expected effect: training centres with steeper uphill facilities condition GM/BF more intensely; indirect proxy for hindlimb propulsive muscle fitness — data availability: available in JRA public horse registration data
- `training_centre_slope_profile` — float: estimated mean uphill gradient at primary training facility (%) — derivation: geographic/facility data (JRA public information) — expected effect: positive correlation with GM/BF conditioning intensity; supports finishing speed — interaction with distance: more relevant for sprinters (propulsive demand per unit time higher)
- `daily_walk_minutes` / `daily_trot_minutes` — float: from training logs — derivation: JRA training records (if available) — expected effect: 60–80 min walk + 5–15 min trot at JRA centres; gradient × duration = cumulative muscle activation; greater uphill walk duration contributes meaningfully (GM/BF respond at walk on upslope)
- `recent_training_days_uphill` — count of uphill training days in prior 30 days — derivation: training logs — expected effect: positive for propulsive fitness — data availability: likely restricted to trainer records
- Caution: this study is walk/trot only; do NOT directly extrapolate iEMG values to gallop performance; treat training-centre gradient as a qualitative conditioning environment proxy, not a quantitative force predictor
