## Abstract (verbatim)

> After publication of the epic report on equine exercise physiology by Matsuba and Shimamura in 1933, papers on exercise physiology of the racehorse in Japan began appearing in scientific journals and increased in number. In 1944, respiration during exercise at a walk, trot, and canter was measured by recording expiratory sounds with a microphone attached near the nostril. Respiratory frequency during cantering was synchronized with stride frequency, and expiratory sounds were found to occur during the stance phase of the trailing forelimb. Development of a radiotelemetry system in 1964 for electrocardiogram recording enabled the first recording of an equine electrocardiogram during field exercise that included fast galloping and calculation of heart rate (HR) during exercise. During low intensity exercise including walking, trotting, cantering and extended cantering, HR increased from 45 beat/min during pre-exercise to 150 beat/min at an extended canter. HR increased to 200 beat/min or more in most horses during 100 m of high-intensity sprint galloping. When blood lactate was measured after 3 days of draft work in 12 warhorses in 1934, no increase in blood lactate was found. The erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) was decreased by intense exercise and also decreased as training increased. It was suggested that measuring changes in ESR and body weight in relation to training might become useful as a screening index of training, condition, and fatigue. This evaluation method was named the "ESR-body weight method."

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

This review belongs to feature family **C (exercise physiology/fitness)** and is the companion to 27_1610. It synthesises the foundational Japanese exercise physiology data for Thoroughbreds (1930s–1970s), documenting three physiological systems with direct bearing on performance modelling:

1. **Heart rate (HR) as a fitness/intensity proxy:** The 1964 radiotelemetry study by Nomura et al. established that HR increases linearly with exercise intensity — from 45 bpm at rest to 150 bpm at extended canter to ≥200 bpm during 100 m sprint galloping. In modern performance modelling, HR thresholds (particularly the speed at which HR exceeds 200 bpm, known as V200) are key indicators of aerobic capacity. A horse whose V200 occurs at higher speeds is fitter and expected to perform better. These reference values anchor the Japanese context.

2. **Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) as a training-status indicator:** ESR decreases after intense exercise AND decreases progressively as training load increases. This non-specific but easily measured parameter captures both acute physiological response and chronic training adaptation. The "ESR-body weight method" — using ESR change + body weight change as a composite screening index — is a historical precedent for modern multi-marker fitness panels. If ESR data are available from JRA quarantine bloodwork, changes over the racing season could encode training status.

3. **Respiratory coupling and gait efficiency:** RF:STF 1:1 synchronisation at canter/gallop is a biomechanical efficiency marker. Departures from coupling (irregular RF:STF) reflect fatigue or gait transitions. These are not directly available in race databases but inform stride-frequency–based features if GPS or accelerometry data become available.

The quantitative HR thresholds and ESR direction effects from this paper, combined with the recovery-rate framework from 27_1610, form the physiological basis for interpreting blood-test results and training-load features in the finishing-position pipeline.

## Background & objective

After the landmark 1933 Matsuba & Shimamura report, Japanese exercise physiology research proliferated across multiple journals (J. Central Society for Veterinary Medicine, Nihon Juigaku Zasshi, Chikusan Gakkaiho, Experimental Reports of Equine Health Laboratory). This review collects the post-1933 body of work specifically on respiration, heart rate, and blood characteristics during and after exercise in Japanese racehorses and warhorses. The primary objective is to document and credit the historical contributions of Japanese researchers and make this literature accessible to the modern scientific community.

## Materials & methods

**This is a narrative review, not an original study.** The primary studies synthesised are:

- **Okabe & Sugiyama 1944 (respiration):** n=20 horses; measured RF and STF continuously during a 42-min protocol (30 min trot at 170 m/min, 3 min canter at 300–400 m/min, 9 min walk at 100 m/min) using microphone-recorded expiratory sounds.
- **Nomura et al. 1964 (heart rate via radiotelemetry):** First field ECG recording in horses worldwide during fast galloping. Custom-made radiotelemetry system (20 MHz carrier wave, 100 m range); later upgraded to 1 W (5 km range) and portable magnetic tape systems. HR calculated from R-R intervals.
- **Nomura 1960 (treadmill ECG):** Horse walking on specially constructed treadmill at 70 m/min drafting 5 different weights; HR during exercise and recovery via 20 MHz telemetry.
- **Yasuda 1934 (blood lactate):** n=12 warhorses; 3-day draft work (total distance 111 km); blood lactate measured pre/post/30 min/60 min after exercise.
- **Sakurai et al. 1967 (blood characteristics at varying intensities):** Exercise at 1,100 m at 6.7 m/s, 1,100 m at 11.1 m/s, and 1,600 m at 12.5 m/s. Multiple haematological parameters.
- **Hiroe et al. (ESR studies):** n=15 warhorses + 6 local horses; two protocols — faster riding (3 days, 142.5 km total) vs. slower draft work (4 days, 116.5 km total). ESR calculated as median of 1 hr and 2 hr sedimentation values: (1 hr + 2 hr / 2) / 2.
- **Shijo 1937–1938 (blood gas):** O₂ and CO₂ concentrations in arterial and venous blood; n=26 horses (cross-sectional) and n=4 horses (longitudinal over 3-month training period).
- **Thyroid hormones study:** T₃ and T₄ measured from September (yearling) through May (2yo) during training; T₃ as T₃-binding capacity index.
- **Muscle enzyme study:** CK, AST, fructose-diphosphate aldolase in 3 horses running 22,000 m/day for 5 consecutive days (mainly extended trot, partially slow canter). Blood at pre-, mid-, post-, 1 hr, 5 hr post each day; and 1, 2, 3, 5, 7 days post-exercise period.

## Results (detailed — reproduce ALL numbers)

### Respiratory frequency during exercise

| Exercise type                | RF (breaths/min)       | Notes                                                  |
| ---------------------------- | ---------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| Pre-exercise (rest)          | ~15–20 br/min          | Estimated from context                                 |
| Trot 170 m/min at 1 min      | 73 br/min              | (Okabe & Sugiyama 1944)                                |
| Trot 170 m/min at 12 min     | ~100 br/min            | Reached steady state                                   |
| Trot 170 m/min steady state  | >110 br/min thereafter | Average 99.9 br/min for 30 min                         |
| Canter (300–400 m/min)       | Synchronized with STF  | STF avg 105.3 str/min; RF:STF = 1:1                    |
| Walk immediately post-canter | 97 → ~110 br/min       | Slow decrease                                          |
| Immediately after stop       | 80 br/min (rapid drop) | Authors conclude RF post-exercise ≠ RF during exercise |

STF during trotting: ~84 str/min at 1 min, average 83.1 str/min over 30 min.

Sawazaki et al. trot protocols: At 300 m/min trot, RF steady state 95–100 br/min; at 200 m/min trot, RF steady state 75–80 br/min. RF:STF ratios of 1:1, 1:2, or 1:3 observed on straights (systematic coupling disappears on curves).

### Heart rate during exercise (Nomura et al. 1964)

| Exercise intensity                    | HR (beats/min)      |
| ------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| Pre-exercise                          | 45                  |
| Walk                                  | ~55–70 (estimated)  |
| Trot                                  | ~80–120             |
| Canter                                | ~120–140            |
| Extended canter                       | ~150                |
| 100 m high-intensity sprint galloping | ≥200 in most horses |

Treadmill study (Nomura 1960 at 70 m/min, draft work): HR reached steady state within 3 min for light loads; second HR increase ~10 min after start with heavy loads.

### Blood lactate (Yasuda 1934)

3-day draft work (111 km, low intensity): blood lactate showed **no increase** at any time point (Pre, Post, 30 min, 60 min post-exercise).

Sakurai et al. 1967 — blood lactate by exercise intensity:
| Intensity | Distance (m) | Speed (m/s) | Blood lactate post-exercise (mmol/l) |
|-----------|-------------|------------|--------------------------------------|
| Low | 1,100 | 6.7 | 0.7 |
| Moderate | 1,100 | 11.1 | 7.3 |
| High | 1,600 | 12.5 | 12.1 |

Sakurai et al. also measured RBC count, WBC count, Hb, haematocrit, erythrocyte resistance, blood lactate (all increased), and eosinophils, ESR, blood water content (all decreased) after intense exercise.

### Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR)

- After faster exercise (3-day riding, 142.5 km): ESR decreased by **14.5 mm** compared with before exercise.
- After slower draft work (4-day, 116.5 km): ESR did not differ from pre-exercise.
- ESR tends to be low when erythrocyte count is high.
- ESR decreased progressively as training increased (fitter horses show lower ESR).
- "ESR-body weight method" proposed as screening index of training condition and fatigue.

### Blood gas (Shijo 1937–1938)

- Arterial O₂: 20.81%; venous O₂: 12.08%.
- Plasma CO₂: arterial 46.60%; venous 57.85% (n=26 horses).
- In 4 horses over 3-month training: average venous CO₂ 61.6% (stable); venous O₂ 12.7% with relatively high correlation between O₂ concentration and erythrocyte count throughout training.
- Erythrocyte count and leukocyte count increased with intense exercise; neutrophils increased, lymphocytes decreased (Shijo 1938).

### Thyroid hormones during training (yearling → 2yo)

- T₃ (binding capacity index): decreased clearly in early training; remained constant in latter half.
- T₄: decreased in middle stage; increased markedly in final stage (highest training intensity). Authors interpreted T₄ elevation as indicator of "reserve strength."

### Muscle enzymes (long-distance exercise study: 22,000 m/day × 5 days)

- CK: peaked maximally at **5 hr after exercise**; increased more with higher intensity. Recommended as most useful index of exercise severity.
- AST: did not increase markedly with intensity but remained at relatively high levels and increased gradually during the exercise period. Recommended as index for overtraining.
- Fructose diphosphate aldolase: intermediate pattern between CK and AST.

## Discussion & interpretation

The authors provide context for each historical study, noting technological limitations (no direct in-field ECG until 1964, pulse rate auscultation used as HR proxy in the 1933 Matsuba study). Key interpretations:

- The synchronisation of RF with STF at canter/gallop (expiration occurs at trailing-forelimb stance phase) is a biomechanical coupling that constrains breathing efficiency during high-speed exercise — a fundamental constraint on aerobic capacity.
- ESR as a training index: its non-specificity is acknowledged, but its directional value (decrease = fitter / more adapted) and accessibility (simple blood test) make it potentially useful as part of a composite fitness panel.
- The absence of lactate increase after low-intensity draft work contrasts with the marked elevation at racing speeds (12.1 mmol/l at 12.5 m/s), confirming that lactate is only a relevant fatigue marker above a threshold speed — consistent with modern lactate threshold (LT) concepts.
- The ESR-body weight method anticipates modern composite fitness indices combining physiological variables with body condition assessment.

## Limitations

- All primary studies cited are historical (1934–1978); analytical methods (auscultation, sedimentation) are far less precise than modern equivalents.
- Sample sizes are generally small (n=3–26 per study) and protocols vary, limiting generalisability.
- No direct link between any physiological measure and race finishing position; performance inferences are indirect.
- Warhorses and draft horses (most studies 1934–1960) differ from modern racehorses in training load, breed characteristics, and exercise intensity; some results may not translate.

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `hr_max_gallop_proxy` — estimated maximum HR during race gallop (≥200 bpm reference) — source: not directly available; HR monitoring data if available from training centre records — expected effect: horses with higher V200 (speed at HR=200) perform better — data availability: currently rare in JRA databases
- `esr_change_training` — change in ESR over training period (negative = improving fitness) — source: JRA quarantine/training centre bloodwork if available — expected effect: decreasing ESR trajectory → better condition → positive finishing position association — data availability: potentially in JRA clinical records
- `blood_lactate_post_exercise` — post-gallop blood lactate (mmol/l) — source: training centre lab records — expected effect: lower lactate at standard gallop speed = better aerobic fitness; expected negative correlation with finishing position — threshold: ~4 mmol/l (LT2) as key boundary
- `ck_post_exercise` — creatine kinase activity 5 hr post-gallop — source: training centre bloodwork — expected effect: elevated CK = muscle damage / overtraining risk; very high CK predicts scratch or poor performance
- `ast_trend` — AST trend over training period — source: periodic blood sampling — expected effect: rising AST despite rest = overtraining indicator; positive predictor of sub-par performance
- `rf_stf_coupling` — binary indicator of RF:STF synchronisation during exercise — source: only available from motion/audio sensors; not in standard databases — do NOT use as a standard feature without specialised sensor data
- **Note:** All blood-based features require sex- and age-specific normalisation (see 27_1606 for reference ranges). Males and females differ significantly in baseline values.
