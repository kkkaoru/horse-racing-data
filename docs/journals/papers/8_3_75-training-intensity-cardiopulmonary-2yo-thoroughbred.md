## Abstract (verbatim)

> We investigated the effect of training intensity difference on cardiorespiratory parameters in twelve 2 year-old Thoroughbred horses during 8 weeks of training (5 days/week). Six horses were trained according to conventional training method including cantering (Canter group), while other 6 horses were exercised only by walking and trotting throughout the same period (Trot group). Each horse performed an incremental exercise test on a treadmill before (Pre-test) and after (Post-test) the training period. In the Pre-test, there were no significant differences between the Canter group and the Trot group regarding V̇O₂max (154.7 ± 7.8 ml/kg/min in the Canter group, 147.2 ± 7.5 ml/kg/min in the Trot group). After the training period, V̇O₂max was significantly higher in the Canter group (165.0 ± 11.6 ml/kg/min) compared to the Trot Group (150.3 ± 14.7 ml/kg/min). There were no differences in either group regarding the relationship between HR and speed, or La and speed in the Pre-test. During the Post-test, changes in HR in relation to the speed tended to be lower in Canter group, and La was also slightly lower in this group. Peak values of PCV increased significantly in both groups after the training, however, there were no differences between the groups. These results suggest that even low intensity training such as trotting can maintain V̇O₂max. This indicates that higher intensity training involving cantering is not necessarily required merely to maintain cardiorespiratory function of 2 year-old Thoroughbred in early stage of training. However the findings also indicates that higher intensity training is required to substantially improve cardiorespiratory function in 2 year-old Thoroughbred horses.

## Relevance to finishing-position (着順) prediction

This paper belongs to **Feature family C (exercise-physiology/fitness)**. It is a controlled randomised trial — on actual 2-year-old JRA Thoroughbreds at Hidaka Training Farm — directly comparing conventional canter-inclusive training vs. trot-only training on VO₂max, HR–speed relationship, lactate–speed relationship, and PCV. All parameters are measured pre- and post-training on a standardised treadmill protocol, providing clean within-subject estimates of training-induced adaptation.

The key quantitative result is that canter training raises VO₂max by 6.7% (154.7 → 165.0 ml/kg/min) vs. 2.1% for trot-only training (147.2 → 150.3 ml/kg/min), and the post-training difference is significant (p < 0.05). This means that training type — specifically whether cantering at 80–90% HRmax was performed — is a measurable determinant of aerobic ceiling at race debut. For a finishing-position model, training-type history before first race (or before a given season start) is a potentially strong feature encoding the horse's expected VO₂max and metabolic efficiency.

The paper also shows that trot-only training does not increase VO₂max but maintains it, while canter training increases it. This implies a non-linear relationship: the threshold is at training intensity ≥ 80–90% HRmax (cantering). Submaximal HRmax proxies derived from trackwork speed–HR data (if available) could be used to classify training intensity and infer VO₂max tier.

The body weight divergence (Canter −10 kg; Trot +10 kg over 8 weeks on identical diet) is also notable: relative VO₂max (ml/kg/min) was partly driven by lower body weight in the Canter group, not just absolute oxygen uptake. Body weight change during training is a feasible feature from JRA/NAR 馬体重 records.

## Background & objective

After breaking, Japanese racehorses begin structured early training. In Hokkaido (main Thoroughbred production area), the period from breaking to initial training coincides with cold/snowy winter when cantering on outdoor tracks is difficult; some horses are trained with trot-only for weeks. Prior work (Art and Lekeux 1993; Evans and Rose 1988; Knight et al. 1991) established that submaximal training increases VO₂max in adult horses. For 2-year-olds, Hiraga et al. (1995) showed that long slow distance (LSD) cantering had little effect on VO₂max, suggesting intensity matters. The question: does trot-only (40% HRmax) training maintain or increase VO₂max compared with canter (80–90% HRmax) training in 2-year-olds already in early training? This directly informs winter training protocols in Hokkaido.

## Materials & methods

**Subjects:** 12 two-year-old Thoroughbred horses (Table 1):
| Group | Pre-test age (months) | Pre-test BW (kg) | Post-test age (months) | Post-test BW (kg) |
|-------|----------------------|-------------------|------------------------|-------------------|
| Canter (n=6) | 21.7 ± 0.7 | 449.2 ± 32.6 | 23.6 ± 0.6 | 439.4 ± 24.7 |
| Trot (n=6) | 21.5 ± 1.1 | 461.3 ± 45.7 | 22.5 ± 1.1 | 470.5 ± 45.1 |

All broken to handling/riding in November of their yearling year, then trained in a riding hall (40 m square) at trot and slow canter through December.

**Training protocol (January–March, 8 weeks, 5 days/week):**

Canter group (Table 2 — full schedule):

- December: Indoor wood-tip course 1,000 m: Walk 2,200 m, Trot 1,200 m, Canter 1,000 m (5 days/week)
- January: Indoor wood-tip 1,000 m: W-2,000 m, T-1,400 m, C-2,000 m (4 days/week); Indoor dirt 600 m: W-3,700 m, T-1,300 m, C-2,000 m (1 day/week)
- February: Mix of wood-tip and dirt; W-2,000–3,700 m, T-1,300–1,400 m, C-2,000 m (5 days/week)
- March: Indoor dirt: up to W-3,400–3,700 m, T-1,100–1,400 m, C-1,800–2,400 m (5 days/week)
- Final stage cantering: up to 1,000 m at 800 m/min (~13.3 m/s)

Trot group: Walking (~30 min) + Trotting (~10 min, ~2,000 m) throughout all 8 weeks. No cantering.

HR during training monitored at week 8 with Polar Bandage-XL HR monitor (Finland).

**Treadmill exercise test (Kagra Mustang-2200, Switzerland):**

- 10% incline
- Warm-up: 5 min trot at 4.0 m/s, then 5 min rest
- Incremental test: 1.8, 2.7, 3.4, 4.5, 5.4, 6.8, 9.0, 10.8, 11.6 m/s, 1 min per step
- Recovery: walk at 1.0 m/s for 15 min at 10% incline
- Test terminated when horse could not maintain position without assistance

**Measurements:**

- VO₂ and VCO₂: continuous, bias-flow ventilatory system (Erickson et al. 1994)
- HR: HR monitor, last 30 s of each step
- Blood lactate (La): YSI-1500 Sport automated analyser, last 15 s of each step; intravenous catheter left jugular
- PCV: micro-haematocrit method

**Statistics:** ANOVA for repeated measurements; significance p < 0.05; results as mean ± SD.

## Discussion & interpretation

**VO₂max interpretation:** The significant post-test Canter > Trot difference (165.0 vs. 150.3 ml/kg/min) is partly attributable to body weight divergence (Canter −10 kg, Trot +10 kg). Absolute VO₂max (l/min) was similar between groups and both increased slightly. The relative VO₂max advantage in Canter is thus a combined effect of genuine aerobic adaptation and body composition change. The authors appropriately acknowledge both contributions.

**Comparison with adult training studies:** Knight et al. (1991) found similar VO₂max increases at 40% and 80% VO₂max intensities in adult horses, suggesting intensity may matter less for adults than for 2-year-olds. Art and Lekeux (1993) showed that low-intensity aerobic training alone produced no significant VO₂max change, but subsequent 3 weeks of high-intensity interval training produced a substantial increase — consistent with the current Canter vs. Trot dichotomy.

**"Maintenance" finding:** The Trot group VO₂max barely changed (2.1% increase), suggesting that 2-year-old Thoroughbreds at this training stage have already experienced VO₂max increases during the breaking process (Hiraga et al. 1997; J. Equine Sci. 8: 21–24) and that low-intensity trot can only maintain, not further elevate, the aerobic ceiling. This has direct practical implications: in winter Hokkaido conditions, trot-only training preserves fitness but does not build it.

**HR efficiency:** Lower HR at given speeds post-Canter training matches the classical "economy of exercise" adaptation — the heart pumps more oxygen per beat (higher stroke volume) after high-intensity training, reducing required HR at submaximal speeds. This is the basis for using submaximal HR (e.g., HR at 600 m/min) as a fitness proxy in JRA trackwork monitoring.

**PCV:** Training-intensity-independent increase confirms that splenic RBC mobilisation at maximal exercise increases with any type of training. PCV at peak exercise is a universal training adaptation and cannot distinguish high- vs. low-intensity conditioned horses.

**Practical implication for Japan:** Initial race entries of 2-year-olds in Japan are in June–July. Horses trained in Hokkaido through cold winter months with trot-only workouts may arrive at race debut with lower VO₂max (150 vs. 165 ml/kg/min) compared to horses with canter-inclusive winter training. Training location and training type before debut are potential model features.

## Limitations

- Small n (6 per group); study was adequately powered for VO₂max but underpowered to detect moderate HR and La differences
- Only 2-year-olds; adult horses may respond differently (Knight et al. 1991)
- Training at Hidaka (Hokkaido); horses not raced; no performance outcome data
- 8-week study; whether advantages persist through a racing season is unknown
- Body weight confound: same diet but different weight trajectories; genetic/individual appetite effects not controlled
- No muscle biopsy to confirm fibre-type or mitochondrial adaptations
- Pre-test VO₂max was similar between groups but Canter group was numerically 7.5 ml/kg/min higher (not significant); slight randomisation imbalance

## Feature-engineering notes for the model

- `vo2max_ml_kg_min` — relative maximal oxygen uptake (ml/kg/min) from standardised treadmill test; Canter-trained 2-year-olds ≈ 165, Trot-only ≈ 150; **not routinely available** in race databases but present in ERI research records and some training centre monitoring programmes; expected effect: negative on finishing rank (higher VO₂max → better performance)
- `training_type_pre_debut` — categorical: "canter-inclusive" vs. "trot-only" winter training; derivable from training logs at Hidaka YTF if available; proxy for VO₂max tier at debut
- `body_weight_change_8wk_kg` — change in 馬体重 over the 8 weeks before a race (from race-card records); Canter cohort −10 kg, Trot cohort +10 kg; **directly available** from JRA/NAR 馬体重 records for each start; negative weight change during hard training = metabolic conditioning signal; expected effect: moderate weight decrease → better relative VO₂max, positive for performance
- `hr_at_600mpm_bpm` — heart rate at 600 m/min during trackwork (submaximal HR); Canter group HR 190–210 bpm at this speed (80–90% HRmax); lower post-training HR at same speed signals improved aerobic economy; **available if JRA/NAR trackwork HR is recorded** (Polar monitors are used); expected effect: lower submaximal HR → better finishing position
- `pcv_peak_pct` — peak PCV during maximal exercise test; both groups 55–58% pre-test, 58–61% post-test; training-intensity-independent; use as generic fitness/conditioning signal, not training-type discriminator; **available from pre-race blood panels**; interaction with distance (PCV matters more for longer races)
- `trackwork_speed_max_mpm` — maximum training speed (m/min) in the month before race; horses in Canter group reached 800 m/min; this is derivable from JRA trackwork time records (時計); expected effect: higher trackwork speed → higher VO₂max tier → better performance; **directly available**
- **Interaction**: `training_type × race_distance_m` — canter-trained horses' VO₂max advantage is more impactful over longer distances (≥1600 m) where aerobic ceiling constrains performance
- **Caution**: Body weight decrease may confound VO₂max relative values; always include 馬体重 as a covariate when using weight-change features
